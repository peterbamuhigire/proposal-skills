# Case Interview Frameworks for Proposal Methodology Design

Source: *Hacking the Case Interview* by Taylor Warfield (2017). These frameworks, originally designed for consulting case interviews, translate directly into structured methodology sections for consulting proposals. The underlying logic is identical: decompose a client problem into MECE analytical components, apply quantitative and qualitative tools, and converge on an evidence-based recommendation.

---

## 1. The Eight-Bucket Framework System

The book's core framework strategy is to memorise eight analytical buckets and select three to four for any given engagement. This maps directly to methodology design: for any proposal, select the most relevant analytical lenses from a standard repertoire, then customise sub-activities to the specific assignment.

### The Eight Buckets

1. **Market Attractiveness** -- Market size, growth rate, average profit margins, major trends and changes, new technologies, new regulations, market maturity (developing vs. mature), market convergence with adjacent markets.

2. **Competitive Landscape** -- Who are the competitors, market share distribution, competitor product lines, competitor capabilities, points of differentiation, barriers to entry (economic and non-economic).

3. **Company Attractiveness / Company Capabilities** -- Product line breadth, product differentiation, market share, profitability, distribution channels, partnerships, buying power, go-to-market strategy, geographic presence, growth trajectory.

4. **Customer Segmentation and Needs** -- Customer segment identification, segment characteristics, needs and preferences per segment, how customers are changing, profitability per segment.

5. **Financial Considerations** -- Revenue elements, cost elements, pricing strategy, breakeven timeline, cost of acquisition, financial exit strategy, profitability analysis.

6. **Synergies** -- Revenue synergies (cross-selling, new customer bases, shared infrastructure), cost synergies (headcount reduction, overhead consolidation, increased buying power), synergy realisability, timeline to realise.

7. **Risks and Mitigations** -- Risk identification, likelihood and impact assessment, mitigation strategies. A versatile bucket applicable to any engagement.

8. **Create Your Own Bucket** -- A custom analytical lens specific to the assignment that does not fit neatly into buckets 1-7.

### Application to Proposal Methodology

When designing a methodology section, treat these eight buckets as a checklist of possible analytical phases. For a given ToR:

- **Step 1:** Mentally run through all eight and select the three to four most relevant to the assignment objective.
- **Step 2:** For each selected bucket, define two to three specific sub-activities tailored to the client context.
- **Step 3:** Sequence the selected buckets into methodology phases.

This guarantees a custom methodology for every proposal -- never a recycled generic framework -- while ensuring no major analytical dimension is overlooked.

## 2. Case Type Frameworks as Methodology Templates

The book provides sample frameworks for common case types. Each maps to a consulting proposal methodology structure:

### Market Entry Methodology

Use when the assignment involves evaluating whether a client should enter a new market, launch a new service line, or expand geographically.

| Phase | Analytical Focus | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| Market Attractiveness Assessment | Size, growth rate, average margins | Is this market large enough and growing? |
| Company Capabilities Assessment | Leverageable capabilities, relevant experience, risk tolerance | Can the client actually compete? |
| Competitive Landscape Analysis | Market concentration, competitor differentiation, barriers to entry | How hard is it to enter and survive? |
| Financial Feasibility | Profitability projections, breakeven timeline | Will it be profitable, and when? |

### Profitability Diagnosis Methodology

Use when the assignment involves diagnosing why a client is losing money, why performance has declined, or how to improve financial results.

| Phase | Analytical Focus | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| Financial Decomposition | Revenue (quantity x price) vs. costs (variable + fixed) | Where exactly is money being lost? |
| Competitive Landscape Review | Competitor actions, new entrants, competitor profitability | Is this a company-specific or market-wide problem? |
| Customer Segment Analysis | Preference shifts, purchasing behaviour changes, customer attrition | Are customers leaving or changing? |
| Market Trends Review | New technologies, new regulations, market structural changes | Are external forces driving the decline? |

### Merger and Acquisition Evaluation Methodology

Use when the assignment involves evaluating a potential acquisition, merger, partnership, or institutional consolidation.

| Phase | Analytical Focus | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| Target Attractiveness Assessment | Profitability, differentiated capabilities, brand strength | Is the target worth acquiring? |
| Market Attractiveness Assessment | Market size, growth rate, average margins of target's market | Is the target's market attractive? |
| Synergy Analysis | Revenue synergies, cost synergies, realisability, timeline | What value does the combination create? |
| Financial Evaluation | Acquisition price fairness, breakeven analysis, exit strategy | Does the deal make financial sense? |

### Pricing Strategy Methodology

Use when the assignment involves pricing a new product, service, or offering.

| Phase | Analytical Focus | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| Customer Willingness-to-Pay Analysis | Survey/focus group findings, ability to pay, insurance/subsidy factors | How much will customers actually pay? |
| Competitor Pricing Benchmarking | Competitor prices, competitor product attributes, relative positioning | Where do competitors price, and why? |
| Cost-Plus Profitability Analysis | Production costs, margin targets, breakeven requirements | What price covers costs and meets targets? |
| Alternative Pricing Strategy Evaluation | Discounting, bundling, subscription, tiered pricing | Which pricing model maximises revenue? |

## 3. Quantitative Analysis Patterns

### Market Sizing (Top-Down Method)

The book strongly recommends top-down over bottom-up for market sizing. The standard structure:

1. Start with total population (national or regional)
2. Segment by relevant demographic (age, income, geography, industry)
3. Estimate percentage of each segment that is a potential customer
4. Estimate consumption frequency per customer
5. Estimate average price or spend per transaction
6. Multiply across to derive market size

**Methodology application:** Use this structure when a proposal methodology requires demand estimation, needs assessment quantification, market opportunity sizing, or baseline establishment. Present the segmentation logic as part of the analytical approach.

**Key principle:** Structure the approach first (no numbers), get alignment, then populate with data. This translates to proposals as: describe the analytical framework in the methodology, explain the data sources separately.

### Breakeven Analysis

The standard equations:

- **Profit Equation:** Profit = (Quantity x Price) - [(Quantity x Variable Costs) + Fixed Costs]
- **Breakeven Equation:** Quantity x (Price - Variable Costs) = Fixed Costs

Three of the four variables are given; solve for the fourth.

**Methodology application:** Use when a proposal methodology includes financial feasibility analysis, investment appraisal, cost-benefit analysis, or sustainability assessment. Present the breakeven framework as the quantitative backbone of the financial analysis phase.

### Profitability Decomposition

The standard decomposition tree:

```
Profit
|-- Revenue
|   |-- Quantity sold
|   |-- Price per unit
|-- Costs
    |-- Variable costs (cost per unit x quantity)
    |-- Fixed costs
```

**Methodology application:** When a proposal involves financial diagnosis, this decomposition tree becomes the analytical framework for the financial assessment phase. Systematically identify whether the problem is revenue-driven or cost-driven, then drill into the specific driver.

### Weighted Average / Mix Analysis

Demonstrated in the Diamond Co. case: overall profitability can decline even when individual product margins hold steady, if the sales mix shifts towards lower-margin products.

**Methodology application:** Use when analysing portfolio performance, service line profitability, or resource allocation efficiency. The methodology should explicitly state that mix effects will be isolated from margin effects.

## 4. Structured Problem-Solving Approach

### The Issue Tree / Logic Tree

The book's entire framework strategy is built on issue tree logic:

1. **Define the top-level objective** (from the ToR)
2. **Decompose into three to four major branches** (the buckets)
3. **Each branch has two to three sub-branches** (the sub-activities)
4. **Branches must be MECE** -- no overlap, no gaps
5. **Every branch must be relevant** to the top-level objective

This maps directly to methodology phase design:

- The top-level objective is the assignment objective from the ToR
- Major branches become methodology phases
- Sub-branches become key activities within each phase
- MECE compliance ensures no duplication and no scope gaps

### Hypothesis-Driven Analysis

The book demonstrates this throughout its case examples. The pattern:

1. Form an initial hypothesis based on available information
2. Structure analysis to test the hypothesis
3. Gather data that confirms or refutes
4. Refine the hypothesis based on evidence
5. Converge on a conclusion

**Methodology application:** Frame each methodology phase as testing a specific hypothesis branch. This transforms a methodology from a list of activities into an analytical narrative with intellectual momentum.

## 5. Communication and Presentation Structures

### The Conclusion Structure (Top-Down Recommendation)

The book prescribes this exact structure for all recommendations:


"I recommend [recommendation] for the following [N] reasons:

1. [Evidence-based reason]
2. [Evidence-based reason]
3. [Evidence-based reason]

For these reasons, I recommend [recommendation].

For next steps, I would like to look into:

1. [Further analysis area]
2. [Further analysis area]"


**Methodology application:** This is the structure the methodology should promise for the final deliverable. Signal in the methodology that all recommendations will follow a structured format: recommendation first, supporting evidence second, next steps third. This reassures evaluators that outputs will be actionable.

### Mini-Frameworks for Structuring Qualitative Analysis

Four structuring pairs that create instant analytical organisation:

| Pair | Use When |
|---|---|
| **Internal / External** | Analysing factors affecting an organisation (internal capabilities vs. external environment) |
| **Short-term / Long-term** | Evaluating benefits, costs, impacts, or recommendations with different time horizons |
| **Economic / Non-economic** | Distinguishing financial factors from non-financial ones (governance, capacity, reputation) |
| **Quantitative / Qualitative** | Separating measurable metrics from judgement-based assessments |

**Methodology application:** Use these as structuring devices within methodology phase descriptions. For example: "Phase 2 will assess barriers to entry, examining both economic barriers (capital requirements, economies of scale) and non-economic barriers (regulatory compliance, institutional knowledge, brand recognition)."

## 6. Business Concepts as Analytical Tools

The book covers fundamental business concepts that function as analytical tools in methodology design:

### Market Structure Analysis
- **Fragmented vs. concentrated markets** -- Determines competitive intensity, barriers to entry, and strategic positioning options
- **Market share analysis** -- Revenue in market / total market size
- **Barriers to entry** -- Capital, technical knowledge, brand name, distribution channels, economies of scale, technology, regulation, product differentiation

### Value Chain Analysis
- **Supplier-Manufacturer-Distributor-Retailer chain** -- Identify where the client sits and where value is created or lost
- **Vertical integration assessment** -- Does the client control multiple stages?

### Pricing Strategy Toolkit
Three pricing methods, each with distinct data requirements:

1. **Customer willingness-to-pay** -- Survey/focus group based; most direct but requires primary data
2. **Competitive benchmarking** -- Price against competitor offerings; requires comparable products
3. **Cost-plus pricing** -- Production cost plus target margin; guarantees margins but ignores demand

### Financial Metrics
- **Profit margin** = Profit / Revenue (measures efficiency of converting revenue to profit)
- **Return on investment (ROI)** = Profit from investment / Investment cost (measures investment attractiveness; always clarify time horizon)
- **Price sensitivity** -- How quantity demanded changes with price changes; critical for revenue optimisation
- **Economies of scale** -- Cost advantages from increased production volume; creates a virtuous cycle of lower costs, lower prices, higher volume

### Revenue Growth Strategies
- **Organic growth:** Increase revenue from existing products (volume or price) or sell new products
- **Inorganic growth:** Mergers, acquisitions, partnerships

### Cost Reduction Strategies
- **Variable cost reduction:** Switch suppliers, use substitutes, reduce material per unit, renegotiate prices
- **Fixed cost reduction:** Renegotiate contracts, invest in efficient equipment, consolidate facilities
- Note: variable costs are generally easier to reduce than fixed costs

### Synergy Analysis Framework
- **Revenue synergies:** Access to new customer bases, cross-selling, shared distribution channels
- **Cost synergies:** Headcount reduction from eliminating redundancies, reduced overhead from consolidating functions, increased buying power
- Always assess: Are synergies actually realisable? What is the timeline?

## 7. Analytical Sense-Checking

The book emphasises always performing a sense check on quantitative results before presenting them:

1. **Reasonableness test** -- Does this number make sense given what I know?
2. **Benchmarking** -- Can I compare this to a known reference point?
3. **Order of magnitude check** -- Am I in the right ballpark?

**Methodology application:** Build sense-checking into the methodology as a quality assurance step. For every quantitative analysis phase, state that results will be validated against benchmarks, comparable projects, or known reference data before being presented to the client.

## 8. Situation Assessment Framework

The book identifies key dimensions for understanding any client situation. Use as a diagnostic checklist in inception and scoping phases:

- What does the company/organisation do?
- How is it performing in its market/sector?
- What major actions has it taken recently?
- How is the broader market/sector performing?
- What is the competitive/institutional landscape?
- What have competitors/peers been doing differently?
- What are the stated objectives (and are there unstated ones)?

## Summary: How to Use This Reference

When designing a methodology section for a proposal:

1. **Identify the assignment type** -- Market entry? Profitability diagnosis? M&A evaluation? Pricing? Mixed?
2. **Select the matching framework template** from Section 2 as a starting point
3. **Run through the eight buckets** from Section 1 to verify no major analytical dimension is missing
4. **Build the issue tree** per Section 4 -- top-level objective, three to four branches (phases), two to three sub-branches (activities) per phase
5. **Embed quantitative analysis** from Section 3 where the ToR requires financial, market, or feasibility analysis
6. **Structure qualitative analysis** using the mini-frameworks from Section 5
7. **Apply the conclusion structure** from Section 5 to describe how the final deliverable will present recommendations
8. **Build in sense-checking** from Section 7 as a quality assurance element

## 9. Process Bottleneck Framework

**Use for**: operations improvement, value chain optimisation, efficiency reviews, process reengineering assignments.

For cases involving processes, efficiency, or capacity problems, decompose the end-to-end process and examine each step for constraints:

| Step | Action | Key Question |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Map the full process chain from input to output | What are the sequential steps? |
| 2 | For each step, assess whether it is the constraint | Is this step limiting overall throughput? |
| 3 | Identify the bottleneck step | Where does the system slow down? |
| 4 | Generate improvement options specific to that step | What interventions address the constraint? |

**Proposal methodology language:** "Our approach maps the end-to-end process from [input] to [output], systematically assessing each step to identify the binding constraint on throughput before designing targeted interventions."

## 10. Stakeholder-Based Framework

**Use for**: healthcare, education, public sector, multi-party institutional assignments where multiple actors influence outcomes.

Structure the analysis around each stakeholder group and the levers they control:

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Identify all relevant stakeholders in the ecosystem |
| 2 | For each stakeholder, identify the levers they control |
| 3 | Assess what actions each stakeholder can take to address the problem |
| 4 | Prioritise stakeholders by impact potential |

**Template (drug adoption example):**
- Patients — awareness, willingness to request
- Physicians — awareness, willingness to prescribe
- Insurers — reimbursement policy, formulary inclusion
- Company — production capacity, distribution readiness

**Proposal methodology language:** "Phase 2 will assess stakeholder readiness across [list], examining the levers each controls and the incentives required to drive adoption."

## 11. Competitive Response Framework

**Use for**: reactive strategy assignments, market positioning, responding to disruption or new entrants.

When a competitor takes a specific action, structure the analysis:

| Component | Key Questions |
|---|---|
| **Threat sizing** | How large and direct is the threat? Does the competitor have differentiation? |
| **Customer response** | How will customers react? How likely are they to switch? |
| **Competitor response** | Will other competitors also react? How? |
| **Strategic options** | Do nothing / Match / Counter-attack / Differentiate / Exit |

**Proposal methodology language:** "The methodology sequences threat assessment before strategy development — sizing the competitive threat, modelling customer and market response, then evaluating strategic options."

## 12. Criteria-Based Option Evaluation

**Use for**: any assignment requiring selection among alternatives — site selection, technology choice, policy options, institutional reform pathways.

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Define evaluation criteria BEFORE assessing options |
| 2 | Assess each option against each criterion |
| 3 | Weight criteria by importance (agreed with the client) |
| 4 | Select the option that performs best across weighted criteria |

**Proposal methodology language:** "We will evaluate [options] against [N] criteria: [list]. For each criterion, we will assess which option performs best and why, using a weighted scoring matrix agreed with the client before evaluation begins."

## 13. Expected Value Decision Framework

**Use for**: investment appraisal, risk-adjusted decision making, scenario analysis, go/no-go decisions.

For decisions with uncertain outcomes, calculate expected value:

**Formula:** EV = Σ (Probability of Scenario × Financial Outcome)

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Identify discrete scenarios (e.g., market growth vs. decline) |
| 2 | Assign probabilities to each scenario |
| 3 | Within each scenario, identify sub-outcomes |
| 4 | Assign probabilities and financial outcomes to sub-outcomes |
| 5 | Calculate expected value for each path |
| 6 | Weight by scenario probability for overall expected value |
| 7 | Decision rule: positive EV = proceed; negative EV = do not |

**Proposal methodology language:** "Financial feasibility will be assessed using expected value analysis across [N] scenarios, weighted by probability of occurrence, to provide a risk-adjusted basis for the investment decision."

## 14. Build vs. Buy vs. Partner Framework

**Use for**: capability development strategy, market entry, institutional reform options, technology acquisition decisions.

| Mode | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|
| **Build internally** | Full control, no integration risk | Slow, requires investment, hiring |
| **Partnership / JV** | Access to partner's capabilities, shared risk | Shared profits, less control, dependency |
| **Acquire** | Immediate capabilities, brand, customer base | High cost, integration risk, cultural clash |

**Proposal methodology language:** "The options appraisal will evaluate three capability acquisition modes — build, partner, and acquire — against [criteria], with a recommended pathway and implementation sequence."

## 15. Adjacent Market Proximity Model

**Use for**: growth strategy, diversification, market expansion assignments.

Structure adjacent market opportunities by distance from core:

| Tier | Description | Feasibility | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core-adjacent** | Same domain, different product | Highest | Lowest |
| **Product-adjacent** | Same product type, different domain | High | Low-Medium |
| **Sector-adjacent** | Same broad sector, different product type | Medium | Medium |
| **Sector-distant** | Same sector, entirely different business model | Lowest | Highest |

**Principle:** Closest adjacencies are most feasible and likely to succeed. Evaluate from inside out.

**Proposal methodology language:** "Growth opportunities will be mapped by proximity to the core business, with closer adjacencies evaluated first for feasibility and risk-adjusted return."

## 16. Sales Cannibalisation Analysis

**Use for**: product portfolio strategy, new offering launch, service line expansion.

**Formula:**
```
Net Profit Impact = [New Product Volume × (Price - Variable Cost)]
                  - Additional Fixed Costs
                  - Σ [Cannibalised Volume per Product × Margin per Product]
```

**Key insight:** A new product can appear profitable in isolation but destroy value when cannibalisation of higher-margin existing products is accounted for.

**Proposal methodology language:** "The financial analysis will account for potential cannibalisation of existing revenues, isolating the net incremental profit contribution of the proposed [product/service]."

## 17. Eight First-Principles Strategies for Problem Structuring

From *Conquer Your Consulting Case Interview* — eight distinct lenses for generating MECE top-level structures. Any one can be applied to any problem:

| # | Strategy | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Process Steps** | Map the flow of how a good is produced or service rendered | Operations, value chain, efficiency |
| 2 | **Components** | Identify physical/digital/human components needed | Infrastructure, system design |
| 3 | **Segmenting** | Break by business segments, products, or customers | Portfolio analysis, market assessment |
| 4 | **Matrix Approach** | Combine two perspectives into a grid (segments × criteria) | Option evaluation, comparison |
| 5 | **Problem Drivers** | List variables that directly influence the outcome | Root cause analysis, KPI design |
| 6 | **Mathematical Representation** | Express the target as a formula | Financial targets, KPI-driven scoping |
| 7 | **Using Opposites** | Create exhaustive coverage through antonyms | Ensuring MECE compliance |
| 8 | **Stakeholder Approach** | Structure around key stakeholders | Multi-party assignments |

**Proposal application:** When designing a methodology's conceptual framework, select the most appropriate structuring strategy from these eight. The matrix approach is especially powerful for evaluation frameworks in proposals.

## 18. SCORE Storytelling Framework

For structuring engagement narratives in proposals and presentations:

| Element | Description | Proposal Application |
|---|---|---|
| **S — Situation** | Brief description of context | Client background in understanding of assignment |
| **C — Complication** | Issues faced, why it was challenging | The problem or gap that creates the need |
| **O — Outcome Expectation** | Worst possible outcome if no intervention | Creates urgency — "If nothing changes, [consequence]" |
| **R — Remedial Action** | Detailed actions to overcome challenges (80-90% of content) | The methodology — what we will do |
| **E — End Result** | Successful outcome | Expected benefits and deliverables |

**What makes SCORE different from SCQA:** The "O" (Outcome Expectation) element creates stakes before the action, building urgency. SCQA moves from Question to Answer; SCORE moves from worst-case to intervention to result.

**Proposal language pattern:** Use SCORE for past experience narratives: "The client faced [situation]. [Complication] threatened [worst outcome]. We [remedial actions]. The result was [measurable end result]."

## 19. Three Idea Expansion Techniques

For developing depth beneath any top-level methodology structure:

| Technique | Direction | Method |
|---|---|---|
| **Expansion** | Top-down | Take each bucket, create 3-5 sub-areas, expand each into concrete actions |
| **Extremes** | Lateral | Imagine the extreme action, then dial back to feasible variations |
| **Linking** | Bottom-up/sideways | Start with one idea, go down (detail), up (generalise), or sideways (adjacent) |

**Proposal application:** When a methodology section feels thin, apply these techniques to develop richer sub-activities. "Extremes" is particularly useful for generating the full range of options before narrowing to recommendations.
