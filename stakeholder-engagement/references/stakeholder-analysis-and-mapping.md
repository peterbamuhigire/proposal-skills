# Stakeholder Analysis, Mapping, and Classification

Reference material for the stakeholder-engagement skill. Provides detailed guidance on stakeholder identification, classification models, analysis frameworks, and mapping techniques. Use when designing stakeholder analysis components for proposals or when the ToR requires a stakeholder engagement plan.

Sources: Worsley (Stakeholder-led Project Management, 2nd ed., 2020), Harrin (Engaging Stakeholders on Projects, 2020), Rendtorff (Stakeholder Theory, 2016), Huemann (Rethink Project Stakeholder Management, 2016), Bosenberg (Stakeholder Mapping Deep Dive, 2023).

---

## 1. Stakeholder Identification Methods

### Three Core Approaches (Harrin)

| Method | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Stakeholder lists** | Brainstorm all possible stakeholders; start broad, narrow later | Initial identification |
| **Stakeholder segments** | Use pre-defined categories (40+ types) to prompt identification | Ensuring no groups are overlooked |
| **Ask known stakeholders** | Each identified stakeholder names others who should be involved | Discovering hidden stakeholders |

### For Role-Based Stakeholders (Worsley)

| Technique | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **OBS analysis** | Use organisational breakdown structure to identify stakeholders by unit | Ensuring no organisational groups are missed |
| **Governance checklists** | Standard project roles (PRINCE2, PMI, PPDA) | Starting point for role understanding |
| **"Who else should I talk to?"** | Structured exercise with sponsor and core team | Gaining agreement on scope boundaries |

### For Agenda-Based Stakeholders (Worsley)

| Technique | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus groups** | Small group (max 10) brainstorm using scenario-based questions | Complex analysis of stakeholder landscape |
| **Semi-structured interviews** | Private one-on-one conversations | Where private dialogue yields more than group settings |
| **PESTLE analysis** | Strategic external environmental scan | Prompting broad thinking, avoiding identification bias |
| **Snowball sampling** | Cascading approach — ask stakeholders to identify others | Building relationships with fringe and marginalised groups |
| **Stakeholder visualisation** | Maps, geographic mapping, media profiling, location scouting | Identifying missed groups in spatial contexts |

### Two Critical Premises (Worsley)

1. **Do not do it alone** — set up identification as a collaborative process
2. **It does not only happen at the beginning** — some stakeholders only emerge as project impacts become clearer

## 2. Stakeholder Classification Systems

### Primary/Secondary/Key (Bosenberg)

- **Primary stakeholders** — directly affected by the assignment (end users, beneficiaries, staff whose roles change)
- **Secondary stakeholders** — not directly involved but can influence or be influenced
- **Key stakeholders** — from either group but distinguished by high influence or interest

### Role-Based vs Agenda-Based (Worsley)

| Type | Characteristics | Identification Method |
|---|---|---|
| **Role-based** | Direct, definable role in the project (sponsor, users, suppliers) | Governance structures, domain knowledge, checklists |
| **Agenda-based** | No easy-to-define relationship beyond interests and ability to impact | Collaborative investigation, PESTLE, snowball sampling |

### Seven Stakeholder Types (Bosenberg)

1. **Internal** — employees, management, departments
2. **External** — customers, suppliers, partners
3. **Regulatory/Authority** — government bodies, certification bodies
4. **Community/Social** — local communities, NGOs, advocacy groups
5. **Financial** — investors, shareholders, banks
6. **Influencers/Opinion Leaders** — media, industry leaders
7. **Passive** — observers with peripheral interest

### Voluntary vs Involuntary (Clarkson, via Rendtorff)

- **Voluntary stakeholders** — chose to invest human or financial capital; bear risk by choice
- **Involuntary stakeholders** — placed at risk by the organisation's activities without choosing to be

**Proposal application:** In safeguards and GESI sections, distinguish between stakeholders who opted in and those affected without consent. This distinction drives grievance redress mechanism design.

### Normative vs Derivative (Phillips, via Rendtorff)

- **Normative stakeholders** — those to whom the organisation has a moral obligation (arising from cooperative benefit)
- **Derivative stakeholders** — those whose actions must be accounted for because they can affect normative stakeholders (media, competitors, activists)

**Proposal application:** In governance proposals, normative stakeholders get decision-making seats; derivative stakeholders get consultation mechanisms.

### Four-Tier Classification (Lepineux, via Rendtorff)

1. **Archetypal actors** — shareholders, employees, clients, suppliers
2. **Recognised actors** — banks, unions, public authorities, international organisations, NGOs
3. **Controversial actors** — competitors, media, activists, natural environment
4. **Public actors** — regulatory and governmental bodies

## 3. Hidden Stakeholder Categories (Worsley)

Three types that project managers must anticipate:

| Type | Behaviour | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Sleepers** | Have not yet woken up to the project; may sleep on or awaken at the worst possible time | Late entry can be disastrous — they have a habit of waking up at the worst possible time |
| **Spoilers** | Know about the project but choose when and how to show interest | Purposefully choose the most obstructive time to appear |
| **Lurkers (Passives)** | Loiter around the edge, preferring not to get involved but watching | At least 40% of stakeholders sit in this category (D'Herbemont and Cesar) |

### Faith-holders, Hate-holders, Fake-holders (Luoma-aho, via Shander)

| Type | Description | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Faith-holders** | Supporters — maintain them; they attract new stakeholders | Nurture; use as ambassadors |
| **Hate-holders** | Detractors — engage them for invaluable insight | Converting them creates the most ardent advocates |
| **Fake-holders** | Not genuinely affected (bots, bad-faith actors) | Identify and exclude from engagement |

## 4. Stakeholder Analysis Frameworks

### 4.1 Power-Interest Matrix (Multiple sources)

The standard tool. Two axes — Power (influence over outcomes) and Interest (attention to the project):

| | Low Interest | High Interest |
|---|---|---|
| **High Power** | Keep satisfied — efficient high-level communication, executive summaries, showcase alignment to strategic goals | Manage closely — active participation in decisions, frequent detailed updates, two-way transparent communication |
| **Low Power** | Monitor — minimal resource investment, as-needed communication | Keep informed — regular updates, forums for engagement, accessible language, acknowledge contributions |

**Common mistakes (Worsley):**
- Assuming sponsors are genuinely interested (they may not be)
- Equating power with organisational position rather than power within the project context
- Analysis must always be relative to the specific project, not the organisation generally

### 4.2 Stakeholder Salience Model — Mitchell, Agle & Wood (1997)

Three attributes determine salience:
- **Power** — capacity to impose will on the organisation
- **Legitimacy** — socially accepted and expected claim
- **Urgency** — time-sensitivity and criticality of the claim

Seven stakeholder types from attribute combinations:

| Type | Attributes | Salience | Engagement Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Definitive** | Power + Legitimacy + Urgency | High | Must be included in decision-making; core stakeholders |
| **Dominant** | Power + Legitimacy | Medium | Formal engagement mechanisms; steering committee representation |
| **Dangerous** | Power + Urgency | Medium | Monitor closely; can disrupt if not managed; meet agenda or isolate impact |
| **Dependent** | Legitimacy + Urgency | Medium | Advocate on their behalf; they lack power to act alone; may influence power-holders |
| **Dormant** | Power only | Low | Watch and recheck; beware of over-communicating |
| **Discretionary** | Legitimacy only | Low | Keep informed; CSR and goodwill activities |
| **Demanding** | Urgency only | Low | Low priority; beware being drawn into over-focusing |

**Proposal application:** Use this typology in stakeholder analysis sections to demonstrate you can distinguish between groups requiring active management versus monitoring.

### 4.3 Sociodynamics Model — D'Herbemont & Cesar (via Worsley)

Two dimensions: **Synergy** (energy in support) and **Antagonism** (energy in opposition). Eight stakeholder attitudes:

| Attitude | Synergy | Antagonism | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Zealots** | Very High | None | Great champions but poor persuaders — cannot appreciate others' views |
| **Golden Triangles** | High | Low | Best influencers — positive supporters who relate to others' positions. Target as allies. |
| **Waverers** | Medium | Medium | Critical targets — genuinely influence the passives; asking "what's in it for me?" |
| **Passives (Dead-weights)** | Low | Low | Silent majority (~40%+); the real prize — direction they move determines success |
| **Opponents** | Low | High | Against the project but sensitive to influence; addressable through engagement |
| **Mutineers** | None | Very High | Against the project and insensitive to any influence — isolate their impact |
| **Moaners** | Low | Medium | High negative energy, low positive; distinguish from waverers |
| **Schismatics** | Medium | High | Split loyalties; both support and oppose aspects |

**The Magpie Effect (Worsley):** Over-focusing on the loudest voice (typically opponents) at the expense of broader engagement. Also manifests as "focus on friends" — reinforcing existing relationships while neglecting new ones the project needs.

### 4.4 Stakeholder Expectations Matrix (Clarke, via Rendtorff)

| Stakeholder | Direct Expectations | Information Required |
|---|---|---|
| Employees | Remuneration, job security, training | Company reports, negotiations |
| Shareholders | Dividends, capital appreciation | Annual reports |
| Clients | Quality, service, security, value for money | Documentation, publicity |
| Suppliers | Stable long-term relationship | Prompt payment |
| Government | Legal compliance, employment, accurate data | Official reports |
| Public | Operational safety, community contribution | Safety reports |
| Environment | Sustainable resources, non-polluting activities | Environmental reports |

**Proposal application:** Adapt this matrix for any ToR to show you understand what each stakeholder group expects from the consulting engagement.

### 4.5 Stakeholder Interest Intensity Index (Bourne & Walker, via Worsley)

Quantifies stakeholder interest and influence against specific project success areas:
- Creates a Vested Interest Index (VII) showing which success factors each stakeholder cares about most
- Reveals where stakeholder agendas differ and where they align
- Useful for prioritising engagement effort by success factor

### 4.6 Management OF vs Management FOR Stakeholders (Huemann)

| Dimension | Management OF | Management FOR |
|---|---|---|
| **Orientation** | Project-centric | Values-based |
| **Core question** | How can stakeholders serve the project? | What is fair for all stakeholders? |
| **Prioritisation** | By power, legitimacy, urgency | By fairness and moral obligation |
| **Risk** | Manipulative orientation | May slow decisions |
| **Best for** | Technical deliverables with clear outcomes | Community-facing, reform, and infrastructure projects |

**Proposal application:** Propose combining both approaches — different stakeholder groups require different approaches, and the appropriate approach changes across project phases.

## 5. The Stakeholder Project Continuum (Worsley)

Projects vary in stakeholder complexity. The continuum determines how much stakeholder engagement shapes the project:

| Type | Characteristics | Stakeholder Role | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stakeholder-Neutral (Type 0-1)** | Unambiguous outcomes; role-based stakeholders; communication peaks at start and implementation | Stakeholders are informed | Simple upgrade, office move |
| **Stakeholder-Sensitive (Type 2)** | Clear goals but changes impact valued practices; mix of role and agenda-based stakeholders | Stakeholders influence how the project is conducted | Office relocation requiring behavioural change |
| **Stakeholder-Led (Type 3)** | Solution determined by what stakeholders will commit to; large numbers of influential agenda-based stakeholders | Planning IS the planning of engagement | Public infrastructure, institutional reform |

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. Where on the continuum does the project sit now?
2. What factors could shift its position?
3. How does the level of stakeholder complexity affect management approach?

**Proposal application:** In methodology sections, classify the assignment on this continuum and explain how your stakeholder approach is calibrated accordingly. This demonstrates you do not apply a one-size-fits-all template.

## 6. Seven-Step Stakeholder Map Creation Process (Bosenberg)

1. **Identify all possible stakeholders** — brainstorming + cross-team input
2. **Categorise by characteristics** — internal vs external; type of influence (authority, resources, expertise, interest)
3. **Analyse interest and influence** — assess each stakeholder's level on both dimensions
4. **Map on the matrix** — place each stakeholder in the appropriate quadrant
5. **Develop engagement strategies per quadrant** — tailored approach for each group
6. **Define communication plans** — frequency, channels, content tailored to quadrant
7. **Regularly update** — review at milestones, adapt strategies as positions shift

---

## 7. Stakeholder Map Update Process (Bosenberg)

### Ten Steps

1. Review current map and project status
2. Identify changes in stakeholder landscape (new stakeholders, changed roles)
3. Gather information (stakeholder analysis, collect feedback)
4. Update stakeholder information (roles, interests, influence levels)
5. Reassess quadrant placements
6. Update engagement strategies
7. Validate updates (cross-check with other documents, seek confirmation)
8. Share updates with project team
9. Establish routine schedule for regular updates
10. Document lessons learned and insights

### Seven Signs the Map Needs Updating

- Changes in project scope or objectives
- Organisational changes (restructuring, personnel changes)
- Shifts in external environment (market, regulatory)
- Stakeholder feedback indicating unanticipated concerns
- Current engagement strategies proving ineffective
- Inconsistencies with other project documents (risk register, communication plan)
- Intuition based on observed behavioural shifts

## 8. Sources of Stakeholder Power (Worsley, via Lukes)

### Dimension 1 — Overt Power

| Source | Description |
|---|---|
| **Positional (Authority)** | From hierarchical position |
| **Resource** | Control over resources that enable action |
| **Expertise** | Respect for knowledge and skills bearing on the matter |
| **Negative (Veto)** | Power to block — ultimately the basis of democracy |

### Dimension 2 — Influence Power

| Source | Description |
|---|---|
| **Coercive** | Based on fear of punishment |
| **Status** | Social approval, standing in community |
| **Charisma** | Personal magnetism |
| **Reward** | Ability to incentivise |
| **Connection** | Links to others regarded as having power |

### Dimension 3 — Covert Power

- Control of agendas and information
- "He who sets the agenda controls the outcome of the debate"
- Determines what governance groups think about, not what they think

### Stakeholder Power Strategies

- **Withholding/constraining resources** — restricting access or putting conditions on use
- **Coalition-building** — alliances with others sharing common agendas
- **Credibility-building and communication** — using media to increase legitimacy of claims
- **Conflict escalation** — troublemaking to slow projects and awaken sleepers

**Proposal application:** In stakeholder analysis sections, show that you understand power operates at multiple levels — not just organisational hierarchy. This distinguishes proposals from those that equate seniority with influence.

## 9. Sustainable Development Integration (Huemann)

Four principles for integrating sustainability into stakeholder analysis:

1. **Underpinning values** — strive for transparency, fairness, participation
2. **Balancing interests** — balance economic, ecological, and social interests of stakeholders
3. **Broadened temporal scale** — consider short-, medium-, and long-term perspectives, including future stakeholders
4. **Broadened spatial scale** — consider local, regional, and global stakeholders

**Modified stakeholder analysis format:**
- For each stakeholder: assess their economic, ecological, and social interests
- Assess short-term vs long-term impacts
- Assess local vs global implications
- Link stakeholder analysis to the investment/business case

**Proposal application:** In sustainability and GESI sections, show that stakeholder analysis considers future generations and environmental stakeholders — not just current, vocal groups.

## 10. RACI as Stakeholder Tool (Harrin + Worsley)

Beyond task assignment, RACI reveals stakeholder dynamics:

- **R**esponsible — creator of the deliverable
- **A**ccountable — gives approval (only one per task)
- **C**onsulted — opinions sought before decisions
- **I**nformed — receives one-way communication after decisions
- **S**upportive (RASCI variant) — provides resources or general support

**Three perspectives on any role (Worsley):** what the person thinks it is, what others think it is, what the person actually does. The power of RACI lies in creating clarity where ambiguity exists or is deliberately maintained.

**The RACI Triangle (Harrin):** At project level — one Accountable at top, several Responsible, larger Consulted group, largest Informed group. Flags consensus-based decisions (multiple R's = potential delay).

## 11. Stakeholder Analysis Quality Assurance

### Principles (Multiple sources)

- Stakeholder analysis is transitory — power, interest, and urgency change constantly (Harrin)
- Analysis works both ways — stakeholders are assessing you just as you assess them (Harrin)
- Keep analysis confidential — anything recorded should be sayable to the person's face (Harrin)
- Analysis must always be relative to the specific project, not the organisation generally (Worsley)
- Do not equate organisational seniority with project-level power (Worsley)

### Four Blocking Behaviours in Stakeholder Identification (Worsley)

1. **"Let's get on with it"** — sponsor wants to proceed before anyone notices
2. **"Just tell me what I need to do"** — PM fears more consultation = unsolvable complexity
3. **"It's a technical project"** — belief that no real change will occur
4. **"Don't worry, I'll talk to them"** — reluctant network-sharer

### Five Stakeholder Myths (Worsley)

1. "We manage our stakeholders" — you manage engagement, not people
2. "Everybody is a stakeholder" — the real challenge is focusing on the right stakeholders
3. "We know our stakeholders" — assumptions are made without adequate exploration
4. "It is all about communication (and more is better)" — poor communication causes more problems than none
5. "Some projects do not need stakeholder engagement" — disinterest can rapidly turn to violent opposition
