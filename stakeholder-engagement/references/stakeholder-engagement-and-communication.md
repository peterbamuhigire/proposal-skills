# Stakeholder Engagement, Communication, and Consultation

Reference material for the stakeholder-engagement skill. Provides detailed guidance on engagement lifecycles, communication frameworks, consultation methods, facilitation techniques, and group engagement approaches. Use when designing stakeholder engagement and communication components for proposals.

Sources: Harrin (Engaging Stakeholders on Projects, 2020), Worsley (Stakeholder-led Project Management, 2nd ed., 2020), Huemann (Rethink Project Stakeholder Management, 2016), Shander (Stakeholder Whispering, 2025), Bosenberg (Stakeholder Mapping Deep Dive, 2023).

---

## 1. The Stakeholder Engagement Life Cycle (Harrin)

Every stakeholder passes through four stages:

| Stage | Purpose | Activities |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Identification** | Discover who is impacted or affected | Brainstorming, segment analysis, snowball sampling |
| **2. Early Engagement** | Establish communication preferences, build first impressions | Learn management styles, understand point of view, identify supporters and resisters |
| **3. Mature Engagement** | Relationship based on past interactions | Stakeholder is embedded, aware of goals and expectations; ongoing management of unsupportive stakeholders |
| **4. Dissolution** | Stakeholder's involvement ends | Transfer relationships to permanent organisation; may need re-onboarding if involvement resumes |

---

## 2. Stakeholder Relationship Management Process (Huemann)

A four-phase iterative process framed as relationship management:

1. **Starting up** — identify stakeholders, analyse expectations, define potential impacts, plan first engagement activities
2. **Managing** — perform engagement activities at different levels; combine instrumental and values-based approaches per group
3. **Controlling** — periodically review stakeholder strategy; redesign relationships as needed; strategy changes during the project are normal
4. **Closing down** — final analysis of relationships; emotional close-out; explicit transfer of relationships to permanent organisation

### Methods by Process Step (Huemann)

| Process Step | Methods |
|---|---|
| **Starting up** | Stakeholder definition, analysis workshops, scenario techniques, stakeholder interviews, communication/engagement plan |
| **Managing** | Project newsletter, presentations, website, events, social media, workshops, individual meetings, integration of representatives in project organisation |
| **Controlling** | Project scorecard, stakeholder analysis review, strategy adjustment |
| **Closing down** | Final project marketing, "thank you" communication, future-oriented workshops, opening ceremony, symbolic acts, explicit transfer |

## 3. The Engagement Journey (Harrin)

For each stakeholder, identify their current position and move them one step:

1. **Resistant → Indifferent** — Use the Five Whys to uncover root cause of resistance
2. **Indifferent → Supportive** — Help them see relevance; connect project to their interests (WIIFT — What's In It For Them)
3. **Supportive → Proactive** — Empower them to champion the change; give them ownership

**Critical rule (Worsley):** You can only normally move stakeholders one place. An opponent to a waverer, not a mutineer to an ally.

---

## 4. The Whispering Evolution (Shander)

Three stages of stakeholder engagement maturity:

| Stage | Approach | Orientation |
|---|---|---|
| **Stakeholder Management** | Unilateral; defend against demands | Organisation-centric |
| **Stakeholder Engagement** | Collaborative; seat at the table | Two-way but surface |
| **Stakeholder Whispering** | Deep collaborative probing; guided self-discovery | Need-centric |

**Core insight:** Stakeholders do not know what they need. They know what they want, but wants and needs diverge because of cognitive biases and fast-thinking shortcuts. The consultant's role is to help stakeholders discover their own real needs — through questions, not telling.

## 5. The Engagement Formula (Harrin)

**Understanding + Action + Influence = Engagement**

### Dual Appeal Model

Engagement requires winning both:
- **Hearts** (emotional appeal) — how stakeholders feel about the project's effect on them
- **Minds** (rational appeal) — how confident they feel the work being done is the right work

### Types of Influence

- **Position-based** — authority from role or title
- **Knowledge-based** — expertise and information
- **Personality-based** — charisma, relationships, trust

## 6. Purposeful Communication Framework (Worsley)

Six distinct purposes for communication, each demanding different approaches:

### 6.1 Communication as Information-Giving

- Broadcasting project status to informed stakeholders
- **Pitfall:** regular communication becoming routine and losing effectiveness
- Must focus on audience needs; review and re-check effectiveness regularly

### 6.2 Communication as Information-Seeking

- Critical questions: who should we speak to, about what, who has the authority or expertise?
- Requires understanding stakeholders' sources of power
- **Warning:** wrong people + wrong questions = undeliverable expectations

### 6.3 Communication as Coordination

- Extreme detail required for tightly time-bound projects and complex team structures
- Success lies in the detail — hour-by-hour checkpoint schedules, escalation processes

### 6.4 Communication as Marketing

- Promotes the project positively to build goodwill
- Key question: what can we do that is likely to be well-received by those stakeholders that matter?

### 6.5 Communication as Persuasion

- Attempts to change stakeholder positions and align them with project aims
- Requires detailed planning based on well-investigated stakeholder perspectives
- **Persuasion plan fields:** stakeholder name, grouping, current rating, target rating, objections, WIIFT, strategy, influencer, commentary

### 6.6 Communication to Inspire Action

- Hearts-and-minds mobilisation — not about directing but inspiring autonomous action
- Key question: who is the right person to influence and inspire which stakeholder groups?

**Proposal application:** In communication plan sections, show that you design communication for specific purposes — not just "regular updates." Each communication activity should have a clear purpose from these six categories.

## 7. Communication Strategies by Stakeholder Quadrant (Bosenberg)

| Quadrant | Communication Style | Frequency | Content Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| High Power / High Interest | Clear, concise, strategic | Frequent, detailed | Direct benefits, strategic alignment, two-way dialogue |
| High Power / Low Interest | Efficient, high-level | As needed, milestone-based | Executive summaries, organisational goal alignment |
| Low Power / High Interest | Inclusive, engaging | Regular, consistent | Accessible language, feedback loops, recognition |
| Low Power / Low Interest | Simple, direct | As needed | Only necessary information |

## 8. Stakeholder Engagement Techniques (Harrin)

### Ten Methods

1. **Expectation mapping** — document what each stakeholder expects from the project and what the project expects from them; create a two-way expectation register
2. **Concerns mapping** — document stakeholder concerns; track resolution; identify themes and patterns
3. **Personal contacts notebook** — record communication preferences, interests, personal details to build rapport
4. **Using others to engage** — identify influential allies who can engage resistant stakeholders on your behalf
5. **Celebrating success** — acknowledge contributions publicly; celebrate milestones to reinforce engagement
6. **Active project marketing** — proactively promote value, progress, and successes (newsletters, intranet, presentations)
7. **Actively seek input** — create structured opportunities for input (surveys, workshops, one-to-ones)
8. **Informal opportunities** — corridor conversations, coffee chats, social events
9. **Gamification** — set clear goals, build narrative, give rapid feedback, keep it simple, make it special
10. **Testing engagement** — vary approaches to evaluate what works; change timing, format, messenger, channel, or content

### Expectation Alignment (Huemann)

- Raising expectations without ability to deliver creates severe backlash
- Set clear boundaries: "This has already been decided" / "This is not allowed due to regulations" / "This will be too expensive"
- For future or absent stakeholders, appoint someone to represent their interests
- Transparency about decision-making processes builds trust even when influence is limited

## 9. Consultation Design (Worsley)

### Six Requirements for Genuine Consultation

1. **Fair representation** of all stakeholders — not just the easy ones
2. **Complete and contextualised information** — big picture for all participants
3. **Broad consideration** of all options
4. **Committed and realistic negotiations** — negotiators must have power to compromise
5. **Appropriate and planned-out consultation process**
6. **Relevant and acceptable consultation mechanisms**

### Extended Stakeholder Management Process (Worsley)

Four additional steps beyond the basic circular model:

1. **Internal preparation and alignment** — build internal support and advocates before external engagement
2. **Build trust** — factor in different levels of willingness to trust; tailor approach accordingly
3. **Consult** — genuine consultation meeting the six requirements above
4. **Respond and implement** — visibly demonstrate how stakeholder input was heard, considered, and factored in. "Make sure they can hear their voice in the read-back."

## 10. Stakeholder Questioning Techniques (Shander)

### The Five Whys (Toyota)

Ask "Why?" repeatedly (approximately five times) to get from surface request to root cause:
- Production line down → conveyor broke → part jammed → someone bumped machine → floor wet → janitor was sick
- Root cause = custodial staffing, not equipment

### The Dance of the Six Ws

Who, What, Where, When, Why, How — not as a checklist but as a dance. Each question leads to another, creating a rhythm that reveals underlying needs. More productive than linear questioning because slight disorientation drives insight.

### Diverge and Converge

- Alternate between divergent questions (why? what if? how?) and convergent questions (when? where? what specifically?)
- Do not converge too quickly — constructive tension from back-and-forth drives deeper insight
- Eventually orbit close enough to the "truth" to act

### Chunky Segmentation

Break a stakeholder's request into chunks and investigate each one separately. Based on Miller's "Magical Number Seven" — decompose complex statements into manageable pieces.

### The Art of the (Im)Possible

- **Constraints are your friend** — define what cannot be done to clarify what can
- **Premortem (Kahneman):** "Imagine we are a year into the future. The outcome was a disaster. Write a brief history of the disaster."
- **Magic Wand questions:** "If you had a magic wand, how would you…?" — triggers blue-sky thinking
- Dance between constraint-driven and unconstrained imagination

### Rework Questions (Fried & Hansson, via Shander)

Foundational questions for any stakeholder engagement:
- Why are you doing this?
- What problems are you solving?
- Is this actually useful?
- Are you adding value?
- Will this change behaviour?
- Is there an easier way?
- What could you be doing instead?
- Is it really worth it?

## 11. Facilitation and Meetings (Harrin)

### Meeting Management Process

**Before:**
- Prepare in advance (agenda, outcomes, materials)
- Keep meetings small (5–8 people for decision-making)
- Establish credibility; brief attendees so there are no surprises

**During:**
- **Contract at the start** — state purpose, desired outcomes, ground rules, roles, time boundaries
- Manage stress (yours and others')
- Plan a response if conflict arises
- Manage interruptions (accept senior executives will check phones; plan for it)
- Use the parking lot technique for off-topic items

**After:**
- Circulate actions, owners, deadlines
- Follow up on commitments

### Virtual Meeting Considerations

- Camera-on policy where appropriate
- More structured facilitation needed (explicit turn-taking)
- Shorter sessions with breaks
- Use chat function for engagement

## 12. Group Engagement Techniques (Shander)

### Whispering En Masse

- Have participants work alone first, then come together (Kahneman: independent judgements before group discussion)
- Manage the **Magpies and Elk** dynamic — bold personalities dominate; shy ones stay silent; create space for everyone
- MIT collective intelligence research: group performance correlates with average social sensitivity, equality of conversational turn-taking, and proportion of females
- Ask: "What do you think about what she/he just said?" — gets divergent opinions and teaches participants to engage each other
- **Embarrass yourself:** starting with an embarrassing story measurably increases both quantity and quality of ideas

### Stop/Start/Continue Exercise (Harrin)

A retrospective technique for engagement improvement:
- **Stop** — what engagement activities should we stop doing?
- **Start** — what should we begin doing?
- **Continue** — what is working well and should continue?

## 13. Engaging Project Sponsors (Harrin)

Five principles:

1. **Flex your style** to suit their preferences
2. **Respect their time** — be concise, come prepared
3. **Show you can be trusted** — deliver on commitments
4. **Demonstrate competence** — show you know what you are doing
5. **Tell the truth** — even bad news; sponsors value honesty

---

## 14. Seven Principles of Stakeholder Engagement (Worsley)

1. **Stakeholders should have a say** in decisions that affect them
2. **Their contributions will influence decisions** — and they are told how
3. **Seek out those potentially affected by or interested in** a decision
4. **Seek input on how they may wish to participate** — be innovative in mechanisms
5. **Provide information, time, and space** for meaningful participation — plan the project around the engagement, not the engagement around the project
6. **Remember stakeholders are human** — they make mistakes; create safe environments for exploration
7. **Relationships are key** — trust, commitment, and collaboration are built upon them

---

## 15. Evaluating Engagement Effectiveness (Harrin)

Four approaches:

1. **Measure stakeholder satisfaction** — surveys, feedback
2. **Track engagement activities** completed vs planned
3. **Monitor stakeholder behaviour changes** — are resistant stakeholders moving toward support?
4. **Vary one element at a time** to test effectiveness

---

## 16. Five Learning Points for Stakeholder Communication (Worsley — Cape Town IRT Case)

1. For every engagement, consider beforehand the possible consequences and how to follow through
2. If you cannot follow through, seriously reconsider engaging with that group at that time
3. Always acknowledge input received; make clear something will be done
4. Use their language in the feedback — make sure stakeholders can hear their voice
5. It never hurts to be polite; show respect regardless of disagreement

## 17. Product Breakdown Structure by Stakeholder Type (Worsley)

How scope structuring changes with stakeholder complexity:

| Project Type | PBS Approach |
|---|---|
| **Stakeholder-Neutral** | Technical deliverables with communication as an add-on product |
| **Stakeholder-Sensitive** | Integrated structure combining technical and people-focused products (e.g., "Agreed Office Design" and "Staff Ready for the Move" as primary products) |
| **Stakeholder-Led** | PBS organised by stakeholder groups; the primary driver of scope IS the stakeholder groups |

**Key insight (Worsley):** "The way we structure the scope matters. Without integration, people elements get disconnected from their purpose and de-scoped."
