# Stakeholder Dynamics, Influence, and Conflict Resolution

Reference material for the stakeholder-engagement skill. Provides detailed guidance on resistance management, conflict resolution, influence without authority, cognitive biases affecting stakeholder interactions, difficult stakeholder management, and ethical principles. Use when designing stakeholder risk management, resistance strategies, or governance components for proposals.

Sources: Harrin (Engaging Stakeholders on Projects, 2020), Worsley (Stakeholder-led Project Management, 2nd ed., 2020), Shander (Stakeholder Whispering, 2025), Rendtorff (Stakeholder Theory, 2016), Huemann (Rethink Project Stakeholder Management, 2016), Bosenberg (Stakeholder Mapping Deep Dive, 2023).

---

## 1. Resistance Management Framework (Harrin)

### Identifying Resistance — Behavioural Indicators

- Avoiding meetings or declining invitations
- Silence in discussions
- Agreeing in meetings but not following through
- Actively voicing opposition
- Body language cues (crossed arms, looking away, leaving early)
- Delays in completing tasks
- Going over your head to complain

### Root Causes of Resistance

- Fear of the unknown
- Loss of control, status, or competence
- Bad timing or change fatigue
- Lack of trust in the project team or leadership
- Disagreement with the approach
- Previous bad experiences with change
- Not understanding why the change is needed

### Ten Strategies for Engaging Resisters (Harrin)

1. **Don't be difficult to work with** — be approachable, professional, responsive
2. **Be realistic with expectations** — accept not everyone will be enthusiastic
3. **Acknowledge you've noticed** — name the resistance without blame
4. **Listen** — seek to understand before trying to influence
5. **Ask for their help** — give them a role; involvement builds ownership
6. **Thank them** — acknowledge any contribution, however small
7. **Go via the gatekeeper** — use a trusted intermediary with influence over the resister
8. **Don't make things worse** — avoid escalating defensiveness
9. **Persuade with data and stories** — rational evidence plus emotional narrative
10. **Escalate** — as a last resort, involve their manager or the sponsor
11. **Ignore them** — if the stakeholder truly cannot block progress, redirect energy

### Stakeholder Risk (Harrin)

Treat resistant stakeholders as a project risk. Add to the risk register:
- Description of the risk (what could go wrong if they remain disengaged)
- Impact assessment
- Mitigation actions (engagement strategies)
- Owner (who will manage the relationship)

## 2. Conflict Resolution Framework (Harrin)

### Conflict Sources on Projects

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Task conflict** | Disagreements about the work itself |
| **Process conflict** | Disagreements about how work is done |
| **Relationship conflict** | Interpersonal friction |

### Conflict by Project Phase

| Phase | Common Conflicts |
|---|---|
| **Kick-off** | Scope disagreements, priority conflicts, role ambiguity |
| **Planning** | Resource allocation, timeline disputes, methodology disagreements |
| **Delivery** | Quality standards, workload, change requests, personality clashes |
| **Close** | Acceptance criteria, lessons learned blame, transition disputes |

### Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI)

Two dimensions — Assertiveness (satisfying own concerns) and Cooperativeness (satisfying others' concerns):

| Mode | Assertive? | Cooperative? | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Avoiding** | Low | Low | Issue is trivial; emotions need to cool; lacking information |
| **Accommodating** | Low | High | Relationship matters more than winning; you are wrong; building goodwill |
| **Competing** | High | Low | Quick decisive action needed; important issues where you are right; protecting against exploitation |
| **Compromising** | Medium | Medium | Goals moderately important; temporary settlement; time pressure |
| **Collaborating** | High | High | Both concerns too important to compromise; integrative solution needed; building commitment |

### Six-Step Conflict Resolution Process (Harrin)

1. **Pick the location and environment** — neutral, private, comfortable
2. **Gather the facts** — understand what happened from multiple perspectives
3. **Research and recognise stakeholder power and personal views** — understand politics and personal stakes
4. **Meet and agree on the issue** — ensure all parties agree on what the conflict actually is
5. **Reflect and discuss** — explore options, seek common ground
6. **Escalate if necessary** — if resolution is not possible at your level

### De-escalation Phrases

- "I can see this matters to you…"
- "Help me understand your perspective…"
- "What would a good outcome look like for you?"
- "Let's focus on what we can agree on…"

## 3. Difficult Stakeholder Management (Bosenberg)

### Ten-Point Protocol

1. **Understand their perspective** — empathise
2. **Maintain professionalism and composure**
3. **Establish clear and open communication**
4. **Set and enforce boundaries**
5. **Seek to resolve conflicts proactively**
6. **Build rapport and trust**
7. **Involve them in the process** — inclusive decision-making
8. **Know when to escalate**
9. **Document interactions and outcomes**
10. **Focus on the bigger picture** — do not take it personally

## 4. Barriers to Effective Stakeholder Engagement (Shander)

| Barrier | Description |
|---|---|
| **Imagination deficit** | Stakeholder cannot envision alternatives |
| **Seeing but not caring** | Stakeholder sees the issue but will not act |
| **Fear** | Fear of change, failure, or looking foolish |
| **White-knuckled control** | Stakeholder refuses to let go of their idea |
| **Sacred cows** | Topics that cannot be questioned |
| **Need for approval** | Stakeholder seeks affection, not truth |
| **Reluctant answerer** | Stakeholder will not engage in conversation |
| **Inattention** | Stakeholder is distracted, not listening |
| **Whispering by proxy** | Cannot access the real decision-maker |
| **Genius leader** | Leader believes they are always right |
| **Passive-aggressive leader** | Appears to engage but undermines |
| **Culture** | Organisational culture discourages questioning |
| **You** | Your own biases, ego, and limitations |

### Prequalifying Engagement Readiness (Shander)

**Brown M&Ms Test** (from Van Halen): look for small signals that reveal whether a stakeholder is genuinely open to engagement:
- Do they ask questions back?
- Do they show curiosity about the process?
- Do they accept when challenged, or shut down?
- Are they open to alternatives?

**Critical rule:** If you cannot talk directly to key stakeholders, acknowledge that failure probability rises sharply.

## 5. Influence Without Authority

### Cialdini's Six Principles Applied to Stakeholder Engagement (Worsley + Shander)

| Principle | Mechanism | Stakeholder Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Authority** | People follow those perceived as credible experts | Use authoritative figures for communications; develop and demonstrate expertise |
| **Likeability** | Greater compliance when requester is liked | Communications led by familiar, respected faces; asking questions makes you likeable |
| **Reciprocity** | Favours create obligation | Address stakeholders' top wants first; listening and deep engagement prompt reciprocity |
| **Consistency** | Aligned with own beliefs and prior commitments | Show alignment with organisational values; get stakeholders to commit publicly to even considering a new idea |
| **Social Proof** | Safer to follow the crowd | Bring external success stories; use examples from the same industry, role, or similar situation |
| **Scarcity** | Fear of missing out is more potent than desire to gain | Position participation as a limited opportunity; exclusive information increases leverage |

### Additional Influence Principles (Shander)

- **Explain "because"** — Ellen Langer's research showed a 50% increase in compliance simply by adding "because…" to a request
- **Build relationships** — the bigger your network, the more you can trade, find allies, and access exclusive information
- **Warning:** combining too many persuasion techniques often fails — they may seem overwhelming or raise people's guard

## 6. Cognitive Biases in Stakeholder Interactions (Shander)

| Bias | Effect on Stakeholder Engagement |
|---|---|
| **Anchoring** | Stakeholders over-rely on first information received |
| **Authority bias** | Subordinates defer without thinking; shuts down System 2 reasoning |
| **Availability heuristic** | Recent or emotional events distort probability judgement |
| **Bandwagon effect** | Group consensus can be fast-thinking groupthink |
| **Confirmation bias** | Stakeholders seek information confirming preconceptions |
| **Courtesy bias** | Stakeholders give socially correct rather than true opinions |
| **Curse of knowledge** | Experts cannot think from a less-informed perspective |
| **Framing effect** | Same information, different conclusions depending on presentation |
| **Functional fixedness** | Using tools and methods only in traditional ways |
| **Groupthink** | Desire for harmony overrides critical evaluation |
| **Not invented here** | Aversion to external ideas and products |
| **Plan continuation bias** | Failure to recognise original plan is no longer appropriate |
| **Reactance** | Doing the opposite of what someone wants, to preserve freedom |
| **Reactive devaluation** | Devaluing proposals because they came from an adversary |
| **Semmelweis reflex** | Rejecting new evidence that contradicts existing paradigm |
| **Status quo bias** | Preferring things to stay the same |
| **Bikeshedding** | Disproportionate weight to trivial issues, avoiding complex ones |

**Proposal application:** In methodology sections, explain how your consultation approach mitigates bias — anonymous surveys for courtesy bias, independent pre-work before group sessions for groupthink, triangulation for confirmation bias. This demonstrates methodological sophistication.

## 7. Active Listening Framework (Shander, via Rogers & Farson)

Four components:

1. **Focus** — listen for meaning, not just words; pattern recognition for anomalies and missing information
2. **Interruption-free** — listen sequentially, not while planning your response
3. **Demonstrated attention** — body language, verbal cues, reflecting back ("Got it, you want X")
4. **Nonverbal cues** — read emotions, body language, facial expressions; these reveal comfort or discomfort

**Goal: Total Meaning** — content + feeling/attitude underlying the content.

**Key finding (Rogers & Farson):** "When people are listened to sensitively, they tend to listen to themselves with more care and to make clear exactly what they are feeling and thinking."

Four quadrants based on Goleman's EI model, adapted for projects:

| | **Self** | **Others** |
|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** | Self-awareness (know your emotions, strengths, limitations) | Social awareness (empathy, organisational awareness, service orientation) |
| **Management** | Self-management (self-control, adaptability, initiative, optimism) | Relationship management (influence, developing others, conflict management, teamwork) |

## 9. Stakeholder Competency Framework (Harrin)

Ten competencies for effective stakeholder engagement:

1. **Contextual awareness** — understanding the organisational environment, political dynamics, and decision-making processes
2. **Cultural awareness** — sensitivity to national, organisational, and team cultures
3. **Influencing** — ability to shape behaviour without formal authority
4. **Negotiation** — reaching agreements that satisfy multiple parties; prepare BATNA
5. **Listening** — active listening including what is not said
6. **Business acumen** — understanding finances, strategy, and operations
7. **Resilience** — ability to adapt and recover during workplace stress
8. **Credibility** — being seen as competent and trustworthy; deliver on promises
9. **Assertiveness** — expressing views confidently without aggression
10. **Ethical awareness** — acting with integrity and transparency

## 10. Relationship Building Framework (Bosenberg)

Eight practices for sustained stakeholder relationships:

1. **Consistent and open communication** — transparency, consistency
2. **Show appreciation and recognition**
3. **Build trust through reliability and integrity** — deliver on promises, ethical practices
4. **Engage in active listening**
5. **Foster inclusivity and participation**
6. **Manage conflicts proactively**
7. **Provide value and benefits** — demonstrate impact
8. **Maintain flexibility and adaptability**

## 11. Ethical Principles for Stakeholder Engagement

### Freeman's Ten Normative Principles (via Rendtorff)

1. Acknowledge and actively monitor the concerns of all legitimate stakeholders
2. Listen to and openly communicate with stakeholders about their concerns and contributions
3. Adopt processes and modes of behaviour sensitive to the concerns and capabilities of each constituency
4. Recognise the interdependence of efforts and rewards among stakeholders; achieve a fair distribution
5. Work cooperatively with other entities to ensure risks and harms are minimised and compensated
6. Avoid activities that might jeopardise inalienable human rights or give rise to unacceptable risks
7. Acknowledge potential conflicts between your own role as stakeholder and your responsibilities
8. Serve society and contribute to the common good
9. No stakeholder should be treated merely as a means to an end
10. Stakeholder engagement should be based on procedural fairness and equal respect

### Perceived Fairness Test (Huemann, via Bosse, Phillips & Harrison)

Stakeholders accept "good enough benefits" instead of maximum benefits when fairness is perceived in:
- Distribution of benefits
- Project processes and procedures
- Ways of interacting

### Deliberative Principles (Rendtorff, via Habermas)

Stakeholder engagement should follow principles of communicative action:
- Free and equal participation in dialogue
- No coercion — arguments win on merit
- All affected parties have a right to participate
- Decisions are legitimate only if they could be accepted by all affected

**Proposal application:** In governance and institutional strengthening proposals, reference deliberative principles to justify inclusive engagement methods. This provides philosophical grounding that evaluators in public sector and donor-funded assignments expect.

## 12. Systemic Constellation Methods (Huemann)

Three spatial analysis methods adapted from systems therapy to stakeholder analysis:

### Systemic Board Constellation

- Uses blocks or abstract figures on a board to represent stakeholders spatially
- Single or group setting; three facilitation styles (centralised, cooperative, individualised)
- Process: initial picture → discussion → movements → final picture
- Benefits: creates distance from the problem; enables abstraction; quick and cheap

### Systemic Constellation with External Representatives

- People physically represent stakeholders in a room
- Representatives report body sensations, feelings, and uncensored thoughts from their spatial position
- Reveals hidden agendas, conflicts, and relationship dynamics not written down
- Includes warm-up, systemic interview, constellation, movements, debriefing, and follow-up

### Benefits of Systemic Methods

1. **Implicit knowledge becomes explicit** — spatial positioning reveals hidden dynamics
2. **Better understanding of conflict parties** — emotional level becomes visible
3. **Identify measures and actions** — solutions can be simulated in real time
4. **Stakeholders' stakeholders become visible** — reveals second-order stakeholders that are critical but overlooked

## 13. Project Success Dimensions Over Time (Worsley, via Shenhar)

| Dimension | Timeframe | Stakeholders |
|---|---|---|
| **Project Efficiency** | During delivery | Commissioners, funders, project team |
| **Project Utility** | Transition to operations | Target recipients, users (potentially thousands) |
| **Project Financial Value** | Post-operationalisation | Groups with return-on-investment expectations |
| **Corporate/Societal Value** | Long-term | Broader, more unpredictable, potent groups |

**Proposal application:** Show that your stakeholder engagement strategy is calibrated to the success dimension most relevant to the assignment phase. Short-term technical assignments focus on efficiency stakeholders; institutional reform focuses on societal value stakeholders.

## 14. Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety (Huemann)

"Variety can only be absorbed by variety." Applied to stakeholder management: do not oversimplify complex stakeholder landscapes. Build adequate structures and use multiple methods to match the complexity of the situation. A single engagement approach for a complex, multi-stakeholder programme signals weak methodology.

## 15. Five Systemic Principles (Huemann, via Varga von Kibéd & Sparrer)

1. **Principle of observing membership criteria** — everyone has the same right to be acknowledged as part of the system
2. **Principle of direct chronology** — within a system, an older member takes precedence
3. **Principle of inverse chronology** — a newer system takes precedence over an older system
4. **Principle of precedence for higher contribution** — whoever makes a larger contribution takes precedence
5. **Principle of precedence for performance and skills** — development of personal skills is considered

**Two metaprinciples:**
- What is given must be recognised (everything that exists must be considered)
- There is an order in the basic principles (when principles conflict, a hierarchy applies)
