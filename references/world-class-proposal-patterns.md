# World-Class Proposal Patterns

Patterns extracted from McKinsey & Company and Deloitte Consulting sample proposals. These patterns should be applied across all proposal skills to elevate output to top-tier consulting standards.

---

## 1. Cover Letter Patterns

### McKinsey
- **Personal credential hook**: the signing partner leads with their own relevant credential ("As a fourth generation US Army veteran and former US Department of Veterans Affairs executive…"). This is not boilerplate — it is a personal connection to the client's mission.
- **One-paragraph scope confirmation**: confirms the proposal addresses the specific solicitation, citing the exact reference numbers and procurement vehicle.
- **Gratitude and commitment close**: "Thank you again for the opportunity to bid: we admire New Jersey's focus on this effort and are committed to doing whatever we can to ensure its success."
- **Dual contacts**: a named senior partner for substantive questions and a separate contracts manager for procurement questions.

### Deloitte
- **Four differentiators stated up front**: the cover letter names the four specific capabilities that make Deloitte the right firm, in bold. These become the organising theme of the entire proposal.
- **Value statement in the cover letter**: "This translates into opportunities of greater value, an increase in the speed to impact, and most importantly lower risk for the Board."
- **Dual signatories**: both a Director and a Principal sign.

### Pattern to adopt
State the firm's differentiators in the cover letter — not generic, but named as themes that the full proposal then proves. The cover letter is a preview of the argument, not a formality.

## 2. Executive Summary Patterns

### McKinsey
- **Client problem first**: opens by restating the client's stated objective in their own language, demonstrating understanding before mentioning McKinsey.
- **Scale proof**: "over 20,000 consultants", "5,460 engagements for public sector clients across 105+ countries and 375 government agencies". Quantified at scale.
- **Specific local proof**: "McKinsey has served 10 distinct departments, divisions, or other operating units of the State of New Jersey over the course of two administrations spanning more than 10 years." — not just "we have worked in New Jersey".
- **Mission alignment**: connects the firm's history to the client's cause ("McKinsey was founded in 1926 by World War I US Army Veteran James O. McKinsey").
- **Engagement count**: "more than 150 engagements on topics such as org separation, org design, org transformation and workforce strategy" — quantifies directly relevant experience.

### Deloitte
- **Restatement of need in the firm's own analytical language**: Deloitte reframes the client's need through their own analytical lens (the input-output model for higher education), showing they understand the problem at a deeper level.
- **Team composition stated early**: names not just Deloitte but the full team (Deloitte, KH Consulting Group, Ad Astra) and explains why this combination is the right one.
- **Phase overview with value proposition**: each phase is described in one to two sentences with an emphasis on what it produces, not just what it does.
- **Pro-bono investment**: "we propose the inclusion of a planning phase, to be performed pro-bono as our investment in the programme's success" — demonstrates confidence and commitment.

### Pattern to adopt
The executive summary is an argument, not a summary. It makes a case structured around three to four differentiators, supported by quantified proof. It opens with the client's problem, not the firm's credentials.

## 3. Scope and Methodology Patterns

### McKinsey
- **Three-phase structure with clear naming**: Phase 1: Assess current state; Phase 2: Design future state; Phase 3: Develop transition plan. Each phase is a clear sentence.
- **Activities numbered and specific**: each phase lists numbered key activities with enough detail to be credible ("Conduct 'North Star' vision workshops with Governor's Office and DMAVA leadership to set aspirations").
- **Named frameworks and tools**: references "McKinsey's organizational design framework" by name — proprietary methodology is a differentiator.
- **Client inputs explicit**: specifies what the firm needs from the client at each step (interviews, documentation, site visits).
- **Hypothesis-driven approach**: "we develop issue trees and hypothesis trees" — the firm names its problem-solving methodology.

### Deloitte
- **Phase 0 (pro-bono planning)**: adds a planning phase before the paid work begins, showing investment in the relationship.
- **Objectives, Activities, Key Deliverables table per phase**: every phase has a structured table with three columns. This is repeated consistently throughout.
- **Programme vs. project framing**: "This leads us to approach the Board's request as a programme, rather than a project, to produce a portfolio of opportunities to be managed and monitored" — elevates the framing.
- **Organisational engagement as a formal workstream**: not just a mention of stakeholder management but a dedicated section with proprietary tools (e.g., "As One" diagnostic).
- **Results Management Office (RMO)**: proposes a dedicated implementation tracking function, showing they think beyond delivery to sustained results.
- **Cross-reference structure**: methodology section constantly refers forward ("Details are found in section 6.0 Methodology") and backward, creating a cohesive document.

### Pattern to adopt
Structure methodology as numbered phases with named activities, explicit deliverables, and client inputs. Use structured tables (Objectives / Activities / Deliverables) for each phase. Name proprietary tools and frameworks. Always include a governance and engagement workstream alongside the technical work.

## 4. Qualifications and Experience Patterns

### McKinsey
- **Engagement count**: "in the last 5 years have completed more than 150 engagements on topics such as…" — not just a list of projects, but a volume proof.
- **Geographic and institutional specificity**: "served 10 distinct departments, divisions, or other operating units of the State of New Jersey over the course of two administrations" — shows depth with this specific client.
- **Sector centres of excellence**: references "McKinsey Center for Government (MCG)" — a dedicated practice, not just a generalist firm.
- **Thought leadership as proof**: references McKinsey Quarterly, McKinsey Global Institute, and McKinsey on Government publications as evidence of deep investment in the domain.
- **Third-party recognition**: includes press quotes and awards recognising the firm's work.

### Deloitte
- **Summary table first, detail second**: Table 1 lists recent experience in a scannable format, then the narrative expands on selected projects.
- **Project spotlights with consistent structure**: Client → Challenge → Scope → Key Objectives Achieved. Every spotlight follows the same format.
- **Quantified outcomes always**: "$18-$34M in annual savings", "$5.2 billion for the resulting budget that passed", "savings of approximately $54 million". Every project has a number.
- **Breadth proof**: "served more than 200 higher education institutions" and then lists 30+ named clients. Volume proves capability.
- **Higher education benchmarking study**: references a proprietary benchmark database as a tool the firm brings — not just experience but data.
- **Reference contacts provided**: each project includes a named reference contact.

### Pattern to adopt
Lead with a summary table of all relevant projects. Follow with detailed spotlights in a consistent format. Every project must have a quantified outcome. Include reference contacts. Prove both breadth (volume of engagements) and depth (specific results for this type of client).

## 5. Team and Staffing Patterns

### Deloitte
- **Organisational chart of the proposed team**: Figure 5 shows the team hierarchy visually.
- **Team capabilities table**: Table 6 maps each team member to their capabilities and relevant experience in a structured matrix.
- **Named individuals with relevant credentials**: each team member is named with their specific relevant qualifications, not just a generic role description.
- **Team structure rationale**: explains why the team is composed this way — what each person brings and how they complement each other.

### McKinsey
- **Key personnel section is concise**: focuses on the most senior staff with one-paragraph summaries of why they are the right person for this assignment.
- **Full resumes in appendix**: CVs are in an appendix, not cluttering the main proposal body.

### Pattern to adopt
Present the team as a deliberate composition, not just a list of names. Show how the team members complement each other. Keep detailed CVs in an appendix. Include a visual organogram and a capabilities matrix.

## 6. Pricing and Financial Patterns

### McKinsey
- **Total cost stated clearly**: "$3,994,610.00" — unambiguous.
- **Invoice schedule tied to milestones**: payments linked to deliverables, not calendar dates.
- **Assumptions and disclosure section**: clearly states what is and is not included.

### Deloitte
- **Cost by phase**: Table 20 breaks cost by phase, making it easy to understand what each phase costs.
- **Hourly rates table**: Table 22 shows individual hourly rates for each team member by role.
- **Deliverable schedule**: Table 21 maps deliverables to dates — the financial proposal directly cross-references the work plan.

### Pattern to adopt
Financial proposals must cross-reference the work plan. Break costs by phase and by team member. State assumptions clearly. Tie every payment to a deliverable.

## 7. Document Structure and Presentation Patterns

### Both firms
- **Table of contents with page numbers**: both proposals have detailed tables of contents.
- **List of tables and list of figures**: Deloitte has separate lists for tables and figures.
- **Consistent headers and footers**: every page carries the client name, proposal reference, and confidentiality notice.
- **Numbered exhibits**: McKinsey numbers every exhibit with a descriptive caption.
- **Heavy use of figures and diagrams**: both proposals use visual elements to break up text and communicate complex ideas quickly.
- **Cross-referencing**: sections refer forward and backward to other sections, creating a cohesive document.
- **Confidentiality notice on every page**: both firms include confidentiality/proprietary restrictions.

### Pattern to adopt
Professional proposals are not just text. They include tables, figures, diagrams, and cross-references. Every page should carry consistent branding. Include a detailed table of contents. Number and caption all visual elements.

## 8. Differentiating Language Patterns

### What top firms do that weaker proposals don't

1. **They quantify everything**: not "extensive experience" but "150 engagements in 5 years". Not "significant savings" but "$5.2 billion".
2. **They name the client's problem before their own capabilities**: the client's challenge comes first, then the firm's response.
3. **They use proprietary names**: "McKinsey's organizational design framework", "Deloitte's ValuePrint business case tool", "As One diagnostic" — branded methodology is a differentiator.
4. **They show investment in the sector**: thought leadership publications, benchmarking studies, and dedicated practice areas prove commitment beyond this single bid.
5. **They think in programmes, not projects**: framing the work as a programme with governance, engagement, and results management elevates the proposal.
6. **They offer something extra**: pro-bono planning phases, proprietary benchmarks, dedicated centres of excellence — giving the client more than what was asked for.
7. **They write to the evaluation criteria**: every section directly addresses what evaluators will score, not just what the firm wants to say.
8. **They structure for scannability**: summary tables before detail, consistent formatting, visual elements, and cross-references make the document easy to evaluate.

## 9. Buyer Role Awareness
Write for the evaluation committee, not one reader. Every proposal is read by 3-5 people with different agendas:
- **Economic Buyer** (budget holder) — wants ROI, bottom-line impact, risk management
- **User Buyer** (operational staff) — wants to know how the project affects their daily work; if ignored, can sabotage
- **Technical Buyer** (procurement/compliance) — checks compliance with ToR, scoring criteria, format requirements
- **Coach** (your internal advocate) — needs ammunition to champion your proposal in committee discussions

Ensure each section contains something for each buyer type.

## 10. Theme Architecture
Identify 3-4 persuasive themes and weave them through EVERY section:
- Sources: hot buttons from the ToR, evaluation criteria (published or implied), your competitive advantages
- Each theme should appear in: understanding of assignment, methodology, team composition, and relevant experience
- Example themes: "local presence and contextual understanding", "knowledge transfer from day one", "proprietary diagnostic tools", "proven track record in [sector]"

---

## 11. The Proposal as Performance
The proposal itself is a demonstration of service quality:
- Responsiveness (turnaround time, thoroughness of ToR analysis) signals how you will perform during the engagement
- Naming the client's problem more precisely than they can signals genuine expertise
- Cultural sensitivity in tone and approach signals compatibility
- Boilerplate is fatal — generic proposals that could apply to any client virtually guarantee a loss in competitive situations

---

## 12. Relationship Signals
The TRUST framework (Wickham):
- **Truthful** — acknowledge challenges honestly; short-term discomfort builds long-term credibility
- **Responsive** — engage totally in the client's world; show you have studied their context
- **Uniform** — be consistent in ideas and attitudes across all sections
- **Safe** — formalise confidentiality; provide constant reassurance of data protection
- **Trained** — demonstrate competence through specific, quantified evidence

---

## 13. Column Fodder Warning
If you have no realistic chance of winning, decline gracefully. Being listed as a third option to justify a predetermined choice wastes resources and damages reputation. Qualifying signals: incumbent advantage, pre-existing relationship, unusually specific ToR language that matches one firm's capabilities.
