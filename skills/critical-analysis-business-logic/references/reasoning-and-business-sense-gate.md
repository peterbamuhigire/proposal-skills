# Reasoning and Business-Sense Gate

Use this gate before drafting high-stakes proposal content and again before final review. It distils critical-thinking, essential-question, mental-model, design-thinking, and strategic-thinking material into proposal practice.

## 1. Essential Questions

| Area | Question |
|---|---|
| Client decision | What must the evaluator believe to award this assignment? |
| Real problem | What is the problem beneath the ToR language? |
| Scope | What is included, excluded, assumed, and dependent on the client? |
| Evidence | What proof shows we can do this work? |
| Method | Why is this approach the right way to solve the problem? |
| Alternatives | What might a competitor propose, and why is our route stronger? |
| Constraints | What could block delivery: data access, approvals, politics, budget, skills, time, geography, procurement rules? |
| Value | What measurable benefit will the client get: better decisions, lower risk, compliance, efficiency, revenue, service quality, sustainability? |
| Ethics | Who is affected by the assignment and what safeguards are needed? |

## 2. Proposal Argument Map

Use this table for every major section.

| Section claim | Evidence | Warrant | Assumption | Countercase | Proposal implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| What we want the evaluator to believe | Project, credential, data, method, or client fact | Why the evidence proves the claim | What must be true | Strongest objection | What we will write, include, or change |

Do not turn weak evidence into confident language. If the evidence is thin, either find proof, narrow the claim, or mark it as an assumption.

## 3. Business-Sense Checks

### Client Value Logic

- The proposal names the client's actual decision, pain, mandate, or operational pressure.
- The proposed work creates a benefit the client can recognise and defend.
- The value is concrete enough to appear in an executive summary, methodology, and M&E framework.

### Delivery Logic

- Activities lead to outputs, outputs lead to outcomes, and outcomes answer the ToR.
- Each phase has a purpose, deliverable, owner, dependency, and review point.
- The methodology matches the proposed level of effort and team composition.
- Critical-path activities receive enough time and senior attention.

### Commercial Logic

- Price matches scope, seniority, travel, data collection, tools, QA, and reporting burden.
- Payment milestones match deliverables and cash-flow realities.
- Assumptions and exclusions protect the bidder without sounding defensive.

### Evidence Logic

- Past experience is relevant to this client, geography, sector, assignment type, or method.
- Claims about capability are shown through outcomes, not adjectives.
- Market, regulatory, or technical claims are sourced or clearly framed as assumptions.

## 4. Mental-Model Pass

Use these models as short reviewer questions:

- **Inversion:** What would make this proposal fail technically or commercially?
- **Second-order effects:** What happens after the recommendation is implemented?
- **Base rates:** What usually goes wrong in comparable assignments?
- **Incentives:** Who must cooperate, and why would they?
- **Opportunity cost:** What is the client choosing not to fund if they accept this?
- **Map versus territory:** Does the proposal assume a cleaner client environment than reality?
- **Circle of competence:** Are we claiming expertise beyond available proof?
- **Margin of safety:** Is there buffer for delays, missing data, approvals, and field conditions?

## 5. Achievability Gate

A proposal is not convincing unless it passes these checks:

- Team roles match the methodology.
- Person-days match the work plan.
- Budget matches the work plan and staffing schedule.
- Stakeholder engagement is possible within the timeline.
- Data collection volume is realistic for the geography and access conditions.
- Quality assurance is built into the work, not added at the end.
- Risks include prevention, detection, escalation, and fallback actions.
- Sustainability or handover is designed into delivery where the ToR expects lasting change.

## 6. Final Reviewer Checklist

- [ ] The proposal has one clear win theme.
- [ ] Every section supports that theme.
- [ ] Major claims have evidence or labelled assumptions.
- [ ] The strongest objection is answered before the evaluator raises it.
- [ ] Methodology, staffing, work plan, risk, M&E, and budget reconcile.
- [ ] The language is confident but not exaggerated.
- [ ] The proposed work is feasible under the actual client, country, and procurement constraints.
