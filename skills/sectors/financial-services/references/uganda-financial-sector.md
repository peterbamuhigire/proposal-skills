# Uganda Financial Sector Profile

## 1. Sector Overview

Uganda's financial sector comprises 22 commercial banks, 4 microfinance deposit-taking institutions (MDIs), 5 credit institutions, approximately 900 savings and credit cooperative organisations (SACCOs), 143 insurance companies, 63 pension funds, 203 forex bureaus, and 58 money remitters. The sector is regulated primarily by the Bank of Uganda (BoU), the Capital Markets Authority (CMA), the Insurance Regulatory Authority (IRA), and the Uganda Retirement Benefits Regulatory Authority (URBRA).

## 2. Banking Sector Dominance

Banking dominates the financial landscape, accounting for 83% of total financial sector assets. As at December 2024, total banking sector assets stood at UGX 52.98 trillion, equivalent to 26.6% of GDP.

### Market Concentration

The sector is moderately concentrated, with the top five banks holding 56.1% of total assets:

| Rank | Bank | Market Share (Assets) |
|------|------|-----------------------|
| 1 | Stanbic Bank Uganda | 19.5% |
| 2 | Centenary Bank | 13.4% |
| 3–5 | Three other major banks | ~23.2% combined |

### Prudential Indicators

- **Capital adequacy ratio**: 24.64% (2024), well above the regulatory minimum of 8%, indicating strong capitalisation across the sector.
- **Liquidity coverage ratio**: 352.6%, reflecting ample liquidity buffers.
- **Non-performing loan (NPL) ratio**: 3.98% (2024), down from 5.27% in 2020, signalling improved asset quality.

### Profitability

- **Return on assets (ROA)**: above 3%, consistently strong by regional standards.
- **Return on equity (ROE)**: 16–17%, indicating healthy profitability for shareholders.
- **Net interest margin**: 10.9% (2024), driven by yields on advances exceeding 16%.

### Lending and Deposit Rates

- **Average lending rate**: 18.8% (2024).
- **Cost of deposits**: rising from 2.3% to 3.2%, reflecting increased competition for funds.
- **Interest rate spreads**: among the highest in sub-Saharan Africa, limiting affordable credit access for businesses and households.

## 3. Capital Markets

The Uganda Securities Exchange (USE) had a market capitalisation of UGX 32.3 trillion (approximately USD 8.6 billion) as at 2024, equivalent to roughly 4% of GDP. Trading volumes remain thin, with limited secondary market activity. The capital markets have yet to become a significant channel for corporate or government long-term financing beyond treasury securities.

## 4. Insurance Sector

Insurance penetration remains below 1% of GDP, among the lowest in East Africa. The sector is characterised by low public awareness, limited product innovation, and a concentration of premiums in motor and property lines. Regulatory reforms are ongoing to strengthen governance and expand coverage.

## 5. Mobile Money: The Transformative Force

Mobile money has fundamentally reshaped Uganda's financial landscape and now surpasses traditional banking in reach:

- **Registered accounts**: 50.5 million (2024).
- **Active users**: 34.6 million.
- **Annual transaction value**: UGX 253.7 trillion (approximately USD 67 billion as at mid-2025).
- **Comparison**: mobile money accounts exceed the 24 million accounts held in the traditional banking system.

Mobile money serves as the primary financial access channel for most Ugandans, particularly in rural areas where bank branch penetration is limited. It facilitates person-to-person transfers, bill payments, savings, credit, and increasingly serves as the rails for government-to-person payments and merchant transactions.

## 6. Pension Sector

The National Social Security Fund (NSSF) is the dominant institutional investor, with assets under management making it one of the largest pools of domestic capital. There is growing interest in channelling pension assets towards infrastructure financing, though regulatory and risk frameworks for such investments are still maturing. The sector comprises 63 pension funds in total, with NSSF accounting for the overwhelming majority of assets.

## 7. Minimum Capital Requirements

In June 2024, the minimum capital requirement for commercial banks was raised to UGX 150 billion (approximately USD 39.6 million). This measure aims to strengthen bank resilience, encourage consolidation among smaller institutions, and improve the sector's capacity to finance larger transactions.

## 8. Anti-Money Laundering and FATF Compliance

Uganda was removed from the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) grey list in February 2024, following sustained reforms to its anti-money laundering and counter-terrorism financing framework. This removal signals improved international confidence in Uganda's financial integrity systems and reduces compliance burdens for correspondent banking relationships.

## 9. Regulatory Reforms (2024)

A significant wave of regulatory reforms was enacted in 2024:

- **Financial Institutions (Corporate Governance) Regulations**: strengthening board composition, risk oversight, and related-party transaction controls for commercial banks and credit institutions.
- **Microfinance Corporate Governance Regulations**: extending similar governance standards to MDIs and Tier 4 microfinance institutions.
- **Tier 4 Digital Lending Guidelines**: regulating the growing digital and mobile lending market, addressing consumer protection concerns around predatory lending practices.
- **Fraud Mitigation Guidelines**: requiring financial institutions to implement enhanced fraud detection, reporting, and response mechanisms, particularly relevant given the rapid growth of digital financial services.

## 10. Key Risks and Challenges

### Government Domestic Borrowing

High levels of government domestic borrowing continue to crowd out private sector credit. Treasury securities offer banks risk-free returns, reducing incentives to lend to the private sector, particularly to MSMEs and agriculture.

### Cyber Risks

Rapid digitisation, particularly through mobile money and digital banking, has increased exposure to cyber threats. The sector requires continued investment in cybersecurity infrastructure, incident response capabilities, and regulatory frameworks for digital financial services.

### Interest Rate Spreads

Uganda's interest rate spreads remain among the highest in sub-Saharan Africa, reflecting structural inefficiencies including high operating costs, information asymmetries, and limited credit reference infrastructure. These spreads constrain access to affordable credit for productive investment.

### Financial Inclusion Gaps

Despite mobile money's reach, significant gaps persist in access to formal savings, credit, and insurance products, particularly for women, youth, and rural populations.

### Financial Inclusion Trends

Formal financial inclusion has risen from 58% (2018) to 68% (2023), but a persistent gender gap remains: female inclusion stood at 62% and male inclusion at 75% in 2023, compared with 54% and 63% respectively in 2018. Closing this gap requires targeted interventions in women's digital literacy, agent banking in rural areas, and gender-responsive product design.

### Foreign Direct Investment and Remittances

Foreign direct investment (FDI) inflows reached USD 2.99 billion in 2023, up from USD 1.19 billion in 2020, reflecting growing investor confidence in Uganda's economy. Remittance inflows totalled USD 1.43 billion in 2023, constituting a significant and relatively stable source of external financing that supports household consumption and, increasingly, small business investment.

## 11. Opportunities for Development Interventions

- **Capital markets deepening**: expanding the range of instruments (corporate bonds, green bonds, infrastructure bonds) and improving secondary market liquidity.
- **Insurance market development**: micro-insurance products, agricultural index insurance, and health insurance expansion.
- **Pension-to-infrastructure linkage**: developing frameworks for NSSF and other pension funds to invest in infrastructure through blended finance structures.
- **Digital financial services regulation**: building on the 2024 reforms to create a comprehensive fintech regulatory sandbox and consumer protection framework.
- **Credit infrastructure**: strengthening credit reference bureaus, collateral registries, and alternative credit scoring using mobile money data.
- **MSME finance**: developing guarantee schemes, value chain financing, and tailored products to bridge the SME financing gap.

## 12. SDG Pathway Context

Finance is central to two of Uganda's SDG acceleration pathways identified in the UN Country Analysis 2025:

- **Pathway 1 — Industrialisation**: achieving structural economic transformation requires medium-to-long-term investment finance for manufacturing, agro-processing, and value addition. Capital markets deepening, pension-to-infrastructure linkages, and development of longer-tenor lending products are critical enablers.
- **Pathway 10 — Partnerships, Financing and Data**: strengthening domestic revenue measures, expanding blended finance instruments, and deploying results-based financing mechanisms are essential for closing Uganda's development financing gap. This pathway emphasises the need for innovative financial instruments that crowd in private capital alongside official development assistance.
