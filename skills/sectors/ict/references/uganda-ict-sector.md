# Uganda ICT and Digital Economy Profile

## 1. National Backbone Infrastructure

Uganda has invested significantly in digital infrastructure through the National Backbone Infrastructure (NBI) project, which comprises 4,387 km of fibre-optic cable. The network connects 53 districts, 11 border posts, and 1,480 government offices, providing the foundational connectivity layer for public-sector digital services and commercial internet service provision.

## 1a. Mobile Phone Ownership

Individual mobile phone ownership remains very low: 38% (2014) rising to only 43% (2024). This slow growth over a decade underscores fundamental affordability and access barriers that constrain digital adoption across the population.

## 2. Internet Penetration

Despite backbone infrastructure investments, internet penetration stands at only 10.3%, one of the lowest rates in the East African Community. This low penetration constrains the ability of micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) to participate in the digital economy, limits access to online government services, and reduces the effectiveness of digital platforms for agriculture, health, and education.

## 3. Mobile Money as Digital Infrastructure

Mobile money has become Uganda's most pervasive digital platform:

- **Registered accounts**: 50.5 million (2024).
- **Active users**: 34.6 million.
- **Function**: serves as the de facto digital payments infrastructure, extending financial services to populations unreached by traditional banking or internet-based platforms.

Mobile money infrastructure is increasingly the backbone for fintech innovation, merchant payments, government disbursements, and cross-border remittances.

## 4. Digital Adoption and the Rural Gap

Digital adoption remains low overall, with rural areas particularly underserved. Key constraints include:

- **Last-mile infrastructure gaps**: fibre-optic connectivity has not extended to most rural sub-counties and parishes, leaving communities dependent on mobile network coverage.
- **Affordability**: whilst broadband costs have been reduced, internet-enabled devices and data bundles remain unaffordable for a large proportion of the population, particularly in rural areas.
- **Digital literacy**: limited ICT skills development constrains both supply and demand for digital services. Many potential users lack the skills to navigate digital platforms for commerce, governance, or learning.

## 4a. Innovation Ranking

Uganda ranks 124/139 on the Global Innovation Index (2025), reflecting limited R&D investment, weak intellectual property frameworks, and constrained technology transfer mechanisms. This ranking underscores the need for targeted innovation ecosystem development alongside basic digital infrastructure expansion.

## 5. ICT Skills Development Gap

There is a recognised gap between the ICT skills produced by the education system and those demanded by the digital economy. The deficit spans basic digital literacy for the general population, intermediate technical skills for MSMEs, and advanced software development, data science, and cybersecurity skills for the technology sector. Addressing this gap is critical for Uganda to realise the employment and productivity potential of its youthful population.

## 6. Government Digital Systems

The Government of Uganda has deployed a range of digital systems to improve public financial management, human resource management, and service delivery:

| System | Function |
|--------|----------|
| IFMS (Integrated Financial Management System) | Public financial management and budgeting |
| HCM (Human Capital Management) | Government payroll and human resource management |
| IRAS (Integrated Revenue Administration System) | Tax administration (Uganda Revenue Authority) |
| e-GP (Electronic Government Procurement) | Public procurement processes |
| NMIS (National Management Information System) | Data collection and reporting across sectors |
| Electronic Single Window | Trade facilitation and customs clearance |

These systems have improved efficiency and transparency but require continued investment in integration, user training, and cybersecurity.

## 7. Digital Taxation and Regulatory Environment

Regressive digital taxes have been identified as a constraint to digital economy growth. Taxes on mobile money transactions and social media usage have faced criticism for discouraging digital adoption, particularly among low-income users. A review of digital taxation policy is needed to balance revenue objectives with the imperative of expanding digital inclusion.

Data protection and e-commerce regulations require strengthening to build consumer confidence, protect personal data, and create a predictable regulatory environment for digital businesses.

## 8. Opportunities for the Digital Economy

### Fintech

Uganda's mobile money ecosystem provides a strong foundation for fintech innovation in payments, lending, insurance, and savings products. The 2024 Tier 4 Digital Lending Guidelines provide an initial regulatory framework for this growing sub-sector.

### E-Commerce

Growing urban connectivity and mobile money penetration create conditions for e-commerce expansion, though logistics infrastructure and consumer protection frameworks need strengthening.

### Business Process Outsourcing (BPO)

Uganda's English-speaking, youthful workforce and improving connectivity position the country for BPO opportunities, provided ICT skills development and broadband reliability are addressed.

### Youth and the Demographic Dividend

With 72% of the population under 30 years of age, Uganda is exceptionally well-positioned to harness the digital economy for employment creation. However, realising this potential requires coordinated investment in digital skills, affordable connectivity, and an enabling regulatory environment.

## 9. NDP IV Targets

The Fourth National Development Plan (NDP IV) identifies digital transformation as a priority, with targets including:

- Expanding broadband coverage to underserved areas.
- Strengthening digital government services.
- Building ICT skills at all levels of the education system.
- Creating an enabling environment for innovation and technology-driven enterprises.

## 9a. SDG Pathway 8 — Digital Transformation and Energy Access

ICT falls within SDG Pathway 8, which recognises that digital transformation and energy access are mutually reinforcing enablers of inclusive growth:

- Digital tools can measurably lift earnings for micro-enterprises through access to markets, financial services, and information.
- Digital government systems (e-GP, IFMS, open data platforms) strengthen transparency and accountability.
- Expanding affordable connectivity and digital literacy are preconditions for realising the productivity gains envisaged under NDP IV.
- Energy access (currently constrained in rural areas) is a binding prerequisite for digital adoption — without reliable electricity, digital infrastructure investments yield limited returns.

## 10. Key Constraints Summary

| Constraint | Impact |
|------------|--------|
| Low mobile phone ownership (43%, 2024) | Fundamental access barrier; only 5pp growth in a decade |
| Low internet penetration (10.3%) | Limits MSME participation, digital service adoption |
| Last-mile infrastructure gaps | Rural areas excluded from digital economy |
| Affordability of devices and data | Constrains demand even where coverage exists |
| ICT skills deficit | Reduces both supply and demand for digital services |
| Regressive digital taxes | Discourages adoption among price-sensitive users |
| Weak data protection and e-commerce regulation | Limits consumer confidence and business predictability |
| Cybersecurity vulnerabilities | Growing risk as digital services expand |
| Low innovation ranking (124/139 GII, 2025) | Weak R&D ecosystem and technology transfer |

## 11. Implications for Development Interventions

Proposals targeting Uganda's ICT sector should address the dual challenge of expanding infrastructure (particularly last-mile connectivity) whilst simultaneously building the demand side through digital literacy, affordable access, and relevant local content and services. The mobile money ecosystem provides a proven platform upon which to build more sophisticated digital financial and non-financial services. Any intervention should account for the regulatory environment, particularly around digital taxation and data protection, as these significantly shape the viability of digital economy initiatives.
