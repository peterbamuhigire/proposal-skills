# UN Trust Funds Guide — Project Proposals Through UNDP

Detailed guide for preparing project proposals to United Nations Trust Funds administered through UNDP channels. Covers the five-part project document pro forma, budget line numbering, government endorsement requirements, and appraisal criteria.

---

## Applicable Funds

| Fund | Focus Area |
|---|---|
| UN Trust Fund for Ageing | Programmes for older persons |
| UN Voluntary Fund on Disability | Disability inclusion and rights |
| UN Trust Fund for Family Activities | Family-focused development |
| UN Youth Fund | Youth development and participation |

All four funds use the same project document format and appraisal process.

---

## Key Characteristics

- **Seed money:** US$5,000-20,000. The fund supplements other resources — it does not replace them.
- **Catalytic orientation:** Prioritises innovative, catalytic action over routine programming.
- **Government concurrence required:** NGO applicants must demonstrate government endorsement.
- **Three-party agreement:** Government, Executing Agent, and United Nations all sign.
- **Completion evaluation mandatory:** Every project must be evaluated at the end.
- **UNDP channel:** Requests submitted through or in consultation with the local UNDP office.

## Project Document Pro Forma — Five Parts

### Cover Page (Prepare Last)

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| Fund name | Which of the four funds |
| Project number | Assigned by UN — leave blank at submission |
| Project title | Concise, descriptive |
| Executing Agent | Organisation that signs the agreement and bears full responsibility |
| Implementing Agent(s) | Organisations assisting with specific aspects |
| Project location | Country, region, specific sites |
| Starting date and duration | Proposed start + number of months |
| Government inputs | In-kind (local currency) + cash |
| Fund inputs | In US dollars |
| Third-party cost-sharing | Source, currency, amount |
| Summary description | Brief paragraph |
| **Three signatures** | Government, Executing Agent, United Nations |

### Part I: Background and Justification

Must address all 10 points:

1. **Current situation** of the beneficiary group (social, economic, demographic context)
2. **The problem or critical issue** being addressed (specific, evidence-based)
3. **Relationship to national development strategies** and policies (explicit cross-references)
4. **Complementary programmes** and activities (what else is being done, and how this project fits)
5. **How the need was determined** and how beneficiaries were involved in identification and planning
6. **How government officials were involved** — critical for NGO applicants; demonstrates alignment
7. **What government assistance will be provided** if the project is approved (commitment, not aspiration)
8. **What non-governmental resources are available** (NGOs, private sector, community contributions)
9. **Relevant experience and capabilities** of the Executing Agent (track record, analogous projects)
10. **Resources the Executing Agent will provide** for planning, implementation, and follow-up (co-financing)

**Strategy:** Points 5-7 are where NGO applicants frequently lose. Government involvement must be demonstrated concretely — meetings held, endorsement letters obtained, counterpart resources committed. Vague references to "government support" are insufficient.

### Part II: Objectives

**A. Development Objective**
- How the target social group is positioned in national strategies
- What national development objectives the project contributes to
- How the project contributes to improved well-being at the macro level

**B. Immediate Objectives**
Expected changes among beneficiaries:
- New skills acquired
- Increased income or livelihood options
- Improved health or well-being outcomes
- Greater awareness or empowerment
- Potential for extension to other locations or sectors

**Strategy:** Development objectives are strategic (contribute to national goals); immediate objectives are tangible (measurable changes in beneficiaries' lives). Both must be present.

### Part III: Project Implementation and Management Plan

**A. Expected Results**
- Overall results upon completion
- Potential unintended effects (positive and negative)
- Quantified outputs where possible ("in quantitative terms, to the extent possible")

**B. Activities and Work Plan**

| Objectives/Outputs/Activities | Responsible Party | Start | Finish |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Objective 1] | | | |
| Output 1.1 | | | |
| Activity 1.1.1 | [Name/role] | [Month] | [Month] |

**C. Project Beneficiaries**
- Who benefits (direct and indirect) and how many
- How they were involved in design
- Their role in implementation
- Their role in evaluation

**D. Institutional Setting**
- Where operations are located
- Who is responsible for planning and management
- How the project coordinates with other programmes
- **Sustainability and self-financing provisions after completion** — this is explicitly required

### Part IV: Project Monitoring and Evaluation

- Periodic monitoring mechanisms (who monitors, how often, what indicators)
- Who prepares progress reports and final technical reports
- Who handles expenditure accounting
- **Completion evaluation is required** for all Fund-assisted projects
- How beneficiaries are involved in evaluation

### Part V: Budget

Two separate budget outlines required — one for UN contribution (in US$) and one for Government/Implementing Agent contribution (in local currency).

## What the Fund Will NOT Finance

- Construction or repair of premises (except minor adaptations essential for the project)
- Support staff salaries (administrative support is limited)
- Routine operational costs of the Executing Agent
- Activities already fully funded from other sources

---

## Appraisal Criteria

Proposals are appraised by an interdisciplinary team of UN specialists against three criteria:

### 1. Consistency with Fund Terms of Reference
- Does the project fit the specific fund's mandate?
- Does it target the correct beneficiary group?
- Is the scale appropriate (seed money, not full programme)?

### 2. Relationship to National Development Strategies
- Is the project aligned with country priorities?
- Does Part I demonstrate explicit linkages to national policies and programmes?
- Is government concurrence genuine and substantive?

### 3. Logic of Proposal Design, Content, Work Plan, and Budget
- Is the proposal internally coherent? (Do objectives, activities, results, and budget align?)
- Is the work plan realistic? (Can the activities be completed in the stated timeframe?)
- Is the budget reasonable? (Are costs justified? Is co-financing adequate?)

### Appraisal Priorities

| Priority | Implication |
|---|---|
| **Catalytic and innovative** | Routine activities are deprioritised. Frame every project as catalytic — seed money that triggers broader change. |
| **Seed money, not full funding** | Proposals requesting 100% UN funding are disadvantaged. Show co-financing. |
| **Least developed countries** | LDCs and special-category developing countries receive priority attention. |
| **Complex multi-year proposals** | Should use standard UNDP project document format instead of the trust fund pro forma. |

## Government Endorsement — Critical for NGO Applicants

### What Is Required
- Written endorsement or no-objection from the relevant government ministry
- Evidence of government official involvement in project identification and planning
- Specific commitment of government assistance (counterpart staff, facilities, data access)

### How to Obtain It
- Contact the relevant government ministry early in the proposal development process
- Consult the local UNDP office — they can advise on procedures and the appropriate counterpart
- Allow sufficient time — government endorsement can take weeks to obtain
- Frame the project as contributing to the government's own development priorities

### What Happens Without It
- The proposal cannot be processed
- No amount of proposal quality compensates for missing government concurrence

---

## Reporting and Evaluation Requirements

### During Implementation
- Periodic progress reports (frequency determined by project duration)
- Expenditure accounting against the approved budget

### At Completion
- Final technical report
- Final financial statement
- **Completion evaluation** — mandatory for all Fund-assisted projects
- Beneficiary involvement in evaluation is expected

---

## Strategic Recommendations

1. **Frame as catalytic:** Use the word "catalytic" explicitly. Show how seed money triggers broader change that outlasts the project.
2. **Show co-financing from multiple sources:** Government + Executing Agent + community contributions + any other donors.
3. **Secure government endorsement first:** Before investing time in the proposal, confirm that government concurrence is obtainable.
4. **Prioritise in-service training over fellowships:** The fund explicitly prioritises in-country training over international fellowships and study tours.
5. **Quantify results:** "In quantitative terms, to the extent possible" — this instruction appears in the guidelines.
6. **Attach TORs for all personnel and subcontractors:** These are required annexes, not optional.
7. **Follow the prescribed budget line numbering exactly:** Non-compliance signals unfamiliarity with UN procedures.
8. **Address sustainability explicitly in Part III.D:** Post-completion viability is a mandatory section, not an optional one.
9. **For proposals exceeding US$20,000:** Be aware they may be circulated to other donors for third-party co-financing — the fund itself may not cover the full amount.
10. **Consult the local UNDP office:** They are the channel for submission and can provide guidance on format, government endorsement procedures, and alignment with country programme priorities.
