# Uganda Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH) Sector Profile

## 1. Water Access

### National Coverage
- **81.1% of households** use an improved water source (2024 Population and Housing Census), up from 68% in 2019/20 — meaningful progress over four years.

### Urban-Rural Disparities
- **Urban**: 86.6% access to improved water.
- **Rural**: 77.4% access to improved water.
- The 9.2 percentage-point gap reflects ongoing underinvestment in rural water infrastructure and the challenges of serving dispersed populations.

### Water Sources
- **39.2% of households** rely on boreholes as their main water source.
  - Rural: 48.9% use boreholes.
  - Urban: 25.0% use boreholes.
- Borehole dependence underscores the limited reach of piped water systems outside major urban centres.

### Safely Managed Drinking Water (UN 2025)

The "improved source" figures above are significantly higher than "safely managed" rates. Safely managed drinking water requires water on premises, available when needed, and free from contamination — a much stricter standard.

- **Safely managed drinking water: only 18% nationally** (urban 40%, rural 19%) — 2024.
- The gap between 81.1% using an improved source and 18% with safely managed water highlights that most Ugandans still face barriers of distance, reliability, or water quality even when an improved source exists.

### Distance and Accessibility
- Distance to water sources remains a key barrier, particularly in rural and semi-arid areas. Long collection times — disproportionately borne by women and girls — reduce time available for productive activities and schooling.

## 2. Sanitation

### National Coverage
- Only **43.6% of households** use improved sanitation facilities — less than half the population.

### Urban-Rural Disparities
- **Urban**: 60.9% access to improved sanitation.
- **Rural**: 31.8% access to improved sanitation.
- The 29.1 percentage-point urban-rural gap in sanitation is far wider than the gap in water access, reflecting the greater difficulty and cost of sanitation infrastructure in rural settings.

### Safely Managed Sanitation (UN 2025)

As with water, safely managed sanitation rates are far lower than improved facility rates. Safely managed sanitation requires use of an improved facility that is not shared with other households and where excreta are safely disposed of in situ or transported and treated off-site.

- **Safely managed sanitation: only 20% nationally** (urban 25%, rural 10%) — 2024.
- The gap between 43.6% using improved facilities and 20% with safely managed sanitation reflects widespread shared facilities, lack of containment, and inadequate faecal sludge management.

### Open Defecation
- **6.7% of the population** practises open defecation nationally (AfDB CDN 2025).
  - Rural: 9.2%.
  - Urban: 3.0%.
- The UN Country Analysis 2025 reports lower figures: **4% nationally** (urban 2%, rural 4%). The difference likely reflects different data sources and reference periods; proposals should note both figures and cite the source used.
- **Karamoja sub-region**: 60.2% of households have no sanitation facility — an extreme outlier driven by pastoralist livelihoods, poverty, and historical marginalisation.
- **Kampala**: 0.5% without facilities — the best-served area nationally.

### School Sanitation
- **Pit latrine-to-pupil ratio in schools**: 1:80, far exceeding national standards and contributing to absenteeism, particularly among adolescent girls.

## 3. Public Health Consequences

- Unimproved sanitation and unsafe water are among the leading drivers of **diarrhoeal diseases in children under 5**, a significant contributor to child morbidity and mortality.
- Poor WASH conditions in health facilities undermine infection prevention and control, with implications for maternal health outcomes and epidemic preparedness.
- The intersection of low sanitation coverage (43.6%) with high population density in informal urban settlements creates particular risks for cholera and other waterborne disease outbreaks.

## 4. Urban Waste Management and Housing (UN 2025)

- **Urban solid waste disposal**: open dumping or burning accounts for 55% of waste, open dumpsites handle 36%, and only 9% reaches a sanitary landfill. The absence of modern waste management infrastructure poses serious environmental and public health risks.
- **Housing deficit**: Uganda faces a housing deficit of **7.3 million units**, with consequences for urban sanitation planning — informal settlements without adequate sanitation or waste collection drive much of the urban WASH burden.

## 5. SDG Pathway Context

WASH falls under **SDG Pathway 7** (UN Country Analysis 2025). This pathway prioritises investments in rural water supply, urban network rehabilitation, and waste management to reduce the disease burden. It also encompasses industrial pollution control and cleaner production. Proposals should frame WASH interventions within this pathway, linking improved water and sanitation to reduced healthcare costs, enhanced productivity, and environmental sustainability.

## 6. Implications for Proposals

Proposals targeting Uganda's WASH sector should consider the following priorities:

1. **Rural sanitation acceleration**: Closing the 31.8% rural improved sanitation rate through community-led total sanitation (CLTS), sanitation marketing, and affordable latrine technologies.
2. **Open defecation elimination**: Targeting Karamoja (60.2% without facilities) and other high-prevalence sub-regions with culturally appropriate, context-specific interventions.
3. **Piped water expansion**: Reducing borehole dependence (48.9% in rural areas) by extending piped schemes to small towns and rural growth centres.
4. **School WASH**: Bringing the 1:80 latrine-to-pupil ratio down to national standards, with gender-separated facilities to support girls' retention.
5. **Urban sanitation in informal settlements**: Addressing the specific challenges of high-density, low-income urban areas where sewerage is absent and pit latrines are inadequate.
6. **WASH in health facilities**: Ensuring all health facilities meet minimum WASH standards to support infection prevention and safe delivery.
7. **Sustainability and maintenance**: Addressing the functionality gap — ensuring that boreholes and latrines remain operational through community management, spare parts supply chains, and local government oversight.
8. **Climate resilience**: Incorporating drought-proofing and flood-resilient designs into water infrastructure, particularly in water-stressed sub-regions.
9. **Safely managed services**: Moving beyond "improved source" targets to pursue safely managed water (currently 18%) and sanitation (currently 20%) — requiring investments in water quality, on-premises supply, and faecal sludge management.
10. **Urban waste management**: Addressing the 55% open dumping/burning rate through integrated solid waste management systems, recycling, and sanitary landfill development.
11. **Housing and WASH integration**: Linking the 7.3 million-unit housing deficit to WASH planning, ensuring new housing includes adequate water supply and sanitation from design stage.
