# EAC E-Commerce BDS Programme Design
Acknowledgement: Shared by Peter Bamuhigire, techguypeter.com, +256 784 464178.

## Overview

Use this skill to design a credible e-commerce business development services programme for East African companies. It joins enterprise support, digital trade, company selection, diagnostics, technical assistance, expert-pool management, monitoring, and learning into one delivery model.

The skill is optimised for the GIZ EU-EAC DEEP style of assignment: select at least ten companies, assess needs, co-create tailored technical assistance for at least five, manage a part-time expert pool, document lessons, and support cross-border digital trade.

## Use When

- Drafting a technical methodology for donor-funded BDS, accelerator, enterprise-support, e-commerce, or cross-border digital trade work.
- Designing company selection, diagnostics, TA packages, expert-pool deployment, engagement retention, or monitoring.
- Translating a ToR into a delivery architecture that must be credible under tight page limits.

## Do Not Use When

- The assignment is only a marketing campaign, standalone training, or software implementation.
- You do not have the ToR objectives, phases, team model, and selection constraints.
- You need only a social-media call for applications; use the campaign skill instead.

## Required Inputs

- ToR objectives, phases, milestones, outputs, reports, and evaluation grid.
- Personnel structure, fee-day budget, and expert-pool roles.
- Target geography, languages, eligibility constraints, and geographic balance expectations.
- Policy frame and evidence base: EAC E-Commerce Strategy, AfCFTA digital trade context, DCED/ILO/World Bank/OECD/ITC guidance where relevant.

## Workflow

1. Write the theory of change as a results chain: inputs, activities, outputs, outcomes, impact, assumptions, and monitoring questions.
2. Separate eligibility criteria from award criteria. Eligibility screens pass/fail compliance; award criteria score innovation, scalability, cross-border potential, traction, readiness, inclusion, and geographic balance.
3. Design the needs-assessment-to-TA pipeline. Standardise diagnostic domains, then let company evidence decide the TA package.
4. Reconcile expert-pool deployment to person-days. Allocate roles, cadence, backstopping, quality review, and engagement tactics realistically.
5. Build the results framework. Each indicator must have a source, frequency, disaggregation, validation rule, and owner.
6. Create the learning mechanism. Convert company evidence into anonymised case notes, reusable training material, and good-practice synthesis without exposing confidential data.
7. Compress for the proposal. Only include claims that map to a scored criterion or prove delivery feasibility.

## Quality Bar

- Every causal arrow in the theory of change has an assumption and monitoring question.
- Selection distinguishes eligibility from award scoring.
- Diagnostics lead directly to tailored assistance, not generic training menus.
- Expert-pool plans reconcile to available fee-days and calendar reality.
- Monitoring indicators derive from the results chain.
- Learning outputs protect company confidentiality.

## Anti-Patterns

- A generic capacity-building narrative with no business-model viability logic.
- Selecting firms by size alone instead of capability, readiness, and growth potential.
- A TA menu disconnected from diagnostics and unit economics.
- Expert-pool claims that cannot fit the fee-day budget.
- Monitoring indicators with no source or validation rule.

## Outputs

- Programme theory of change and assumptions register.
- Company selection framework and scoring matrix.
- Needs-assessment-to-TA package architecture.
- Expert-pool deployment and engagement model.
- Results framework and dashboard handoff.
- Lessons-learned and scale-up framework.
