# SaaS Win Themes and Discriminators

Win-themes and discriminator language for SaaS implementation and SaaS product-development proposals. Use inside the Executive Summary, the methodology selling paragraphs, and the briefing for any oral presentation. Discriminators are the load-bearing reasons the buyer should award to this agency rather than to a competent alternative.

A win theme is a single sentence the evaluator carries from the proposal into the award meeting. A discriminator is the specific evidence that makes the theme defensible.

## The Six Standard Win Themes for Premium SaaS Bids

For each engagement, select three to five themes and tune to the specific buyer.

### 1. Vertical Depth That Shows in the Method

- **Theme**: We have built and operated SaaS in [vertical] before, and the methodology reflects that.
- **Discriminators**:
  - Named comparable engagements in [vertical] with outcome figures.
  - Methodology that addresses the specific integrations, regulators, and workflow particularities of [vertical] — not a generic SaaS template.
  - Team members named who have prior [vertical] experience.
  - Domain SMEs identified by name, with publications, certifications, or sector roles.
  - Vertical-specific risk register entries already populated.

### 2. SaaS Engineering Literacy, Not Multi-Customer Software Rebranded

- **Theme**: We design, build, and operate SaaS as the discipline it is — control plane, application plane, tenant isolation, tenant context, cost attribution.
- **Discriminators**:
  - Control plane vs application plane explicitly named in the methodology.
  - Tenant isolation model decided in Phase 1 by Architecture Decision Record.
  - Per-tenant cost attribution from day one.
  - Tenant onboarding automated, not operations-assisted.
  - Continuous release cadence rather than per-customer scheduled releases.

### 3. Regulator-Grade Trust and Compliance

- **Theme**: We build for the regulator, not just the product team.
- **Discriminators**:
  - Pre-emptive security questionnaire pack with the proposal.
  - Tenant-isolation rationale tied to regulator stance.
  - Data-residency commitments specific to the buyer's jurisdiction.
  - Sub-processor register with role, location, and contractual flow-down.
  - BCDR test cadence stated and measurable.
  - Named certifications (SOC 2, ISO 27001, sector-specific) inherited or owned.

### 4. Engineered Adoption, Not Hoped-for Adoption

- **Theme**: We engineer adoption with customer success, lifecycle communications, and measurable value-realisation reviews.
- **Discriminators**:
  - Customer success engagement scoped, named, and priced.
  - Lifecycle communications scoped as a paid workstream, not a marketing afterthought.
  - 30/60/90-day value-realisation reviews dated in the MAP.
  - Save plays defined for the predictable churn signals.
  - Two-of-everything for client-side critical operating roles by go-live.

### 5. Transparent and Defensible Commercial Model

- **Theme**: Our pricing logic is transparent, our fees are defensible, our payment milestones tie to value events.
- **Discriminators**:
  - Driver-based budget construction, not lump sums.
  - Payment milestones tied to deliverables and value events, not calendar dates.
  - Trade options (scope, term, payment) prepared rather than pure discount.
  - PSAR framing for the implementation fee vs subscription cost.
  - TCO comparison over 5 years; payback inside [N] months.

### 6. Operating Capability That Survives the Engagement

- **Theme**: Our engagement ends with the client able to operate, optimise, and expand without us.
- **Discriminators**:
  - Capacity-transfer workstream with measurable outcomes.
  - Runbook hand-over for control plane, application plane, support, success, lifecycle, and security operations.
  - Named client counterparts paired with agency leads from Phase 2.
  - 90-day support window followed by a clear retainer or self-operation transition.
  - Exit clause and data-return commitment in the MSA.

## Tuning Themes to Buyer Levels

| Buyer | Themes to Emphasise |
|---|---|
| CEO / MD | Engineered adoption + operating capability + commercial model |
| CFO | Commercial model + business case + TCO |
| CIO / CTO | SaaS engineering literacy + integration realism + operating capability |
| CISO | Regulator-grade trust + compliance + risk register |
| COO | Engineered adoption + operating capability + change management |
| Sponsor (sector executive) | Vertical depth + business case + adoption |
| Procurement | Transparent commercial + compliance with framework + references |

## Discriminator Sentence Patterns

Use these patterns when stating a discriminator inside the proposal:

- "Unlike a generic SaaS implementation, our methodology treats [specific aspect] as a first-class concern, evidenced by [specific evidence]."
- "Where some vendors offer [common feature], we additionally deliver [specific differentiator], because [reason]."
- "We have done this before, specifically [named comparable], with [measured outcome]."
- "Our [specific artefact] is the operational proof of this claim, available on request and reproduced in [reference]."

## Ghost-Pack Language

When the agency knows or can reasonably anticipate the competitor's positioning, use ghost-pack language to surface the gap without naming the competitor:

- "Some vendors will offer a price below ours by removing tenant isolation engineering. We do not remove it because [regulator / risk / cost] makes it non-negotiable."
- "Some vendors will promise three-month go-live by skipping the security review. We do not skip it because [regulator / liability] makes it non-negotiable."
- "Some vendors will bundle customer success invisibly inside the licence cost. We separate it because [accountability / staffing / measurement] requires it."

Ghost-pack language must be factually accurate and never personal. The discipline is to surface the trade-off the evaluator otherwise would not see.

## Win-Theme Testing — Before Final Submission

Before submitting, every win theme must pass these tests:

- **Specific**: would a reviewer recognise it as different from a generic claim?
- **Evidenced**: is there a discriminator (named comparable, artefact, or quantified outcome)?
- **Relevant**: is it on the buyer's mind, not just the agency's preference?
- **Defensible under questioning**: can the team defend it under cross-examination in an oral presentation?
- **Coherent**: does it cohere with the rest of the proposal (methodology, work plan, team, pricing)?

A win theme that fails any of these should be removed or rewritten.

## Anti-Patterns

- "We are the leading provider of [generic capability]" — leading by whose measure?
- "World-class" — not a discriminator.
- "Innovative" — not a discriminator; describe the innovation specifically.
- "Trusted partner" — not a discriminator; show the trust evidence.
- Listing six identical themes that all sound like "we are good at this" — pick three and prove them.
