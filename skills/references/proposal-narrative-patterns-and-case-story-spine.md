# Proposal Narrative Patterns and Case Story Spine

Derived from local HTML extractions on storytelling in design, design-decision articulation, strategic UX writing, proposal persuasion, and evaluator communication.

## Evaluator Journey

Every major proposal should guide the evaluator through this journey:

1. Recognition: "They understand our situation."
2. Confidence: "They have a credible answer."
3. Proof: "They have done comparable work or can evidence the method."
4. Control: "They understand risks, approvals, budget, and implementation."
5. Decision: "Awarding to them is defensible."

## Narrative Spine

Use this structure across cover letter, executive summary, understanding, methodology, and price:

- Current situation: the client's context and pressure.
- Complication: the cost, risk, opportunity, or failure point.
- Decisive question: what the assignment must answer.
- Proposed answer: the firm's method and deliverables.
- Proof: comparable results, evidence, team, or validated assumptions.
- Implementation path: phases, gates, support, and measurement.
- Outcome: what improves and how it will be judged.

## Case Study Story Card

Use this template for premium relevant-experience entries:

```text
Client and context: [Who, where, and why it mattered]
Challenge: [Specific issue, constraint, or risk]
Our role: [Lead, specialist, implementer, advisor, support partner]
Decision logic: [Why this approach was selected]
Intervention: [What was done]
Result: [Quantified or verifiable outcome]
Relevance: [Why this proves fit for the current bid]
```

## Design Rationale Pattern

When explaining a website, software, UX, AI, or service decision, use:

- Client goal: what business or service outcome the decision supports.
- User need: what user behaviour, constraint, or pain point it addresses.
- Evidence: analytics, research, stakeholder input, testing, comparable experience, or standard.
- Trade-off: what was prioritised and what was deliberately avoided.
- Sign-off point: what the client will review and approve.

## Storyboard Use

Use a storyboard, journey example, or scenario when it helps evaluators see:

- the user's before-and-after experience
- where a handoff, error, or delay is removed
- how a prototype will test adoption before full build
- why a technical choice matters to a non-technical stakeholder

## Style Rules

- Make the story compact and factual.
- Avoid theatrical language.
- Use one governing idea per section.
- End each section with value, decision, or next-step logic.
