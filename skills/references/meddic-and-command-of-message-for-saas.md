# MEDDIC and Command of Message for SaaS Proposals

Qualification overlay for SaaS implementation and SaaS product-development proposals. MEDDIC, MEDDPICC, and CHAMP are widely used SaaS qualification mnemonics — this reference adapts them to the proposal-writing context so the engine produces proposal text that signals qualified, defensible, and evaluator-aware thinking.

## When to Use

- Writing the Understanding of the Assignment section, the Executive Summary, or the Win Plan annex.
- Reviewing a proposal draft for missing buyer logic before submission.
- Designing the Mutual Action Plan and the close plan inside the proposal.
- Briefing the agency's account team before the bid-day presentation or BAFO.

## MEDDPICC, in Engine Vocabulary

The mnemonic is industry-standard; the engine re-states each element in proposal-facing language so it can be used without naming the framework explicitly to evaluators.

- **Metrics** — the measurable outcomes the engagement must improve. Stated in the proposal as the "Value at Stake" block: baseline, target, value, sensitivity.
- **Economic Buyer** — the executive who can sign and defend the spend. Named in the proposal as the "Executive Sponsor" the engagement is designed to serve.
- **Decision Criteria** — the criteria the buyer will actually use to award. Stated as the "How this proposal answers your evaluation criteria" matrix in the Executive Summary.
- **Decision Process** — the steps from shortlist to signature, with owners and dates. Stated as the "Path to Award" block (and the MAP after award).
- **Paper Process** — the legal, procurement, and security review steps. Stated as the "Procurement Readiness" block and addressed in the Trust and Compliance section.
- **Identify Pain** — the diagnosed pain chain. Stated as the "Pain Chain" block in Understanding of the Assignment.
- **Champion** — the internal advocate who carries the proposal across the line. Stated as a sponsor/champion/blocker map in the win-plan annex (internal use, not always submitted).
- **Competition** — the realistic alternatives (incumbent renewal, point solution, build-in-house, do-nothing). Stated as the "Why this approach over the alternatives" block.

## CHAMP, in Engine Vocabulary

Useful when MEDDPICC is too heavy for SMM-segment SaaS proposals.

- **Challenges** — the diagnosed pain.
- **Authority** — the named decision owner.
- **Money** — the budget logic and what it buys.
- **Prioritisation** — why this assignment must proceed now versus competing priorities.

## Command of Message — Translating to Proposal Language

The "Command of Message" discipline (from Force Management, widely used in SaaS GTM) frames every value claim through six lenses. The engine re-states them as the *Six-Lens Value Claim* and uses them inside the Executive Summary, the Methodology selling paragraphs, and the business case:

1. **Before** — the named pain or risk the buyer experiences today.
2. **After** — the named state the buyer reaches with the proposed approach.
3. **Required Capabilities** — the specific capabilities the buyer needs to move from Before to After.
4. **How the Proposed Approach Delivers Each Capability** — methodology, team, governance, and proof that creates the capability.
5. **Quantified Outcome** — the measurable difference in metric, time, cost, or risk.
6. **Proof** — comparable engagement, named client where possible (or anonymised vertical/scale comparator), with outcome figure.

Every load-bearing value claim in a premium SaaS proposal must pass the Six-Lens test. Claims that cannot be stated in this form should be removed.

## Proposal Section Mapping

| Section | MEDDPICC / CHAMP / Command of Message Application |
|---|---|
| 01 Cover Letter | Names the Executive Buyer, the Critical Event, the Pain (one line each). |
| 02 Executive Summary | Six-Lens Value Claim for the top three differentiators; Decision Criteria matrix. |
| 03 Understanding of the Assignment | Pain Chain; Metrics (Value at Stake); Decision Process; Paper Process. |
| 06 Methodology | Each phase's selling paragraph passes Six-Lens. |
| 07 Team Composition | Named agency leads who will engage the Economic Buyer and the Champion. |
| 08 Work Plan | Path to Award + MAP through to go-live. |
| 10 Financial Proposal | Money block tied to outcome metric and payback. |

## Qualification Gate Before Submitting

Before final submission, the engine should be able to answer in one sentence each:

- Which metric, on which baseline, by when, worth how much?
- Who is the Economic Buyer? Are they actually in the room?
- What are the evaluation criteria and how does the proposal answer each?
- What is the next decision and when is it scheduled?
- What is the procurement, legal, and security review path and how long does each step take?
- What is the diagnosed pain chain, and what evidence backs it?
- Who is the Champion and what do they need to win the internal argument?
- What are the realistic alternatives and how does this proposal compare?

A proposal that cannot answer these questions is not yet qualified. Either request clarification, declare assumptions explicitly, or escalate to a no-bid decision.

## Anti-Patterns

- Naming MEDDIC, MEDDPICC, CHAMP, or Command of Message inside the client-facing proposal — these are internal mnemonics, not client artefacts.
- Treating MEDDPICC as a checklist of activities rather than a qualification gate. The output is a proposal, not a filled-in template.
- Using investor-grade vocabulary (Rule of 40, magic number) with non-investor buyers — it signals tone deafness.
- Asserting the Six-Lens Value Claim without proof — every claim needs a comparable outcome figure or a clearly stated assumption with sensitivity.
