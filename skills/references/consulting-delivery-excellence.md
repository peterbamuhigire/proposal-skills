# Consulting Delivery Excellence

Frameworks and standards for world-class consulting delivery. This reference ensures that proposals do not merely promise quality — they describe specific, proven methods for delivering it. Draw from this file when writing methodology sections, quality assurance components, and implementation approaches.

---

## 1. The McKinsey Problem-Solving Process

The gold standard for structured consulting analysis, from *The McKinsey Mind*:

### Step 1: Form an Initial Hypothesis

Before any data gathering, state a preliminary answer to the overriding question. This is not a guess — it is an informed position based on available information, sector knowledge, and experience.

**Why:** Starting with a hypothesis focuses the analysis. Without one, consultants collect data aimlessly. With one, every data point either supports or refutes a specific proposition.

### Step 2: Quick and Dirty Test (QDT)

Subject the hypothesis to a rapid plausibility check before investing significant effort:
- Does it pass the common sense test?
- Is there readily available data that immediately refutes it?
- Have similar hypotheses been tested in comparable engagements?

If the QDT kills the hypothesis, reformulate before proceeding. This saves the client's money and the team's time.

### Step 3: Build an Issue Tree

Decompose the hypothesis into MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) branches:
- Each branch is a testable sub-hypothesis
- Branches must not overlap (mutually exclusive)
- Together, branches must cover all possibilities (collectively exhaustive)
- Typically 3-5 branches at the first level, each with 2-3 sub-branches

### Step 4: Develop a Work Plan

Convert the issue tree into a work plan:
- Each branch becomes a workstream or phase
- For each branch: what data is needed, who will gather it, how long it will take
- Prioritise branches by impact and feasibility — the 80/20 rule applies: focus on the 20% of analyses that will yield 80% of the insight

### Step 5: Test and Iterate

As data comes in, branches are confirmed or eliminated:
- Confirmed branches are developed further
- Eliminated branches are documented (why they were ruled out) and set aside
- The hypothesis is refined with each iteration
- The process converges on root cause and recommendation

### Proposal application

Describe this process in the methodology section. Show the evaluator that analysis will be structured and efficient, not open-ended. Reference the initial hypothesis explicitly: "Based on our preliminary review, we hypothesise that… Our methodology is designed to test this rigorously."

## 2. The 6-Step Consulting Workflow

From *Consulting Management EXPLAINED* — the end-to-end structure of a consulting engagement:

| Step | Action | Key Questions | Deliverable |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Define** | What is the real problem? What is the scope? What are the constraints? | Problem statement, scope document |
| 2 | **Structure** | How should the problem be decomposed? What framework applies? | Issue tree, conceptual framework |
| 3 | **Prioritise** | Which issues matter most? Where is the 80/20? | Prioritised issue list, work plan |
| 4 | **Analyse** | What does the data say? What are the root causes? | Analysis outputs, findings |
| 5 | **Solve** | What are the options? Which is recommended? Why? | Recommendations with rationale |
| 6 | **Communicate** | How do we present this for maximum impact? | Final deliverable, presentation |

### The 80/20 throughout

At every step, ask: "What is the 20% of work that will yield 80% of the value?" This prevents scope creep, analysis paralysis, and gold-plating. In proposals, this translates to: "Our methodology focuses effort on the highest-impact areas first, ensuring that the client begins to see value early in the engagement."

## 3. Structure-Focus-Design-Embed (SFDE)

A four-phase framework for solution implementation:

| Phase | Purpose | Activities |
|---|---|---|
| **Structure** | Organise the problem space | Map stakeholders, define scope, establish governance, align team |
| **Focus** | Identify what matters most | Prioritise issues, select quick wins, define success criteria |
| **Design** | Create the solution | Develop options, test with stakeholders, refine, document |
| **Embed** | Make the solution stick | Train users, establish processes, transfer knowledge, monitor adoption |

**Critical insight:** Most consulting failures occur in the Embed phase — not because the solution was wrong, but because it was not adequately transferred to the client. A world-class proposal explicitly describes how solutions will be embedded, not just delivered.

## 4. Value Realisation Method (VRM)

From *Consulting Essentials* — the method for connecting consulting outputs to measurable client value:

```
Free Cash Flow
    ↑
Operational Levers (revenue, cost, asset efficiency)
    ↑
Business Processes (the specific processes that drive the levers)
    ↑
Initiatives (the consulting interventions that improve the processes)
```

### Application to proposals

When writing methodology sections for implementation projects, trace the chain:
1. Name the initiative (what we will do)
2. Identify the business process it improves
3. Link to the operational lever it affects
4. Quantify or qualify the financial impact

**Example:** "We will redesign the procurement approval workflow [initiative], reducing the average number of approval steps from 7 to 3 [process improvement], which directly reduces procurement cycle time [operational lever], yielding an estimated 15-25% reduction in procurement costs [financial impact]."

This chain of logic is what transforms a methodology from a list of activities into a value proposition.

## 5. The Done-Done Standard

A consulting deliverable is not complete when the first draft is written. It is complete when it meets the Done-Done standard:

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Done** | The work is technically complete — analysis finished, document drafted |
| **Done-Done** | The work is complete, checked, proofed, formatted, and ready to present as a professional deliverable to any audience |

### Done-Done checklist for consulting deliverables

- [ ] Content is accurate and all claims are supported by evidence
- [ ] Document is professionally formatted with consistent headers, fonts, and spacing
- [ ] All exhibits are numbered, captioned, and cross-referenced
- [ ] Spelling and grammar have been checked (British English for East African contexts)
- [ ] Executive summary can stand alone as a complete argument
- [ ] Recommendations are specific, actionable, and prioritised
- [ ] The document passes the "overnight test" — would you be comfortable if the client saw it right now?

### Proposal application

Reference the Done-Done standard in the methodology's quality assurance section: "Every deliverable undergoes our Done-Done quality process: peer review, editorial review, and a final quality gate before submission to the client."

## 6. Drucker's Five Most Important Questions

From *Consulting Drucker* — Peter Drucker's framework for organisational assessment. These five questions, applied at the start of any engagement, cut through complexity to the essentials:

| # | Question | What It Reveals |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **What is our mission?** | Whether the organisation has clarity of purpose — or is drifting |
| 2 | **Who is our customer?** | Whether the organisation knows whom it serves — primary and supporting customers |
| 3 | **What does the customer value?** | Whether the organisation delivers what matters — not what it assumes matters |
| 4 | **What are our results?** | Whether the organisation measures what matters — qualitative and quantitative |
| 5 | **What is our plan?** | Whether the organisation has a coherent path forward — mission, goals, objectives, action steps, budget |

### The Ignorance Approach

Drucker's most counterintuitive principle: approach every new engagement with deliberate ignorance, not assumed expertise. "What everybody knows is usually wrong." Question the obvious. Do not assume that because you have seen similar problems before, this situation is the same.

**Proposal application:** "Our methodology begins with structured discovery — not to validate assumptions, but to understand this institution's unique context, constraints, and dynamics. We bring frameworks and experience, but we apply them to what we find, not to what we assume."

### The Staff Study Method

Drucker's structured approach to recommendation development:

1. **Problem definition** — What is the problem we are trying to solve? (Not the symptoms)
2. **Relevant factors** — What factors are relevant? What data do we need?
3. **Alternative courses** — What are the possible solutions? (Including "do nothing")
4. **Analysis** — Evaluate each alternative against criteria
5. **Conclusions** — What does the analysis tell us?
6. **Recommendations** — What specific actions should be taken, by whom, by when?

This structure ensures that recommendations are grounded in analysis, not opinion.

## 7. Design Thinking for Consulting

The five-phase Design Thinking process applied to consulting engagements:

| Phase | Consulting Application |
|---|---|
| **Empathise** | Immerse in the client's operational reality — interviews, observation, shadowing, journey mapping |
| **Define** | Synthesise findings into a clear problem statement — not the ToR's version, but the real underlying problem |
| **Ideate** | Generate multiple solution options — brainstorm, benchmark, draw from cross-sector experience |
| **Prototype** | Test solutions at small scale before full implementation — pilot, proof of concept, simulation |
| **Test** | Validate with real users and data — iterate based on feedback before scaling |

**When to reference in proposals:** Use Design Thinking language when the assignment involves innovation, service design, user-facing systems, or situations where the "right answer" is not known in advance. "Our approach applies Design Thinking principles — we prototype and test solutions with end users before committing to full-scale implementation."

## 8. Quick Diagnosis and Solution Validation

### Quick Diagnosis Checklist

Before diving into detailed analysis, run this rapid assessment:

1. What is the stated problem? (Client's words)
2. What is the real problem? (Our interpretation after initial review)
3. Who are the key stakeholders and what are their positions?
4. What has been tried before? Why did it fail or succeed?
5. What constraints exist? (Budget, timeline, political, regulatory)
6. What does success look like? (Client's definition)
7. What are the quick wins? (Visible improvements within 30 days)

### Solution Validation Checklist

Before recommending any solution, test it against:

1. **Feasibility** — Can this actually be implemented given the client's constraints?
2. **Acceptability** — Will key stakeholders accept this? Will users adopt it?
3. **Sustainability** — Will this continue to work after we leave?
4. **Scalability** — Can this scale across the organisation or be replicated?
5. **Risk** — What could go wrong, and can the risks be mitigated?
6. **Value** — Does the benefit justify the cost and effort?

**Proposal application:** "Every recommendation undergoes our six-point validation framework — testing for feasibility, acceptability, sustainability, scalability, risk, and value — before inclusion in the final deliverable."

## 9. The Griffin Dilemma Framework

Seven recurring dilemmas in consulting engagements, from *Strategic Consulting*. Awareness of these prevents common project failures:

| Dilemma | Description | Prescribed Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope creep vs. rigid scope** | Client requests additional work beyond the ToR | Establish a formal change control process; additional scope requires written agreement and may require additional budget |
| **Speed vs. thoroughness** | Pressure to deliver quickly vs. need for rigorous analysis | Agree quality standards upfront; use the 80/20 rule to focus on high-impact areas; never compromise analytical integrity for speed |
| **Independence vs. alignment** | Maintaining objective recommendations vs. aligning with client preferences | Present evidence-based findings honestly; offer options rather than a single recommendation; never suppress uncomfortable findings |
| **Individual insight vs. institutional buy-in** | A brilliant recommendation that lacks organisational support | Always validate findings with stakeholders before finalising; use the prewiring technique; build consensus through the process |
| **Short-term wins vs. long-term sustainability** | Quick fixes that may undermine lasting change | Include both — quick wins for momentum, structural changes for sustainability; sequence appropriately |
| **Standardisation vs. customisation** | Applying proven frameworks vs. tailoring to local context | Start with frameworks, adapt to context; never apply a template without customisation; never customise so much that the framework loses its power |
| **Client dependency vs. capability building** | Doing the work ourselves vs. building client capacity to do it | Default to capability building; every engagement should leave the client stronger; include knowledge transfer in every phase |

## 10. Six Core Consulting Mission Types

From *Strategic Consulting* — different assignment types require different approaches:

| Mission Type | Purpose | Typical Deliverables |
|---|---|---|
| **Diagnostic** | Understand what is happening and why | Assessment report, root cause analysis, benchmarking results |
| **Strategy** | Determine what to do | Strategic plan, roadmap, options appraisal with recommendation |
| **Organisation** | Design how to structure for execution | Organisational design, governance framework, role definitions |
| **Implementation** | Execute the plan | Implemented systems, trained staff, operational processes |
| **Coaching** | Build individual or team capability | Skills transfer, mentoring outputs, capacity building reports |
| **Interim management** | Fill a capability gap temporarily | Managed function, operational outputs, transition plan |

**Proposal application:** Identify which mission type(s) the ToR describes, and structure the methodology accordingly. Most complex assignments combine 2-3 mission types in sequence (typically Diagnostic → Strategy → Implementation).

## 11. The 90/10 Principle

From *Consulting Essentials*: 90% of a consulting project's value comes from 10% of the activities — typically the activities closest to the client's core decision. The remaining 90% of activities are necessary but do not directly drive the outcome.

**Implication for proposals:**
- Identify the 10% activities and give them prominence in the methodology
- Ensure senior team members are allocated to these activities, not the routine work
- In the work plan, show that the critical-path activities receive disproportionate attention

**Methodology language:** "Our work plan allocates senior expertise to the highest-impact activities — [name them] — ensuring that the analysis driving the client's key decisions receives our most experienced professionals."

## 12. Theory of Abandonment

From *Consulting Drucker*: before recommending what to start, recommend what to stop. Every organisation carries activities, processes, products, and commitments that no longer serve their purpose. Abandonment frees resources for what matters.

**Proposal application:** In diagnostic and strategy assignments, include a systematic review of what should be abandoned:
- "Our assessment will include a structured review of existing initiatives, processes, and commitments to identify those that no longer serve the [institution's] mission and should be discontinued to free resources for higher-priority actions."

This is a powerful differentiator — most consultants only recommend what to add, not what to remove.

## 13. Seven Sources of Innovation (Drucker)

When proposals involve innovation, digital transformation, or new programme design, reference Drucker's seven sources, ordered from most to least reliable:

| # | Source | Reliability | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **The unexpected** | Highest | An unexpected success or failure in operations reveals an unmet need |
| 2 | **Incongruity** | High | Gap between reality and assumptions — "we thought X but data shows Y" |
| 3 | **Process need** | High | A bottleneck or weakness in an existing process that demands a fix |
| 4 | **Industry/market structure change** | Moderate | Regulatory reform, new entrants, technology disruption |
| 5 | **Demographics** | Moderate | Population shifts, urbanisation, age structure changes |
| 6 | **Changes in perception** | Lower | Cultural shifts in how people view a sector or service |
| 7 | **New knowledge** | Lowest | Scientific or technological breakthroughs (longest lead time, highest uncertainty) |

**Proposal application:** When proposing innovation or transformation work, ground the approach in the more reliable sources first. "Our methodology begins with the most actionable sources of innovation — process needs and operational incongruities — before exploring broader structural and market changes."

## 14. Primum Non Nocere — First, Do No Harm

From Drucker, applied to consulting: before implementing any change, assess whether the proposed intervention could make things worse. Not all problems require intervention, and not all interventions are improvements.

### The test

Before recommending any action, ask:
1. What is the worst that could happen if this recommendation is implemented?
2. Is that worse than the current situation?
3. Can the risk be mitigated?
4. Is the potential benefit worth the potential harm?

**Proposal application:** "Our approach applies the precautionary principle — every recommendation is assessed not only for its potential benefit but also for its potential to create unintended negative consequences. We will not recommend change for its own sake."

## 15. The Professional Services Tightrope

From *The Seven Principles of Professional Services* — successful delivery requires maintaining balance between competing tensions:

| Tension | Balance Required |
|---|---|
| Client's business improvements | AND the firm's time/profitability goals |
| Pushing hard to keep the engagement progressing | AND stopping if damaging issues arise |
| Being fully engaged with the client's business | AND being fully engaged with your own firm |
| Solving problems by diving into details | AND guiding the client by focusing on the bigger picture |

**The 75/25 Rule:** Subject matter expertise accounts for roughly 75% of what is required for successful delivery. The remaining 25% is relationship management, expectation management, and engagement discipline. Proposals that demonstrate mastery of the 25% differentiate from technically competent but relationally weak competitors.

## 16. Always Know What "Done" Looks Like

Before starting any engagement, establish mutually agreed completion criteria. "Done" is not the same as "success" — sometimes the definition must fall back from success to a safe, professional conclusion.

### Minimum elements of "Done"

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| Detailed scope description | What is and is not included |
| Expected effort | Resources required |
| Price | Fee structure and total |
| Completion criteria | How we objectively measure that the engagement is complete |
| Responsibilities | Who is responsible for each component |
| Change process | How "done" can be modified (formal change control) |
| Exclusions | What is explicitly NOT included |

### Prescribing Done

Use expert status to prescribe what the client needs rather than endlessly asking what they want:

**Language pattern:** "Our experience, based on more than [X] implementations, is that [alternative approach] is not worth pursuing. It is difficult to design, costly to deploy, and prone to failure. We strongly recommend [prescribed approach], which has proven effective across [N] comparable engagements."

### Customer Acceptance Techniques

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **Frequent check-ins** | Iterative validation to catch misalignment early |
| **Client-written acceptance tests** | Clients define their own success criteria with expected results |
| **Partial acceptance** | Document which deliverables have been accepted and which have not |
| **Conditional acceptance** | Accept with conditions for completing outstanding items |

**Proposal application:** "Each phase concludes with a formal acceptance gate. The client reviews deliverables against pre-agreed criteria and provides written acceptance before the next phase begins."

## 17. The Triple Constraints (Iron Triangle)

**Scope + Effort + Time = Quality**

If any one constraint shifts, the others must shift proportionally to maintain quality. There is no free lunch.

| Constraint | Definition | Measured In |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope** | Depth and breadth of work | Requirements, deliverables |
| **Effort** | Total use of resources | Person-days, not currency |
| **Time** | Calendar duration | Working days |
| **Quality** | Resultant product of the three | Not a constraint — an output |

**The Yestimate warning:** A "Yestimate" is an estimate designed primarily to make the client say "yes" rather than to reflect reality. It creates expectation misalignment from day one. Proposals must resist the temptation to under-estimate for competitive advantage.

**Proposal application:** "Our pricing reflects a realistic assessment of the effort required. We have structured the engagement so that scope, effort, and timeline are balanced to deliver the quality the [client] expects."

## 18. Manage Expectations — The EUR Framework

From *Seven Principles* — the core framework for difficult conversations during delivery:

### EUR: Empathy, Understanding, Resolution

| Phase | Action | Key Principle |
|---|---|---|
| **Empathy** | Let the client express concerns without interruption. Validate feelings. Take detailed notes. Do NOT admit fault. | Empathy is feeling WITH someone, not FOR someone |
| **Understanding** | Gather all perspectives. Identify where views differ. Understanding does not mean agreeing. | Unify the problem statement before attempting solutions |
| **Resolution** | Take ownership of driving the resolution process. Set expectations using Who, What, When, and How. | Own the process even if you don't own the problem |

### Selling the Resolution

| Technique | Language |
|---|---|
| **Focus on outcome** | "Do we really need to argue about the process when the result will be the same?" |
| **Be prescriptive** | Use expert status to recommend the path forward |
| **"Yes, if…"** | "Yes, we could do that, if we are willing to [consequence]." Never say "no" — redirect |

### Proactive Escalation

The strongest form of expectation management: identify the misalignment before the client does and escalate it with a proposed resolution. This demonstrates trusted advisor status.

**Proposal application:** "Our engagement methodology includes structured expectation management — weekly alignment checks, formal change control, and proactive escalation of any risk to delivery before it impacts outcomes."

## 19. F.A.A.S.T. Quality Standard

Five core quality traits for consulting delivery:

| Trait | Description | Self-Assessment Question |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Always focused on the client's outcomes, not the firm's process | Am I focused on the same outcome the client is focused on? |
| **Attention to Detail** | Master the client's complex details through functional decomposition | Have I broken this problem into component parts that add up to the whole? |
| **Accountable** | Four-way accountability: you→firm, firm→you, team→client, client→team | Is every responsibility assigned and understood? |
| **Skilled** | Continuously hone both technical and soft skills | Am I the right person for this task, or should I delegate? |
| **Trustworthy** | The culmination of all other traits — trust is built one deliverable at a time | Would the client trust me with their most sensitive information? |

**Proposal application:** "Our delivery teams operate to the F.A.A.S.T. standard — maintaining focus on client outcomes, attention to detail through structured decomposition, clear accountability, continuously updated skills, and the trust that comes from consistent quality."

## 20. Frontload Every Engagement

From *The McKinsey Edge* — the first week sets the impression for the entire engagement. Peak output should occur on day 1-2, not ramp up gradually.

### The Basket of Essentials (First Week Checklist)

| # | Action | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Get senior leaders to spend time with you | Establishes relationship and alignment before work begins |
| 2 | Complete the end storyline up front | Reduce everything to 3 guiding questions before making any deliverables |
| 3 | Ask the right questions early | Perspective, stakeholder/relationship, scope/goal, risk/constraint questions |
| 4 | Quick due diligence on team members | 30-minute 1-on-1s about learning goals and working styles |
| 5 | Organise all necessary meetings | Lock in progress reviews and workshops in week one |

### The One-Week Answer

Have a credible preliminary answer to the engagement's core question within the first week. This demonstrates pace and builds client confidence.

**Proposal application:** "Our methodology frontloads the engagement — within the first week, we will present preliminary findings and a refined work plan, giving [client] early confidence in the direction of the analysis."

## 21. The Double-Clicking Communication Technique

From *The McKinsey Edge* — prepare every topic at three levels of depth so any stakeholder can "click" deeper:

| Level | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Level 1** | The one-line answer (headline) | "Procurement cycle time can be reduced by 40%" |
| **Level 2** | The 30-second expansion (3-4 supporting points) | "Three drivers: approval bottleneck, manual documentation, supplier fragmentation" |
| **Level 3** | Detailed drill-down on any supporting point | Full analysis of the approval bottleneck with data and recommendations |

### The Four C-Level Questions

What every senior executive wants to hear:

1. What is the overall project status — good or bad?
2. What are one or two examples that support the status?
3. What do I plan to do about any problems?
4. What can the executive do to help?

**Proposal application:** "All client communications follow a structured top-down format — headline finding, supporting evidence, and detailed analysis available on request — ensuring that senior stakeholders receive the level of detail they need."

## 22. Pull vs. Push Communication

From *The McKinsey Edge* — two fundamentally different communication approaches:

| Mode | Method | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Push** | Telling people what to think | Creates resistance, less effective |
| **Pull** | Asking questions that lead people to your conclusion | Creates buy-in, more effective |

**The rule:** Fewer words = more authority. Every unnecessary word dilutes the message.

### Five Types of Second-Order Questions

Beyond surface-level questions, these five types probe the underlying dynamics:

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Dream situation** | "What if…" | "What if budget were not a constraint — what would you do?" |
| **Options and alternatives** | "What else could we…" | "What other approaches have been considered?" |
| **Other's shoes** | "How does [stakeholder] see…" | "How would the regional directors view this change?" |
| **Need to believe** | "What would need to be true for…" | "What would need to be true for this to work?" |
| **Next steps** | "What is the real, practical…" | "What is the first concrete action?" |

**Proposal application:** When describing stakeholder engagement or workshop facilitation in the methodology, reference the pull communication approach: "Our facilitation methodology uses guided questioning rather than directive presentations, building client ownership of findings and recommendations."

## 25. Design Sprint (Google Ventures)

A five-day compressed discovery-to-testing methodology. Use when the ToR requires rapid prototyping, solution validation, or user-centred design:

| Day | Focus | Output |
|---|---|---|
| **Monday** | Map the problem, set goals, identify target | Problem map, sprint questions, target area |
| **Tuesday** | Generate solutions individually | Sketched solution concepts |
| **Wednesday** | Decide on best solution, create storyboard | Storyboard for prototype |
| **Thursday** | Build a realistic prototype | Testable prototype |
| **Friday** | Test with real users, learn | Validated or invalidated assumptions |

**Preparation (Week -1):** Recruit test users, assemble cross-functional team, gather background data.
**Follow-up (Week +1):** Document findings, plan next iteration, build momentum for implementation.

**Proposal application:** "We will conduct a structured Design Sprint — compressing the discovery-to-testing cycle into five days — to validate key assumptions before committing to full-scale implementation."

## 26. Thinking Map vs. Activity Map

From *Consulting Essentials* — a critical distinction in methodology design:

| Map Type | Purpose | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **Thinking Map** | The intellectual framework — what decisions must be made and what evidence is needed | Hypotheses, issues, analytical questions, decision points |
| **Activity Map** | The operational plan — who does what and when | Gantt charts, task assignments, milestones, dependencies |

**The rule:** The Thinking Map must precede the Activity Map. A work plan without a thinking map is a list of activities with no analytical purpose. Three types of thinking maps:

1. **Decision Matrix** — what decisions will be made, what information drives each decision
2. **Issue Tree** — MECE decomposition of the problem into testable branches
3. **Hypothesis Approach** — initial hypothesis with structured tests to confirm or refute

**Proposal application:** Structure every methodology section with the thinking map first (conceptual approach, analytical framework, hypothesis) and the activity map second (phases, tasks, timeline). This signals that the firm thinks before it acts.

## 27. Three Requirements of Change

From Holly Benson via *Consulting Essentials* — every change initiative requires three conditions, and failure in any one blocks adoption:

| Requirement | Description | Consulting Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Know what is expected** | People must clearly understand what the new behaviour, process, or system requires of them | Clear communication, job aids, process documentation |
| **Have the skills and tools** | People must be equipped to meet the new expectations | Training, technology, resources, support structures |
| **Be held accountable** | Consequences (positive and negative) must exist for meeting or failing to meet expectations | Performance metrics, management oversight, incentive alignment |

**Proposal application:** "Our change management approach addresses all three requirements for sustainable adoption: ensuring stakeholders understand what is expected, equipping them with the skills and tools to meet those expectations, and establishing accountability mechanisms to sustain the change."

## 28. Eight Archetypal Consulting Dilemmas

From *Consulting Essentials* — eight recurring tensions that every consulting engagement must navigate. Naming the dilemma in the methodology demonstrates sophisticated understanding:

| Dilemma | Tension |
|---|---|
| **Head vs. Heart** | Data-driven analysis vs. stakeholder emotions and politics |
| **Inside vs. Outside** | Internal perspective (client's view) vs. external perspective (market/benchmark view) |
| **Cost vs. Benefit** | Investment required vs. value delivered |
| **Product vs. Market** | Improving the offering vs. expanding the audience |
| **Change vs. Stability** | Driving transformation vs. maintaining operational continuity |
| **Known vs. Unknown** | Working with available data vs. exploring uncharted territory |
| **Competing Priorities** | Multiple valid objectives that cannot all be maximised simultaneously |
| **Content vs. Process** | Getting the technical answer right vs. getting the organisational process right |

**Proposal application:** In the conceptual approach, name the dilemma the assignment must navigate: "This engagement sits at the intersection of [change and stability] — [client] must transform its [system/process] while maintaining operational continuity. Our methodology is designed to manage both simultaneously."

## 29. Puzzles vs. Mysteries

From *Consulting Essentials* — a diagnostic for classifying the engagement type:

| Type | Description | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Puzzle** | The problem is known; the solution requires finding specific information | Structured data gathering, gap analysis, benchmarking |
| **Mystery** | The problem itself is unclear; the engagement requires "problem finding" before problem solving | Exploratory research, hypothesis generation, stakeholder dialogue |

**Proposal application:** Most ToRs describe puzzles, but many real engagements are mysteries. Signal awareness of this: "While the ToR frames this as [specific problem], our experience suggests that the underlying dynamics may be more complex. Our inception phase is designed to validate the problem definition before committing to a solution pathway."

## 30. Secure-Substantial-Sequential Value Framework

From *Consulting Essentials* — three attributes that every consulting engagement must deliver:

| Attribute | Description | How to Demonstrate |
|---|---|---|
| **Secure** | Value must be credible and defensible — the client must believe it is real | Evidence-based methodology, benchmarked projections, conservative estimates |
| **Substantial** | Value must be significant enough to justify the investment | Quantified benefits, clear ROI, comparison against cost of inaction |
| **Sequential** | Value must be delivered incrementally — not all at the end | Phase-gated deliverables, quick wins, progressive results |

**Proposal application:** "Our methodology delivers value sequentially — each phase produces tangible outputs that build towards the final deliverable, giving [client] progressive returns on investment rather than a single end-of-engagement delivery."

## 31. Five Design Principles for Solutions

From *Consulting Essentials* — a framework for evaluating any proposed solution architecture, operating model, or system design:

| Principle | Description | Test |
|---|---|---|
| **Functional** | The solution works as intended | Does it solve the stated problem? |
| **Flexible** | The solution adapts to changing conditions | Can it accommodate future changes without redesign? |
| **Fluid** | The solution flows — no unnecessary friction or bottleneck | Are there manual handoffs, approval loops, or process gaps? |
| **Flat** | The solution minimises unnecessary hierarchy and complexity | Is the governance proportionate to the risk? |
| **Familiar** | The solution is intuitive to users | Will users adopt it without extensive retraining? |

**Proposal application:** "Every solution option will be evaluated against five design principles: functional (does it work?), flexible (does it adapt?), fluid (does it flow?), flat (is it simple?), and familiar (will users adopt it?)."

## 33. Nonaka's SECI Knowledge Spiral

From *Knowledge Management and Business Strategies* — the foundational model for knowledge creation and transfer in organisations:

| Mode | From → To | Method | Consulting Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Socialisation** | Tacit → Tacit | Observation, mentoring, shared experience | Pair working with client counterparts, on-the-job coaching |
| **Externalisation** | Tacit → Explicit | Articulation, documentation, concept creation | Codifying processes, writing SOPs, creating frameworks |
| **Combination** | Explicit → Explicit | Systemising, integrating, categorising | Database design, report consolidation, system integration |
| **Internalisation** | Explicit → Tacit | Learning by doing, practice, simulation | Training programmes, pilot implementations, hands-on workshops |

**Proposal application:** When the ToR involves knowledge management, capacity building, or institutional strengthening, describe the knowledge transfer approach using the SECI model: "Our knowledge transfer methodology moves through four modes — from tacit knowledge sharing through socialisation, to explicit documentation, systematic integration, and finally internalisation through practice."

## 34. Performance-Driven Knowledge Management — 7-Step Framework

From Massey et al. via *Knowledge Management and Business Strategies* — validated at IBM and Nortel. A complete engagement methodology for KM assignments:

| Step | Action | Deliverable |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Identify business objectives** | Prioritised objectives linked to strategic plan |
| 2 | **Map knowledge requirements** | Knowledge audit — what knowledge is needed, where it exists, where gaps are |
| 3 | **Assess current KM practices** | KM maturity assessment using importance-vs-effectiveness gap analysis |
| 4 | **Design KM interventions** | KM strategy with prioritised initiatives |
| 5 | **Develop enabling infrastructure** | Technology, process, and governance recommendations |
| 6 | **Implement KM initiatives** | Pilot and rollout plan |
| 7 | **Measure and refine** | KM performance metrics linked back to Step 1 objectives |

**Proposal application:** Use as the methodology backbone for any assignment involving knowledge management, institutional memory, lesson learning, or organisational learning systems.
