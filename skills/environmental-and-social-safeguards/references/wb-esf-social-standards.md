# World Bank Environmental and Social Framework — Social Standards

Dense practitioner reference covering ESS2, ESS4, ESS5, and ESS7. Paragraph numbers reference the World Bank ESF (2018, updated 2024). Load this file when writing methodology, social management plans, or safeguards sections for World Bank-financed projects.

---

## 1. ESS2: Labour and Working Conditions

### 1.1 Objectives (para. 1)

1. Promote safety and health of project workers.
2. Promote fair treatment, non-discrimination, and equal opportunity.
3. Protect project workers, including vulnerable workers (women, persons with disabilities, children of working age, migrant workers, contracted workers, community workers, primary supply workers).
4. Prevent use of all forms of forced labour and child labour.
5. Support principles of freedom of association and collective bargaining in a manner consistent with national law.
6. Provide accessible means for project workers to raise workplace concerns.

### 1.2 Scope — Four Categories of Project Workers (paras. 3–8)

| Category | Definition | Key characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct workers** (para. 3) | Employed or engaged directly by the Borrower, including civil servants assigned to the project | Full ESS2 applies |
| **Contracted workers** (para. 4) | Employed or engaged by third parties to perform work related to core functions of the project | Borrower exercises due diligence on third party; monitoring required |
| **Community workers** (para. 5) | Provide labour as community participation in project activities | Proportionate application; must be voluntary |
| **Primary supply workers** (para. 6) | Employed by primary suppliers providing goods/materials essential to core functions | Limited application: child labour, forced labour, serious safety issues |

**Core functions** (para. 4, fn. 3): production and/or service processes essential to a specific project activity without which the project cannot continue. Ancillary or support functions (catering, security, cleaning) are not core unless directly related to project objectives.

### 1.3 Government Civil Servants (paras. 8–9)

Limited application for civil servants working in connection with the project:

- Provisions on child and forced labour apply (paras. 17–20).
- Provisions on OHS apply (paras. 24–30) to extent technically and financially feasible.
- Other ESS2 requirements do not apply to civil servants — governed by existing terms of public sector employment.
- The Borrower provides ESA information on applicable civil service terms for Bank review.

### 1.4 Labour Management Procedures (paras. 9–10)

The Borrower develops and implements **Labour Management Procedures (LMP)** applicable to the project. The LMP sets out how project workers will be managed in accordance with national law and ESS2. It addresses:

- Type and characteristics of project workers, estimated numbers, timing.
- Applicable national labour legislation.
- Applicable ESS2 requirements for each category.
- Identification of key labour risks.
- Responsible staff/departments.
- Policies and procedures for managing contracted and primary supply workers.
- Brief overview of OHS measures.
- Grievance mechanism arrangements.

The LMP is proportionate to risks and impacts, and is prepared as early as possible during project preparation. For FI projects, the FI develops a framework and each sub-borrower prepares an LMP.

### 1.5 Terms and Conditions of Employment (paras. 10–13)

**Documentation** (para. 10): project workers receive clear, understandable information on their terms and conditions, including:

| Element | Details |
|---|---|
| Hours of work | Regular hours, overtime limits, rest periods |
| Wages | Payment frequency, deductions (lawful only), overtime compensation |
| Leave | Annual leave, sick leave, maternity/paternity leave per national law |
| Benefits | Social security, pension, insurance as applicable |
| Termination | Notice period, severance, reasons for dismissal |
| Workplace conduct | Disciplinary procedures, anti-harassment policies |

**Key requirements:**
- Wages paid regularly, at least monthly, directly to workers (para. 11).
- Adequate rest periods: at least one day off in seven, reasonable limits on overtime (para. 11).
- Written contracts for all direct workers (para. 10).
- No fees charged to workers for recruitment (para. 11, fn. 5).
- Termination only for valid reasons; no retaliatory dismissal for raising grievances (para. 12).
- Record-keeping of employment terms, working conditions, hours worked (para. 13).

### 1.6 Non-Discrimination and Equal Opportunity (para. 14)

- Employment decisions based on principles of non-discrimination.
- No discrimination on basis of personal characteristics unrelated to inherent job requirements: gender, race, nationality, ethnic/social origin, religion, disability, age, sexual orientation, gender identity, union membership, political affiliation.
- Special measures of protection or assistance for disadvantaged or vulnerable workers (women, people with disabilities, migrant workers, children of working age) are not discrimination.
- Where national law is inconsistent with ESS2, the Borrower seeks to carry out project activities in a manner consistent with ESS2 requirements to the extent feasible.

### 1.7 Workers' Organisations and Collective Bargaining (paras. 15–16)

- In countries where national law recognises workers' rights to form and join workers' organisations and bargain collectively, the Borrower must respect these rights.
- Workers' organisations function independently, without interference.
- No discrimination or retaliation against workers who participate or seek to participate.
- Where national law restricts these rights, the Borrower does not restrict alternative mechanisms for workers to express grievances and protect rights regarding working conditions.
- Where national law is silent, the Borrower does not discourage workers from forming or joining organisations of their choosing.

### 1.8 Child Labour and Minimum Age (paras. 17–19)

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Minimum age** (para. 17) | 14 years, or the minimum age under national law if higher; must not be below the age for completing compulsory education |
| **Young workers (14–18)** (para. 18) | May be employed subject to: (a) not economically exploitative; (b) no interference with education; (c) not harmful to health or development; (d) appropriate risk assessment for workplace hazards |
| **Hazardous work** (para. 19) | Persons under 18 must not be employed in hazardous work — work that by its nature or circumstances is likely to harm health, safety, or morals; includes night work, work with dangerous machinery/chemicals/substances, heavy loads, underground, at heights, confined spaces |

Child labour assessment is mandatory for all project worker categories, including primary supply chains.

### 1.9 Forced Labour (para. 20)

No forced labour in any form. Forced labour indicators include:

- Work or service exacted under menace of penalty.
- Restrictions on freedom of movement.
- Retention of identity documents or personal belongings.
- Threats of denunciation to authorities.
- Debt bondage.
- Deceptive recruitment practices.
- Withholding of wages.
- Excessive overtime under coercion.

Trafficking in persons is a form of forced labour. The Borrower must not employ trafficked persons and must take measures to prevent and respond to risks of trafficking in the project context.

### 1.10 Grievance Mechanism for Workers (paras. 21–23)

A **separate grievance mechanism** for project workers (distinct from ESS10 community-level mechanism):

- Available to all direct and contracted workers from commencement.
- Proportionate to nature and scale of project, risks and impacts.
- Provides timely, transparent, accessible resolution.
- Anonymous complaints permitted.
- No retribution against workers who raise grievances.
- Does not impede access to other judicial or administrative remedies.
- Workers informed of the mechanism at time of recruitment.

For community workers: simplified, accessible mechanism appropriate to the nature of the work.

### 1.11 Occupational Health and Safety (paras. 24–30)

**Hazard identification and risk assessment** (para. 24):
- Identify potential hazards to project workers, particularly those that may be life-threatening.
- Implement preventive and protective measures, including modification/substitution/elimination of hazardous conditions.
- Apply the General and Industry-Specific **World Bank Group Environmental, Health, and Safety Guidelines (EHSGs)**.

**Key OHS measures** (paras. 25–29):

| Measure | Requirements |
|---|---|
| **PPE** | Provided at no cost to workers; appropriate to hazards; training in use |
| **Training** | Regular OHS training; induction for new workers; refresher training; records kept |
| **Emergency preparedness** | First aid, fire safety, evacuation procedures; access to first aid equipment |
| **Incident reporting** | Recording and investigation of work-related incidents, injuries, diseases; reporting to relevant authorities per national law |
| **Accommodation** (para. 28) | Where provided: clean water, sanitation, adequate space, fire safety, freedom of movement; guidance in IFC/EBRD Workers' Accommodation standard |
| **Welfare facilities** | Adequate sanitation, drinking water, rest areas, changing facilities |
| **Hazardous substances** | Identification, labelling, safe handling, storage, disposal; safety data sheets |
| **Working at height, confined spaces** | Specific control measures, permits, trained personnel |
| **Right to remove** (para. 27) | Workers can remove themselves from imminent danger without retaliation |

**Monitoring** (para. 30): regular review and improvement of OHS performance; analysis of trends; preventive and corrective actions.

### 1.12 Contracted Workers (paras. 31–33)

- Borrower assesses capacity and commitment of third parties to meet ESS2 requirements.
- Contractual provisions require third parties to implement ESS2-consistent measures.
- Borrower monitors third-party performance: periodic audits, inspections, reports.
- Third-party grievance mechanisms must be assessed; Borrower provides supplementary mechanism if existing one is inadequate.
- Contracted workers must have access to a grievance mechanism.
- Where government is the contracting entity for civil works, specific arrangements for managing civil works contractors.

### 1.13 Community Workers (paras. 34–38)

- Labour must be provided on a **voluntary basis**.
- Community workers informed of their rights, including right to refuse work.
- Working conditions: reasonable hours, rest periods, safe working environment.
- Application of ESS2 is proportionate: child labour and forced labour prohibitions always apply; OHS measures proportionate to nature and scope of activities.
- Simplified grievance mechanism.
- Community workers are not expected to receive wages necessarily but must not be coerced.

### 1.14 Primary Supply Workers (paras. 39–42)

- Applies where there is a **substantial risk** of child labour, forced labour, or serious safety issues in the primary supply chain.
- The Borrower identifies such risks through assessment.
- Where identified: Borrower requires primary supplier to identify and address risks; monitors on an ongoing basis.
- Remedial measures may include: supplier audit, corrective action plans, alternative sourcing.
- Specific assessment considerations: country of origin, sector, known risks, vulnerability of workforce.
- Where government capacity is limited, Borrower works with government to progressively address risks.

## 2. ESS4: Community Health and Safety

### 2.1 Objectives (para. 1)

1. Anticipate and avoid adverse impacts on the health and safety of project-affected communities during the project life-cycle.
2. Promote quality, safety, and climate change considerations in infrastructure design and construction.
3. Avoid or minimise community exposure to project-related traffic and road safety risks, diseases, and hazardous materials.
4. Have in place effective measures to address emergency events.
5. Ensure that safeguarding of personnel and property is carried out in a manner that avoids or minimises risks to project-affected communities.

### 2.2 Infrastructure and Equipment Design and Safety (paras. 4–6)

- Design, construct, operate, and decommission structural elements in accordance with **national law** and **GIIP** (Good International Industry Practice), including the **WBG EHSGs**.
- Engage **competent professionals** for design, engineering, and construction supervision.
- Consider incremental risks of **climate change** in design (structural resilience, drainage, natural hazard exposure).
- Apply **universal access** principles: ensure physical access for persons with disabilities and other vulnerable groups where technically and financially feasible.
- For new buildings and structures: structural integrity and safety assessments (seismic, wind, flood, fire) using codes appropriate to the jurisdiction and hazard profile.

### 2.3 Safety of Services (para. 7)

Where the project involves delivery of services to communities (e.g., water supply, electricity, transport):
- Establish and implement management systems to ensure safe, reliable service delivery.
- Apply quality and safety standards consistent with GIIP.
- Regularly monitor service delivery quality and safety.

### 2.4 Traffic and Road Safety (paras. 8–9)

- Assess and monitor traffic and road safety risks from project-related transport activities.
- Where the project involves significant road transport: develop a **traffic management plan** addressing:

| Element | Details |
|---|---|
| Route planning | Avoid sensitive receptors (schools, hospitals, markets) where feasible |
| Speed management | Enforce speed limits, speed bumps, signage |
| Driver management | Training, fitness assessment, substance testing, driving hour limits |
| Vehicle standards | Roadworthiness inspections, maintenance records, safety equipment |
| Pedestrian safety | Walkways, crossings, barriers, lighting at construction sites |
| Incident response | Reporting, investigation, corrective action |
| Community awareness | Notifications of heavy vehicle movements, safety campaigns |

- Collaboration with relevant local authorities on traffic management.

### 2.5 Ecosystem Services (paras. 10–11)

- Where the project may affect **ecosystem services** that communities depend upon, identify and avoid/minimise impacts.
- Two categories:

| Type | Examples | Mitigation approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Provisioning services** | Food, freshwater, fuel, fibre, medicinal plants | Avoid/minimise disruption; offset or compensate |
| **Regulating services** | Water purification, flood control, erosion prevention, pollination, climate regulation | Maintain ecological processes; alternative measures |

- Assess dependency of project-affected communities, particularly vulnerable and disadvantaged groups.

### 2.6 Community Exposure to Health Issues (paras. 12–14)

- Assess and address risks of:

| Health risk category | Key concerns |
|---|---|
| **Water-borne diseases** | Contamination of water sources from construction/operations |
| **Vector-borne diseases** | Creation of breeding habitats (stagnant water, borrow pits) |
| **Communicable diseases** | HIV/AIDS, STIs, COVID-19 — especially from **labour influx** |
| **Non-communicable diseases** | Exposure to dust, noise, vibration, air pollution |
| **Labour influx** (para. 13) | Gender-based violence, sexual exploitation and abuse (SEA/SH), pressure on local services, social conflict, communicable disease spread, increased cost of living |

**Labour influx management** measures:
- Labour Influx Management Plan (where significant influx expected).
- Workers' Code of Conduct (mandatory for all project workers).
- Prioritise local recruitment.
- Establish accommodation away from communities where feasible.
- Awareness programmes on communicable diseases.
- Coordinate with local health services.
- SEA/SH risk assessment and mitigation measures.
- Community-level grievance mechanism (ESS10) accessible to affected persons.

### 2.7 Management and Safety of Hazardous Materials (paras. 15–16)

- Avoid or minimise community exposure to hazardous materials (chemicals, pesticides, wastes, radioactive materials).
- Where use/storage/transport of hazardous materials is necessary:
  - Follow national law and GIIP (WBG EHSGs, GHS classification).
  - Emergency containment and response measures.
  - Labelling, material safety data sheets, restricted access.
  - Safe transport procedures.
  - Disposal in accordance with ESS3 and national regulations.
  - Avoid manufacturing, trade, or use of chemicals/hazardous materials subject to international bans (Stockholm Convention, Rotterdam Convention, Montreal Protocol).

### 2.8 Emergency Preparedness and Response (paras. 17–20)

**Risk Hazard Assessment (RHA):**
- Identify potential emergency events arising from natural and human-made hazards.
- Assess probability, severity, and exposure.
- Consider project-related and external hazards.

**Emergency Response Plan (ERP)** must include:

| Element | Requirements |
|---|---|
| Emergency organisation | Roles, responsibilities, chain of command, contact details |
| Communication | Alert systems, notification protocols, liaison with government emergency agencies |
| Procedures | Evacuation routes, assembly points, shelter-in-place, medical response, spill response |
| Equipment and resources | Emergency equipment, supplies, vehicles, first aid |
| Training and drills | Regular training for workers and community members; periodic drills (at least annually) |
| Community engagement | Notification of risks, engagement in emergency planning where appropriate |
| Review and update | Post-incident review, periodic update of ERP |
| Third-party coordination | Coordination with government emergency services, hospitals, fire services, police |

- The ERP must be disclosed to affected communities.
- Post-emergency: assess response effectiveness, implement corrective actions, report to the Bank.

### 2.9 Security Personnel (paras. 21–25)

- Applies where the Borrower engages **government or private security** to protect project assets, personnel, or property.

| Requirement | Details |
|---|---|
| **Risk assessment** (para. 21) | Assess security risks; identify potential for security-related incidents |
| **Proportionality** (para. 22) | Security measures proportionate to identified risks |
| **Rules of engagement** (para. 22) | Written rules on use of force — only as last resort, minimum necessary, proportionate |
| **Vetting** (para. 23) | Screen security personnel for past abuses, human rights violations, criminal records |
| **Training** (para. 23) | Train security personnel on ESS4 requirements, use of force rules, human rights, community engagement |
| **Monitoring** (para. 24) | Regular monitoring of security arrangements; investigate allegations of unlawful/abusive acts |
| **Reporting** (para. 25) | Report security incidents to the Bank promptly |
| **Grievance mechanism** | Affected communities can raise concerns about security personnel conduct through ESS10 mechanism |

### 2.10 Safety of Dams (Annex 1, paras. 1–16)

**New dams** (paras. 1–7):
- Designed and constructed by experienced and competent professionals.
- Adopt and implement dam safety measures throughout project cycle.
- Establish an **independent Panel of Experts (PoE)** for large or high-hazard dams.
- PoE reviews investigation, design, construction, start of operations, and first filling.
- PoE comprises three or more experts in relevant fields (dam engineering, hydrology, geology).

**Existing dams and dams under construction (DUC)** (paras. 8–11):
- Where the project relies on an existing dam: assess dam safety, operational status.
- Commission a dam safety assessment by independent dam specialists.
- Implement necessary remedial work.

**Dam safety reports** (paras. 12–14):
- Dam safety plan with detailed technical specifications.
- Instrumentation plan and monitoring protocols.
- Operations and maintenance plan.
- Emergency preparedness plan for dam failure scenarios.
- Regular dam safety inspections and reporting.

**Small dams** (paras. 15–16): generic dam safety measures designed by qualified engineers; PoE not required for small, low-hazard dams but basic safety standards apply.

## 3. ESS5: Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use, and Involuntary Resettlement

### 3.1 Objectives (para. 1)

1. Avoid involuntary resettlement or, when unavoidable, minimise by exploring project design alternatives.
2. Avoid forced eviction.
3. Mitigate unavoidable adverse social and economic impacts from land acquisition or restrictions on land use by providing timely compensation at replacement cost and assisting displaced persons in efforts to improve, or at least restore, livelihoods and living standards.
4. Improve living conditions of poor or vulnerable persons who are physically displaced through provision of adequate housing, access to services and facilities, and security of tenure.
5. Conceive and execute resettlement activities as sustainable development programmes providing sufficient investment resources.
6. Ensure resettlement activities are planned and implemented with appropriate disclosure of information, meaningful consultation, and informed participation of affected persons.

### 3.2 Scope of Application (para. 4)

ESS5 applies to permanent or temporary land acquisition, restrictions on land use, or access restrictions arising from the project, including:

| Type (para. 4) | Description |
|---|---|
| **(a)** Land rights acquired through expropriation or other compulsory procedures | Standard government acquisition with compensation |
| **(b)** Land rights acquired through negotiated settlements if expropriation would have occurred on failure of negotiations | Negotiated purchase under threat of compulsory acquisition |
| **(c)** Restrictions on land use or access to assets | Legally designated protected areas, parks, restrictions on grazing/fishing |
| **(d)** Restrictions on access to natural resources in legally designated parks/protected areas resulting in adverse impacts | Conservation-induced displacement |
| **(e)** Relocation of people without formal, traditional, or recognisable rights to land they occupy | Informal settlers, encroachers |
| **(f)** Displacement of people from public land used without formal rights | Persons on state/public land |
| **(g)** Land rights or claims relinquished by individuals or communities without full payment of compensation | Underpayment situations |
| **(h)** Land acquisition or restrictions arising from project-related activities not directly funded by the project | Associated facilities, linked activities |

### 3.3 Exclusions (para. 5)

ESS5 does **not** apply to:
- **Voluntary market transactions** — willing buyer/willing seller with no compulsory acquisition backstop; but the Bank may require documentation that the transaction is genuinely voluntary.
- **Land use planning** — government regulation of land use in the public interest that does not involve specific project-related acquisition.
- **Voluntary land donations** — subject to stringent conditions (see Section 3.11 below).
- **Refugees from natural disasters, conflict, or famine** — addressed through separate policies; but ESS5 applies if these persons are affected by project-related land acquisition.

### 3.4 Eligibility Classification (para. 10)

Three categories of affected persons:

| Category | Entitlement |
|---|---|
| **(a) Formal legal rights** | Compensation at replacement cost for land/assets; relocation assistance; livelihood restoration |
| **(b) Recognisable claims** under national law | Same as (a) — recognised through a process identified in the resettlement plan; land rights regularised where possible |
| **(c) No legal right or recognisable claim** | No compensation for land, but entitled to: resettlement assistance, compensation for improvements/structures/crops, livelihood restoration, other assistance |

The **cut-off date** (para. 11) determines eligibility. Persons who encroach after the cut-off date are not entitled to compensation or resettlement assistance. The cut-off date is established through the census and communicated to the affected community.

### 3.5 Replacement Cost (para. 12, fn. 7)

| Asset type | Replacement cost methodology |
|---|---|
| **Agricultural land** | Market value of land of equal productive use/potential in the vicinity, plus transaction costs (registration, transfer taxes) |
| **Urban land** | Market value of land of equivalent size and use, with equivalent infrastructure/services, in the vicinity, plus transaction costs |
| **Structures** | Cost of constructing a replacement structure of at least equivalent quality and area, using prevailing market prices for materials and labour, without depreciation deduction, plus transaction costs |
| **Crops and trees** | Market value at full maturity, calculated at current market rates |
| **Other assets** | Fair market value |

Key principles:
- No deduction for depreciation, salvage materials, or previous government investment.
- Transaction costs (registration, stamp duty, legal fees) included.
- Where markets are absent: independent valuation using comparable methodologies.
- Compensation paid **before** displacement occurs.

### 3.6 Compensation and Benefits for Affected Persons (paras. 13–15)

- Compensation at replacement cost paid **prior to displacement**.
- Transitional support: moving allowance, temporary accommodation, income support during transition period.
- Development assistance: skills training, access to credit, job placement, livelihood start-up grants.
- Particular attention to **vulnerable groups**: women-headed households, elderly, persons with disabilities, ethnic minorities, landless persons, youth.
- Land-for-land compensation preferred for persons whose livelihoods are land-based.
- Cash compensation appropriate where: land is not the preferred option, livelihoods are not land-based, livelihoods can be restored through cash.

### 3.7 Community Engagement (paras. 16–18)

- Meaningful consultation with affected communities from project inception.
- Informed participation in planning, implementation, and monitoring of resettlement.
- Special attention to needs of vulnerable groups.
- Disclosure of draft resettlement instruments in a timely, accessible manner in local languages.
- Continued engagement throughout implementation.

### 3.8 Grievance Mechanism (para. 19)

- Established as early as possible in project preparation.
- Accessible, affordable, and understandable.
- Transparent procedures with clear timelines.
- Addresses disputes about land valuation, eligibility, and compensation entitlements.
- Does not impede access to judicial or administrative remedies.
- Must be functional before displacement begins.

### 3.9 Planning and Implementation (paras. 20–23)

| Element | Requirements |
|---|---|
| **Census** (para. 20) | Complete census of affected persons and inventory of affected assets; establishes cut-off date |
| **Socio-economic survey** (para. 20) | Baseline data on income, livelihood, land use, vulnerability; basis for livelihood restoration planning |
| **Resettlement plan** (para. 21) | Developed proportionate to risks and impacts; see Annex 1 for minimum content |
| **Cut-off date** (para. 22) | Communicated to affected communities; established through census; no retroactive claims |
| **Implementation schedule** (para. 23) | Resettlement activities completed before project civil works commence in affected area |
| **Monitoring** (para. 23) | Internal monitoring of resettlement implementation; independent monitoring for significant impacts |
| **Completion audit** | Independent assessment to verify livelihood restoration achieved |

### 3.10 Physical Displacement (paras. 24–28)

**Housing and relocation:**
- Replacement housing of at least equivalent standard, or cash compensation at replacement cost.
- Relocation sites: adequate infrastructure, services, locational advantages at least equivalent to old site.
- Security of tenure at relocation sites.
- Choices offered between feasible resettlement options.

**Forced eviction prohibition** (para. 26):
- The Borrower will not carry out or sanction forced evictions.
- Forced eviction = permanent or temporary removal against the will of persons without provision of, and access to, appropriate forms of legal or other protection.
- Legitimate expropriation with fair compensation and due process is not forced eviction.

**Vulnerable groups** (para. 28):
- Specific measures to ensure adequate housing for vulnerable displaced persons.
- Priority attention to women-headed households, elderly, persons with disabilities.
- Adequate transitional support.

### 3.11 Economic Displacement (paras. 29–33)

**Livelihood restoration** — three categories:

| Livelihood type | Restoration approach |
|---|---|
| **Land-based** (para. 30) | Replacement land of equivalent productive potential preferred; agricultural support, inputs, extension services; adequate transition period |
| **Wage-based** (para. 31) | Skills training, job placement support; transitional income support; priority in project employment where feasible |
| **Enterprise-based** (para. 32) | Cash compensation for assets; business re-establishment support; transitional income support; access to credit |

- Economically displaced persons may need no physical relocation but lose income sources or means of livelihood.
- Where land acquisition causes loss of income: compensation for lost income during transition, plus livelihood restoration measures.
- Monitoring of livelihood restoration outcomes until pre-project levels are restored or improved.

### 3.12 Voluntary Land Donation (para. 5, fn. 10)

Six conditions must **all** be satisfied:

1. The land is not the sole or primary source of income or livelihood for the donor.
2. Donations do not significantly reduce the donor's remaining land area.
3. The expected benefits from the project are clearly identified and agreed upon by the donor.
4. The donor is aware that refusal is an option and has confirmed willingness to donate voluntarily, without coercion.
5. The land is free of encumbrances, claims, or encroachers.
6. Independent verification of the voluntary nature (through a third party or community leaders).

Additional requirements:
- Written documentation of the donation with signatures/thumbprints.
- Grievance mechanism available.
- Proportionate due diligence (smaller donations require less process; larger ones need more verification).
- Donation cannot occur where there is a power imbalance between the donor and the project authority.

### 3.13 Resettlement Instruments (Annex 1)

**Resettlement Plan** — minimum content:

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| Description of the project | Components involving land acquisition, rationale for displacement |
| Census results | Numbers of affected persons by category, affected assets inventory |
| Socio-economic baseline | Income, livelihoods, social organisation, vulnerability data |
| Legal framework | Applicable laws, gap analysis with ESS5, measures to bridge gaps |
| Eligibility | Criteria, entitlements by category, cut-off date |
| Valuation | Methodology for replacement cost calculation |
| Compensation and benefits | Entitlement matrix, livelihood restoration measures |
| Community engagement | Consultation record, ongoing engagement plan |
| Grievance mechanism | Procedures, timelines, escalation |
| Implementation schedule | Phasing, coordination with civil works |
| Budget | Itemised budget with contingency, source of funds |
| Monitoring | Indicators, frequency, reporting, completion audit |
| Institutional arrangements | Responsible agencies, capacity, coordination |

**Resettlement Framework**: prepared when the project will involve sub-projects or components whose design is not finalised at appraisal. Sets principles, policies, eligibility criteria, and procedures for preparing and approving individual resettlement plans during implementation.

**Process Framework**: prepared when the project involves restrictions on access to natural resources in legally designated parks/protected areas. Describes participatory process for determining restrictions, identifying affected communities, and establishing mitigation measures.

## 4. ESS7: Indigenous Peoples/Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Traditional Local Communities

### 4.1 Objectives (para. 1)

1. Ensure the development process fosters full respect for human rights, dignity, aspirations, identity, culture, and natural resource-based livelihoods of IP/SSAHUTLC.
2. Avoid adverse impacts on IP/SSAHUTLC or, when avoidance is not possible, minimise, mitigate, and/or compensate.
3. Promote sustainable development benefits and opportunities in a manner that is accessible, culturally appropriate, and inclusive.
4. Improve project design and promote local support through meaningful consultation tailored to IP/SSAHUTLC, ensuring FPIC in the three specified circumstances.
5. Obtain, document, and disclose the FPIC of affected IP/SSAHUTLC when the three specified circumstances apply.
6. Recognise, respect, and preserve culture, knowledge, and practices; provide opportunity to adapt to changing conditions in a manner and timeframe acceptable to them.

### 4.2 Identification Criteria (para. 8)

All four characteristics must be present **in varying degrees**:

| # | Criterion | Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Self-identification** as members of a distinct indigenous social and cultural group, and recognition of this identity by others | Community self-recognition; external recognition by neighbouring communities, government, or other stakeholders |
| 2 | **Collective attachment** to geographically distinct habitats, ancestral territories, or areas of seasonal use/occupation, and to natural resources in these areas | Historical occupation; seasonal migration patterns; spiritual/cultural ties to specific lands; communal land tenure |
| 3 | **Customary cultural, economic, social, or political institutions** that are distinct or separate from those of the mainstream society or culture | Traditional governance structures; customary law; distinct livelihood systems; own language; distinct spiritual practices |
| 4 | **A distinct language or dialect**, often different from the official language(s) of the country or region | Indigenous language spoken at community level; may also speak national language |

**Sub-Saharan African context** (para. 7, fn. 10): the term "Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Traditional Local Communities" is used in recognition that in many SSA countries, Indigenous Peoples are not identified as such. The same criteria and requirements apply.

### 4.3 Scope (paras. 5–6)

ESS7 applies when IP/SSAHUTLC:
- Are **present in** or have **collective attachment to** the project area.
- The project may affect them positively or adversely.

"Project area" includes the area of influence — direct, indirect, and cumulative impacts, as well as areas used for associated facilities or activities.

Where IP/SSAHUTLC are the sole or overwhelming majority of direct project beneficiaries, the requirements may be integrated into the overall project design, but a separate IPP or IPPF may still be required.

### 4.4 Social Assessment (paras. 12–15)

Where IP/SSAHUTLC are present in or have collective attachment to the project area, the Borrower conducts a **social assessment** to evaluate:

- Baseline conditions: demographic, social, cultural, economic, political characteristics.
- Land and resource use patterns, including seasonal use.
- Customary governance structures.
- Cultural heritage, sacred sites, and areas of significance.
- Potential positive and adverse project impacts.
- Gender analysis within the IP/SSAHUTLC community.
- Assessment of vulnerability, including to project impacts.
- Views and concerns of IP/SSAHUTLC regarding the project.

The social assessment involves meaningful consultation with affected IP/SSAHUTLC and is undertaken by qualified social scientists with expertise in the relevant communities.

### 4.5 Indigenous Peoples Plan (paras. 16–18, Annex A)

The **Indigenous Peoples Plan (IPP)** or **Indigenous Peoples Planning Framework (IPPF)** must include:

| Element | Content |
|---|---|
| Baseline data | Summary of social assessment findings |
| Analysis of impacts | Positive and adverse impacts, including cumulative |
| Avoidance/mitigation measures | Actions to avoid, minimise, or compensate adverse impacts |
| Culturally appropriate benefits | Measures to ensure IP/SSAHUTLC receive social/economic benefits in culturally appropriate manner |
| Gender analysis | Assessment of gender-specific impacts and measures |
| Community engagement | Consultation strategy, process, and outcomes to date |
| FPIC documentation | Where applicable: evidence of FPIC process and outcomes |
| Grievance mechanism | Culturally appropriate mechanism (see Section 4.10) |
| Monitoring | Indicators, participatory monitoring with IP/SSAHUTLC representatives |
| Budget and timeline | Itemised budget, implementation schedule |
| Institutional arrangements | Responsible entities, capacity-building measures |

**IPPF** is prepared when sub-projects/components are not yet defined at appraisal. Establishes principles, process, and criteria for preparing site-specific IPPs during implementation.

### 4.6 Meaningful Consultation Tailored to IP/SSAHUTLC (paras. 19–22)

Requirements beyond general ESS10 consultation:

- **Culturally appropriate** methods, timelines, and venues.
- Conducted through **legitimate representatives** (traditional leaders, elders, community organisations).
- Adequate time for **internal community decision-making** processes (which may require longer than standard consultation periods).
- Conducted in **indigenous language(s)** with translation/interpretation as needed.
- Access to relevant **project information** in understandable format, sufficiently in advance.
- **Gender-inclusive**: ensure participation of women and youth.
- Free from **coercion, intimidation, or manipulation**.
- Documented and disclosed.

### 4.7 Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) (paras. 24–33)

**Definition** (para. 25): FPIC is not a veto right. It is established through good faith negotiation between the Borrower and affected IP/SSAHUTLC. FPIC does not require unanimity; it may be achieved even if some individuals or groups disagree.

**Three elements:**

| Element | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Free** | No coercion, intimidation, or manipulation; no inducements that compromise free choice |
| **Prior** | Consent sought sufficiently in advance of commencement; adequate time for indigenous consultation/consensus processes |
| **Informed** | Information provided on: nature, size, pace, duration, area of project; purpose; potential impacts (positive and negative); likely benefits; rights, risks, responsibilities; fair and equitable compensation; proposed benefit-sharing |

**Three circumstances requiring FPIC** (paras. 24–25):

| Circumstance | Para. | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **(1) Impacts on lands and natural resources subject to traditional ownership/customary use** | 29 | Where the project will have significant impacts on land, resources, territories traditionally owned, customarily used, or occupied |
| **(2) Relocation of IP/SSAHUTLC from their communally held/customarily used lands** | 30 | Physical relocation from ancestral or traditional lands; FPIC required in addition to ESS5 compliance |
| **(3) Significant impacts on cultural heritage** | 31 | Impacts on heritage sites, practices, knowledge, natural features of cultural significance; commercial use of cultural heritage |

**FPIC process** (paras. 26–28):
- Good faith negotiation between Borrower and affected communities.
- Documented: process, participants, issues raised, agreements reached.
- Where consent is not obtained: the Borrower does not proceed with the specific project activities that would trigger the FPIC requirement, unless agreement is subsequently reached.
- The Bank reviews the process and its outcome.
- Time-bound but flexible — respects indigenous decision-making processes.

### 4.8 Impacts on Lands and Natural Resources (paras. 29, 33–37)

**Legal recognition of tenure** (para. 33):
- Where IP/SSAHUTLC have traditional ownership or customary use rights not formally recognised by national law, the Borrower works with the government and communities to ensure legal recognition of such ownership/use before project activities commence.
- Where legal recognition is not possible, the Borrower takes measures to protect these rights, including through the resettlement/livelihood restoration framework.

**Commercial development** (paras. 34–37):
- Where the project involves commercial development of natural resources on IP/SSAHUTLC lands:
  1. FPIC required (circumstance 1 above).
  2. Fair and equitable **benefit-sharing** arrangements negotiated with affected communities.
  3. Benefits may include: employment, revenue-sharing, equity participation, community development funds, preferential procurement.
  4. Benefit-sharing arrangements documented in a written agreement.
  5. Independent advice available to IP/SSAHUTLC during negotiations.

### 4.9 Relocation (paras. 30, 38–40)

- **Avoidance is the first priority** — explore all feasible alternative project designs.
- Where relocation is unavoidable:
  - FPIC required (circumstance 2).
  - Full compliance with ESS5.
  - Relocation to lands/territories of equivalent quality, status, and cultural significance.
  - Return to original lands if the reasons for relocation cease to exist.
  - Culturally appropriate compensation.
  - Land-for-land preferred over cash compensation.

### 4.10 Cultural Heritage (paras. 31, 41–43)

- **Avoidance priority**: where a project may significantly impact cultural heritage of IP/SSAHUTLC, priority given to avoidance.
- Where avoidance is not feasible: FPIC required (circumstance 3).
- Includes: sacred sites, ceremonial areas, burial grounds, areas of cultural significance, natural features of cultural importance, traditional knowledge.
- **Commercial use of cultural heritage** (para. 43):
  - FPIC required.
  - Fair and equitable benefit-sharing.
  - Intellectual property considerations.
  - Documentation and preservation measures agreed with the community.

### 4.11 Grievance Mechanism (para. 22, fn. 17)

Requirements for IP/SSAHUTLC-specific grievance mechanism:

- **Culturally appropriate**: designed in consultation with IP/SSAHUTLC, reflecting customary dispute-resolution practices.
- Accessible: in indigenous language(s), through representatives recognised by the community, at accessible locations.
- Does not impede access to judicial, administrative, or Bank-level remedies (Inspection Panel/Accountability Mechanism).
- Separate from or integrated with project-level mechanism (ESS10), but must address IP/SSAHUTLC-specific concerns.
- Covers all aspects: FPIC process, benefit-sharing, land/resource impacts, cultural heritage, relocation.
- Anonymous complaints permitted.
- Regular reporting on grievances received and resolution outcomes.

|---|---|---|

## Key Cross-References Between Standards

| Interaction | Implication |
|---|---|
| ESS5 + ESS7 | Relocation of IP/SSAHUTLC requires both ESS5 compliance and FPIC under ESS7 |
| ESS4 + ESS2 | Labour influx management involves both community-side (ESS4) and worker-side (ESS2) measures |
| ESS4 + ESS1 | Emergency preparedness and hazard assessment feed into the ESIA under ESS1 |
| ESS7 + ESS8 | Cultural heritage of IP/SSAHUTLC triggers both ESS7 FPIC and ESS8 protections |
| ESS2 + ESS10 | Separate grievance mechanisms required: worker mechanism (ESS2) and community mechanism (ESS10) |
| ESS5 + ESS10 | Meaningful consultation during resettlement planning aligns with ESS10 stakeholder engagement requirements |
