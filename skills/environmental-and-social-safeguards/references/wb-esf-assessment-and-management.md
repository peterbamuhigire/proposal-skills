# World Bank ESF: Assessment, Resource Efficiency, and Stakeholder Engagement

Reference covering ESS1 (Assessment and Management of E&S Risks and Impacts), ESS3 (Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention), and ESS10 (Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure) under the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (ESF, effective 1 October 2018).

---

## 1. ESS1: Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts

ESS1 is the overarching standard. It establishes the Borrower's responsibilities for assessing, managing, and monitoring environmental and social risks throughout the project life cycle. All other ESSs are read in conjunction with ESS1.

### 1.1 Objectives (para. 1)

- Identify, evaluate, and manage the environment and social risks and impacts of the project in a manner consistent with the ESSs.
- Adopt a mitigation hierarchy approach: (a) anticipate and avoid risks and impacts; (b) where avoidance is not possible, minimise or reduce risks and impacts to acceptable levels; (c) once risks and impacts have been minimised or reduced, mitigate; and (d) where significant residual impacts remain, compensate for or offset them.
- Adopt differentiated measures so that adverse impacts do not fall disproportionately on the disadvantaged or vulnerable, and they are not disadvantaged in sharing development benefits and opportunities.
- Utilise national environmental and social institutions, systems, laws, regulations, and procedures in the assessment, development, and implementation of projects, whenever appropriate.

### 1.2 Mitigation Hierarchy (paras. 5, 27)

| Step | Action | Examples in Practice |
|------|--------|---------------------|
| **Avoid** | Redesign project to eliminate the risk entirely | Route realignment to avoid resettlement; site selection away from critical habitat |
| **Minimise** | Reduce the severity, duration, or extent of the impact | Phased construction to limit simultaneous disturbance; noise barriers during sensitive hours |
| **Mitigate** | Apply measures to restore or rehabilitate | Revegetation of disturbed areas; livelihood restoration programmes |
| **Compensate / Offset** | Address residual impacts that cannot be mitigated | Biodiversity offsets; replacement housing at full replacement cost |

The hierarchy is sequential — each step is applied only after the preceding step has been exhausted. Compensation or offset is a last resort and must achieve at minimum no net loss for significant impacts (para. 27).

### 1.3 Environmental and Social Assessment Process (paras. 18–25)

The Borrower must conduct an Environmental and Social Assessment (ESA) proportionate to the risks and impacts of the project. Key requirements:

- **Scope and scale** are proportionate to the project's risk classification (para. 18).
- The assessment considers all relevant E&S risks and impacts, including those identified in ESSs 2–10 (para. 19).
- It covers the project's **area of influence**: (a) the primary project site and related facilities; (b) associated facilities not funded by the Bank but essential to the project; (c) cumulative impacts from the project combined with other developments; and (d) areas potentially affected by unplanned but predictable developments (para. 20).
- Assessment of risks and impacts considers **direct, indirect, and cumulative impacts**, including transboundary and global impacts (para. 21).
- **Applicable legal and institutional framework** of the Borrower country must be reviewed, including national laws, obligations under international treaties, and relevant Bank Group EHS Guidelines (para. 22).
- Where the project involves existing facilities or activities, an **environmental and social audit** may be required (para. 23).
- The assessment applies the **precautionary principle** where scientific uncertainty exists about the nature or scope of impacts (footnote 12).

### 1.4 Assessment Tools (paras. 23–25, Annexes 1–2)

| Tool | When Used | Key Content |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **ESIA** (Environmental and Social Impact Assessment) | High and Substantial risk projects with significant, diverse, or unprecedented E&S impacts | Full assessment of risks, impacts, alternatives, mitigation; see indicative outline below |
| **ESMP** (Environmental and Social Management Plan) | All risk levels; may stand alone for Moderate risk projects | Mitigation measures, monitoring plan, institutional arrangements, cost estimates |
| **ESMF** (Environmental and Social Management Framework) | When project components or subprojects are not defined at appraisal | Sets principles, procedures, and screening criteria for future subproject assessments |
| **SESA** (Strategic Environmental and Social Assessment) | Policy, plan, or programme lending; sector-wide projects | Examines E&S implications of proposed policies, plans, or strategies |
| **Environmental and Social Audit** | Existing facilities or operations | Reviews existing conditions, identifies gaps with ESS requirements, defines corrective actions |
| **Hazard or Risk Assessment** | Projects involving hazardous materials, processes, or natural hazards | Identifies hazards, evaluates likelihood and severity, defines prevention and emergency measures |
| **Cumulative Impact Assessment** | Where project interacts with other existing, planned, or reasonably predictable developments | Evaluates combined effects on valued environmental and social components (VECs) |
| **Social and Conflict Analysis** | Fragility, conflict, and violence (FCV) settings | Analyses conflict dynamics, social tensions, and how the project interacts with them |

### 1.5 Environmental and Social Commitment Plan — ESCP (paras. 42–46)

The ESCP is the binding agreement between the Borrower and the Bank on E&S measures and actions.

**Purpose:** Sets out material measures and actions required for the project to meet ESS requirements within agreed timeframes (para. 42).

**Content:**
- Specific measures and actions the Borrower will take, with clear timeframes.
- Reference to applicable ESSs and relevant national requirements.
- Actions relating to institutional capacity, staffing, training, and budget.
- Actions relating to monitoring and reporting.
- Forms part of the legal agreement between the Borrower and the Bank (para. 43).

**Adaptive management:** The ESCP is a living document. It may be updated during implementation to reflect changes in project design, unanticipated impacts, or evolving circumstances. Updates require Bank agreement (para. 44). If the Borrower fails to implement material ESCP measures, the Bank may exercise remedies under the legal agreement (para. 46).

### 1.6 Project Monitoring and Reporting (paras. 47–51)

- The Borrower monitors E&S performance against the ESSs and the ESCP throughout the project life cycle (para. 47).
- Monitoring must be **proportionate** to the nature, scale, and risks of the project (para. 48).
- Where required, the Borrower engages **independent third-party specialists** to verify monitoring information (para. 49).
- The Borrower prepares regular monitoring reports and submits them to the Bank, covering:
  - Progress on implementation of ESCP measures
  - Status of mitigation measures and their effectiveness
  - Stakeholder engagement activities
  - Functioning of the grievance mechanism
  - Any material E&S incidents or accidents (para. 50)
- **Incident reporting**: The Borrower must promptly notify the Bank of any incident or accident related to the project that has, or is likely to have, a significant adverse effect on the environment, affected communities, the public, or workers (para. 51).

### 1.7 Stakeholder Engagement and Disclosure under ESS1 (paras. 52–55)

ESS1 establishes the general obligation; ESS10 provides the detailed requirements. Under ESS1:

- The Borrower engages with stakeholders, including affected communities, throughout the project life cycle (para. 52).
- Stakeholder engagement is proportionate to the nature and scale of the project and its risks and impacts (para. 53).
- The Borrower discloses information to allow stakeholders to understand the project's risks and impacts and potential mitigation measures (para. 54).
- Engagement must begin as early as possible in the project cycle to allow stakeholder input to influence project design (para. 55).

### 1.8 Management of Contractors (Annex 3)

Annex 3 requires the Borrower to manage contractor E&S performance through:

- **Contractual provisions**: E&S requirements are incorporated into procurement documents and contracts (para. 1).
- **Contractor ESMP**: The contractor prepares and implements an ESMP (or equivalent management plan) consistent with the project ESMP (para. 2).
- **Monitoring**: The Borrower monitors contractor compliance, including through site inspections and audits (para. 3).
- **Reporting**: Contractors report to the Borrower on E&S performance, including incidents and accidents (para. 4).
- **Remedies**: Contracts include provisions for non-compliance, including corrective action, penalties, and termination (para. 5).
- **Subcontractors**: The contractor ensures subcontractors meet the same requirements (para. 6).

### 1.9 Use of Borrower's Environmental and Social Framework (paras. 13–17)

The Bank may agree to use all or part of the Borrower's own ES framework if it is materially consistent with the ESSs. Requirements:

- The Bank assesses the Borrower's framework during project preparation (para. 13).
- The framework must be assessed against the objectives of each applicable ESS (para. 14).
- Where gaps exist, the Borrower proposes measures to address them (para. 15).
- The Bank may require additional measures to address identified gaps (para. 16).
- Use of the Borrower's framework does not exempt the project from compliance with ESSs (para. 17).

### 1.10 Risk Classification (paras. 36–41)

The Bank classifies each project into one of four risk categories:

| Classification | Criteria | Assessment Requirements |
|---------------|----------|------------------------|
| **High** | Significant, diverse, or unprecedented E&S risks and impacts | Full ESIA, detailed ESMP, independent review, enhanced supervision |
| **Substantial** | Less significant than High but potentially significant E&S risks and impacts | ESIA or targeted assessments, ESMP, regular supervision |
| **Moderate** | Limited adverse E&S risks and impacts; few are irreversible | ESMP (may be standalone), standard supervision |
| **Low** | Minimal or no adverse E&S risks and impacts | No formal ESIA/ESMP required; ESCP still applies |

- Classification considers: type, location, sensitivity, scale, nature, and magnitude of the potential impacts; capacity and commitment of the Borrower; and involvement of third parties (para. 37).
- Classification may change during preparation or implementation as more information becomes available (para. 38).
- The Bank's E&S due diligence is commensurate with the classification (para. 41).

### 1.11 ESIA Indicative Outline (Annex 1)

1. **Executive summary** — concise overview of significant findings and recommended actions
2. **Legal and institutional framework** — applicable laws, regulations, standards, treaties; Bank EHS Guidelines; institutional responsibilities; E&S governance arrangements
3. **Project description** — purpose, nature, scale, location, duration, phasing; project area of influence; details relevant to identification of risks and impacts
4. **Baseline data** — physical, biological, and socioeconomic conditions in the project area; data collection methods; data gaps and uncertainties
5. **Environmental and social risks and impacts** — direct, indirect, cumulative, and transboundary; positive and negative; short- and long-term; quantified where possible
6. **Mitigation measures** — following the mitigation hierarchy; specific, measurable, and time-bound; residual impacts after mitigation
7. **Analysis of alternatives** — systematic comparison of feasible alternatives including the "without project" scenario; environmental, social, technical, and financial criteria
8. **Design measures** — project design features that avoid or minimise impacts
9. **Key measures and actions for the ESCP** — summary of binding commitments
10. **Appendices** — ToR for the ESIA; detailed data; stakeholder engagement records; specialist studies

### 1.12 ESMP Indicative Outline (Annex 2)

1. **Mitigation measures** — each significant impact linked to specific mitigation actions, responsibility, and timing
2. **Monitoring programme** — parameters to be monitored, methods, frequency, locations, detection limits, reporting thresholds
3. **Capacity development and training** — institutional strengthening, technical assistance, training programmes required
4. **Implementation schedule and cost estimates** — phased timeline with budget for all ESMP activities
5. **Integration with project** — how ESMP activities are integrated into the overall project implementation, procurement, and supervision arrangements

## 2. ESS3: Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention

ESS3 recognises that increased economic activity and urbanisation generate pollution and consume finite resources. It requires the Borrower to implement technically and financially feasible resource efficiency and pollution prevention measures.

### 2.1 Objectives (para. 1)

- Promote the sustainable use of resources, including energy, water, and raw materials.
- Avoid or minimise adverse impacts on human health and the environment by avoiding or minimising pollution from project activities.
- Avoid or minimise project-related emissions of short- and long-lived climate pollutants.
- Avoid or minimise generation of hazardous and non-hazardous waste.
- Minimise and manage the risks and impacts associated with pesticide use.

### 2.2 Resource Efficiency Requirements (paras. 7–9)

The Borrower implements technically and financially feasible measures to improve the efficient use of energy, water, and raw materials, as well as other resources:

- **Energy**: Evaluate options to reduce energy consumption during design and operation. Consider energy-efficient technologies, processes, and practices. Where the project is a significant consumer of energy, quantify energy use and benchmark against comparable facilities (para. 8).
- **Water**: Assess water use and efficiency. Where feasible, adopt measures to reduce water consumption, including recycling and reuse (para. 8).
- **Raw materials**: Evaluate options to reduce consumption of raw materials and increase use of recycled/renewable inputs where technically and financially feasible (para. 9).
- **Benchmarking**: Where internationally recognised benchmarks are available (e.g., IFC/World Bank Group EHS Guidelines), use these to evaluate resource efficiency (para. 9).

### 2.3 Water Use Management (paras. 10–11)

- Where the project has a potentially significant impact on water resources, conduct a **water balance** assessment covering supply, demand, and return flows (para. 10).
- Assess the **cumulative impacts** of water use in the context of other users and environmental flows (para. 10).
- Where feasible, implement water efficiency improvements: demand management, recycling/reuse, rainwater harvesting, leak reduction, process optimisation (para. 11).
- For projects in water-stressed areas, demonstrate that water use does not exceed sustainable yield and that it does not adversely affect other users' access (para. 11).

### 2.4 Pollution Prevention and Management (paras. 12–16)

Core principles:

- **Avoid or minimise** pollution during design, construction, and operation (para. 12).
- Where avoidance is not feasible, minimise and control pollutant releases using **Best Available Technology (BAT)** that is technically and financially feasible (para. 13).
- Consider ambient conditions and the **assimilative capacity** of the receiving environment; stricter measures may be needed where ambient standards are already exceeded or ecosystems are degraded (para. 14).
- Address **historical pollution** where the project may exacerbate pre-existing contamination or where contaminated land is part of the project site (para. 15).
- Apply the **polluter pays principle**: the Borrower bears the cost of pollution prevention and control (para. 16).

Applicable standards: WBG EHS Guidelines; national emission and discharge standards (apply the more stringent).

### 2.5 Air Pollution Management (paras. 17–18)

- **GHG estimation**: For projects that are expected to or currently produce significant greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions (above 25,000 tonnes CO₂-equivalent annually), the Borrower quantifies direct (Scope 1) and indirect (Scope 2) emissions, and evaluates technically and financially feasible options to reduce or offset them (para. 17).
- **Emission characterisation**: For point and fugitive sources, characterise emissions and compare against applicable WBG EHS Guidelines and national standards (para. 18).
- Evaluate alternatives and controls: fuel switching, end-of-pipe controls, process changes, operational practices.

### 2.6 Hazardous and Non-Hazardous Waste Management (paras. 19–22)

**Avoidance hierarchy** (para. 19):

1. Avoid waste generation
2. Reduce waste at source
3. Reuse and recycle
4. Treat waste to reduce hazard
5. Dispose of residual waste in an environmentally sound manner

**Hazardous waste** (paras. 20–21):
- Characterise waste streams and manage according to WBG EHS Guidelines.
- Maintain **chain of custody** documentation from generation through transport, treatment, and final disposal.
- Ensure that waste is disposed of at facilities that meet applicable standards.
- Avoid transboundary movement of hazardous waste except in accordance with the **Basel Convention** on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and Their Disposal (para. 21).

**Non-hazardous waste** (para. 22):
- Apply the same avoidance hierarchy.
- For significant volumes, prepare a waste management plan covering segregation, storage, collection, transport, and disposal.

### 2.7 Chemicals and Hazardous Materials (paras. 23–25)

- Avoid the manufacture, trade, and use of chemicals and hazardous materials subject to international bans or phase-outs under applicable conventions (para. 23):

| Convention | Scope |
|-----------|-------|
| **Stockholm Convention** | Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs) — elimination or restriction |
| **Rotterdam Convention** | Prior Informed Consent for certain hazardous chemicals and pesticides in international trade |
| **Montreal Protocol** | Substances that deplete the ozone layer |
| **Minamata Convention** | Mercury — reduction and elimination |

- Where restricted chemicals are used, apply best practice controls, substitution where feasible, and worker protection measures (para. 24).
- Manage hazardous materials through their full life cycle: procurement, transport, storage, use, and disposal (para. 25).

### 2.8 Pesticide Management (paras. 26–30)

- Prefer **Integrated Pest Management (IPM)** or **Integrated Vector Management (IVM)** approaches that reduce reliance on chemical pesticides (para. 26).
- When chemical pesticides are required, select products that are:
  - Effective against the target species
  - Have minimal effect on non-target species and the environment
  - Are of the lowest hazard class feasible (WHO Classification of Pesticides by Hazard)
  - Are registered and approved in the Borrower country (para. 27)
- **Procurement criteria**: Do not finance products in WHO Classes Ia (extremely hazardous) or Ib (highly hazardous), or formulations of products in Class II that are restricted under applicable international agreements, unless justified and controlled (para. 28).
- **Pest Management Plan (PMP)**: Required where the project involves significant use of pesticides, introduces new pest management practices, or involves large-scale pest control. Content includes: pest identification, IPM/IVM approach, pesticide selection criteria, handling and application procedures, monitoring, training, and disposal of containers and residuals (para. 29).
- Community health and safety: Address risks to communities from pesticide use, including contamination of water supplies, food, and soils (para. 30).

## 3. ESS10: Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure

ESS10 recognises the importance of open and transparent engagement with project stakeholders as an essential element of good international practice. It applies to all projects supported by the Bank through Investment Project Financing.

### 3.1 Objectives (para. 1)

- Establish a systematic approach to stakeholder engagement that helps Borrowers identify stakeholders and build and maintain a constructive relationship with them, in particular project-affected parties.
- Assess the level of stakeholder interest and support for the project and enable stakeholders' views to be taken into account in project design and E&S performance.
- Promote and provide means for effective and inclusive engagement with project-affected parties throughout the project life cycle on issues that could potentially affect them.
- Ensure that appropriate project information on E&S risks and impacts is disclosed to stakeholders in a timely, understandable, accessible, and appropriate manner and format.
- Provide project-affected parties with accessible and inclusive means to raise issues and grievances and allow Borrowers to respond to and manage such grievances.

### 3.2 Stakeholder Identification and Analysis (paras. 5–10)

The Borrower identifies the different stakeholders, differentiating between:

**Project-affected parties** (para. 5): individuals, groups, or local communities that are or may be affected by the project, including:
- Those directly affected (land acquisition, physical displacement, economic displacement, restriction of access to resources)
- Those indirectly affected (downstream communities, users of affected services)

**Other interested parties** (para. 6): individuals, groups, or organisations with an interest in the project due to its location, characteristics, impacts, or matters related to public interest, including:
- National and local authorities
- Civil society organisations and NGOs
- The private sector
- The media
- Donor and development agencies

**Disadvantaged or vulnerable groups** (paras. 8–10): individuals or groups who may be more likely to be adversely affected or less able to take advantage of project benefits, requiring differentiated engagement. May include:
- Persons below the poverty line
- Women-headed households
- Persons with disabilities
- Ethnic, religious, or linguistic minorities
- Indigenous Peoples (also ESS7)
- Children and youth
- Elderly persons
- Those who may be subject to discrimination or marginalisation

The Borrower must identify such groups and determine whether differentiated measures are needed to ensure their effective participation (para. 10).

### 3.3 Stakeholder Engagement Plan — SEP (paras. 11–18)

The Borrower prepares and implements a **Stakeholder Engagement Plan (SEP)** proportionate to the nature, scale, risks, and impacts of the project.

**Content** (para. 12):

| SEP Element | Requirements |
|-------------|-------------|
| Project description and area of influence | Summary of project, its phases, and geographic scope |
| Stakeholder identification and analysis | List of affected and interested parties; analysis of interests, concerns, and influence; identification of disadvantaged/vulnerable |
| Engagement programme | Methods, tools, timing, and frequency of engagement activities for each stakeholder group; differentiated approaches for disadvantaged/vulnerable |
| Disclosure of information | What information will be disclosed, when, in what formats, and in what languages |
| Timetable | Schedule of engagement activities aligned with project phases and decision points |
| Resources and responsibilities | Budget, staffing, and institutional arrangements for implementing the SEP |
| Grievance mechanism | Description of the process for receiving and resolving complaints |
| Monitoring and reporting | How engagement activities and their effectiveness will be monitored and reported |

**Timing** (para. 13): The SEP is prepared as early as possible in the project cycle. A draft is disclosed before project appraisal.

**Framework approach** (para. 14): Where subprojects are not yet defined, a **Stakeholder Engagement Framework (SEF)** establishes principles and procedures; site-specific SEPs are then prepared for each subproject when identified.

**Review and update** (para. 15): The SEP is a living document, reviewed and updated periodically to reflect changing circumstances, new stakeholders, or lessons learned during implementation.

### 3.4 Information Disclosure Requirements (paras. 19–21)

The Borrower discloses information to enable stakeholders to understand the project's E&S risks and impacts and potential mitigation measures. Six categories of information are disclosed:

1. **Project purpose, nature, and scale**, and the duration of proposed activities (para. 19(a))
2. **Risks and potential impacts** on local communities and individuals, and proposed mitigation measures (para. 19(b))
3. **Proposed stakeholder engagement process**, highlighting how stakeholders can participate (para. 19(c))
4. **Time and venue** of proposed public consultation meetings, and the process by which meetings will be notified, summarised, and reported (para. 19(d))
5. **Grievance mechanism process** and how to access it (para. 19(e))
6. **Assessment documents** (ESIAs, ESMPs, ESCPs, etc.) when available (para. 19(f))

**Timing and format** (paras. 20–21):
- Disclosure occurs in a timely manner, before project decisions are made, to allow stakeholder input to influence decisions.
- Information is disclosed in relevant local languages and in a manner that is accessible and culturally appropriate.
- For non-literate or semi-literate stakeholders, use alternative formats: oral presentations, visual materials, radio, community meetings.
- Information must be accessible to disadvantaged or vulnerable groups through appropriate methods.

### 3.5 Meaningful Consultation — Eight Characteristics (para. 22)

The Borrower undertakes a process of **meaningful consultation** that provides stakeholders with opportunities to express their views on project risks, impacts, and mitigation measures. Meaningful consultation is characterised by:

1. **Begins early** in the project planning process and is carried out on an ongoing basis as the project develops (para. 22(a))
2. **Provides stakeholders with relevant information** in advance of consultation, in a timely, understandable, accessible, and appropriate format (para. 22(b))
3. **Conducted in a manner that is free of external manipulation, interference, coercion, discrimination, and intimidation** (para. 22(c))
4. **Is gender-inclusive and responsive**, tailored to the needs of disadvantaged and vulnerable groups, and facilitates their effective participation (para. 22(d))
5. **Is based on prior disclosure** and dissemination of relevant, adequate, and timely information, including draft assessment documents (para. 22(e))
6. **Considers and responds** to feedback provided by stakeholders, with an explanation of how stakeholder feedback was taken into account (para. 22(f))
7. **Supports active and inclusive engagement** with project-affected parties (para. 22(g))
8. **Is documented** and disclosed (para. 22(h))

### 3.6 Engagement During Implementation and External Reporting (paras. 23–26)

- The Borrower continues stakeholder engagement throughout project implementation, not only during preparation (para. 23).
- Engagement during implementation focuses on: progress of ESCP implementation, effectiveness of mitigation measures, emerging E&S issues, and project changes (para. 24).
- **External reporting**: The Borrower provides periodic reports to project-affected parties on E&S performance, at a frequency and level of detail proportionate to the risks and impacts. Reports cover:
  - Implementation of ESCP measures
  - Status of the grievance mechanism (number and types of grievances, resolution status)
  - Monitoring results
  - Material E&S incidents (para. 25)
- Reporting format must be accessible to the intended audience; for affected communities, summaries in local languages (para. 26).

### 3.7 Grievance Mechanism (paras. 27–33, Annex 1)

The Borrower establishes and implements a grievance mechanism to receive and facilitate resolution of concerns and grievances of project-affected parties related to E&S performance.

**Design principles** (paras. 27–29):

- **Proportionate** to the risks and adverse impacts of the project (para. 27).
- **Accessible**: available to all project-affected parties, including disadvantaged or vulnerable groups (para. 28).
- **Prompt**: addresses concerns promptly using an understandable and transparent process (para. 28).
- **Culturally appropriate**: designed in consultation with stakeholders and in a manner that is culturally appropriate (para. 28).
- **No fees**: available at no cost and without retribution to the party raising the concern (para. 29).
- **Does not impede access** to judicial or administrative remedies (para. 29).
- **Confidential**: handles complaints confidentially where requested (para. 30).

**Annex 1 requirements** for the grievance mechanism:

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| **Receipt and registration** | Multiple uptake channels (in person, phone, letter, email, suggestion box); log all complaints with unique reference number, date, description |
| **Acknowledgement** | Acknowledge receipt within a defined period (typically 5–10 working days) |
| **Assessment and investigation** | Assess complaint; gather facts; determine if the complaint relates to E&S performance |
| **Response** | Provide a clear response to the complainant within a defined timeframe; explain the decision and any corrective action |
| **Escalation** | Where the complainant is not satisfied, provide a clear escalation path (e.g., senior management, independent review, mediation) |
| **Monitoring and reporting** | Track complaints, resolution times, and outcomes; report aggregated data as part of project reporting |
| **Feedback loop** | Use grievance data to identify systemic issues and improve project performance |

**Additional considerations** (paras. 31–33):
- Workers' grievance mechanisms are addressed under ESS2 (Labour and Working Conditions) and may be separate.
- For projects with significant E&S risks, the grievance mechanism should include provisions for independent review or mediation.
- Inform stakeholders about the existence of the World Bank Grievance Redress Service (GRS) and the Inspection Panel as additional avenues.

### 3.8 Organisational Capacity and Commitment (paras. 34–35)

- The Borrower demonstrates organisational capacity to implement the SEP, including sufficient qualified staff, budget, and institutional arrangements (para. 34).
- Where capacity is insufficient, the Borrower commits to capacity-building measures, which are reflected in the ESCP (para. 35).
- Assign clear responsibility for stakeholder engagement functions, including a designated point of contact for stakeholders (para. 34).

| ESS1 Element | ESS3 Connection | ESS10 Connection |
|-------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Risk classification | Determines depth of resource efficiency/pollution assessment | Determines scope and intensity of engagement |
| ESIA | Assesses pollution, resource use, GHG | Engagement findings feed into ESIA; ESIA disclosed to stakeholders |
| ESMP | Includes pollution prevention and resource efficiency measures | Monitoring results reported to affected parties |
| ESCP | Commits to ESS3 measures with timeframes | Commits to SEP implementation and grievance mechanism |
| Mitigation hierarchy | Core principle for pollution/waste management | Stakeholders consulted on mitigation options |
| Monitoring and reporting | Tracks ESS3 indicators (emissions, waste, water use) | External reporting to communities on E&S performance |
| Contractors | Must comply with ESS3 through contractual provisions | Contractor activities subject to stakeholder engagement and grievance mechanism |

## Proposal Integration Notes

When writing methodology sections for World Bank-financed projects:

1. **Reference the specific ESS paragraphs** when describing your approach — evaluators check for this.
2. **Use the exact terminology**: "mitigation hierarchy," "meaningful consultation," "area of influence," "project-affected parties."
3. **Link assessment tools to risk classification** — demonstrate understanding that the depth of assessment is proportionate.
4. **Show the ESCP as central** — the Bank's results framework tracks ESCP implementation.
5. **Demonstrate the feedback loop** between ESS10 (stakeholder input) and ESS1 (assessment and management) — stakeholder engagement is not a standalone activity but feeds into adaptive management.
6. **For ESS3, quantify** — propose GHG estimation methods, water balance calculations, waste characterisation. Evaluators value technical specificity.
7. **Address disadvantaged/vulnerable groups explicitly** — a common weakness in competing proposals is generic stakeholder engagement that does not differentiate.
8. **Grievance mechanism design** is often underspecified in proposals — include uptake channels, timeframes, escalation, and feedback loops to stand out.
