# Website Design Proposal Gate

Use this gate when a proposal includes website design, website development, SEO, web content, ecommerce, landing pages, portals, or web application frontends.

## 1. Win Theme

- State the website's business role: attract qualified traffic, convert enquiries, support sales, reduce support load, improve public trust, enable ecommerce, publish content, serve members, or improve stakeholder communication.
- Name the audience and the decision the website must help them make.
- State the core promise in evaluator language: what will be better after the website is delivered.
- If the proposal is premium, frame the website as a business asset and trust system, not a page build.

## 2. Website Development Philosophy

Use this philosophy unless the ToR requires different language:

- Strategy before design: objectives, audience, proof, content, SEO, and conversion path are defined before visual production.
- Content before decoration: pages are structured around search intent, buyer questions, proof, and action.
- Simplicity as quality: navigation, forms, and journeys reduce user effort while preserving necessary detail.
- Performance, accessibility, security, and SEO are built in from the start.
- The website is measured after launch: analytics, events, search visibility, and conversion quality are part of delivery.
- Ownership and handover matter: the client receives documentation, training, account access, and maintainable content structures.

## 3. Methodology Workstreams

For a serious website proposal, include these workstreams unless explicitly out of scope:

| Workstream | Core activities | Reviewable artefact |
|---|---|---|
| Discovery and strategy | Objectives, audience, competitors, analytics, stakeholder interviews | Website strategy brief |
| Content and SEO architecture | Page inventory, search intent, internal links, metadata model, schema plan | Content/SEO map |
| UX and information architecture | Sitemap, user journeys, wireframes, form flows | UX architecture pack |
| Visual design system | Tokens, components, key templates, responsive states | Design system and prototypes |
| Development | Frontend, CMS or data layer, integrations, forms, analytics | Staging website |
| QA and hardening | Accessibility, performance, security, browser/device checks, SEO validation | QA report and fix log |
| Launch | Redirects, DNS, backups, Search Console, analytics, social previews | Launch checklist and go-live report |
| Training and support | Admin training, documentation, support window, improvement backlog | Handover pack |

## 4. Stack Explanation

Explain stack choices through business value:

- Static-first or hybrid frontend: speed, security, SEO, low maintenance, and excellent Core Web Vitals for marketing sites.
- CMS: justified when the client has recurring publishing needs and staff to manage content.
- WordPress or similar CMS: justified when editor familiarity, plugin ecosystem, or client operations make it the best fit; budget for hardening, updates, backups, and plugin risk.
- Ecommerce: justified when catalogue, checkout, stock, fulfilment, tax, payment, and support workflows are defined.
- Custom web app: justified only when workflows, roles, data, integrations, or authenticated services cannot be handled by a simpler site.
- Analytics and search tooling: GA4 or privacy-friendly equivalent, Search Console/Bing Webmaster, event tracking, sitemap, schema, `llms.txt`, and conversion reporting.

Do not list technologies without saying why they are appropriate for the client.

## 5. Website Content And SEO Scope

A proposal is incomplete if it promises a high-performing website but leaves content vague.

Specify who owns:

- copywriting and editing
- translation or localisation
- photography, graphics, video, and icons
- content migration from the old site
- blog/resource content
- metadata, schema, internal links, and redirects
- case studies, testimonials, reviews, credentials, and proof assets
- launch announcements, social snippets, and email copy

For premium proposals, recommend at least one authority asset: diagnostic, guide, checklist, benchmark, calculator, case study, comparison page, or buyer's guide.

## 6. Costing Drivers

Price from work drivers, not page count alone:

- discovery depth and stakeholder count
- number of unique templates and total pages
- content creation, editing, translation, and migration volume
- SEO research, schema, redirect mapping, and technical audit
- design-system depth and prototype fidelity
- CMS complexity and editor training
- integrations: CRM, payment, booking, email, inventory, ERP, analytics, chat, maps, social feeds
- ecommerce catalogue size, product variants, checkout, taxes, shipping, refunds, abandoned cart, and fulfilment
- accessibility, performance, security, browser/device QA, and fixes
- launch risk: old-site redirects, DNS, hosting migration, uptime requirements, rollback
- support window, maintenance, reporting, and post-launch optimisation

## 7. Costing Ranges And Assumptions

Use ranges only as planning anchors. Replace them with supplier quotes or internal fee policy before submission.

| Scope type | Typical proposal logic | Costing note |
|---|---|---|
| Foundation authority site | strategy, core pages, SEO foundation, analytics, launch | Should include discovery, copy/content polish, QA, and 30-day support |
| Growth website | deeper content architecture, service pages, proof system, SEO hub, conversion events | Budget for interviews, copywriting, content migration, and 60-day optimisation |
| Premium authority system | positioning, content hub, case studies, custom design system, dashboards, advanced SEO/GEO, 90-day optimisation | Price as a strategic asset, not a website commodity |
| Ecommerce | catalogue, product pages, checkout, payment, fulfilment, emails, analytics | Include operations design, test orders, refunds, tax/shipping, and support |
| Web application/front-end portal | authenticated flows, roles, data, APIs, dashboards, security, testing | Requires requirements, architecture, QA, support, and change-control budget |

For Peter/TechGuyPeter premium agency work, do not imply sub-premium website delivery. If the budget cannot support quality, reduce scope or recommend paid discovery instead of discounting the standard.

## 8. Acceptance Criteria

Include measurable acceptance criteria:

- approved strategy brief and sitemap
- approved content/SEO map
- approved responsive designs for key templates
- no critical accessibility issues
- performance budget passed on priority pages
- valid schema and sitemap
- analytics events tested
- forms, emails, payments, and integrations tested
- security headers/backups/rollback verified where applicable
- client trained and handover pack delivered

## Red Flags

- The proposal promises SEO without content, internal links, schema, redirects, or measurement.
- The methodology jumps from design to launch with no QA or content migration.
- The financial proposal excludes content, hosting, licences, integrations, maintenance, or training without saying so.
- The stack is fashionable but not tied to client capacity or business needs.
- The proposed budget cannot support the promised level of premium quality.
