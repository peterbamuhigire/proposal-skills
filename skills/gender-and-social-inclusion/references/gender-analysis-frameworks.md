# Gender Analysis Frameworks — Practitioner Reference

> Synthesised from March, Smyth & Mukhopadhyay (Oxfam, 1999), UN Women Intersectionality Toolkit (2022), and Bohnet (Harvard, 2016). Oriented to East African consulting proposals (Uganda, Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania).

---

## 1. Gender Analysis Frameworks Comparison

| Framework | Origin | Approach | Key Tools | Best For | Limitations |
|-----------|--------|----------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Harvard Analytical** | Overholt et al., 1985 | WID / efficiency | Activity Profile, Access & Control Profile, Influencing Factors, Checklist | Baseline data collection; making women's work visible; gender-neutral entry point with resistant stakeholders | Top-down; ignores power dynamics; treats men and women as homogeneous groups; static snapshot |
| **Moser** | Caroline Moser, 1993 | GAD / transformative | Triple Role Mapping, Practical Needs vs Strategic Interests, Policy Approach Classification | Gender-aware policy design; programme planning where political transformation is the goal | Requires political commitment; practical/strategic distinction can be oversimplified; less participatory |
| **Gender Analysis Matrix (GAM)** | Rani Parker | Participatory / bottom-up | 4×4 matrix (labour, time, resources, culture × women, men, household, community) | Community-level participatory analysis; projects where communities define their own criteria | Time-intensive; depends on facilitation quality; may not capture structural causes |
| **CVA** | Mary Anderson | Humanitarian / resilience | Capacities & Vulnerabilities grid (physical, social, motivational × gender) | Disaster response; conflict settings; resilience programming; refugee contexts in East Africa (Bidi Bidi, Dadaab, Mahama) | Designed for emergencies; less suited to long-term development; limited institutional analysis |
| **Longwe Empowerment** | Sara Longwe | Empowerment / feminist | 5-level equality ladder (welfare → access → conscientisation → participation → control) | Measuring empowerment progress; project design with explicit equality objectives | Linear/hierarchical assumption; levels not always sequential in practice; can feel prescriptive |
| **Social Relations** | Naila Kabeer, IDS | GAD / institutional | Institutional analysis (state, market, community, family × 5 dimensions), Kabeer's empowerment chain | Structural analysis of inequality; policy reform; understanding how institutions reproduce disadvantage | Conceptually demanding; harder to operationalise quickly; requires skilled analysts |

**Selection rule of thumb**: Use Harvard for data-gathering baselines, Moser for policy-oriented programmes, GAM for participatory community work, Longwe for empowerment measurement, Social Relations for institutional reform, and CVA for humanitarian or conflict-affected contexts.

## 2. Harvard Analytical Framework

### Overview

The Harvard Analytical Framework (also called the Gender Roles Framework) was the first systematic gender analysis tool. It uses an efficiency argument — showing that ignoring women's contributions leads to poor project design — making it effective as a gender-neutral entry point with stakeholders who resist explicitly feminist language.

People-Oriented Planning (POP), developed by UNHCR, adapts the Harvard framework for refugee and displacement settings.

### Tool 1: Activity Profile

Maps who does what, disaggregated by gender and age. In East African contexts, include seasonal variation (agricultural calendars differ by region) and distinguish paid from unpaid labour.

| Activity | Women/Girls | Men/Boys | Time of Year | Location | Paid/Unpaid |
|----------|-------------|----------|--------------|----------|-------------|
| Land preparation | ○ | ● | Feb–Mar | Farm | Unpaid |
| Planting | ● | ○ | Mar–Apr | Farm | Unpaid |
| Weeding | ● | ◐ | Apr–Jun | Farm | Unpaid |
| Harvesting | ● | ● | Jul–Aug | Farm | Unpaid |
| Marketing/sale | ◐ | ● | Aug–Sep | Market | Paid |
| Water collection | ● | ○ | Year-round | Borehole | Unpaid |
| Childcare | ● | ○ | Year-round | Home | Unpaid |

Legend: ● = primary responsibility, ◐ = shared, ○ = minimal involvement.

### Tool 2: Access and Control Profile

Distinguishes between access (ability to use a resource) and control (ability to decide how a resource is used and disposed of). Critical distinction in East African customary land tenure systems.

| Resource / Benefit | Access |  | Control |  |
|-------------------|--------|--|---------|--|
|  | Women | Men | Women | Men |
| Agricultural land | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ |
| Cash income from crops | ◐ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ |
| Credit / loans | ◐ | ✓ | ◐ | ✓ |
| Education | ◐ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ |
| Technology / equipment | ✗ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ |
| Political representation | ◐ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ |

### Tool 3: Influencing Factors

Contextual factors that shape gender relations: legal framework, economic conditions, demographic trends, cultural norms, political structures, education levels, international influences.

In East Africa, key influencing factors include:
- **Legal**: Constitution of Uganda (Art. 33), Kenya (Art. 27), Rwanda (Art. 9); land tenure laws; inheritance customs
- **Economic**: Subsistence agriculture dominance; women's limited access to formal credit; mobile money as equaliser
- **Cultural**: Patrilineal inheritance; bride price practices; early marriage pressures; regional variation (e.g., Karamoja vs central Uganda)
- **Political**: Rwanda's 61% women in parliament vs regional average; Uganda's affirmative action seats; Kenya's two-thirds gender rule
- **Demographic**: Youth bulge (70%+ under 30 in Uganda); rural-urban migration patterns; female-headed households

### Tool 4: Project Cycle Checklist

Questions at each project stage: Who benefits? Who is excluded? Does the project change the gender division of labour? Does it shift access and control? Are gender-disaggregated data being collected?

### When to Use

- Baseline studies and situation analyses
- Projects requiring rapid gender profiling
- Contexts where stakeholders are resistant to explicit gender language (the efficiency argument)
- Data collection design for M&E frameworks

## 3. Moser Framework

### Triple Roles Analysis

Women typically perform three roles simultaneously; men's community politics role is often distinct:

| Role | Definition | Examples (East African context) | Visibility |
|------|------------|--------------------------------|------------|
| **Productive** | Work producing goods/services for consumption or trade | Farming, trading, wage employment, market vending | Recognised, sometimes counted |
| **Reproductive** | Childbearing, domestic labour, care work sustaining the household | Cooking, water/fuel collection, childcare, healthcare-seeking | Invisible, unpaid, unrecognised |
| **Community managing** | Voluntary collective work to maintain community resources | Water committee membership, school meetings, savings groups (chamas/SACCOs) | Unpaid, seen as natural extension of reproductive role |
| **Community politics** (men) | Formal political and leadership roles | LC councils, clan leadership, land boards | Recognised, often paid or status-conferring |

### Practical Needs vs Strategic Interests

| Dimension | Practical Gender Needs | Strategic Gender Interests |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **Definition** | Needs arising from existing gender roles and immediate conditions | Interests arising from women's subordinate position; challenge the gender order |
| **Time horizon** | Short-term, immediate | Long-term, transformative |
| **Examples** | Water point closer to home; maternity health services; fuel-efficient stoves; income from handicrafts | Land ownership rights; equal pay legislation; political representation; freedom from GBV; shared domestic labour |
| **Approach** | Service delivery, infrastructure | Legal reform, consciousness-raising, organising |
| **Risk if ignored** | Projects fail to engage women; no buy-in | Projects reinforce existing inequalities; no transformation |
| **Typical donors** | Infrastructure, WASH, health programmes | Governance, rights-based, feminist programmes |

**Proposal application**: Most development projects address practical needs. Strong proposals show how addressing practical needs creates pathways toward strategic interests — e.g., a WASH project that also establishes women-led water management committees (practical → participation → control).

### Five Policy Approaches

| Approach | Focus | Role of Women | Meets Practical Needs | Meets Strategic Interests | Example |
|----------|-------|---------------|----------------------|--------------------------|---------|
| **Welfare** | Basic needs delivery | Passive recipients | Yes | No | Food distribution to mothers |
| **Equity** | Legal/structural equality | Active participants | Yes | Yes | Land rights reform |
| **Anti-poverty** | Income generation | Productive workers | Yes | Partially | Microfinance for women's groups |
| **Efficiency** | Women as untapped resource | Economic agents | Yes | No (instrumentalises women) | Women in agriculture value chains |
| **Empowerment** | Self-reliance and organising | Decision-makers and agents | Yes | Yes | Women's political leadership programmes |

### Gender Planning Matrix

For each project component, assess: which gender roles are affected, whether practical needs or strategic interests are addressed, what policy approach is implied, and whether the intervention reinforces or transforms existing gender relations.

## 4. Gender Analysis Matrix (GAM)

### Matrix Template

The GAM is completed by community members themselves in facilitated workshops, not by external analysts. Each cell is marked with (+) positive impact, (−) negative impact, or (0) no change.

| Impact Category | Women | Men | Household | Community |
|----------------|-------|-----|-----------|-----------|
| **Labour** (changes in tasks, workload, skill requirements) | | | | |
| **Time** (changes in time allocation, time saved or added) | | | | |
| **Resources** (changes in access to capital, land, credit, training) | | | | |
| **Culture** (changes in social norms, roles, status, beliefs) | | | | |

### Example: Irrigation Scheme in Rural Uganda

| Impact Category | Women | Men | Household | Community |
|----------------|-------|-----|-----------|-----------|
| **Labour** | (−) More weeding and harvesting work | (+) Less manual watering | (+) More household labour needed | (+) New skills in water management |
| **Time** | (−) Less time for other activities | (+) Time saved on watering | (0) Neutral | (0) Neutral |
| **Resources** | (−) No land title to secure benefits | (+) Higher yields, more income | (+) More food, income | (+) Improved infrastructure |
| **Culture** | (−) Men control irrigation decisions | (+) Enhanced status as irrigators | (0) Neutral | (+) Shift toward commercial farming |

### Participatory Process Steps

1. **Introduce the tool** — explain the matrix, use local language, provide examples
2. **Define the project/intervention** to be analysed
3. **Facilitate cell-by-cell discussion** — community members fill each cell with (+), (−), (0) and narrative explanation
4. **Discuss patterns** — where do women bear costs while men capture benefits?
5. **Identify mitigation actions** — community-designed solutions to negative impacts
6. **Validate and prioritise** — rank actions by feasibility and impact

**East African facilitation notes**: Conduct separate sessions for women and men initially (mixed groups risk women deferring to men). Use visual aids and local examples. In pastoralist areas (Karamoja, Maasai), adapt for mobile livelihoods. Allow sufficient time — minimum half-day per community.

## 5. Longwe Women's Empowerment Framework

### Five Levels of Equality

Each level is a prerequisite for the next. At each level, three conditions must be addressed: recognition of the issue, positive action to address it, and equal participation in decision-making.

| Level | Definition | Indicators (East African examples) | Typical Interventions |
|-------|------------|-----------------------------------|----------------------|
| **1. Welfare** | Basic material well-being (without agency) | Nutrition status; maternal health access; household income | Service delivery: health facilities, food security programmes |
| **2. Access** | Equal access to resources (land, credit, education, employment) | Girls' enrolment ratios; women's access to credit; land titles held by women | Remove barriers: scholarships, affirmative credit, legal aid for land rights |
| **3. Conscientisation** | Awareness that gender roles are socially constructed and can be changed | Participation in gender awareness sessions; attitudes toward GBV; support for girls' education | Community dialogue; SASA! methodology; male engagement; school-based programmes |
| **4. Participation** | Equal participation in decision-making at all levels | Women in management positions; women in local council committees; women in budget processes | Quotas; leadership training; mentoring; women's caucuses; participatory budgeting |
| **5. Control** | Equal control over resources, benefits, and decision-making | Women's land ownership rates; women's control of household income; women in senior governance | Legal reform; constitutional provisions; institutional transformation |

### Application to Project Design

When designing a project, assess the current level achieved for the target population, then design interventions that address the current level and build toward the next. A common proposal error is targeting welfare (Level 1) when access (Level 2) is the binding constraint.

**Example**: A digital literacy programme for women in Kampala. If women already have welfare (Level 1) and access to devices (Level 2), the intervention should focus on conscientisation (understanding that tech careers are not only for men) and participation (women in tech decision-making forums), not just providing more devices.

## 6. Social Relations Approach

### Institutional Analysis

Gender inequality is produced and reproduced by four key institutions. Each institution operates through five dimensions of social relations:

**Four Institutions**:
- **State** — laws, policies, public services, regulation
- **Market** — employment, trade, value chains, financial services
- **Community** — norms, customs, collective action, local governance
- **Family/Kinship** — household division of labour, inheritance, marriage, care

**Five Dimensions of Social Relations**:

| Dimension | Questions to Ask | East African Example |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Rules** | What are the formal and informal rules? Who makes them? | Uganda Land Act allows spousal consent, but customary practice often overrides it |
| **Resources** | What resources are used, produced, and distributed? | Women produce 80% of food but control <20% of agricultural income |
| **People** | Who is included/excluded? On what basis? | Youth, PWDs, and ethnic minorities excluded from procurement opportunities |
| **Activities** | Who does what? Who gets what? | Women manage community health but men hold salaried CHW positions |
| **Power** | Who decides? Who can negotiate? Who can opt out? | Male clan elders control land allocation; women cannot easily challenge decisions |

### Kabeer's Empowerment Framework

A three-part chain linking resources to outcomes:

```
Resources (preconditions)  →  Agency (process)  →  Achievements (outcomes)
- Material                    - Decision-making      - Well-being
- Human                       - Bargaining           - Capabilities
- Social                      - Negotiation          - Functioning
- Institutional               - Resistance           - Voice
                              - Collective action
```

**Empowerment** = the expansion of people's ability to make strategic life choices in a context where this ability was previously denied.

Key distinction: **Choice** requires alternatives, the ability to choose differently, and the perception that alternatives exist. In contexts of deep poverty or rigid social norms, the absence of alternatives is itself a dimension of disempowerment.

### Institutional Gender Audit Template

For each institution, assess across all five dimensions:

| Institution | Rules | Resources | People | Activities | Power | Priority Actions |

| Market | | | | | | |
| Community | | | | | | |
| Family | | | | | | |

## 7. Intersectionality Framework (UN Women)

### Core Principle

Intersectionality recognises that people experience multiple, overlapping forms of discrimination simultaneously. A disabled woman in rural Karamoja faces compounding disadvantages of gender, disability, geography, ethnicity, and poverty that cannot be understood by examining any single dimension alone.

### What Intersectionality Is and Is Not

| Intersectionality IS | Intersectionality IS NOT |
|---------------------|-------------------------|
| An analytical lens for understanding compounding disadvantage | Simply adding categories together (gender + disability ≠ intersectionality) |
| Context-specific — identities matter differently in different settings | A fixed checklist of vulnerable groups |
| Focused on structural and systemic causes | Blaming individuals for their marginalisation |
| Attentive to power and privilege, including our own | Only about the most marginalised |
| A basis for inclusive, rights-based action | An excuse for inaction ("too complex to address") |

### Eight Intersectionality Enablers

| # | Enabler | Definition | Proposal Application |
|---|---------|------------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Reflexivity** | Examine your own position, power, and assumptions | Acknowledge consultant positionality in methodology |
| 2 | **Dignity, choice, autonomy** | Respect participants' agency and self-determination | Informed consent processes; participant-led data collection |
| 3 | **Accessibility and universal design** | Design for diverse needs from the outset | Accessible venues; materials in local languages; disability-inclusive formats |
| 4 | **Diverse knowledges** | Value indigenous, experiential, and non-academic knowledge | Include community researchers; oral methodologies; local frameworks |
| 5 | **Intersecting identities** | Analyse how multiple identities compound advantage/disadvantage | Disaggregate by gender × age × disability × location × income |
| 6 | **Relational power** | Examine how power operates between and within groups | Power analysis in stakeholder mapping; safe feedback mechanisms |
| 7 | **Time and space** | Recognise that identities and power shift across time and context | Longitudinal analysis; context-sensitive timing of consultations |
| 8 | **Transformative and rights-based** | Aim for structural change, not just inclusion | Theory of change addresses root causes; accountability mechanisms |

### Analyse → Adapt → Assess Action Framework

| Phase | Purpose | Key Questions | Tools |
|-------|---------|---------------|-------|
| **Analyse** | Understand the intersectional context | Who is excluded? What systems produce exclusion? What data exist (and what gaps)? | Intersectionality Context Analysis; Power Flower; disaggregated secondary data review |
| **Adapt** | Design interventions that respond to intersectional realities | How must the project be redesigned? What barriers must be removed? Who should lead/participate? | Safe Spaces methodology; Analysis-to-Adaptation matrix; participatory design workshops |
| **Assess** | Evaluate whether interventions reached the most marginalised | Did outcomes improve for the most disadvantaged? Were there unintended negative effects? | Disaggregated outcome data; participatory evaluation; Most Significant Change stories |

### LNOB (Leave No One Behind) Application

The SDG commitment to Leave No One Behind requires identifying who is left behind, why, and what to do about it. Five LNOB factors:

1. **Discrimination** — by gender, age, ethnicity, disability, religion, sexual orientation
2. **Geography** — remote, conflict-affected, or underserved areas
3. **Governance** — lack of voice, representation, or legal protection
4. **Socioeconomic status** — poverty, landlessness, informal employment
5. **Shocks and fragility** — climate events, displacement, health crises

In East Africa, typical LNOB priority groups: women and girls, youth (especially NEET), persons with disabilities, refugees and IDPs, pastoralist communities, ethnic minorities, elderly persons, LGBTQI+ persons (noting legal restrictions in Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania).

## 8. Behavioural Design for Gender Equality (Bohnet)

### Core Insight: De-Bias Systems, Not Minds

Unconscious bias training has limited evidence of effectiveness. Instead, redesign systems, processes, and decision architectures so that bias cannot operate. This applies to project design, procurement, recruitment, and institutional reform.

### Key Design Interventions

| Intervention | Principle | Application in East African Projects |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------------------------|
| **Blind evaluation** | Remove identifying information from assessments | Anonymise applications in grant/procurement processes; blind scoring of beneficiary selection |
| **Structured interviews** | Pre-set questions scored against rubrics | Standardise beneficiary selection; structured assessment of training participants |
| **Batch evaluation** | Compare candidates/options jointly rather than individually | Evaluate all proposals together rather than sequentially; comparative beneficiary ranking |
| **Default inclusion** | Make the inclusive option the default | Automatic enrolment of women in programmes; opt-out rather than opt-in for gender-responsive budgeting |
| **Transparency** | Publish criteria, processes, and outcomes | Public scoring criteria for procurement; published disaggregated results |
| **Role models and norms** | Make desired behaviour visible and normal | Female facilitators and team leaders; showcase women-led successes; male champions for gender equality |
| **Nudges** | Small design changes that steer behaviour | Reminders for gender-disaggregated reporting; default gender fields in data forms; prompts in checklists |
| **Goal setting and accountability** | Set specific, measurable targets with consequences | Gender targets in project logframes; gender KPIs in performance contracts; gender action plan with named accountabilities |

### Organisational and Institutional Application

Bohnet's framework applies beyond beneficiary-facing design to the consulting firm's own operations and the client institutions being reformed:

- **Hiring and team composition**: Use structured scoring for team selection; ensure diverse shortlists; evaluate CVs without names where possible
- **Meeting and workshop design**: Structured agendas with timed contributions prevent dominant voices from monopolising; round-robin techniques ensure all participants speak
- **Report and deliverable review**: Use checklists that prompt reviewers to assess gender impact at each section, rather than relying on a single "gender paragraph"
- **Institutional reform projects**: When advising clients on organisational restructuring, embed behavioural design into new HR policies, procurement rules, and service delivery processes

### Application to Project and Programme Design

When designing programmes:
- **Recruitment**: Use structured, criteria-based selection for all beneficiaries, staff, and consultants
- **Training**: Schedule sessions at times/locations accessible to women (avoiding early morning water collection hours, market days)
- **Procurement**: Include gender-responsive criteria in bid evaluation; require gender plans from contractors
- **M&E**: Default to gender-disaggregated data collection; design forms that capture intersectional data
- **Governance**: Require minimum representation on committees; structured agenda items for gender reporting

## 9. Proposal Strategy Notes

### Framework Selection Decision Tree

| Assignment Type | Primary Framework | Supporting Frameworks |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Baseline study / situation analysis** | Harvard Analytical | Social Relations for institutional context |
| **Community development project** | GAM (participatory) | Moser (triple roles), Longwe (empowerment levels) |
| **Policy reform / governance** | Social Relations | Moser (strategic interests), Longwe (Level 4–5) |
| **Service delivery / infrastructure** | Moser (practical needs) | Harvard (activity/access profiles), Bohnet (behavioural design) |
| **Empowerment / rights-based programme** | Longwe | Social Relations (institutional analysis), Intersectionality |
| **Humanitarian / conflict response** | CVA | GAM for community-level, Intersectionality for LNOB |
| **Institutional capacity building** | Social Relations | Longwe (participation/control levels), Bohnet (system redesign) |
| **M&E / evaluation assignment** | Longwe (as measurement ladder) | Intersectionality (Analyse-Adapt-Assess), Harvard (data tools) |
| **Cross-cutting GESI component** | Intersectionality + Moser | Bohnet for design, Longwe for measurement |

### Standard GESI Deliverables

Proposals should offer these deliverables where the ToR permits:

1. **Gender and Social Inclusion Analysis** — baseline assessment using appropriate framework(s)
2. **GESI Action Plan** — time-bound actions, responsibilities, indicators, and budget
3. **GESI Integration in Methodology** — not a standalone annex but woven into every phase
4. **Gender-Disaggregated M&E Framework** — indicators disaggregated by sex, age, disability at minimum
5. **GESI Capacity Building** — training for project staff and stakeholders on gender-responsive implementation
6. **GESI Reporting** — quarterly GESI progress in all project reports; annual GESI review

### Gender Mainstreaming vs Standalone GESI Components

| Approach | When to Use | How It Appears in Proposal |
|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Mainstreaming** (twin-track preferred) | Most assignments; ToR mentions gender as cross-cutting | Every methodology section addresses GESI; gender-disaggregated indicators throughout logframe; GESI lens in stakeholder analysis, risk register, capacity building |
| **Standalone GESI component** | ToR explicitly requires gender assessment, GESI strategy, or gender audit | Dedicated methodology section; specific deliverable (GESI Analysis Report, Gender Action Plan); GESI specialist on team |
| **Twin-track** (gold standard) | Large programmes; donor requires both | Mainstreaming across all components PLUS dedicated GESI deliverables; GESI specialist AND gender-sensitised core team |

### Donor GESI Requirements

| Donor | GESI Framework | Key Requirements | Scoring Impact |
|-------|---------------|------------------|----------------|
| **World Bank** | Gender Tag system; Gender Action Plan | Projects screened for gender gaps at identification; Gender Action Plan required for gender-tagged projects; sex-disaggregated indicators mandatory | Gender responsiveness assessed in technical evaluation; weak GESI can lower quality rating |
| **AfDB** | Gender Marker System (4 categories) | **GG** = gender equality as principal objective; **GM** = gender mainstreamed (significant objective); **SGI** = some gender elements; **NGE** = no gender elements. Gender Action Plan required for GG/GM. Climate-gender nexus expected. | Gender marker assigned at appraisal; proposals targeting GM or GG must demonstrate gender analysis and action plan |
| **UN System (UNDP, UNICEF, UN Women)** | UNCT SWAP Gender Equality Scorecard; UNDP Gender Seal | LNOB mandatory; gender-responsive budgeting; GEN2/GEN3 gender marker (significant/principal); HRBA integration | Gender marker directly affects project approval; GEN3 projects require dedicated gender budget |
| **EU** | Gender Action Plan III (GAP III) | 85% of new actions must be G1 or G2 (gender-marked); sector-specific gender analysis required; intersectionality expected | GAP III compliance is a prerequisite; proposals without gender analysis risk rejection |
| **DFID/FCDO** | Gender Equality Act obligation | Minimum gender analysis in business case; gender-disaggregated logframe; annual gender reporting | Mandatory compliance; not a differentiator but absence is disqualifying |
| **GIZ** | Gender Strategy; safeguards | Gender analysis in project proposal; gender-responsive indicators; gender competence in team | Assessed in technical evaluation |

### Practical Proposal Tips

1. **Name the framework** — do not simply say "we will conduct a gender analysis." State which framework (Harvard, Moser, Social Relations) and why it fits the assignment.
2. **Budget for GESI** — include specific budget lines for gender analysis, GESI training, gender-disaggregated data collection, and GESI specialist time. A credible GESI budget is 3–8% of total project cost.
3. **Staff it** — include a named GESI specialist (or demonstrate core team members' gender expertise with specific credentials).
4. **Go beyond sex-disaggregation** — disaggregate by sex, age, disability, location, income quintile. Propose intersectional analysis where feasible.
5. **Show East African expertise** — reference country-specific gender data (Uganda DHS, Kenya KDHS, Rwanda DHS, Tanzania TDHS), national gender policies, and regional gender frameworks (AU Maputo Protocol, EAC Gender Policy).
6. **Avoid tokenism** — do not add a GESI paragraph as an afterthought. Weave gender analysis into the methodology narrative, the risk register (gender-based risks), the stakeholder analysis (gender-differentiated stakeholders), and the work plan (GESI activities with dedicated time).
7. **Use Bohnet's design lens** — propose concrete system-level changes (structured selection criteria, default disaggregation, transparency mechanisms) rather than vague commitments to "gender sensitisation."
8. **Connect practical to strategic** — show how meeting immediate practical needs (Moser) builds pathways to longer-term strategic interests. This demonstrates analytical depth.
9. **Reference the donor's own framework** — if bidding to AfDB, use their gender marker language (GG/GM/SGI); if World Bank, reference the Gender Tag; if UN, use GEN markers and LNOB.
10. **Quantify the gender gap** — use national statistics to show the specific gender gap the project addresses. Numbers from DHS, World Bank Gender Data Portal, or national bureau of statistics are more persuasive than general statements about inequality.

## 10. East African Legal and Policy Context

### National Gender Frameworks

| Country | Constitution | National Gender Policy | Key Legislation | Affirmative Action |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Uganda** | Art. 33 (rights of women), Art. 32 (affirmative action), Art. 21 (equality) | National Gender Policy 2007 (under revision) | Domestic Violence Act 2010; Employment Act 2006 (sexual harassment); Land Act 1998 (spousal consent) | 1/3 women in local councils; district woman MP seats; PWD representation |
| **Kenya** | Art. 27 (equality), Art. 60 (land), two-thirds gender rule | National Policy on Gender and Development 2019 | Protection Against Domestic Violence Act 2015; Matrimonial Property Act 2013; National Land Commission Act | Two-thirds gender rule (Art. 27(8)) — not yet fully implemented; nominated seats |
| **Rwanda** | Art. 9 (equal rights), Art. 185 (30% minimum in decision-making) | National Gender Policy 2021 | Law on Prevention of GBV 2008; Land Law 1999 (equal inheritance); Labour Law (equal pay) | 30% constitutional minimum; in practice 61% in parliament; gender budgeting mandatory |
| **Tanzania** | Art. 12-13 (equality); Union and Zanzibar constitutions differ | National Gender Policy (under review) | Sexual Offences Special Provisions Act 1998; Land Act 1999 (women's right to own); Law of Marriage Act 1971 | Special seats for women in parliament (30%); local council quotas |

### Regional and Continental Frameworks

- **AU Protocol to the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights on the Rights of Women in Africa (Maputo Protocol, 2003)** — ratified by Uganda, Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania. Covers land rights, political participation, reproductive rights, protection from violence. Reference in proposals demonstrates continental alignment.
- **EAC Gender Policy** — harmonisation of gender legislation across partner states; mutual recognition of gender frameworks.
- **AU Agenda 2063** — Aspiration 6: gender parity in all spheres by 2063; gender-responsive budgeting.
- **Beijing Platform for Action** — 12 critical areas; periodic country reviews provide useful baseline data.
- **CEDAW** — all four countries are signatories; CEDAW Committee concluding observations provide country-specific recommendations useful for proposal context sections.

### Key Gender Data Sources for Proposals

| Source | Coverage | Update Frequency | Use In Proposals |
|--------|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Demographic and Health Survey (DHS) | All 4 countries | Every 5 years | GBV prevalence, maternal health, fertility, education, decision-making |
| World Bank Gender Data Portal | Global | Annual | Cross-country comparisons, labour force, education, legal indicators |
| UNDP Human Development Report (GII, GDI) | Global | Annual | Gender Inequality Index, Gender Development Index |
| National Bureau of Statistics (UBOS, KNBS, NISR, NBS) | Country-specific | Annual/periodic | Labour force surveys, household surveys, poverty data |
| Women, Business and the Law (World Bank) | Global | Annual | Legal barriers index; scoring on mobility, pay, marriage, assets, pension |
| AU Gender Scorecard | Continental | Periodic | Continental benchmarking |
| CEDAW Concluding Observations | Country-specific | Per reporting cycle | Donor-recognised authority; specific recommendations to cite |

### CVA Quick-Reference Grid

For humanitarian and refugee programming (relevant to northern Uganda, western Kenya/Turkana, Rwandan transit settings):

| Category | Capacities | Vulnerabilities |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Physical/Material** | Assets, infrastructure, skills, health | Poverty, displacement, loss of assets, food insecurity |
| **Social/Organisational** | Community networks, leadership structures, women's groups, faith organisations | Breakdown of social networks, loss of community, fragmented governance, GBV risk |
| **Motivational/Attitudinal** | Resilience, cultural identity, hope, agency, collective memory | Trauma, dependency, fatalism, loss of identity, internalised subordination |

Disaggregate each cell by gender, age, disability, and displacement status. In East African refugee settings, host community dynamics must be analysed alongside refugee populations — capacities and vulnerabilities exist on both sides.
