# Document And Section Patterns

Parent skill: [Premium Commercial Writing](../SKILL.md).

Use these patterns to upgrade common commercial writing outputs without changing the document owner's skill.

## Cover Letters

Job: create trust before the evaluator reads the proposal.

Pattern:

1. Name the client's specific situation or decision.
2. State the submission and the outcome the firm is ready to support.
3. Give two or three proof points tied to comparable work, local context, specialist method, or service quality.
4. Confirm compliance, validity, enclosures, and contact details.
5. Close courteously and confidently.

Premium note: a premium cover letter should signal judgement, reliability, and fit. It should not oversell.

## Executive Summaries

Job: make the award decision feel logical.

Pattern:

1. Start with the problem, desired result, and primary benefit.
2. State the governing idea in one sentence.
3. Present three to four pillars: method, proof, team, and value.
4. Summarise scope and timeline only after the value case is clear.
5. Close on outcomes during the assignment, at completion, and after implementation.

Premium note: make the value case before listing activities.

## Proposal Sections

Job: help evaluators score confidently and defend the recommendation.

Pattern:

- Understanding: current state, desired state, gap, constraints, and implications.
- Methodology: phase, purpose, activities, artefacts, decisions, controls, and value.
- Experience: context, challenge, role, intervention, outcome, and relevance.
- Team: why each role exists, what risk it controls, and how coordination works.
- Work plan: sequencing logic, dependencies, review gates, and realistic buffers.
- Financial proposal: effort drivers, assumptions, options, exclusions, and value link.

Premium note: show invisible work. Senior review, QA, discovery, documentation, support, and governance must be visible when they drive the fee.

## Case Studies

Job: prove transferability, not just past activity.

Pattern:

| Element | What To Write |
|---|---|
| Context | Who the client was and what made the situation relevant. |
| Stakes | What risk, cost, delay, opportunity, or user problem mattered. |
| Role | The firm's exact responsibility and authority. |
| Method | The choices made and why they fit the context. |
| Result | Quantified or observable outcome. |
| Relevance | Why this example should reassure the current evaluator. |

Premium note: relevance is the bridge between proof and price confidence.

## Business Documents

Job: help a busy decision maker understand, compare, approve, or act.

Pattern:

1. Decision or question.
2. Short answer.
3. Evidence and options.
4. Risks, assumptions, and constraints.
5. Recommendation and next action.

Premium note: senior documents should make trade-offs visible, not hide them behind polished prose.

## Blogs And Thought Leadership

Job: earn attention, trust, and discoverability.

Pattern:

1. Lead with a concrete problem, observation, example, or answer.
2. State who the article is for and what it will help them decide or do.
3. Use clear headings that answer real questions.
4. Add examples, data, comparisons, and practical steps.
5. Close with a useful next step, not a generic sales pitch.

Premium note: public expertise content should show judgement. It should take a clear position and explain the reasoning.
