# Lean Change Management and Adaptive Approaches

Reference material for the change-management skill. Provides frameworks for managing change in complex, uncertain, or rapidly evolving environments where traditional plan-driven approaches are insufficient. Use when proposing change management for ICT transformations, agile adoptions, digital solutions, or any assignment where the end state cannot be fully defined at the outset.

Sources: Little (2014), Hattula & Köhler (2023), Hearsum (2024).

---

## 1. The Lean Change Management Cycle

**Little's core model:** A non-linear, feedback-driven approach to managing change that borrows from Lean Startup, Agile, and organisational development.

### The Three Phases (Looping, Not Linear)

```
Insights → Options → Experiments → (back to Insights)
```

**Phase 1 — Insights**
Before planning any change, understand the current state. Use multiple methods to gather qualitative and quantitative data about what is happening in the organisation right now.

Practices for generating insights:
- **Lean Coffee** — informal, participant-driven meetings where attendees vote on discussion topics. No agenda is set in advance. Surfaces real concerns rather than managed narratives.
- **Culture Hacking** — small, deliberate interventions that test the organisation's cultural boundaries. Examples: rearranging physical space, changing meeting formats, introducing new rituals. Observe how people react.
- **Agile Retrospectives** — structured reflection sessions asking: What went well? What did not go well? What will we do differently? Run regularly (every 2–4 weeks), not just at project end.
- **Force Field Analysis** — map driving and restraining forces for the proposed change (see change-process-models.md).
- **Information Radiators** — make change progress visible to everyone. Physical or digital dashboards showing what changes are planned, in progress, and completed. Transparency reduces anxiety and rumour.

Assessments for generating insights:
- **ADKAR Assessment** — measure where each stakeholder group sits on the Awareness–Desire–Knowledge–Ability–Reinforcement spectrum. Identifies which element is the bottleneck.
- **OCAI (Organisational Culture Assessment Instrument)** — maps the organisation's current culture against four types: clan, adhocracy, market, hierarchy. Reveals the cultural distance between current state and the culture needed to support the change.
- **Schneider Culture Model** — classifies culture along two axes: people vs company focus, and actuality vs possibility orientation. Produces four culture types: collaboration, control, competence, cultivation.

**Phase 2 — Options**
Once you have enough insight, generate options — not a single plan, but multiple possible approaches. Each option is assessed on:

| Criterion | Question |
|---|---|
| **Cost** | What resources (time, money, people) does this option require? |
| **Value** | What benefit does this option deliver if it works? |
| **Impact** | How many people are affected, and how significantly? |
| **Blast radius** | If this option fails, how much damage does it cause? |
| **Sphere of influence** | Is this within our authority to implement, or do we need approval? |

**Option classification:**
- **Quick wins** — low cost, low risk, visible impact. Do these first to build momentum and credibility.
- **Strategic bets** — high cost, high potential value, significant risk. Require careful experimentation and stakeholder buy-in.
- **Operational improvements** — moderate cost, moderate value, low risk. Steady progress, not transformational.

**Phase 3 — Experiments**
Turn selected options into experiments. An experiment is a time-boxed change initiative with:

1. **Hypothesis** — a testable statement: "We believe that [action] will result in [outcome] because [reasoning]."
2. **Measurements** — specific indicators that will tell you whether the hypothesis was confirmed or refuted
3. **Duration** — a defined time box (typically 2–4 weeks)
4. **Review criteria** — at the end of the time box, decide to:
   - **Pursue** — the experiment worked; continue and expand
   - **Pivot** — partially worked; adjust and try again
   - **Abandon** — did not work; stop and redirect effort

**Proposal application:** For ICT and digital transformation assignments, propose the Lean Change Management cycle as your change management methodology. It signals adaptiveness and positions the firm as modern and evidence-based. Include the Insights→Options→Experiments cycle in your methodology diagram.

## 2. Canvases for Change Planning

### The Improvement Canvas

A one-page visual tool for planning a single change experiment:

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Current state** | What is happening now? (Based on insights gathered) |
| **Urgency** | Why must this change now? What happens if we do nothing? |
| **Target state** | What will be different after the experiment? |
| **Success criteria** | How will we know the experiment worked? |
| **Hypothesis** | Our testable assumption |
| **Actions** | Specific steps to execute the experiment |
| **Measurements** | Quantitative and qualitative indicators |
| **Timeline** | Start date, duration, review date |

### The Strategic Change Canvas

A one-page tool for aligning the entire change programme at the strategic level:

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Vision** | The desired future state in one sentence |
| **Key changes** | The 3–5 major changes required to reach the vision |
| **Stakeholder groups** | Who is affected and how |
| **Change readiness** | Current readiness assessment per group |
| **Success metrics** | Organisation-level outcomes that define success |
| **Risks** | Top risks and mitigation strategies |
| **Quick wins** | Early visible changes to build momentum |
| **Timeline** | High-level phased roadmap |

### The One-Page Change Plan

A consolidated view that replaces lengthy change management strategy documents:

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Why** | Business case for the change (2–3 sentences) |
| **What** | Scope of the change (what is changing and what is not) |
| **Who** | Key stakeholders and their roles |
| **How** | Change approach (framework, key activities) |
| **When** | Phased timeline with milestones |
| **Measures** | How success will be tracked |
| **Risks** | Top 3–5 risks and responses |

**Proposal application:** Offer to produce canvases as deliverables during inception. They are visual, concise, and easy for clients to use. Evaluators value deliverables that the client can actually maintain after the project ends.

## 3. The Change Agent Network

**Little's model for distributed change leadership:**

A change agent network replaces the traditional "change management team" with a distributed network of people across the organisation who facilitate change at the local level.

**Design principles:**
- Recruit volunteers, not assignees — people who are genuinely interested in the change
- Include people from every affected area and level — not just management
- Give change agents real authority to make local decisions, not just a communication role
- Train agents in facilitation, feedback gathering, and conflict resolution — not just messaging
- Create regular forums (weekly or bi-weekly) where agents share what they are seeing on the ground
- Protect agents from retaliation when they surface uncomfortable truths

**Sphere of influence mapping:**
For each change agent, map their sphere of influence — who do they have credibility with, and what changes fall within their authority? This prevents wasted effort on changes outside their control and focuses energy where it can have impact.

## 4. Digital Change Management (Hattula & Köhler)

Specific considerations for change management in digital transformation and technology implementation contexts:

### Why Digital Change Is Different

- **Speed of change** — technology changes faster than organisational culture can adapt
- **Visibility** — digital tools make work visible in ways that were previously hidden (dashboards, audit trails), creating anxiety about surveillance
- **Skill requirements** — digital change typically requires new technical skills from people who chose their career before these technologies existed
- **Continuous updates** — unlike a one-time process change, digital tools evolve continuously (updates, new features, version upgrades), requiring ongoing change management, not a one-off initiative

### Key Practices for Digital Change

1. **Start with the user, not the technology.** Understand how people currently work before proposing how they should work with the new tool. Map current workflows, pain points, and informal workarounds.

2. **Involve end users in design.** Co-creation of digital solutions with end users dramatically increases adoption. When people design their own tools, resistance drops because ownership increases.

3. **Phase the rollout.** Pilot with a willing group, learn, adjust, then expand. Never attempt a "big bang" rollout for a complex digital solution unless regulatory or contractual deadlines require it.

4. **Provide just-in-time training.** Train people on what they need to know right now, not everything the system can do. Follow up with advanced training as they gain confidence.

5. **Create safe spaces for failure.** People need to make mistakes with new technology without consequences. Sandbox environments, practice periods, and explicit "no blame" policies during transition are essential.

6. **Measure adoption, not deployment.** The technology is not "implemented" when it is installed. It is implemented when people are using it correctly and consistently. Track usage metrics, not just go-live dates.

### The Adoption Curve for Digital Solutions

| Stage | Users | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Innovators** (2–3%) | Technology enthusiasts who will try anything new | Involve them in pilot; use them as proof of concept |
| **Early adopters** (10–15%) | Influential staff who see strategic value | Make them change champions; give them visible roles |
| **Early majority** (30–35%) | Pragmatists who need to see evidence before committing | Show them results from pilots; provide solid training |
| **Late majority** (30–35%) | Sceptics who adopt only when it becomes the norm | Peer pressure, management expectation, removing old alternatives |
| **Resisters** (5–15%) | Those who will not adopt willingly | Support individually; address root causes; accept that a small percentage may never fully adopt |

**Proposal application:** For ICT assignments, include the adoption curve in your methodology with explicit strategies per segment. This demonstrates that you understand adoption is not binary (on/off) but a spectrum that requires differentiated interventions.

## 5. Feedback-Driven vs Plan-Driven Change

**Little's core distinction:**

| Dimension | Plan-Driven | Feedback-Driven |
|---|---|---|
| **Planning** | Detailed upfront plan covering the full change lifecycle | Lightweight plan for the next 2–4 weeks; adapts based on feedback |
| **Execution** | Follow the plan; deviations are failures | Execute experiments; deviations are data |
| **Measurement** | Against the original plan (on time, on budget) | Against the intended outcome (did adoption increase?) |
| **Resistance** | A problem to be managed | A signal to be understood |
| **Decision-making** | Centralised (project board, steering committee) | Distributed (change agents empowered to make local decisions) |
| **Learning** | Post-project review | Continuous (retrospectives, action reviews, experiment reviews) |

**When to use each:**
- **Plan-driven:** When the change is well-understood, the end state is clear, the organisation is stable, and the timeline is fixed (e.g., regulatory compliance, office relocation)
- **Feedback-driven:** When the change is complex, the end state is uncertain, the organisation is unstable, and adoption is the critical success factor (e.g., digital transformation, culture change, agile adoption)
- **Hybrid:** Most real-world assignments benefit from a hybrid — plan-driven for structural and technical changes, feedback-driven for people and cultural changes

**Proposal application:** In your methodology, explicitly state whether you are proposing a plan-driven, feedback-driven, or hybrid approach and why. This is a differentiator — most competitors default to plan-driven without justification.

## 6. Measuring Change Success

### Qualitative Measures
- Stakeholder sentiment (surveys, interviews, focus groups)
- Adoption stories (specific examples of people using the new way of working)
- Resistance patterns (are they decreasing, shifting, or increasing?)
- Leadership engagement (are sponsors actively participating?)

### Quantitative Measures
- Adoption rate (percentage of target population using the new system/process)
- Proficiency rate (percentage who can perform tasks correctly without support)
- Error rate (compared to baseline)
- Help desk/support requests (declining trend indicates increasing capability)
- Time to competence (how long from first use to independent operation)

### Leading vs Lagging Indicators

| Leading (predict future success) | Lagging (confirm past success) |
|---|---|
| Training completion rate | Business outcome achievement |
| Communication reach and engagement | Adoption rate after 90 days |
| Change champion network activity | Proficiency assessment results |
| Stakeholder sentiment trend | Help desk request volume trend |
| Experiment success rate | Return on investment |

**Proposal application:** Include both leading and lagging indicators in your M&E framework for the change management component. Leading indicators allow early course correction; lagging indicators demonstrate ultimate success.
