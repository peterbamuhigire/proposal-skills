# Change Process Models and Diagnostic Frameworks

Reference material for the change-management skill. Provides the theoretical and diagnostic models that underpin how change unfolds in organisations. Use when designing the conceptual approach for a change management methodology or when explaining *why* you have chosen a particular change approach.

Sources: Hayes (2002), Hearsum (2024), Little (2014), Adams & Straw (2016).

---

## 1. The Satir Change Model (Five Stages of Human Response)

Virginia Satir's model describes how individuals and groups experience change. It is particularly useful for setting realistic expectations about productivity dips during transition.

1. **Late Status Quo** — everything is familiar and comfortable; performance is stable. People operate on autopilot.
2. **Foreign Element** — a change is introduced. This triggers resistance. The strength of the response depends on how threatening the change feels.
3. **Chaos** — people feel they are losing their identity. Confusion, anger, and a general sense of loss cause a measurable drop in productivity. This stage is unavoidable — the question is how long it lasts.
4. **Transforming Idea, Practice and Integration** — people reach the point where they "get it." They begin integrating the benefits of the change into their new way of working.
5. **New Status Quo** — performance stabilises at a higher level than the original status quo.

**Proposal application:** When proposing change timelines, build in an explicit productivity dip period (Chaos stage). Evaluators who have managed change before will recognise this as realistic. Proposals that promise smooth, linear adoption signal naivety.

**Key insight from Little:** "Try as you may, change cannot be controlled." People fight through the pain of change when they want the outcome badly enough — the change agent's job is to help them find their own motivation, not to impose it.

## 2. Lewin's Three-Stage Model (Unfreeze–Change–Refreeze)

The foundational model in change management, still widely referenced:

1. **Unfreeze** — destabilise the current equilibrium. Create awareness that the current state is unsustainable. Overcome complacency and denial.
2. **Change (Move)** — implement the new processes, systems, structures, or behaviours. This is where most project activities sit.
3. **Refreeze** — stabilise the new state. Embed changes in policies, procedures, performance metrics, and organisational culture so the organisation does not drift back.

**Proposal application:** Use Lewin when the client expects a clear, phased approach. Map your methodology phases to unfreeze (inception, readiness assessment), change (implementation, rollout), and refreeze (reinforcement, sustainability).

**Limitation (Hayes):** Lewin's model assumes a stable starting point and a stable ending point. In reality, many organisations face continuous change where the "refreeze" never fully occurs. For such contexts, use adaptive models (Lean Change Management or Agile approaches) instead.

## 3. The Kübler-Ross Change Curve (Emotional Response to Change)

Adapted from the grief cycle, this model maps the emotional trajectory people follow when faced with significant change:

1. **Shock and denial** — "This won't really happen" or "This doesn't affect me"
2. **Anger and frustration** — "Why is this happening? Who decided this?"
3. **Bargaining and depression** — "Can we keep some of the old ways?" or passive disengagement
4. **Acceptance** — beginning to engage with the new reality
5. **Commitment** — actively embracing and championing the new state

**Proposal application:** Use the change curve to design targeted interventions at each stage. Communication strategies, training timing, and support mechanisms should be calibrated to where people are on the curve, not where the project plan says they should be.

**Key insight (Hayes):** Different stakeholder groups move through the curve at different speeds. Senior management may reach acceptance during planning, while front-line staff are still in denial at go-live. The change plan must account for this stagger.

## 4. Force Field Analysis (Lewin)

A diagnostic tool for understanding the balance of forces acting on a proposed change:

- **Driving forces** — pressures pushing towards the change (external mandate, efficiency needs, competitive pressure, client expectations, regulatory requirements)
- **Restraining forces** — pressures resisting the change (habit, fear of job loss, lack of skills, resource constraints, political interests, cultural norms)

**How to use in proposals:**

1. List the driving and restraining forces for the specific change the assignment addresses
2. Assign relative strength to each force (high/medium/low)
3. Design interventions that either strengthen driving forces or weaken restraining forces — Lewin's insight is that weakening restraints is usually more effective than pushing harder
4. Present the force field analysis as evidence of your understanding of the change dynamics

**Proposal application:** Include a force field analysis in the understanding-of-assignment section (section 03) to demonstrate insight into the client's change landscape. This signals diagnostic rigour.

## 5. The Nadler-Tushman Congruence Model

An open-systems diagnostic model that identifies misalignment as the root cause of organisational dysfunction. Four components must be congruent for an organisation to function effectively:

- **Work** — the tasks and activities performed
- **People** — the skills, attitudes, and behaviours of staff
- **Formal organisation** — structure, policies, procedures, reward systems
- **Informal organisation** — culture, norms, politics, unwritten rules

**The diagnostic question:** When proposing change, identify which components are misaligned. A new IT system (work) fails when staff lack skills (people), when procedures are not updated (formal), or when the culture resists technology (informal).

**Proposal application:** Use the congruence model in methodology sections to show that you address all four dimensions, not just the technical deliverable. This differentiates your approach from firms that focus only on the "hard" side.

## 6. The Burke-Litwin Model of Organisational Performance and Change

A more detailed diagnostic model that distinguishes between transformational and transactional factors:

**Transformational factors** (change the organisation's fundamental character):
- External environment
- Mission and strategy
- Leadership
- Organisational culture

**Transactional factors** (change day-to-day operations within the existing character):
- Structure
- Management practices
- Systems (policies and procedures)
- Work unit climate
- Task and individual skills
- Motivation
- Individual needs and values

**The key insight (Hayes):** Transformational changes require intervention at the leadership and culture level. Transactional changes can succeed with interventions at the systems and practices level. Misdiagnosing a transformational challenge as transactional is the most common cause of change failure — you cannot fix a culture problem with a new system.

**Proposal application:** Use Burke-Litwin to determine the level of intervention your methodology needs to address. If the ToR describes symptoms of misalignment at the transformational level (culture, leadership, strategy), your methodology must include transformational interventions — not just process redesign.

## 7. Complexity and Emergence — Moving Beyond Linear Models

**Hearsum's core argument:** The most dangerous assumption in change management is that change can be planned and controlled in a linear, predictable way. Organisations are complex adaptive systems where:

- Small changes can have disproportionately large effects
- Outcomes are emergent, not predetermined
- Context is decisive — what worked in one organisation will not necessarily work in another
- "Silver bullets" (quick-fix frameworks applied without contextual adaptation) create more harm than good

**The Silver Bullet Lifecycle (Hearsum):**
1. **Creation** — a framework, methodology, or tool is developed in response to a genuine need
2. **Popularisation** — it is packaged, branded, and marketed as a universal solution
3. **Commoditisation** — practitioners apply it mechanically without understanding the underlying principles
4. **Disillusionment** — clients discover it does not work as promised in their specific context
5. **Replacement** — the cycle begins again with a new silver bullet

**The Functional Collusion Trap:** Consultants who sell silver bullets and clients who buy them are locked in a collusion — the consultant needs to appear expert and certain; the client needs to believe the problem has a clean solution. Breaking this collusion requires both parties to embrace uncertainty and inquiry over premature solutionising.

**Proposal application:** In your methodology, explicitly acknowledge complexity. Statements such as "Our approach is adaptive — we will refine our methodology based on what we discover during inception" signal sophistication. Avoid presenting your methodology as a rigid, predetermined set of steps.

**Practical guidance from Hearsum:**
- Lead with curiosity and inquiry, not answers
- Build in reflection and sense-making points throughout the project
- Accept that "not knowing" at the start is a strength, not a weakness — it prevents premature solutions
- Design for emergence: create conditions for change rather than trying to control every outcome

## 8. Types of Change — A Classification Framework

**Hayes's taxonomy of organisational change:**

| Dimension | Options |
|---|---|
| **Scope** | Fine-tuning vs. radical transformation |
| **Origin** | Planned (proactive) vs. emergent (reactive) |
| **Driver** | Externally driven (regulation, market) vs. internally driven (strategy, leadership) |
| **Pace** | Incremental (continuous small changes) vs. revolutionary (rapid, discontinuous) |
| **Level** | Individual behaviour, group/team, organisational structure, inter-organisational |

**Proposal application:** Classify the change the assignment addresses along these dimensions in your understanding section. This demonstrates analytical sophistication and ensures your methodology matches the type of change required. A fine-tuning, incremental change needs a different approach from a radical, revolutionary transformation.

## 9. Adams and Straw's Seven Fundamental Shifts

A practitioner framework for transformational change that focuses on leadership mindset:

1. **Letting Go of the Past** — stop normalising the current state; avoid "reverting to type"; lead from the future, not the past
2. **Developing Breakthrough Ambition** — go beyond predictable targets; make commitments that stretch beyond what seems achievable; declare the breakthrough publicly
3. **Creating a Bold Vision of the Future** — use burning platforms and emotional resonance to make the vision compelling; content and delivery both matter
4. **Engaging the Players** — move beyond top-down mandates; alignment does not require agreement; create empowered participation
5. **Cutting Through the DNA** — identify the organisation's unconscious patterns (unwritten rules, habitual behaviours) and deliberately break them
6. **Keeping the Organisation Future-Focused** — use the power of strong commitments and conversation to sustain momentum
7. **Gaining Energy from Setbacks** — reframe setbacks as learning; shift attention from what failed to what was learned

**Proposal application:** Use the Seven Shifts as a diagnostic and design framework for transformational assignments. In the methodology, map your phases to the shifts: inception (shifts 1–3), implementation (shifts 4–5), sustainability (shifts 6–7).

**Key insight (Adams):** "Context is decisive." The same change initiative will succeed or fail depending on the organisational context. Proposals must demonstrate contextual awareness, not just methodological rigour.
