# Ethical Persuasion and Evaluator Psychology Gate

Derived from local HTML extractions on persuasion, social marketing, marketing measurement, buyer discovery, risk perception, and design communication. Use this as original proposal guidance, not as quoted source text.

## Purpose

Use this gate before writing or reviewing high-stakes proposal sections. It keeps persuasion ethical, evidence-based, and evaluator-friendly.

## Core Rules

- Persuade through relevance, proof, clarity, and practical trade-offs.
- Match the argument to the evaluator's role: executive value, technical feasibility, procurement compliance, user adoption, finance sustainability, and risk control.
- Make strong claims only when the evidence, method, team, budget, and timeline support them.
- Use social proof only when it is specific and comparable.
- Show risk honestly, then show the controls that reduce it.
- Avoid pressure tactics, fake urgency, exaggerated certainty, fear-based manipulation, or unverifiable authority.

## Evaluator Psychology Checks

| Evaluator Concern | Proposal Response |
|---|---|
| "Do they understand us?" | Use the client's context, constraints, users, and decision path. |
| "Can they do this?" | Show comparable evidence, team roles, method, QA, and delivery controls. |
| "Will this work here?" | Address local context, data availability, approvals, infrastructure, capability, and adoption. |
| "Is this worth the price?" | Tie cost to value, risk reduction, speed, quality, support, and total ownership. |
| "What could go wrong?" | State the risk, trigger, mitigation, owner, and escalation route. |
| "Can we defend awarding to them?" | Make the evaluator's decision easy to justify with evidence and compliance. |

## Risk Perception Rules

- Distinguish severity, likelihood, vulnerability, and controllability.
- Do not assume rational evidence alone changes perception; explain what the client can do next.
- Use two-sided arguments where risk is real: acknowledge the issue, then show controls and trade-offs.
- Avoid making low-probability risks sound certain or high-probability risks sound trivial.

## Ethical Persuasion Pattern

1. Name the client's situation or decision.
2. State the practical consequence of action or delay.
3. Present the proposed path.
4. Prove the path with comparable evidence, method, or validated assumptions.
5. Explain the trade-off.
6. End with a clear decision or next step.

## Red Flags

- "Guaranteed transformation" with no baseline, scope, or ownership.
- "Best in class" without a benchmark.
- Scarcity or urgency that the brief does not support.
- Case studies that are not comparable.
- Hiding risks to make the proposal sound easier.
- Using evaluator fear instead of risk-management logic.
