# Uganda Health Sector Profile

## 1. Disease Burden

Uganda faces a persistent triple burden of communicable diseases, non-communicable diseases (NCDs), and epidemic vulnerability.

### Communicable Diseases
- **Malaria** remains the leading cause of morbidity and mortality: 13 million cases and 19,663 deaths recorded in 2021.
- **HIV/AIDS** prevalence stands at 5.5% among adults. New HIV infections have declined significantly — from 68,783 in 2016 to 36,648 in 2024 — reflecting progress in prevention and treatment. Progress along the UNAIDS 89-92-95 cascade indicates improving treatment coverage, though gaps persist in testing and viral suppression.
- **Tuberculosis** incidence is 198 per 100,000 population, compounded by HIV co-infection.

### Non-Communicable Diseases
- NCDs account for 36% of all deaths, creating a dual burden alongside communicable diseases.
- The rise of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and cancers is straining a health system designed primarily around infectious disease control.

### Epidemic Vulnerability
- Uganda has experienced outbreaks of Ebola and Marburg virus disease, exposing weaknesses in surveillance and rapid response.
- The country scores 36.5 out of 100 on the Global Health Security Index, indicating significant gaps in preparedness, detection, and response capacity.

### Nutrition
- **Undernourishment**: 36.9% of the population is undernourished.
- **Food insecurity**: 71.2% of the population experiences moderate or severe food insecurity, constraining health outcomes across all age groups.
- **Stunting**: 29% prevalence among children under five, with long-term consequences for cognitive development and economic productivity.

## 2. Universal Health Coverage and Life Expectancy

- **Universal Health Coverage (UHC) Service Coverage Index**: improved from 43 (2015) to 49 (2021), indicating gradual progress but still well below the SDG 3.8 target of 80.
- **Life expectancy at birth** (2024): total 68.2 years; females 70.1 years; males 66.9 years — a significant gain over the past two decades, though gender and regional disparities persist.

## 3. Morbidity and Productivity Loss

- 21% of the population reported illness in the preceding 30 days.
- Average illness duration: 7 sick days, of which 3 are lost productive days — a substantial drag on household income and national economic output.

## 4. Maternal, Newborn, and Child Health

- **Maternal mortality ratio**: 189 per 100,000 live births (2022), down from 336 per 100,000 in 2016 — a notable improvement but still far above the SDG 3.1 target of 70.
- **Under-5 mortality**: 52 per 1,000 live births.
- Only **42% of births** are attended by skilled health professionals, limiting the ability to manage obstetric complications.

## 5. Reproductive Health and Fertility

- **Total fertility rate**: 4.3 children per woman (DHS 2022), down from 5.8 in 2014 — a sustained decline but still among the highest globally, driving rapid population growth and straining health and education services. The UN SDG analysis records 4.5 for 2024.
- **Modern contraceptive prevalence**: 24%, indicating significant unmet need for family planning.
- **Adolescent birth rate**: 111.4 per 1,000 girls aged 15–19, reflecting early marriage, limited access to reproductive health information, and weak adolescent-friendly services.

## 6. Gender-Based Violence

Gender-based violence (GBV) remains a critical public health and protection concern, with implications for maternal health, mental health, and child development.

- **Physical violence** (ages 15–49, 2022): 44% of women and 39% of men have experienced physical violence.
- **Childhood physical violence** (ages 18–24, 2022): 59% of girls and 68% of boys report experiencing physical violence during childhood.
- **Childhood sexual violence** (ages 18–24, 2022): 35% of girls and 17% of boys report experiencing sexual violence during childhood.

These figures underscore the need for integrated GBV response within health service delivery, including clinical management of survivors, psychosocial support, and referral pathways.

## 7. Health Financing

### Overall Expenditure
- Total health expenditure: UGX 8.71 trillion (FY2020/21).
- Financing mix:
  - Donors: 45.4%
  - Households (out-of-pocket): 29%
  - Government: 25.6%

### Public Spending Shortfall
- Public health spending: approximately **USD 6.8 per capita**, far below the WHO-recommended minimum of **USD 86 per capita** for basic services.
- The health sector receives roughly **8% of the national budget**, well short of the **15% Abuja Declaration target** to which Uganda is a signatory.

### Financial Protection Gaps
- Over **5 million Ugandans** are exposed to catastrophic health costs due to high out-of-pocket expenditure.
- **84% of donor funding** is off-budget, limiting government oversight and creating parallel systems that undermine sector coordination.

## 8. Health Infrastructure and Access

- **72% of the population** lives within 5 km of a health facility — reasonable geographic coverage, though quality and staffing at lower-level facilities remain weak.
- **Hospital bed density**: 5 per 10,000 population — insufficient for a country with Uganda's disease burden and population growth rate.

## 9. Health Workforce

- **Doctor-to-patient ratio**: 1:25,000, compared with the WHO benchmark of 1:1,000 — a 25-fold gap.
- **Overall health workforce density**: 2.6 per 1,000 population, against the WHO threshold of 4.45 per 1,000 needed to deliver essential services.
- Workforce shortages are most acute in rural and hard-to-reach areas, driving inequitable access to care.

## 10. Reform Needs and Implications for Proposals

Proposals targeting Uganda's health sector should address one or more of the following structural challenges:

1. **Domestic financing**: Closing the gap between 8% and the 15% Abuja target through advocacy, PFM reform, and earmarked health taxes.
2. **Workforce expansion and retention**: Training, deployment incentives, and task-shifting strategies to address the 2.6 vs 4.45 density gap.
3. **Primary health care strengthening**: Improving quality at lower-level facilities to reduce referral pressure on hospitals.
4. **Epidemic preparedness**: Strengthening surveillance, laboratory networks, and rapid-response capacity (GHSI score of 36.5).
5. **NCD integration**: Building NCD prevention and management into a system historically focused on communicable diseases.
6. **Financial protection**: Expanding health insurance coverage and reducing catastrophic out-of-pocket expenditure.
7. **Reproductive and adolescent health**: Addressing the 111.4/1,000 adolescent birth rate through youth-friendly services, family planning access, and community-based interventions.
8. **On-budget aid alignment**: Working with donors to bring the 84% off-budget funding onto national systems.
9. **Nutrition and early childhood development**: Tackling 29% stunting and 36.9% undernourishment through integrated nutrition interventions targeting early childhood and adolescent girls.
10. **GBV response integration**: Embedding clinical management of GBV survivors, psychosocial support, and referral pathways within routine health service delivery.

### SDG Pathway Alignment

The UN Uganda SDG Analysis (2025) positions health as central to **Pathway 4: Universal Social Protection and Human Capital**. This pathway prioritises:

- Strengthening **primary healthcare quality** as the foundation of the health system.
- Accelerating progress on **maternal and child health** to reduce preventable deaths.
- Ensuring availability of **essential medicines** at all levels of care.
- Advancing **Universal Health Coverage** to reduce financial risk and catastrophic out-of-pocket expenditure.
- Scaling **nutrition interventions** with particular focus on early childhood and adolescent girls.

Proposals that align with Pathway 4 framing are well positioned for UN system, World Bank, and bilateral funding streams targeting Uganda's SDG acceleration agenda.
