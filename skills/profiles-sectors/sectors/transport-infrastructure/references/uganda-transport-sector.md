# Uganda Transport and Infrastructure Sector Profile

## 1. Road Network

### Network Overview

Uganda's road network totals 159,366 km, classified as follows:

| Category | Length (km) | Share |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| National roads | 20,854 | 13.1% |
| District roads | 38,603 | 24.2% |
| Urban roads | 19,959 | 12.5% |
| Community access roads | 79,947 | 50.2% |
| **Total** | **159,366** | **100%** |

### Paved Road Expansion

The paved national road network has expanded significantly, from 3,264 km in FY2010/11 to over 7,000 km by 2023. This represents a doubling of the paved network in just over a decade, improving connectivity between major urban centres and border posts.

### Persistent Challenges

- **Urban roads**: only 53.3% of planned targets have been achieved, contributing to severe congestion in Kampala and secondary cities.
- **Maintenance deficit**: poor maintenance practices and inadequate funding reduce the durability and lifespan of both paved and unpaved roads.
- **Rural access**: community access roads (50% of the network) remain largely unpaved and poorly maintained, constraining agricultural market access and rural economic activity.
- **Road safety**: the road fatality rate stands at 28.9 per 100,000 people (2023), among the highest in East Africa, driven by poor road design, inadequate enforcement, and the dominance of motorcycles (boda-bodas) in urban and peri-urban transport.

## 2. Rail Network

### Current State

Uganda's railway network comprises 1,266 km of metre-gauge track, of which only 270 km (21.2%) is operational. The remainder is out of service due to decades of underinvestment and deterioration. The operational segments serve limited freight corridors, primarily linking Kampala to the Kenyan border.

### Rehabilitation and Modernisation

- **Tororo–Gulu line**: rehabilitation is underway to restore this strategic northern corridor, which connects to South Sudan and serves agricultural and humanitarian freight.
- **Kampala–Malaba modernisation**: upgrading of the main eastern corridor to improve freight throughput to the Kenyan border and onward to Mombasa port.
- **Kampala–Kasese Standard Gauge Railway**: planned as a new standard-gauge line to serve the western corridor, connecting to mineral and tourism-rich western Uganda and potentially linking to the Democratic Republic of Congo.

## 3. Aviation

- **Entebbe International Airport**: expansion and modernisation is 90% complete, increasing capacity for both passenger and cargo traffic.
- **Kabalega International Airport** (Hoima): construction is 96% complete. This airport will serve the Albertine Graben oil-producing region and the planned refinery at Kabaale.

## 4. Global Competitiveness Ratings

Uganda's infrastructure scores on the Global Competitiveness Index (scale of 1–7) highlight significant quality gaps:

| Infrastructure Type | Score | Assessment |
|--------------------|-------|------------|
| Road quality | 3.7/7 | Below average |
| Rail quality | 1.6/7 | Very poor |
| Port quality | 2.7/7 | Poor (landlocked; refers to inland port and logistics) |
| Air transport quality | 3.6/7 | Below average |

### Logistics Performance Index

Uganda ranks 115th globally on the World Bank's Logistics Performance Index, with an infrastructure score of 2.3 out of 5. This ranking reflects the cumulative effect of poor road maintenance, limited rail capacity, and cumbersome border procedures.

## 5. Northern Corridor and Trade Facilitation

The Northern Corridor (Mombasa–Nairobi–Kampala–Kigali–DRC) is Uganda's primary trade route, handling the majority of the country's imports and exports. Key improvements include:

- **Truck turnaround time**: reduced from 21 days to fewer than 4 days through the introduction of One-Stop Border Posts (OSBPs) and the Electronic Single Window system.
- These reforms have significantly reduced trade costs and improved the predictability of supply chains, though further gains are possible through rail modernisation and digital integration of customs processes.

## 6. Industrial Parks

Uganda has established 11 industrial parks hosting 166 industries and generating over 102,000 jobs. However, none of the parks currently meet international standards in terms of infrastructure quality, utility reliability, environmental management, or service provision. Upgrading these parks to competitive standards is essential for attracting foreign direct investment and developing value-added manufacturing.

## 7. Oil and Gas Infrastructure

Uganda's oil sector is driving major infrastructure investment:

- **East African Crude Oil Pipeline (EACOP)**: a heated pipeline from Hoima to the port of Tanga (Tanzania), one of the largest infrastructure projects in East Africa.
- **Planned refinery**: a USD 4 billion oil refinery at Kabaale, Hoima, intended to process crude for domestic and regional consumption, reducing fuel import dependence.
- **Upstream projects**: the Tilenga project (operated by TotalEnergies) and the Kingfisher project (operated by CNOOC) are the primary production developments in the Albertine Graben.

## 8. Public-Private Partnerships

### Pipeline and Performance

There are 23 PPP projects in the pipeline. However, the average time to reach financial close is 5–7 years, reflecting institutional capacity constraints, lengthy procurement processes, and the complexity of bankable project structuring.

### Notable PPP Examples

- **Umeme electricity distribution concession**: reduced distribution losses from 38% to below 20% and achieved revenue collection rates of 98%, demonstrating the potential of well-structured PPPs.
- **Kampala–Jinja Expressway**: private investors are contributing 48.4% of the total USD 1.55 billion project cost, one of the largest PPP-financed road projects in East Africa.

## 9. Infrastructure Financing Gap

Uganda faces an estimated infrastructure financing gap of USD 1.4 billion annually. This gap spans roads, rail, energy, water, and digital infrastructure. Closing it will require a combination of increased domestic revenue mobilisation, concessional and commercial borrowing, PPPs, and innovative financing instruments such as infrastructure bonds and blended finance.

## 10. Reform Priorities

| Priority | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|
| Road maintenance funding | Current allocations insufficient; preventive maintenance far cheaper than reconstruction |
| Rail rehabilitation | Only 21.2% of the network operational; rail is essential for reducing freight costs |
| Urban transport and planning | Kampala congestion worsening; urban population growing at 8.8% (2014–2021); public transport systems needed |
| PPP institutional capacity | 5–7 year timelines must be shortened through standardised processes and project preparation facilities |
| Industrial park upgrading | None meet international standards; constrains FDI attraction |
| Rural access roads | Critical for agricultural market access and poverty reduction |
| Logistics corridor integration | Further digitisation and harmonisation of border procedures |

## 11. Urbanisation and Urban Infrastructure

Uganda is urbanising rapidly, with the urban population growing at 8.8% between 2014 and 2021. Kampala's daytime population reached 5.5 million in 2024, placing enormous pressure on transport, housing, and municipal services.

### Housing

The housing deficit stands at 7.3 million units, concentrated in urban and peri-urban areas. This gap drives informal settlement growth and complicates urban transport planning, as residents in unplanned areas have limited access to formal road networks and public transport.

### Air Quality

Uganda's cities average 39 µg/m³ of particulate matter, making it the 8th most polluted country globally (2024). Vehicle emissions, construction dust, and waste burning are major contributors, with direct implications for transport policy and urban planning.

### Solid Waste Management

Urban solid waste disposal remains rudimentary: open dumping and burning account for 55% of waste, dumpsites handle 36%, and engineered landfills only 9%. Poor waste management affects road conditions, drainage systems, and overall urban liveability.

## 12. SDG Pathway Context

Transport and infrastructure feature prominently in two of Uganda's SDG acceleration pathways identified in the UN Country Analysis 2025:

- **Pathway 1 — Industrialisation**: structural economic transformation requires road and power reliability upgrades to priority industrial zones, expansion of last-mile distribution networks, and modernisation of storage and cold-chain facilities to support agro-processing and manufacturing value addition.
- **Pathway 9 — Regional Integration**: corridor investments (Northern Corridor, Central Corridor), trade facilitation reforms, and standards harmonisation across EAC partner states are essential for reducing transport costs and improving Uganda's competitiveness as a landlocked economy.

## 13. Implications for Development Interventions

Proposals addressing Uganda's transport and infrastructure sector should recognise the dual challenge of new construction and maintenance of existing assets. The financing gap of USD 1.4 billion annually means that interventions must either mobilise additional resources (through PPPs, innovative finance, or improved domestic revenue) or improve the efficiency of existing spending. Rail rehabilitation offers particularly high returns given that only 21.2% of the network is operational. Urban transport in Kampala and secondary cities is an increasingly urgent priority. Any infrastructure intervention should integrate environmental and social safeguards, gender considerations (particularly women's safety in public transport), and climate resilience into design and implementation.
