# Sustainability Frameworks and Exit Strategies

Reference material for the sustainability-planning skill. Provides frameworks for ensuring project outcomes persist after external funding ends, covering institutional embedding, financial sustainability, environmental sustainability, agricultural sustainability, scaling, and M&E of sustainability. Use when writing sustainability sections, exit strategies, or standalone sustainability plans in proposals.

Sources: Hester & Harrison eds. (2005) — Pretty, Osborn, Butler, Smith chapters; Levinger & McLeod; Rogers & Coates/FANTA; Cooley & Linn (SUM Framework); Senge (1990); OECD-DAC evaluation criteria; general development practice.

---

## 1. Sustainability Concepts for Development Projects

### 1.1 Core Definition

Project sustainability means that the benefits generated by a project continue — and ideally grow — after external funding, technical assistance, and project management structures have been withdrawn. It is not about the project surviving; it is about the *results* surviving.

### 1.2 OECD-DAC Sustainability Criterion

The OECD Development Assistance Committee defines sustainability as: "The extent to which the net benefits of the intervention continue, or are likely to continue, beyond the life of the intervention." In 2019, the revised criteria added a sub-dimension: "including consideration of financial, economic, social, environmental, and institutional capacities of the systems needed to sustain net benefits over time."

Evaluators score sustainability by asking:
- Are the benefits likely to be maintained for an extended period after the project closes?
- What are the major factors influencing the achievement or non-achievement of sustainability?
- Has the project built sufficient institutional capacity, financial resources, and political support to sustain its results?

### 1.3 Sustainability vs Self-Reliance vs Resilience

| Concept | Definition | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Sustainability** | Benefits continue after external support ends | Continuity of outcomes |
| **Self-reliance** | The entity can function independently without external inputs | Independence from aid |
| **Resilience** | The entity can absorb shocks and continue to deliver benefits | Adaptability to disruption |

A sustainable project is not necessarily self-reliant (it may depend on government budget allocations), and a self-reliant entity is not necessarily resilient (it may collapse under unexpected stress). Strong proposals address all three, but sustainability is the minimum evaluators require.

### 1.4 Three Dimensions of Sustainability

**Financial sustainability** — the organisation can fund the continued operation of project-created systems, services, or processes from its own revenues or predictable funding sources.

**Institutional sustainability** — the organisation has the policies, structures, staffing, skills, and management commitment to maintain the project's results as part of its normal operations.

**Environmental sustainability** — the project's outcomes do not deplete natural resources, degrade ecosystems, or create environmental liabilities that undermine long-term benefits.

### 1.5 Sustainability Assessment: When and How

Sustainability should be assessed at four points:

1. **Design stage** — sustainability analysis informs the project design. If the project creates systems that the client cannot afford to maintain, the design is flawed.
2. **Mid-term review** — early warning assessment. Are the conditions for sustainability being created? Are counterparts being trained? Are budgets being integrated?
3. **Completion** — the project completion report assesses whether sustainability conditions are in place.
4. **Post-project evaluation** — typically 2–5 years after closure, an ex-post evaluation determines whether benefits have actually been sustained.

**Sustainability risk assessment matrix:**

| Dimension | Risk Factor | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Financial | Government budget cuts | Medium | Diversify funding; build cost recovery |
| Institutional | Staff turnover in client organisation | High | Train multiple people; document SOPs |
| Institutional | Change of political leadership | Medium | Embed in policy, not just practice |
| Environmental | Resource depletion from project activities | Low–High | Environmental management plan |
| Technical | System maintenance beyond client capacity | Medium | Simplify technology; build local IT capacity |

## 2. Exit Strategy Frameworks

### 2.1 Three Exit Strategy Types (Levinger & McLeod)

**Phase-down** — gradually reduce the scale and intensity of project activities over a defined period. The project does less of the same, allowing local actors to fill the gap incrementally.

- Best for: service delivery projects, community health programmes, agricultural extension
- Timeline: typically 6–18 months before project end
- Risk: local actors may not step up if the reduction is too rapid

**Phase-over** — transfer responsibility for project activities and assets to a local entity (government department, community organisation, NGO, or private sector actor).

- Best for: projects that have created systems, facilities, or ongoing services
- Timeline: transfer planning begins at design stage; formal transfer 3–6 months before closure
- Risk: the receiving entity lacks capacity, resources, or motivation to continue

**Phase-out** — cease all project activities and withdraw completely. The project has achieved its objective (e.g., a one-off infrastructure build, a policy reform, a completed study), and no ongoing activity is required.

- Best for: time-bound deliverables, policy advisory, research assignments
- Timeline: aligned with final deliverable submission
- Risk: if the project has created dependencies that were not recognised

### 2.2 Decision Criteria for Choosing Exit Approach

| Factor | Phase-Down | Phase-Over | Phase-Out |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ongoing activities required? | Yes, reducing | Yes, continuing | No |
| Local entity ready to take over? | Partially | Yes or nearly | Not applicable |
| Assets to transfer? | Few | Significant | None or minimal |
| Recurrent costs? | Declining | Continuing | None |
| Political/institutional support? | Moderate | Strong | Not critical |

### 2.3 Exit Planning Timeline

Exit planning is not a closure activity — it is a design activity. The most common failure in sustainability is treating exit as an afterthought.

| Project Stage | Exit Planning Activity |
|---|---|
| **Design / Inception** | Define exit strategy type; identify receiving entities; include sustainability budget lines; set sustainability indicators |
| **Year 1** | Sign MoUs with receiving entities; begin counterpart training; establish joint governance structures |
| **Mid-project** | Assess counterpart readiness; adjust training; pilot handover of selected activities; conduct sustainability risk review |
| **Penultimate phase** | Begin formal handover; reduce consultant role to mentoring; transfer assets; verify budget integration |
| **Final phase** | Complete handover; conduct joint review; establish post-project support arrangements; document lessons |
| **Post-project** | Provide defined support period (3–6 months); conduct ex-post sustainability assessment |

### 2.4 Common Exit Strategy Failures

1. **Late planning** — exit strategy written in the final quarter when it is too late to build local capacity
2. **Phantom handover** — documents are transferred but no one on the client side has been trained to use them
3. **Capacity mirage** — counterparts attended training but were never given supervised practice with real tasks
4. **Budget orphan** — recurrent costs were never integrated into the client's annual budget, so the system dies when project funds end
5. **Political dependency** — the project champion transfers, retires, or is replaced, and the new leadership has no ownership
6. **Technology mismatch** — systems require skills, connectivity, or infrastructure that the client cannot sustain
7. **Community fatigue** — community-based activities collapse because volunteerism was assumed to be permanent

**Mitigation principle:** For each failure mode, build the counter-measure into the project design, not the exit plan.

## 3. Institutional Sustainability

### 3.1 Institutional Embedding Checklist (Seven Dimensions)

Use this checklist during design and mid-term review to assess whether the conditions for institutional sustainability are being created:

| # | Dimension | Assessment Question | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Policies** | Are project activities reflected in the organisation's policies, strategies, or guidelines? | Policy document with explicit reference |
| 2 | **Budgets** | Are recurrent costs included in the organisation's approved budget? | Budget line items; MTEF inclusion |
| 3 | **Staffing** | Are permanent staff assigned to continue project functions? | Job descriptions; appointment letters |
| 4 | **Systems** | Are project systems (IT, M&E, reporting) integrated into the organisation's standard systems? | System architecture; user accounts |
| 5 | **Culture** | Do staff see the project's activities as part of their core work, not as an externally imposed add-on? | Staff interviews; behaviour observation |
| 6 | **Legal framework** | Is there a legal or regulatory basis for the activities to continue? | Legislation; regulations; directives |
| 7 | **Political will** | Do senior leaders actively support the continuation of project activities? | Statements; resource allocation decisions |

**Scoring:** Rate each dimension as Red (not in place), Amber (partially in place), or Green (fully in place). Any Red dimension at mid-term requires an urgent remediation plan.

### 3.2 Ownership Transfer Mechanisms

**Government absorption** — the government formally takes over project functions, staff (where applicable), and assets. This is the most common mechanism for donor-funded projects.

Requirements:
- Formal government decision (cabinet paper, ministerial directive, or local government resolution)
- Budget allocation in the Medium-Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF)
- Staff positions created or reassigned
- Assets registered on government asset register

**Community management** — a community-based organisation (CBO), water user committee, farmer group, or similar body takes over management of project assets or services.

Requirements:
- Legal registration of the community entity
- Governance structure (constitution, elected leadership, financial management)
- Revenue generation mechanism (user fees, contributions, enterprise)
- Technical capacity for operations and maintenance
- External oversight or accountability mechanism

**Private sector transition** — a private company or social enterprise takes over service delivery, often through a concession, management contract, or outright transfer.

Requirements:
- Viable business model demonstrating profitability or cost recovery
- Contractual framework (service level agreement, licence, concession)
- Regulatory oversight to protect public interest
- Consumer protection mechanisms

### 3.3 Capacity Building for Sustainability

Sustainability requires capacity that outlives the project. The critical link is between the sustainability-planning skill and the capacity-building skill. Key principles:

- **Train multiple people** — never create single points of failure. A minimum of three people per critical function.
- **Training of Trainers (ToT)** — the client can train its own new staff without calling the consultant back.
- **Supervised practice** — training is not complete until the trainee has performed the task independently and had their work quality-checked.
- **Documentation** — standard operating procedures (SOPs), user manuals, and quick-reference guides that a new staff member can use for self-learning.
- **Mentoring period** — a defined period where the consultant is available for questions but does not perform the work.

### 3.4 Governance Structures for Post-Project Continuity

Projects often create governance structures (steering committees, technical working groups, coordination platforms) that dissolve when the project ends. For sustainability:

- Embed the function in an existing governance structure rather than creating a new one
- If a new structure is necessary, secure a formal mandate (terms of reference approved by the parent body)
- Ensure the structure has a budget line, a secretariat function, and a reporting obligation
- Transition the chair from the project to a permanent institutional leader early in the project

### 3.5 Systems Thinking for Institutional Sustainability (Senge)

Peter Senge's five disciplines of the learning organisation provide a foundation for institutional sustainability:

1. **Systems thinking** — see the whole system, not just the project. Understand how project activities interact with the organisation's existing processes, incentives, and power dynamics. A project that ignores the system will be rejected by it.
2. **Personal mastery** — individuals in the client organisation must develop genuine competence and commitment, not just compliance.
3. **Mental models** — surface and challenge assumptions about how things work. If the client believes "things always fall apart after the consultants leave," that belief becomes self-fulfilling unless actively addressed.
4. **Shared vision** — the client organisation must own the vision for the future state, not merely accept the consultant's vision.
5. **Team learning** — the client's teams must learn to learn together, building collective capability that survives individual departures.

**Proposal application:** Reference systems thinking when explaining why your sustainability approach addresses root causes (incentives, structures, culture) rather than symptoms (training, documentation).

## 4. Financial Sustainability

### 4.1 The Three Critical Sustainability Factors (Rogers & Coates / FANTA)

Rogers and Coates identify three factors that must all be present for sustainability:

1. **Sustained resources** — predictable, adequate funding from sources the entity controls or can reliably access
2. **Sustained capacity** — technical, managerial, and administrative ability to continue delivering results
3. **Sustained motivation** — political will, institutional commitment, and individual incentive to continue

If any one factor is absent, sustainability fails. Financial sustainability (sustained resources) is necessary but not sufficient.

### 4.2 Revenue Generation Models

| Model | Description | Best For | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **User fees** | Beneficiaries pay for services | Water supply, health services, IT systems | Excludes the poorest; political resistance |
| **Cost recovery** | Fees cover operating costs but not capital | Infrastructure, utilities | Insufficient for replacement/expansion |
| **Full cost pricing** | Fees cover all costs including depreciation and capital replacement | Commercial services | Market resistance; requires willingness to pay |
| **Social enterprise** | Revenue-generating activity cross-subsidises public benefit | Community development, agriculture | Requires business management skills |
| **Endowment / trust fund** | Investment income funds ongoing activities | Conservation, scholarships | Requires large initial capital; market risk |
| **Government budget** | Recurrent costs absorbed into government budget | Public sector projects | Subject to budget cuts; competing priorities |
| **Donor transition** | Project costs transferred to another donor programme | Multi-donor environments | Creates new dependency; unpredictable |

### 4.3 Budget Integration

The most reliable financial sustainability mechanism for government-funded projects is mainstreaming project costs into the government's recurrent budget. Steps:

1. **Identify recurrent costs** — distinguish one-off project costs from ongoing operational costs (staff, consumables, maintenance, licences, connectivity)
2. **Quantify annual requirements** — produce a clear annual cost estimate for post-project operations
3. **Engage the budget process early** — government budgets are planned 12–18 months in advance. If you want costs in next year's budget, engage this year's planning cycle
4. **Secure MTEF inclusion** — in Uganda and most East African countries, the Medium-Term Expenditure Framework is the binding multi-year budget. Inclusion in the MTEF is stronger than inclusion in a single annual budget
5. **Track budget execution** — inclusion in the budget does not guarantee disbursement. Monitor whether allocated funds are actually released and spent

### 4.4 Phased Financing Reduction Plans

Design a financing schedule that gradually shifts the cost burden from the project to the client:

| Year | Project Funds (%) | Client Funds (%) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | 100 | 0 | Full project financing |
| Year 2 | 75 | 25 | Client covers selected recurrent costs |
| Year 3 | 50 | 50 | Joint financing; client covers all staff costs |
| Year 4 | 25 | 75 | Project covers only technical assistance |
| Year 5 (post-project) | 0 | 100 | Full client financing |

This schedule must be agreed with the client at design stage and tracked through the project's financial monitoring system.

### 4.5 Cost-Benefit Analysis for Sustainability Decisions

When a client questions whether it is worth maintaining a project's outputs, provide a cost-benefit analysis:

- **Costs of continuation** — annual recurrent costs (staff, operations, maintenance, consumables)
- **Costs of abandonment** — loss of investment, cost of restarting later, reputational damage, loss of beneficiary trust
- **Benefits of continuation** — continued service delivery, ongoing efficiency gains, accumulated returns
- **Decision rule** — if the net present value of continuation benefits exceeds the net present value of continuation costs, sustainability is financially rational

### 4.6 Public-Private Partnerships for Service Continuity

Where government capacity or budget is insufficient for sustainability, a PPP model can bridge the gap:

- **Management contract** — private operator manages a government facility for a fee (water treatment plants, hospitals, IT systems)
- **Concession** — private operator invests and operates for a defined period, recovering costs from user fees
- **Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT)** — private sector builds infrastructure, operates it for a return period, then transfers ownership to government
- **Service contract** — government contracts specific maintenance or support functions to the private sector

**East African context:** PPP frameworks exist in Uganda (PPP Act 2015), Kenya (PPP Act 2013), Rwanda (PPP Policy 2016), and Tanzania (PPP Act 2010). Reference the relevant national framework in proposals.

## 5. Environmental Sustainability in Projects

### 5.1 Pretty's Resource-Conserving Agriculture Approaches

Jules Pretty's analysis of 286 projects across 57 developing countries demonstrated that resource-conserving agricultural approaches increased yields by an average of 79% while improving environmental outcomes. The approaches that delivered these results:

- **Integrated Pest Management (IPM)** — biological control, habitat management, resistant varieties, and targeted pesticide use only as a last resort. Reduces pesticide costs by 50–100% while maintaining or improving yields.
- **Conservation agriculture** — minimum tillage, permanent soil cover, crop rotation. Reduces soil erosion, improves water retention, builds soil organic matter.
- **Integrated nutrient management** — combining organic (compost, green manure, crop residues) and inorganic fertilisers to optimise nutrient availability while maintaining soil health.
- **Agroforestry** — integrating trees with crops and/or livestock. Provides shade, nitrogen fixation, erosion control, fuel wood, and diversified income.
- **Water harvesting and conservation** — micro-catchments, contour bunds, terracing, drip irrigation. Improves water use efficiency in rain-fed systems.
- **Livestock integration** — combining crop and livestock systems for nutrient cycling, draught power, and income diversification.

**Key finding for proposals:** Sustainable agriculture is not a trade-off between environment and productivity. The evidence shows that resource-conserving approaches can *increase* yields, particularly in developing country smallholder systems where conventional inputs are underused or misused.

### 5.2 Osborn's Five Principles for Sustainable Land Management

1. **Maintain or enhance soil quality** — prevent erosion, maintain organic matter, avoid compaction, manage pH and nutrient balance
2. **Maintain or enhance water quality and supply** — prevent contamination of water courses, manage irrigation efficiently, protect catchments
3. **Maintain or enhance biodiversity** — protect habitats, maintain crop genetic diversity, support pollinators and natural pest predators
4. **Use inputs efficiently** — maximise output per unit of fertiliser, water, energy, and labour; reduce waste
5. **Minimise pollution** — reduce pesticide run-off, manage livestock waste, control greenhouse gas emissions from agricultural activities

**Proposal application:** Use these five principles as a checklist when designing environmental sustainability components for agriculture, natural resource management, or rural development projects.

### 5.3 Climate-Smart Agriculture

Climate-smart agriculture (CSA) has three pillars:

1. **Sustainably increase productivity and incomes** — food security and livelihoods
2. **Adapt and build resilience to climate change** — drought-resistant varieties, diversification, early warning systems
3. **Reduce or remove greenhouse gas emissions** — reduced tillage, methane management, carbon sequestration in soils and biomass

CSA is now a standard requirement in most donor-funded agricultural projects. Reference the FAO CSA Sourcebook and the World Bank's CSA Country Profiles when designing agricultural sustainability components.

### 5.4 Natural Resource Management for Sustainability

Projects that depend on natural resources must ensure that those resources are not depleted by project activities:

- **Sustainable yield principle** — extraction rates must not exceed regeneration rates (fisheries, forests, groundwater)
- **Carrying capacity** — livestock numbers, visitor numbers, or extraction volumes must remain within the ecosystem's carrying capacity
- **Environmental Management Plan (EMP)** — every project with environmental risk should have an EMP with specific mitigation measures, monitoring indicators, and responsible parties
- **Environmental auditing** — periodic environmental audits during implementation to verify compliance with the EMP

### 5.5 Environmental Sustainability Indicators

| Category | Example Indicators |
|---|---|
| Soil | Soil organic carbon (%), erosion rate (tonnes/ha/yr), pH |
| Water | Water use efficiency (kg/m³), nitrate concentration (mg/l), aquifer recharge rate |
| Biodiversity | Species count, habitat area (ha), pollinator diversity index |
| Climate | GHG emissions (tCO₂e), carbon sequestration (tC/ha/yr), energy use (MJ/unit output) |
| Pollution | Pesticide residue levels, BOD in water courses, particulate matter |
| Resource use | Input use efficiency ratios, waste generation (kg/unit), recycling rate (%) |

## 6. Agricultural Sustainability Specific

### 6.1 Environmental Costs of Conventional Agriculture

Pretty documents the hidden costs that conventional agriculture externalises:

- **Pesticide externalities** — health costs (acute poisoning, chronic illness), water contamination, biodiversity loss, pest resistance requiring ever-stronger chemicals. Estimated external costs of pesticide use in the UK alone: GBP 120 million per year (at time of study).
- **Soil degradation** — erosion, compaction, loss of organic matter, salinisation. Global soil erosion estimated at 25 billion tonnes per year, with replacement costs far exceeding prevention costs.
- **Water contamination** — nutrient run-off causing eutrophication, pesticide contamination of drinking water, salinisation of irrigated lands. Water treatment costs borne by water utilities and consumers, not by the polluting farms.
- **Greenhouse gas emissions** — agriculture contributes approximately 10–12% of global GHG emissions (methane from livestock, nitrous oxide from fertilisers, CO₂ from land use change).
- **Biodiversity loss** — monoculture, habitat destruction, pesticide impact on non-target species. Loss of natural pest predators creates a vicious cycle requiring more chemical inputs.

**Proposal application:** When proposing sustainable agriculture interventions, quantify the externalities of the status quo. This makes the economic case for resource-conserving approaches compelling even before considering yield benefits.

### 6.2 Sustainable Intensification Approaches

Sustainable intensification means producing more food from the same land area while reducing environmental impact. Key approaches:

**Integrated Pest Management (IPM):**
- Pest monitoring and economic threshold decision-making
- Biological control agents (parasitoids, predators, pathogens)
- Cultural practices (crop rotation, intercropping, resistant varieties)
- Chemical control only when thresholds are exceeded, using the least toxic effective option

**Conservation agriculture (CA):**
- Three principles: minimum soil disturbance, permanent organic soil cover, crop diversification
- Reduces labour requirements (no ploughing), conserves moisture, builds soil health over time
- Requires 3–5 years for full benefits; short-term yield may be maintained rather than increased

**Agroforestry systems:**
- Alley cropping, boundary planting, improved fallows, fodder banks
- Nitrogen-fixing trees (Sesbania, Gliricidia, Calliandra) reduce fertiliser costs
- Timber and fruit trees provide diversified income and climate resilience

**Integrated nutrient management:**
- Soil testing to determine actual nutrient needs (not blanket recommendations)
- Organic matter management (composting, green manure, crop residue retention)
- Targeted inorganic fertiliser application to complement organic sources
- Micro-dosing techniques for resource-poor farmers

### 6.3 Farmer Field Schools and Participatory Extension

Farmer Field Schools (FFS) are a proven model for sustainable agricultural knowledge transfer:

- Groups of 20–25 farmers meet weekly for a full growing season
- Learning by doing: farmers conduct experiments in their own fields
- Facilitator guides discovery rather than lecturing
- Topics include IPM, soil management, crop husbandry, and record keeping
- Graduates become community trainers (ToT model), creating a self-sustaining extension system

**Sustainability advantage:** FFS build farmer capacity to observe, analyse, and make decisions — not just to follow instructions. This makes the knowledge adaptive and sustainable because farmers can respond to new problems using the analytical skills they have learned.

### 6.4 Fair Trade vs Free Trade Impacts on Developing Country Agriculture

Butler and Smith (in Hester & Harrison) document the sustainability risks that trade liberalisation poses for developing country agriculture:

**Moral hazards of free trade:**
- Dumping — developed country surpluses sold below production cost, destroying local markets
- Subsidies — OECD agricultural subsidies (approximately USD 300 billion per year at time of study) create an uneven playing field
- Market access restrictions — tariff escalation penalises processed goods from developing countries

**Physical hazards:**
- Disease spread through increased movement of livestock and plant material
- Food safety risks from complex global supply chains
- Contamination traceability challenges

**Environmental hazards:**
- Monoculture expansion to serve export markets, reducing biodiversity
- Deforestation for export crop production
- Environmental costs externalised in price-competitive markets

**Smith's sugar case study (Southern Africa):**
- EU sugar subsidies depressed world prices, damaging competitive producers in Mozambique, Malawi, Zambia, and Swaziland
- Partial reform (Everything But Arms initiative) provided temporary relief but with complex rules of origin
- Illustrates how trade policy far away can undermine agricultural sustainability in Africa

**Proposal application:** When designing agricultural sustainability components, consider the trade policy environment. Sustainable farming practices alone cannot ensure sustainability if farmers face unfair competition from subsidised imports.

### 6.5 Value Chain Sustainability

Agricultural sustainability extends beyond the farm gate:

- **Input supply** — reliable, affordable access to quality seeds, fertilisers, and equipment
- **Production** — sustainable farming practices (sections 6.1–6.3 above)
- **Post-harvest handling** — storage, processing, and transport to reduce losses (in Uganda, post-harvest losses reach 40% for grains, 30–80% for fruits and vegetables)
- **Market access** — aggregation, quality standards, market information, contract farming
- **Finance** — agricultural credit, crop insurance, warehouse receipt systems
- **Policy environment** — trade policy, subsidies, regulations, land tenure

Sustainability planning for agricultural projects must address the weakest link in the value chain, not just the production stage.

### 6.6 Food Security and Nutrition Sustainability

Projects targeting food security must plan for sustainability across four dimensions:

1. **Availability** — sustained production or supply of sufficient food
2. **Access** — sustained economic and physical access to food (income, markets, infrastructure)
3. **Utilisation** — sustained capacity to prepare and consume nutritious diets (knowledge, water, sanitation, health)
4. **Stability** — resilience of the food system to shocks (climate, conflict, price volatility)

## 7. Scaling Up Frameworks

### 7.1 The SUM Framework (Cooley & Linn)

The Scaling Up Management (SUM) Framework provides a structured approach to taking successful pilot projects to scale:

**Four components:**

1. **Drivers of scaling** — the forces that push or pull an innovation to scale:
   - Vision and leadership (a champion who drives the agenda)
   - External catalysts (policy window, crisis, donor priority)
   - Incentives and accountability (rewards for adoption, consequences for non-adoption)
   - Evidence of effectiveness (rigorous evaluation results)

2. **Spaces for scaling** — the dimensions in which scaling occurs:
   - Fiscal/financial space — funding available for scale-up
   - Policy space — enabling policy environment
   - Institutional/organisational space — capacity to manage at scale
   - Political space — political support and absence of opposition
   - Cultural space — social and cultural acceptability
   - Partnership space — alliances that support scaling

3. **Scaling pathways:**
   - **Horizontal (replication)** — expanding the same intervention to new geographic areas or populations
   - **Vertical (institutionalisation)** — embedding the innovation in policy, law, regulation, or institutional systems
   - **Functional (diversification)** — adding complementary activities to the original innovation

4. **Monitoring and evaluation of scaling** — tracking not just the intervention's impact but the scaling process itself (adoption rates, adaptation fidelity, cost per unit at scale)

### 7.2 Conditions for Successful Scaling

| Condition | Description | Assessment Method |
|---|---|---|
| **Political support** | Leaders at relevant levels champion the intervention | Stakeholder analysis; political economy assessment |
| **Institutional capacity** | Organisations at scale have the staff, systems, and resources to implement | Capacity assessment; organisational analysis |
| **Financing** | Sustainable funding exists or can be mobilised at scale | Financial analysis; fiscal space assessment |
| **Simplicity** | The intervention is simple enough to implement at scale without specialised expertise | Complexity assessment; implementation fidelity analysis |
| **Adaptability** | The intervention can be adapted to diverse contexts without losing effectiveness | Variation analysis; core components identification |
| **Evidence** | Rigorous evidence of effectiveness exists to justify scale-up investment | Systematic review; impact evaluation |
| **Demand** | Beneficiaries want the intervention and will participate | Demand assessment; willingness-to-pay studies |

### 7.3 Monitoring Scaling Progress

| Indicator Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Adoption** | Number of sites/districts/organisations implementing; coverage rate |
| **Fidelity** | Percentage of core components implemented correctly; adaptation vs dilution |
| **Quality** | Outcome measures at scale vs pilot; beneficiary satisfaction |
| **Cost** | Unit cost at scale vs pilot; cost-effectiveness at scale |
| **Sustainability** | Percentage of sites still operating 1/2/3 years after initial support |
| **Equity** | Distribution of benefits across population groups; reaching the last mile |

## 8. M&E of Sustainability

### 8.1 Sustainability Indicators by Type

**Institutional sustainability indicators:**
- Number of permanent staff assigned to project functions (target: all functions covered)
- SOPs approved and in use (target: 100% of critical processes documented)
- Functions included in organisational strategic plan (yes/no)
- Budget lines for project functions in approved annual budget (yes/no)
- Governance structure meeting regularly post-project (frequency; attendance)

**Financial sustainability indicators:**
- Recurrent cost recovery rate (revenue / annual operating cost, target: ≥100%)
- Budget execution rate for project functions (disbursed / allocated, target: ≥80%)
- Diversification of funding sources (number of funding sources; concentration risk)
- Financial reserves (months of operating costs covered)

**Environmental sustainability indicators:**
- See Section 5.5 above for detailed environmental indicators
- Compliance rate with Environmental Management Plan measures
- Environmental audit findings (number and severity of non-conformances)

**Social sustainability indicators:**
- Beneficiary satisfaction with services (survey score; trend)
- Equity of access (disaggregated by gender, age, disability, location)
- Community participation rate in governance/management structures
- Complaints and feedback mechanism functioning (yes/no; response rate)

### 8.2 Post-Project Monitoring Approaches

- **Ex-post evaluation** — a formal evaluation 2–5 years after closure, typically commissioned by the donor. Assesses whether benefits have been sustained and identifies factors contributing to or hindering sustainability.
- **Tracer studies** — follow up with individual beneficiaries (e.g., training graduates) to assess whether they are applying skills and experiencing sustained benefits.
- **Routine monitoring by successor entity** — if the project has been phased over, the receiving entity should continue collecting key indicators. Build this into the handover plan.
- **Spot checks** — periodic light-touch visits by the former implementing organisation to verify that systems are still functioning and offer advice.

### 8.3 Completion and Sustainability Audits

A sustainability audit at project completion assesses readiness for independent operation. Structure:

1. **Financial readiness** — are recurrent costs budgeted and funded?
2. **Institutional readiness** — are staff in place, trained, and performing?
3. **Technical readiness** — are systems functioning, maintained, and documented?
4. **Governance readiness** — are oversight structures functioning?
5. **Risk readiness** — have sustainability risks been identified and mitigated?

**Traffic light scoring:** Green (ready), Amber (partially ready — needs attention in the remaining project period), Red (not ready — sustainability at risk).

### 8.4 Lessons Learned Documentation for Sustainability

Every project should document sustainability lessons for future projects:

- What worked well in building ownership and capacity?
- What sustainability risks materialised, and how were they managed?
- What would you do differently in the project design to improve sustainability?
- What external factors affected sustainability (positively or negatively)?
- What is the likely trajectory of the project's benefits over the next 3–5 years?

## 9. Proposal Strategy Notes

### 9.1 When to Include Sustainability and Exit Planning

**Always include sustainability in every proposal.** Evaluators expect it. Even for short-term advisory assignments, a paragraph explaining how the advice will be embedded in the client's operations demonstrates professionalism.

**Include a dedicated sustainability/exit section when:**
- The ToR explicitly requests it
- The project is longer than six months
- The project creates systems, processes, or institutional changes
- The project is donor-funded (donors weight sustainability heavily in evaluation)
- The assignment involves community-level interventions

### 9.2 Standard Sustainability Section Structure for Proposals

1. **Sustainability approach** — state the overall philosophy (2–3 sentences)
2. **Institutional sustainability measures** — how you will embed results in the client's organisation
3. **Capacity building for sustainability** — training, ToT, mentoring, documentation (cross-reference the capacity building section of the methodology)
4. **Financial sustainability** — how recurrent costs will be covered post-project
5. **Exit strategy** — which type (phase-down/over/out) and the handover timeline
6. **Post-project support** — what you offer after formal closure

### 9.3 Sustainability Budget Components

Include these in the financial proposal:

| Item | Description | Typical Allocation |
|---|---|---|
| **Counterpart training** | ToT, mentoring, supervised practice | 5–10% of total budget |
| **Documentation** | SOPs, manuals, guides | 2–3% of total budget |
| **Handover activities** | Joint delivery, supervised handover, asset transfer | 3–5% of total budget |
| **Post-project support** | Helpdesk, remote support, check-in visits | 2–4% of total budget |
| **Sustainability assessment** | Mid-term and completion sustainability audits | 1–2% of total budget |

**Total sustainability investment:** typically 13–24% of the total budget. This may seem high, but proposals that budget adequately for sustainability demonstrate seriousness. Donors increasingly penalise proposals that allocate no resources to sustainability.

### 9.4 Linking Sustainability to Donor Evaluation Criteria

| Donor | How Sustainability Is Evaluated |
|---|---|
| **World Bank** | Rated in ICR (Implementation Completion and Results Report); scored on "Risk to Development Outcome" |
| **AfDB** | Rated in PCR (Project Completion Report); sustainability is one of four main rating dimensions |
| **UNDP** | Evaluated against OECD-DAC criteria including sustainability; UNDP IEO follows up at 2+ years |
| **PPDA Uganda** | Technical evaluation may score sustainability approach as part of methodology (typically 5–10 marks) |
| **EU** | EuropeAid evaluations include sustainability as a mandatory criterion; ex-post evaluations at 3 years |
| **DFID/FCDO** | Annual Reviews and Project Completion Reviews both assess sustainability; VfM framework includes sustainability |

### 9.5 Common Sustainability Commitments in Methodology Sections

Use these as standard building blocks, adapting to each assignment:

- "We will commence exit planning during the inception phase, not at project closure."
- "All activities will be delivered jointly with client counterparts from [month], transitioning to supervised independent delivery by [month]."
- "A Training of Trainers programme will ensure that the client can train new staff without external support."
- "We will work with the [Finance/Planning Department] to integrate recurrent costs into the [year] MTEF submission."
- "A sustainability audit at [milestone] will assess readiness for independent operation against seven dimensions (policies, budgets, staffing, systems, culture, legal framework, political will)."
- "Our post-project support package includes [3/6] months of remote technical assistance at no additional cost."
- "All deliverables will be accompanied by standard operating procedures and user guides designed for self-learning by future staff."

**Persuasion note:** The strongest sustainability argument is not a promise — it is a mechanism. Do not say "we will ensure sustainability." Instead, describe the specific mechanisms (ToT, budget integration, SOP documentation, governance structures) that make sustainability likely. Evaluators trust mechanisms; they distrust promises.
