# Premium Writing Quality Gate

Use this gate before releasing premium proposals, executive writing, case studies, public documents, and commercial articles.

## Strategic Fit

- The opening tells the reader what decision or problem is being addressed.
- The governing idea is visible in one sentence.
- The document uses the client's or reader's real context, not generic market language.
- The offer is positioned around value, risk reduction, control, revenue, compliance, adoption, or service quality.

## Proof Discipline

Every important claim should pass this chain:

| Step | Test |
|---|---|
| Claim | What are we asking the reader to believe? |
| Mechanism | How does the method, team, system, or process create the result? |
| Evidence | What example, number, artefact, benchmark, or credential supports it? |
| Control | What governance, QA, review, risk, or support protects delivery? |
| Implication | Why does this matter for the reader's decision? |

If any step is missing, revise before adding persuasive language.

## Premium Fee Worthiness

- The writing explains what premium work includes: diagnosis, senior judgement, design, review, QA, documentation, support, and measurement.
- Price is linked to value protected or created, not to prestige.
- Lower-cost alternatives are compared through trade-offs: scope, assurance, timeline, support, adoption, and total cost of ownership.
- Commercial options preserve quality boundaries and state what changes between options.

## Evaluator Friendliness

- Headings allow a busy evaluator to understand the argument by scanning.
- Proof appears close to the claim it supports.
- Tables summarise comparisons, phases, deliverables, risks, and options where they reduce reading effort.
- Compliance language is present where required, but it does not bury the value story.
- Risks and assumptions are named early enough to build trust.

## Executive Polish

- Sentences are direct and concrete.
- Paragraphs carry one idea and usually stay under four sentences.
- The writer removes throat-clearing openings, self-congratulation, and vague capability claims.
- The tone is calm, senior, and respectful.
- The close gives a clear next step or decision rationale.

## Red Flags

- "We are uniquely positioned" without proof.
- "Extensive experience" without named examples or outcomes.
- A methodology that lists activities but not decisions, artefacts, or controls.
- A case study that describes work but not relevance to the current buyer.
- An article that chases keywords but does not answer the reader's question.
- Premium pricing language that sounds defensive, apologetic, or inflated.

## Final Pass

Before delivery, ask:

- Would a sceptical evaluator know why this option is worth paying for?
- Can procurement defend the choice internally?
- Does the writing reduce perceived delivery risk?
- Are the strongest facts easy to find?
- Is every promise achievable under the team, timeline, data, approvals, and budget?
