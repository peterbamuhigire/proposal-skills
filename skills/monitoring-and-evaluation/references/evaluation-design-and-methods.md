# Evaluation Design, Methods, and Data Collection

Reference material for the monitoring-and-evaluation skill. Provides detailed guidance on evaluation types, research methods (qualitative, quantitative, and mixed), data collection approaches, sampling, and evaluation quality standards. Use when designing evaluation components for proposals or when the ToR requires a standalone evaluation.

Sources: UNDP Handbook (2009), Seasons (2020), Civicus/Shapiro, IFRC Handbook (2002), ILO Basic Principles.

---

## 1. Evaluation Types and Purposes

### By Timing

| Type | When | Purpose | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Formative (mid-term)** | During implementation | Improve the programme while it is still running | Is the programme on track? What needs adjusting? |
| **Summative (final/end-of-project)** | At completion | Judge overall success and accountability | Did the programme achieve its objectives? Was it worth the investment? |
| **Ex-post** | 1–5 years after completion | Assess sustainability and long-term impact | Are the results sustained? What lasting change occurred? |
| **Real-time** | Ongoing during implementation | Rapid feedback for adaptive management (especially in emergencies) | What is happening right now? What needs immediate attention? |

### By Approach (Civicus/Shapiro)

| Approach | Purpose | Focus Questions | Methodology |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Goal-based** | Assess achievement of goals and objectives | Were goals achieved? Efficiently? Were they the right goals? | Comparing baseline and progress data |
| **Decision-making** | Provide information for decisions | Is the project effective? Should it continue? How might it be modified? | Assessing options related to context, inputs, process, product |
| **Goal-free** | Assess the full range of effects, intended and unintended | What are all the outcomes? What value do they have? | Independent determination of needs and standards; qualitative and quantitative techniques |
| **Expert judgement** | Use of expertise | How does a professional rate this project? | Critical review based on experience, informal surveying, subjective insights |

**Best practice (Civicus):** Combine approaches. The best evaluators use goal-based assessment as the foundation but remain open to goal-free findings (unintended consequences) and expert judgement (contextual interpretation).

### OECD-DAC Evaluation Criteria

The international standard for evaluation, used by all major donors. Every evaluation should address these six criteria:

| Criterion | Question |
|---|---|
| **Relevance** | Is the intervention doing the right things? Does it address the needs and priorities of beneficiaries and the country context? |
| **Coherence** | How well does the intervention fit with other interventions in the same context? Is there complementarity or duplication? |
| **Effectiveness** | Is the intervention achieving its objectives? To what extent are outcomes being realised? |
| **Efficiency** | How well are resources being used? Could the same results be achieved with fewer resources, or more results with the same resources? |
| **Impact** | What difference does the intervention make? What are the broader effects — positive and negative, intended and unintended? |
| **Sustainability** | Will the benefits last? What is the likelihood that outcomes will continue after the intervention ends? |

**Proposal application:** When proposing evaluation activities, explicitly state which OECD-DAC criteria the evaluation will address. This signals international evaluation literacy and is expected in World Bank, UNDP, and bilateral donor proposals.

## 2. Evaluation Design

### The Four-Step Process Model (Seasons)

**Step 1 — Establish Purpose, Participants, Management**
- Define the evaluation's purpose (accountability, learning, improvement, or all three)
- Identify the primary intended users (who will use the findings and for what decisions)
- Determine whether the evaluation will be internal, external, or mixed
- Establish an evaluation advisory committee for quality assurance
- Address ethical considerations (informed consent, confidentiality, do no harm)

**Step 2 — Develop Evaluation Design Foundations**
- Identify evaluation questions (driven by the information needs of intended users)
- Select the evaluation approach (goal-based, goal-free, or mixed)
- Determine the appropriate methodology (qualitative, quantitative, or mixed methods)
- Design the data collection strategy (instruments, sampling, timeline)
- Define the analysis approach (statistical, thematic, comparative)

**Step 3 — Conduct Evaluation and Report Results**
- Collect data according to the design
- Analyse data using the planned methods
- Triangulate findings across multiple data sources and methods
- Prepare the evaluation report with findings, conclusions, and recommendations
- Present findings to stakeholders and facilitate discussion

**Step 4 — Sustain Plan Monitoring and Evaluation**
- Ensure evaluation findings feed into decision-making (management response)
- Track implementation of evaluation recommendations
- Document lessons learned for future programming
- Build institutional capacity for ongoing M&E

### Evaluation Matrix

A planning tool that links evaluation questions to data sources, methods, and indicators:

| Evaluation Question | Indicator(s) | Data Source | Collection Method | Analysis Method |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| To what extent has the system improved processing times? | Average processing time (before/after) | System logs, user surveys | Data extraction, survey | Statistical comparison |
| How satisfied are users with the new process? | User satisfaction score | User survey | Structured questionnaire | Descriptive statistics, disaggregation |
| What unintended effects have occurred? | Emergent themes | Key informant interviews | Semi-structured interview | Thematic analysis |

**Proposal application:** Include an evaluation matrix in your proposal to demonstrate that you have thought through how each evaluation question will be answered. This is a differentiator — most competitors list evaluation questions but do not map them to methods.

## 3. Impact Evaluation Designs

Impact evaluation answers the counterfactual question: "What would have happened without the intervention?" This is the most rigorous form of evaluation and is increasingly required for large donor-funded programmes.

### Experimental Design (Randomised Controlled Trial)

- Randomly assign eligible participants to a treatment group (receives the intervention) and a control group (does not)
- Compare outcomes between the two groups at endline
- Any difference can be attributed to the intervention (if the randomisation was properly implemented)
- **Gold standard** for causal attribution but ethically and practically challenging in many development contexts

### Quasi-Experimental Designs

When randomisation is not possible, use quasi-experimental methods to approximate a counterfactual:

| Design | Method | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Difference-in-differences** | Compare changes over time between a treatment group and a comparison group | When pre-intervention data exists for both groups |
| **Propensity score matching** | Statistically match treatment and comparison individuals based on observable characteristics | When a comparable untreated group can be identified |
| **Regression discontinuity** | Compare individuals just above and just below an eligibility threshold | When programme eligibility is determined by a clear cutoff |
| **Interrupted time series** | Analyse trends in outcomes before and after the intervention | When long-term time series data is available |

### Non-Experimental Designs

When neither experimental nor quasi-experimental approaches are feasible:

| Design | Method | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| **Before-after comparison** | Compare outcomes before and after the intervention | Cannot attribute change to the intervention (other factors may be responsible) |
| **Cross-sectional comparison** | Compare treatment and non-treatment groups at a single point in time | Selection bias — groups may differ in unobserved ways |
| **Contribution analysis** | Build a plausible case that the intervention contributed to observed changes using multiple evidence sources | Cannot prove causation, only plausible contribution |
| **Most Significant Change** | Collect and analyse stories of change from beneficiaries and stakeholders | Subjective; not generalisable |
| **Outcome harvesting** | Identify outcomes first, then work backwards to determine the intervention's contribution | Does not require pre-defined outcomes; good for complex programmes |

**Proposal application:** When the ToR mentions "impact evaluation," specify which design you will use and why. If a full RCT is not feasible (as is often the case in consulting assignments), propose a quasi-experimental or contribution analysis approach and explain the rationale. This demonstrates methodological sophistication.

## 4. Research Methods

### Quantitative Methods (Seasons)

Methods that produce numerical data for statistical analysis:

| Method | Application | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Structured surveys** | Measure prevalence, attitudes, knowledge across a population | Generalisable if properly sampled; comparable over time | Cannot explain "why"; response bias |
| **Administrative data analysis** | Analyse routine data from systems (MIS, registers, databases) | Low cost; large sample; ongoing | Quality depends on data entry; may not capture what you need |
| **Cost-benefit analysis** | Compare total costs with total benefits in monetary terms | Clear decision criterion (benefit > cost = worthwhile) | Difficult to monetise all benefits |
| **Cost-effectiveness analysis** | Compare costs with non-monetary outcomes (e.g., cost per life saved) | Avoids need to monetise outcomes | Cannot compare across different outcome types |
| **Statistical modelling** | Identify relationships between variables and predict outcomes | Controls for confounding factors | Requires statistical expertise; results depend on model specification |

### Qualitative Methods (Seasons)

Methods that produce descriptive, interpretive data:

| Method | Application | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Key informant interviews** | In-depth understanding from experts and stakeholders | Rich, detailed data; explores "why" and "how" | Not generalisable; interviewer bias |
| **Focus group discussions** | Explore group perspectives, norms, and dynamics | Reveals shared understandings and disagreements | Group dynamics may suppress minority views |
| **Case studies** | Deep analysis of specific instances of change | Contextual richness; reveals causal mechanisms | Not generalisable; selection bias |
| **Direct observation** | Verify processes, behaviours, and conditions | First-hand evidence; reduces self-report bias | Observer effect; limited coverage |
| **Document and content analysis** | Analyse policies, reports, media, and other texts | Non-reactive; reveals institutional positions | May not reflect actual practice |
| **Participatory methods** | Engage beneficiaries as co-researchers (participatory mapping, scoring, ranking) | Empowering; captures local knowledge | Time-intensive; may not meet donor rigour requirements |

### Mixed Methods (Seasons)

The most powerful approach combines quantitative and qualitative methods:

- **Concurrent** — collect quantitative and qualitative data at the same time; compare and integrate findings
- **Sequential explanatory** — collect quantitative data first, then use qualitative methods to explain the patterns found
- **Sequential exploratory** — collect qualitative data first to identify themes, then test them with quantitative data

**Proposal application:** Always propose mixed methods unless the ToR explicitly restricts the methodology. A survey alone tells you *what* happened; interviews explain *why*. Evaluators recognise mixed methods as methodologically stronger than either alone.

## 5. Sampling for Data Collection

### Probability Sampling (Statistical Representativeness)

| Method | When to Use | How |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple random** | Population is homogeneous; complete sampling frame available | Random selection from the full list |
| **Stratified random** | Population has distinct subgroups (e.g., urban/rural, male/female) | Random selection within each subgroup |
| **Cluster sampling** | Population is geographically dispersed; no complete list available | Random selection of clusters (e.g., villages, schools), then sample within clusters |
| **Systematic sampling** | Complete list available; simple random is impractical | Select every nth item from the list |

### Non-Probability Sampling

| Method | When to Use | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| **Purposive** | Need specific types of informants (e.g., managers, beneficiaries) | Not generalisable; selection bias |
| **Snowball** | Hard-to-reach populations (e.g., informal sector workers) | Bias towards connected individuals |
| **Convenience** | Rapid assessments with time and resource constraints | Significant bias; weakest design |
| **Quota** | Need to ensure representation of subgroups without a complete list | Better than convenience, but still not random |

### Sample Size Considerations

- For surveys: determine sample size using statistical power calculations (significance level, effect size, power)
- **Rule of thumb for development evaluations:** 200–400 respondents per comparison group for quantitative impact measurement
- For qualitative methods: aim for "saturation" — continue collecting data until no new themes emerge (typically 15–30 interviews per stakeholder group)
- **Minimum for focus groups:** 6–10 participants per group; 4–8 groups per stakeholder category

**Proposal application:** State your sampling approach and rationale. For large evaluations, include a sample size calculation. For qualitative components, state the saturation principle. This demonstrates methodological rigour.

## 6. Data Quality Assurance

### Five Dimensions of Data Quality (UNDP + ILO)

| Dimension | Definition | How to Assure |
|---|---|---|
| **Validity** | Data measures what it is intended to measure | Pilot-test instruments; use validated scales; define indicators precisely |
| **Reliability** | Same method produces consistent results | Standardise procedures; train enumerators; conduct inter-rater reliability checks |
| **Completeness** | All required data is collected; no gaps | Use data tracking sheets; monitor completion rates; follow up on missing data |
| **Timeliness** | Data is available when needed for decision-making | Set and enforce data collection schedules; automate reminders |
| **Integrity** | Data is protected from deliberate manipulation or unauthorised access | Implement access controls; audit trails; independent verification |

### Quality Assurance Activities by Phase

| Phase | Activities |
|---|---|
| **Pre-collection** | Pilot-test instruments; train enumerators; develop data collection protocols; pre-code questionnaires |
| **During collection** | Supervise enumerators; conduct spot checks; review completed forms daily; resolve inconsistencies immediately |
| **Post-collection** | Double-entry verification; range checks; consistency checks; outlier analysis; data cleaning log |
| **Analysis** | Triangulate across sources; sensitivity analysis; peer review of findings |
| **Reporting** | Disclose methods, limitations, and data quality issues; provide confidence intervals where appropriate |

## 7. Evaluation Quality Standards

### UNDP Evaluation Standards

Every evaluation should meet these quality criteria:

1. **Independence** — evaluators are free from undue influence; they report findings objectively
2. **Impartiality** — evaluation methodology is unbiased; all perspectives are heard
3. **Credibility** — methodology is rigorous; findings are based on evidence; evaluators are competent
4. **Usefulness** — findings are relevant, timely, and actionable; recommendations are specific and feasible
5. **Transparency** — methods, data, and limitations are disclosed; reports are publicly available
6. **Ethical conduct** — informed consent; confidentiality; do no harm; respect for dignity

### Evaluation Report Structure (UNDP)

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Executive summary** | Key findings, conclusions, and recommendations (2–3 pages) |
| **Introduction** | Purpose, scope, and audience of the evaluation |
| **Methodology** | Design, methods, sampling, data sources, limitations |
| **Context** | Programme background, theory of change, key stakeholders |
| **Findings** | Evidence-based answers to each evaluation question, organised by OECD-DAC criteria |
| **Conclusions** | Interpretive judgements based on findings; synthesis of evidence |
| **Recommendations** | Specific, actionable, prioritised, and attributed to responsible parties |
| **Lessons learned** | Generalisable insights for future programming |
| **Annexes** | ToR, evaluation matrix, data collection instruments, list of interviewees, documents reviewed |

### Management Response

Every evaluation should be followed by a management response that:
- Accepts, partially accepts, or rejects each recommendation with justification
- Assigns responsibility for implementing accepted recommendations
- Sets deadlines for implementation
- Tracks progress against commitments

**Proposal application:** When proposing evaluations, include the management response as a deliverable. This shows evaluators that you understand the evaluation is not an end in itself — it is a tool for organisational learning and improvement.

## 8. Joint Evaluations (UNDP)

When multiple organisations contribute to a programme (common in UN joint programmes), joint evaluations are used:

**Benefits:**
- Reduces evaluation burden on the programme and beneficiaries
- Provides a comprehensive picture of collective results
- Promotes harmonisation of evaluation approaches
- Strengthens mutual accountability between partners

**Challenges:**
- Coordination complexity across multiple organisations
- Different evaluation cultures, standards, and timelines
- Difficulty attributing results to specific partners
- Potential for lowest-common-denominator quality

**Design principles:**
- Establish joint governance (steering committee with all partners)
- Agree on evaluation questions, methodology, and quality standards upfront
- Use a single evaluation team (not one per partner)
- Share data and reports across all partners
- Produce a single integrated report with partner-specific findings where relevant

## 9. Evaluation Ethics and Risk Management

### Ethical Principles (UNDP + IFRC)

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| **Informed consent** | All participants must understand what the evaluation involves, how their information will be used, and their right to refuse or withdraw |
| **Confidentiality** | Individual responses must be protected; data must be anonymised in reporting |
| **Do no harm** | Evaluation activities must not put participants at risk (physical, psychological, social, economic) |
| **Cultural sensitivity** | Methods must respect local customs, power dynamics, and communication norms |
| **Inclusivity** | Evaluation must hear from marginalised and vulnerable groups, not just the powerful and articulate |
| **Independence** | Evaluators must be free to report findings honestly, including negative or uncomfortable findings |

### Evaluation Risks

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| **Social desirability bias** | Respondents tell evaluators what they think they want to hear | Use anonymous surveys; train interviewers in neutral probing; triangulate with observation |
| **Clientism** | Evaluation team gives the client what they believe the client wants, not objective findings | Independent evaluation; clear ToR; quality assurance committee |
| **Selective reporting** | Positive findings emphasised; negative findings suppressed | Require full disclosure; independent quality review; public disclosure policy |
| **Attribution error** | Crediting the programme for changes caused by other factors | Use counterfactual designs; contribution analysis; acknowledge limitations honestly |
