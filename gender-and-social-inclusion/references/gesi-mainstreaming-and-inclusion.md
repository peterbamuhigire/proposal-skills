# GESI Mainstreaming and Inclusion

Reference material for the gender-and-social-inclusion skill. Provides frameworks, donor requirements, and practical guidance for integrating gender equality and social inclusion into consulting proposals and project design. East African consulting context (Uganda, Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania).

Sources: UN Women Intersectionality Resource Guide (2022), Oxfam Gender Analysis Frameworks (1999), King (The Fix, 2020), Painter-Morland (Leadership, Gender, and Organization, 2012), Bohnet (What Works, 2016), ECOSOC (1997), World Bank/AfDB/UNDP/EU gender policies, East African national gender policies.

---

## 1. Gender Mainstreaming Fundamentals

### ECOSOC Definition (1997)

"Mainstreaming a gender perspective is the process of assessing the implications for women and men of any planned action, including legislation, policies or programmes, in all areas and at all levels. It is a strategy for making women's and men's concerns and experiences an integral dimension of the design, implementation, monitoring and evaluation of policies and programmes in all political, economic and societal spheres so that women and men benefit equally and inequality is not perpetuated."

This remains the authoritative definition used by all UN agencies, the World Bank, AfDB, and most bilateral donors. Proposal writers should reference it when establishing the conceptual basis for GESI integration.

### Evolution: WID to GAD to Gender Mainstreaming

| Era | Approach | Focus | Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1970s–80s | **Women in Development (WID)** | Add women to existing programmes; women-specific projects | Treated women as a separate category; did not challenge structures |
| 1980s–90s | **Gender and Development (GAD)** | Analyse power relations between men and women; address root causes | Remained project-level; did not change institutional practice |
| 1995 onwards | **Gender Mainstreaming** | Embed gender analysis in all policies, programmes, and budgets at every level | Risk of being reduced to tick-box exercise if not backed by political will and resources |
| 2010s onwards | **Gender-Transformative Approaches** | Challenge and change the underlying norms, power relations, and structures that produce inequality | Requires long-term engagement; harder to demonstrate in short-term projects |

**Proposal application:** When a ToR says "gender mainstreaming", it means the Beijing Platform approach — integration across all components, not a standalone gender component bolted on. When it says "gender-transformative", it expects activities that challenge root causes (norms, laws, power), not just improve access.

### Twin-Track Approach

All credible GESI strategies combine two tracks simultaneously:

1. **Mainstreaming** — integrate GESI considerations into every component: methodology design, data collection, stakeholder engagement, training, M&E, and reporting
2. **Targeted actions** — specific interventions to address barriers faced by particular groups: women-only consultations where mixed groups silence women, accessible formats for persons with disabilities, youth-specific engagement channels

Neither track alone is sufficient. Mainstreaming without targeted actions produces generic commitments. Targeted actions without mainstreaming create parallel structures that collapse when the project ends.

### Gender-Responsive vs Gender-Transformative Continuum

| Level | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Gender-blind** | Ignores gender dimensions entirely | Training schedule that ignores women's care responsibilities |
| **Gender-aware** | Acknowledges gender differences but does not act on them | Situation analysis notes women's lower participation but methodology unchanged |
| **Gender-responsive** | Adjusts activities to address identified gender gaps | Separate consultations for women; childcare at training venues; sex-disaggregated indicators |
| **Gender-transformative** | Actively challenges and seeks to change the norms, roles, and power relations that produce inequality | Male engagement programmes; advocacy for policy change; shifting decision-making structures |

**Proposal application:** Most donor-funded assignments require at minimum gender-responsive design. Assignments focused on governance, institutional reform, or community development increasingly expect gender-transformative elements. State the level explicitly in your methodology.

### Practical Needs vs Strategic Interests

Molyneux (1985), operationalised by Moser (1989):

- **Practical gender needs** — arise from existing gender roles. Improving water access reduces women's collection burden. Practical needs do not challenge the division of labour.
- **Strategic gender interests** — arise from women's subordinate position. Changing land ownership laws, increasing women's political representation, challenging norms around unpaid care work. Strategic interests challenge existing power relations.

Good project design addresses both. A proposal that only addresses practical needs (e.g., "we will ensure women attend training") without touching strategic interests (e.g., "we will support women's participation in decision-making structures") will score lower with experienced evaluators.

## 2. Social Inclusion Dimensions

### Intersectionality (UN Women 2022)

Intersectionality recognises that people hold multiple, overlapping identities — gender, disability, age, ethnicity, race, class, geographic location, religion, sexual orientation and gender identity — and that these identities interact to create compounded disadvantage or privilege. A rural woman with a disability in northern Uganda faces fundamentally different barriers from an urban professional woman in Kampala.

**Key intersecting dimensions for East African projects:**

| Dimension | Relevance |
|---|---|
| **Gender** | Women and girls face systemic barriers in access, participation, decision-making |
| **Disability** | 12–15% of population; physical, sensory, intellectual, psychosocial |
| **Age** | Youth bulge (over 70% under 30 in Uganda); elderly excluded from digital services |
| **Ethnicity/language** | Multiple ethnic groups; minority languages excluded from services |
| **Geographic location** | Rural/urban divide; conflict-affected northern regions; pastoral areas |
| **Displacement** | Refugees (Uganda hosts 1.5M+), IDPs, returnees |
| **HIV status** | PLHIV face stigma, discrimination, economic exclusion |
| **Religion** | Can intersect with gender norms (e.g., girls' education restrictions) |

**Eight enablers for intersectional approach (UN Women):**

1. Intersectional data collection and analysis
2. Meaningful participation of affected groups in design
3. Accountability mechanisms accessible to marginalised groups
4. Capacity building on intersectionality for project teams
5. Intersectional budgeting (resources allocated to most marginalised)
6. Legal and policy frameworks that recognise multiple discrimination
7. Multi-sectoral coordination (GESI issues cross ministry mandates)
8. Monitoring of differential impacts across identity groups

### Disability Inclusion

**Legal framework:** Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD, 2006). Uganda ratified 2008; Kenya 2008; Rwanda 2008; Tanzania 2009.

**Twin-track approach to disability:**

1. **Mainstream** — ensure all project activities are accessible: venues, materials, communication, transport, digital platforms
2. **Targeted** — specific activities for persons with disabilities: assistive technology, sign language interpretation, disability-focused organisations as stakeholders

**Washington Group Short Set of Questions (WG-SS):** The internationally recommended method for identifying persons with disabilities in surveys and data collection. Six domains: seeing, hearing, walking, cognition, self-care, communication. Each rated on a four-point scale (no difficulty, some difficulty, a lot of difficulty, cannot do at all). The recommended cut-off for "disability" is "a lot of difficulty" or "cannot do at all" in at least one domain.

**Proposal application:** When the ToR requires disability inclusion, commit to:
- Using WG-SS in all data collection instruments
- Universal design principles for all outputs (documents, training, digital systems)
- Consulting Organisations of Persons with Disabilities (OPDs) as stakeholders
- Disability-disaggregated data in all reporting
- Accessibility audits of project venues and materials

### Youth Inclusion

East Africa has one of the youngest populations globally. Uganda: 72% under 30; Kenya: 75% under 35; Rwanda: 70% under 30; Tanzania: 65% under 25.

**Key frameworks:**

| Concept | Definition | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Demographic dividend** | Economic growth potential from large working-age population relative to dependants | Only realised with investment in education, health, employment |
| **Youth bulge** | Disproportionately large share of 15–35 year-olds | Risk of social instability if not matched by economic opportunity |
| **NEET** | Not in Education, Employment, or Training | Uganda: 50.9% (worsening); key indicator of youth exclusion |

**Youth inclusion in projects:** Engage youth as agents, not just beneficiaries. Include youth-led organisations in stakeholder mapping. Design activities around youth availability (not school/work hours). Use digital and mobile channels. Set youth participation targets (e.g., minimum 30% of consultation participants aged 18–35).

### Elderly and Age-Sensitive Programming

Older persons (60+) face exclusion from digital services, consultation processes, and benefit-sharing. In East Africa, older persons often serve as primary carers for orphans and vulnerable children. Age-sensitive programming ensures: accessible communication, appropriate consultation timing, recognition of older persons' knowledge and experience, and protection from age-based discrimination.

### Ethnic and Linguistic Minorities

East African countries are multi-ethnic and multi-lingual. Project design must consider: materials in local languages (not just English/Swahili), culturally appropriate consultation methods, sensitivity to historical marginalisation of certain groups (e.g., pastoralist communities, Batwa in Rwanda/Uganda, coastal communities in Kenya/Tanzania), and avoidance of elite capture by dominant groups.

### Persons in Vulnerable Situations

Includes refugees and host communities (Uganda hosts over 1.5 million refugees under its progressive open-door policy), internally displaced persons, persons living with HIV, survivors of gender-based violence, and other groups facing acute vulnerability. Proposals should identify these groups during stakeholder analysis and design specific outreach mechanisms.

### Leave No One Behind (LNOB)

The central promise of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development. LNOB requires identifying who is being left behind, why, and what needs to change. Five key factors (UNDP): discrimination, geography, governance, socio-economic status, and shocks and fragility. Every GESI analysis should map these factors against the assignment context.

## 3. GESI in the Project Cycle

### Identification and Problem Analysis

Conduct GESI-sensitive problem analysis:

- **Who is affected differently?** Disaggregate the problem by sex, age, disability, location, ethnicity
- **What are the root causes of inequality?** Distinguish symptoms (low participation) from causes (cultural norms, legal barriers, economic constraints)
- **Who has voice?** Identify which groups are heard in current decision-making and which are excluded
- **What data exists?** Assess availability of sex- and disability-disaggregated data; identify data gaps

Tools: gender analysis matrix, access and control profile, 24-hour activity clock, institutional gender audit, Harvard Analytical Framework (activity profile, access and control profile, influencing factors).

### Design: The GESI Action Plan

Every proposal for a donor-funded assignment should include a GESI action plan (or embed GESI actions within the methodology). Structure:

| Component | Content |
|---|---|
| **GESI analysis** | Summary of gender and inclusion dimensions; key barriers and opportunities |
| **GESI objectives** | What the project will achieve on gender equality and social inclusion |
| **GESI activities** | Specific actions integrated into each methodology phase |
| **GESI indicators** | Measurable targets with baselines (sex-disaggregated, disability-disaggregated) |
| **GESI responsibilities** | Who is responsible: gender specialist, team leads, M&E officer |
| **GESI budget** | Dedicated budget lines (gender specialist time, accessible materials, targeted consultations) |
| **GESI risks** | Risks to inclusion (backlash, elite capture, tokenism) and mitigation measures |

### Implementation: GESI-Sensitive Methods

| Method | GESI Consideration |
|---|---|
| **Key informant interviews** | Ensure women, youth, persons with disabilities, and minority groups are among KIIs — not just officials |
| **Focus group discussions** | Separate FGDs by sex and age where mixed groups would silence women or youth; accessible venues |
| **Surveys** | Include WG-SS disability questions; train enumerators on GESI-sensitive interviewing; ensure female enumerators available for female respondents |
| **Workshops** | Childcare provision; accessible venues; interpretation; timing that does not exclude women (avoid market days, early morning) |
| **Digital tools** | Consider digital literacy gaps; provide alternatives to smartphone-dependent tools; ensure accessibility standards (WCAG 2.1) |

### M&E: Disaggregation and GESI Indicators

**Minimum disaggregation standard:** All quantitative data disaggregated by sex and age. Where feasible, disaggregate by disability status (using WG-SS), geographic location, and other relevant dimensions.

**GESI outcome indicators (examples):**

| Level | Indicator |
|---|---|
| **Output** | Number and % of women/youth/PWDs participating in project activities |
| **Outcome** | Change in women's decision-making power in target institutions |
| **Outcome** | Change in accessibility of services for persons with disabilities |
| **Impact** | Reduction in gender gap in [sector-specific metric] |

### Reporting: GESI Results

Donors increasingly require dedicated GESI reporting sections in progress reports. At minimum: (a) progress against GESI indicators, (b) GESI challenges encountered and how addressed, (c) examples of how GESI integration changed project decisions or design, (d) disaggregated data tables.

## 4. GBV and SEA Prevention

### GBV in Development Projects

Gender-based violence (GBV) is any harmful act directed at an individual based on their gender. Types: physical, sexual, emotional/psychological, economic, harmful traditional practices (FGM, child marriage, bride price abuse).

Development projects can inadvertently increase GBV risk through:

- **Labour influx** — large numbers of male workers in construction/infrastructure projects
- **Economic shifts** — women's increased income can trigger intimate partner violence from men who feel their status threatened
- **Displacement** — resettlement disrupts social networks and protection mechanisms
- **Power imbalances** — project staff have power over beneficiaries (access to services, employment, information)

### Sexual Exploitation and Abuse (SEA) / Sexual Harassment (SH)

SEA involves actual or attempted abuse of position of vulnerability, differential power, or trust for sexual purposes. All major donors (World Bank, AfDB, UN, EU) now require explicit SEA/SH prevention measures in projects.

### Codes of Conduct

All project workers (staff, consultants, contractors, sub-contractors) must sign a Code of Conduct that:

- Prohibits all forms of GBV, SEA, and SH
- Defines unacceptable behaviours in clear, specific terms
- Requires reporting of witnessed or suspected incidents
- States consequences for violations (disciplinary action, contract termination, legal prosecution)
- Applies 24/7, not just during working hours

### Reporting and Referral Mechanisms

- **Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM)** — accessible, confidential, multiple entry points (in-person, phone, written), with a specific channel for GBV/SEA complaints
- **Referral pathways** — map existing GBV response services (health, psychosocial, legal, safety) before project start; do not create parallel systems
- **Survivor-centred approach** — safety, confidentiality, respect, and non-discrimination; the survivor decides what action to take; do not require survivors to report to police as a condition of support

### GBV Risk Assessment Checklist

For proposals, include assessment of:

1. Does the project involve labour influx or construction?
2. Does the project involve resettlement or displacement?
3. Does the project shift economic power within households?
4. Are there existing high rates of GBV in the project area?
5. Are GBV response services available and accessible?
6. Does the project involve direct contact between project staff and vulnerable beneficiaries?
7. What is the capacity of the implementing agency to manage GBV risks?

**Proposal application:** For any assignment with moderate-to-high GBV risk, include: GBV risk assessment in inception phase, Code of Conduct for all project personnel, GRM with GBV-specific channel, training on GBV prevention for project staff, mapping of referral services, and GBV monitoring indicators.

## 5. Gender-Responsive Budgeting

### Definition and Purpose

Gender-responsive budgeting (GRB) analyses government or project budgets for their differential impact on women and men, girls and boys. It does not mean separate budgets for women — it means ensuring that budget allocations address gender gaps and do not perpetuate inequality.

### Budget Analysis Tools

| Tool | Purpose | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Gender-aware policy appraisal** | Assess policies for their gender impact before budget allocation | Project design phase |
| **Sex-disaggregated beneficiary assessment** | Determine who benefits from expenditure | M&E, mid-term reviews |
| **Gender-disaggregated public expenditure incidence analysis** | Map how expenditure reaches men and women differently | Sector studies, PFM assignments |
| **Gender budget statement** | Government statement of how budget addresses gender priorities | PFM reform, budget support |
| **Gender-aware medium-term expenditure framework** | Integrate gender into forward estimates | Multi-year programme design |

### Application in Project Financial Proposals

When preparing a financial proposal, include explicit GESI budget lines:

| Budget Line | Examples |
|---|---|
| **GESI specialist time** | Gender/inclusion specialist input-days across all phases |
| **Accessible materials** | Braille, large print, sign language interpretation, local language translation |
| **Targeted consultations** | Women-only FGDs, youth workshops, PWD-specific sessions |
| **Childcare/transport** | Enabling women's participation in workshops and consultations |
| **GBV prevention** | Training, Code of Conduct distribution, GRM establishment |
| **GESI data collection** | Additional survey modules (WG-SS), female enumerators, disaggregated analysis |

**Rule of thumb:** GESI-specific budget lines typically represent 3–8% of total project cost for assignments with significant community engagement. Underfunding GESI signals a tick-box approach to evaluators.

## 6. Donor GESI Requirements

| Donor | Marker/Tag System | Key Requirements | Reporting |
|---|---|---|---|
| **World Bank** | Gender tag (project-level, 4 criteria: analysis, actions, indicators, M&E) | Gender analysis in PAD; GBV risk rating (low/moderate/substantial/high); SEA/SH action plan for substantial/high risk; ESF ESS requirements | ICR gender section; disaggregated ISRs |
| **AfDB** | Gender marker: **GG** (gender equality as principal objective), **GM** (gender mainstreamed), **SGI** (some gender integration), **NGE** (no gender elements) | Gender action plan for GM/GG projects; gender analysis in appraisal; GESI indicators in results framework; ISS OS-1 includes gender | PCR gender section; annual gender portfolio review |
| **UNDP** | Gender marker (GEN3/GEN2/GEN1/GEN0); LNOB assessment | Gender analysis mandatory; LNOB assessment; gender parity in staffing; GEN2/3 target for programme portfolio | ROAR gender reporting; gender results tracker |
| **EU** | GAP III (2021–2025); gender marker (G2/G1/G0) | Gender analysis; 85% of new actions G1/G2; SRHR and GBV priorities; gender in M&E | Annual GAP III reporting |
| **DFID/FCDO** | Gender Equality Act 2014; gender marker | Gender impact assessment; GESI in business case; Value for Money includes equity | Annual review gender section |
| **GIZ** | Gender strategy; safeguards | Gender analysis; GG1/GG2 rating; gender-sensitive indicators | Progress report gender section |
| **USAID** | ADS 205; gender analysis requirement | Mandatory gender analysis; do-no-harm; GBV integration | Performance reporting; gender indicators |

**Proposal application:** Before writing any GESI section, check the donor's specific requirements. A World Bank project needs the four gender tag criteria addressed explicitly. An AfDB project needs to demonstrate alignment with the appropriate gender marker category. UNDP requires explicit LNOB analysis.

## 7. East African GESI Context

### Uganda

**Policy framework:**
- National Gender Policy (revised 2007, under review)
- National Action Plan on Women, Peace and Security (UNSCR 1325)
- Persons with Disabilities Act 2006 (revised 2020)
- National Youth Policy 2016
- Equal Opportunities Commission Act 2007
- National Policy on Elimination of GBV 2016

**Key statistics:**
- Gender Inequality Index: 0.524 (high inequality)
- Women in Parliament: 33.8% (constitutionally mandated)
- Gender pay gap: 32.3% (women earn less)
- GBV prevalence: 56% of women have experienced physical violence; 28% sexual violence (UDHS 2016)
- Female land ownership: 39% of agricultural land managed by women, but only 7% women own land individually
- Girls' education: secondary completion rate significantly lower for girls; early pregnancy major factor
- Maternal mortality: 189 per 100,000 live births
- Youth NEET: 50.9% (worsening trend)
- Disability: estimated 12.4% of population; limited accessibility in public services
- Refugees: 1.5M+ (largest host in Africa); majority women and children

**GESI-critical sectors:** agriculture (women do 70–80% of agricultural labour but control few resources), health (maternal health, GBV response, HIV), education (girls' retention, SRHR), governance (women's political participation, local council quotas), infrastructure (GBV risks from labour influx).

### Kenya

**Policy framework:**
- National Policy on Gender and Development 2019
- Constitution 2010 (two-thirds gender rule, Bill of Rights)
- Persons with Disabilities Act 2003
- Sexual Offences Act 2006
- National Policy for Prevention and Response to GBV 2014

**Key statistics:**
- Women in Parliament: 23% (below constitutional two-thirds target)
- FGM prevalence: 21% nationally (over 90% in some counties — Samburu, Somali, Kisii)
- GBV: 45% of women aged 15–49 have experienced physical/sexual violence
- Youth (15–34): 35% of population; NEET rate approximately 39%
- Devolution (47 counties) creates both opportunity and challenge for GESI implementation

**GESI-critical issues:** county-level implementation of gender policies, pastoralist communities, FGM, child marriage in arid/semi-arid counties, urban informal settlements.

### Rwanda

**Policy framework:**
- National Gender Policy 2021
- Gender Monitoring Office (constitutionally mandated)
- National Policy Against GBV 2011
- Law on Persons with Disabilities 2021

**Key statistics and achievements:**
- Women in Parliament: 61% (world's highest)
- Gender gap in primary education: near parity
- Women's land rights: joint registration mandated by law
- GBV: Isange One-Stop Centres provide integrated response

**Challenges despite progress:** economic empowerment lags political representation; rural women face persistent barriers; informal sector dominated by women with low returns; cultural norms on masculinity changing slowly.

### Tanzania

**Policy framework:**
- National Gender Policy (under revision from 1997 version)
- Law of the Child Act 2009
- National Plan of Action for Prevention and Response to Violence Against Children
- Persons with Disabilities Act 2010

**Key statistics:**
- Women in Parliament: 37% (special seats mechanism)
- Child marriage: 31% married before 18 (among highest in East Africa)
- FGM: 10% nationally (over 70% in some regions — Manyara, Dodoma, Arusha)
- Youth: 63% of population under 25
- Women's economic participation constrained by land laws (customary law conflicts with statutory)

**GESI-critical issues:** child marriage, FGM in specific regions, women's land rights, integration of Zanzibar (different legal framework), pastoralist communities.

### Regional: East African Community

The EAC Treaty (Article 121–122) commits partner states to gender mainstreaming. The EAC Gender Policy 2018 provides a regional framework. However, implementation varies significantly across member states. Rwanda leads on political representation; Uganda leads on refugee inclusion; Kenya's devolution creates unique GESI challenges; Tanzania faces persistent cultural barriers in specific regions.

## 8. Proposal Strategy Notes

### GESI Section Structure

When a ToR requires a GESI approach, structure the section as follows:

1. **GESI context** — 2–3 paragraphs on gender and inclusion dimensions of the assignment, using country-specific data
2. **Our approach** — state whether gender-responsive or gender-transformative, and justify
3. **GESI integration across phases** — table showing GESI actions in each methodology phase
4. **GESI action plan** — specific activities, responsibilities, indicators, timeline
5. **Safeguards** — GBV/SEA prevention, do-no-harm, accessible design
6. **GESI monitoring** — indicators, disaggregation plan, reporting

### Standard GESI Commitments

Include these unless the assignment context makes them irrelevant:

- All data collection instruments include sex, age, and disability status disaggregation
- Washington Group Short Set questions included in all surveys
- Separate consultations for women and youth where mixed settings would inhibit participation
- Accessible venues and materials for all project activities
- Female enumerators/facilitators available for female respondents/participants
- Gender specialist included in the team (full-time or part-time depending on assignment scope)
- Code of Conduct signed by all project personnel
- GBV/SEA grievance mechanism established during inception
- GESI indicators included in the results framework
- GESI section included in all progress reports

### GESI Indicators by Sector

| Sector | Output Indicators | Outcome Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Health** | % female health workers trained; # GBV response services supported | Change in maternal health service utilisation; reduction in GBV prevalence |
| **Education** | % girls enrolled; # schools with disability-accessible facilities | Girls' completion rate; reduction in gender gap in learning outcomes |
| **Agriculture** | % women farmers accessing inputs/extension; # women in farmer groups | Women's income from agriculture; women's control over agricultural decisions |
| **Infrastructure** | % women employed in construction; # GBV incidents reported and resolved | Women's travel time reduction; accessibility of infrastructure for PWDs |
| **Governance** | % women in decision-making positions; # gender-responsive policies adopted | Change in women's participation in governance; disability-inclusive service delivery |
| **ICT** | % women trained in digital skills; # accessible digital platforms | Gender gap in digital access; PWD usage of digital services |
| **Water/Sanitation** | % women on water committees; # accessible WASH facilities | Women's time savings; menstrual hygiene management access |

### GESI Budget Line Items

For financial proposals, ensure these are costed explicitly rather than buried in general costs:

- Gender/GESI specialist (input-days and daily rate)
- GESI analysis and assessment (inception phase)
- Accessible materials production (alternative formats, translations)
- Targeted consultations (women-only, youth, PWD-specific sessions)
- Childcare, transport subsidies for participants
- GBV prevention training for project staff
- GRM establishment and operation (including GBV-specific channel)
- Sign language interpretation and other accessibility supports
- GESI data collection (additional modules, female enumerators)
- GESI monitoring and reporting

### GESI Staffing

| Role | When Required | Level of Effort |
|---|---|---|
| **Gender/GESI Specialist** | All assignments with significant community engagement or donor GESI requirements | 15–30% for mainstreaming; 50–100% for gender-focused assignments |
| **Social Safeguards Specialist** | Infrastructure, resettlement, labour influx projects | 20–40% depending on risk level |
| **GBV Specialist** | High GBV-risk projects (construction, displacement, humanitarian) | 25–50% during implementation |
| **Disability Inclusion Specialist** | Assignments with explicit disability component or large-scale service delivery | 10–20% for mainstreaming; higher for disability-focused work |

**Proposal application:** Even when the ToR does not explicitly request a GESI specialist, including one (at appropriate level of effort) signals seriousness about inclusion and scores well with evaluation committees. For small assignments, the GESI function can be combined with the M&E or stakeholder engagement role, provided the person has demonstrable GESI competence.

### Invisible Barriers and Institutional Design (King, Bohnet)

When proposing organisational or institutional reform, go beyond formal policies to address invisible barriers:

- **Achievement barriers** — women's contributions undervalued; credit attributed to male colleagues; performance criteria biased towards male-normative behaviours
- **Cultural barriers** — "old boys' networks", informal decision-making spaces that exclude women, expectations of availability that penalise carers
- **Design barriers** — recruitment processes with biased language, promotion criteria that reward uninterrupted careers, meeting schedules that assume no care responsibilities

**Design fixes (Bohnet):** Use structured interviews (same questions, same order, scored against rubric). Anonymise CVs during shortlisting. Set targets with accountability. Make salary data transparent. Use nudges and choice architecture rather than relying on training alone — behavioural design outperforms awareness-raising.

**Proposal application:** For governance and institutional strengthening assignments, propose specific design interventions (structured processes, transparency mechanisms, accountability targets) rather than generic "gender awareness training." Evaluators who know the evidence will recognise this as more credible.

### Ethics of Care and Relational Leadership (Painter-Morland)

For assignments involving leadership development or organisational transformation, draw on:

- **Gendered organisation theory** — organisations are not gender-neutral; structures, processes, and cultures carry gendered assumptions about what "good performance" or "strong leadership" looks like
- **Relational leadership** — leadership as a process of mutual influence and shared meaning-making, not a set of individual traits; this framing creates space for diverse leadership styles
- **Ethics of care** — decision-making that considers relationships, responsibilities, and the needs of the most vulnerable; applicable to institutional governance, not just social services

**Proposal application:** When proposing institutional assessments or leadership programmes, include analysis of how organisational structures and cultures may systematically disadvantage certain groups. Propose concrete changes to structures and processes, not just individual behaviour change.
