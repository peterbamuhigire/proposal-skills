## 1. Capacity Development Conceptual Foundations

### Definition of Capacity

Capacity is not merely performance or the ability to deliver outputs. It is a **living phenomenon** — the broader ability of people, organisations, and societies to manage their affairs, set and achieve goals, and adapt over time. Capacity encompasses agency, volition, and the collective energy to act purposefully (Ubels et al., 2010).

**Capacity** = the existing ability of an entity to perform functions, solve problems, and achieve objectives.

**Capacity development (CD)** = the process through which capacity is obtained, strengthened, adapted, and maintained over time. CD is not something done *to* an organisation; it is a process organisations *go through*, ideally with ownership and self-direction.

Key distinctions:
- CD ≠ training (training addresses only one factor among many)
- CD ≠ technical assistance (TA may build dependency rather than local capacity)
- CD is non-linear, emergent, and context-dependent
- CD requires attention to power dynamics, ownership, and legitimacy

### Three Levels of Capacity (UNDP/OECD-DAC)

| Level | Focus | Examples |
|-------|-------|----------|
| **Individual** | Knowledge, skills, competencies, motivation, confidence | Technical training, mentoring, secondments, professional development |
| **Organisational** | Internal structures, processes, systems, culture, resources, leadership | Institutional strengthening, MIS design, HR systems, SOPs, governance reform |
| **Enabling environment** | Policy, legal frameworks, political economy, social norms, inter-organisational relations | Policy reform, regulatory frameworks, sector coordination, accountability mechanisms |

All three levels interact. Individual skills are wasted without organisational systems to deploy them; organisational capacity is constrained by the enabling environment. Effective CD proposals address at least two levels simultaneously.

### The 5C Model (ECDPM)

The European Centre for Development Policy Management developed the 5C Model as an empirically grounded framework for understanding what organisations need to function and survive. The five core capabilities apply at all levels — individual, team, organisation, network, sector, and country (Ubels et al., 2010, Ch. 2).

| # | Capability | Description | Diagnostic Questions |
|---|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Act and Self-Organise** | Volition, strategic intent, collective energy, agency, leadership, resource mobilisation | Does the organisation have a clear strategic direction? Can it mobilise resources independently? Is there collective will to act? |
| 2 | **Generate Development Results** | Programmatic outputs and outcomes, service delivery, technical performance | Is the organisation delivering on its mandate? Are outputs meeting quality standards? Are results documented? |
| 3 | **Relate** | Building alliances, gaining support, maintaining legitimacy, leveraging resources, managing external relationships | Does the organisation have effective partnerships? Is it credible with stakeholders? Can it negotiate and advocate? |
| 4 | **Adapt and Self-Renew** | Monitoring, anticipating change, resilience, incorporating new ideas, learning from experience, repositioning | Does the organisation learn from its work? Can it respond to changing circumstances? Does it innovate? |
| 5 | **Achieve Coherence** | Balancing specialisation with integration, preventing fragmentation, maintaining identity and purpose across functions | Are different units working towards common goals? Is there internal consistency? Is the organisation more than the sum of its parts? |

**Critical principle**: All five capabilities are interdependent. An organisation that generates results (C2) but cannot relate (C3) will lose legitimacy. One that adapts (C4) but lacks coherence (C5) will fragment. Proposals should assess and address multiple capabilities, not fixate on C2 (results) alone — the most common donor error.

### Multi-Stakeholder Capacity

Capacity often resides not within a single organisation but across networks and value chains. The Uganda oilseeds value chain example (Ubels et al., Ch. 12) demonstrates how CD interventions must address multiple actors — farmers, processors, traders, regulators — simultaneously to achieve systemic results.

### Practitioner Roles in CD

Effective CD practitioners combine two skill sets (Ubels et al., Ch. 14):
- **Content expertise**: technical knowledge, sector experience, analytical tools
- **Process expertise**: facilitation, relationship building, navigating politics, managing change

The **balanced practitioner** integrates both, knowing when to provide answers and when to facilitate the client's own problem-solving. Over-reliance on content expertise creates dependency; over-reliance on process skills produces superficial engagement.

### Ownership, Authority, and Conflict

CD is inherently political. Changing capacity means changing power relations, resource allocation, and institutional arrangements. Proposals must acknowledge (Ubels et al., Ch. 10):
- Who owns the CD process and whose interests it serves
- How authority structures will be affected
- What conflicts may arise and how they will be managed
- The difference between stated commitment and genuine ownership

## 2. USAID HICD Methodology

### The 7-Step HICD Process

The USAID Human and Institutional Capacity Development model is a cyclical, evidence-based methodology that addresses performance holistically rather than defaulting to training (USAID, 2010).

| Step | Activity | Key Outputs | Tools |
|------|----------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | **Identify Partner Organisations** | Prioritised list of partner organisations aligned with programme objectives | Partner Information Collection Tool |
| 2 | **Obtain Partner Commitment** | MOU or letter of commitment; agreed desired performance standards | MOU Template; Desired Performance Statement |
| 3 | **Form Stakeholder Group** | Cross-functional team to guide the CD process (6–12 members) | Stakeholder Group Formation Tool |
| 4 | **Conduct Performance Assessment** | Documented actual performance vs desired; identified gaps | Performance Assessment Template |
| 5 | **Prepare Performance Solutions Package** | Root cause analysis; prioritised mix of training and non-training solutions | Performance Solutions Package Template |
| 6 | **Implement Performance Solutions** | Delivered interventions across all relevant performance factors | Implementation Plan |
| 7 | **Monitor Change in Performance** | Measured performance improvement; lessons for next cycle | Monitoring Framework; Feedback Loop |

The process is **cyclical**: Step 7 feeds back into Steps 2–4 for continuous improvement. Most CD programmes require 2–3 full cycles to achieve sustainable change.

### The 6 Performance Factors

HICD's core analytical contribution is the **6 Performance Factors** framework for root cause analysis. When performance gaps are identified, the cause is rarely just a lack of knowledge or skills. HICD requires systematic analysis of all six factors before designing solutions (USAID, 2010).

| Factor | Description | Example Root Causes | Example Interventions |
|--------|-------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Information** | Clear expectations, performance standards, guides, feedback mechanisms | No job descriptions; no SOPs; no feedback loops; unclear reporting lines | Develop SOPs, performance standards, job descriptions; establish feedback mechanisms; create management dashboards |
| **Resources and Tools** | Adequate time, budget, materials, equipment, staffing, workspace | Outdated equipment; understaffing; no budget for fieldwork; poor connectivity | Procurement of equipment; budget advocacy; staffing plans; infrastructure upgrades |
| **Incentives** | Financial and non-financial rewards aligned with desired performance | No link between performance and promotion; perverse incentives; poor working conditions | Performance-based pay; recognition programmes; career development pathways; improved conditions |
| **Knowledge and Skills** | Technical and managerial competencies; training; experience | Untrained staff; no induction programme; skills mismatch; outdated knowledge | Formal training; on-the-job coaching; mentoring; study tours; peer learning; cross-training |
| **Capacity** | Individual ability to learn and perform; right person in right role | Mismatched recruitment; cognitive/physical limitations for specific tasks; role overload | Revised job matching; recruitment reform; reasonable accommodation; task redistribution |
| **Motives** | Intrinsic desire to perform; alignment of personal and organisational goals | Low morale; disengagement; conflicting personal priorities; toxic culture | Leadership development; team building; vision alignment; culture change; welfare programmes |

**Key principle**: Training addresses only Factor 4 (Knowledge and Skills). If the root cause is inadequate resources (Factor 2) or misaligned incentives (Factor 3), training will not solve the problem. Proposals that default to training without performance factor analysis are methodologically weak.

### Performance Gap Analysis Process

```
Desired Performance (Step 2)
        │
        ▼
   ┌─────────┐
   │   GAP   │ ← Quantified difference
   └─────────┘
        │
        ▼
Actual Performance (Step 4)
        │
        ▼
Root Cause Analysis (6 Performance Factors)
        │
        ▼
Performance Solutions Package (training + non-training)
```

### HICD vs Training-Only Approaches

| Dimension | Training-Only | HICD |
|-----------|--------------|------|
| Root cause analysis | Assumes skills gap | Analyses all 6 factors |
| Solution design | Courses and workshops | Mix of training, systems, incentives, resources |
| Ownership | Consultant-driven | Stakeholder group-driven |
| Sustainability | Skills decay without reinforcement | Systemic changes embed performance |
| M&E | Attendance and satisfaction | Measured performance change |
| Cost-effectiveness | Often poor (wrong diagnosis) | Higher (addresses actual root causes) |

### Performance Assessment Team Composition

The HICD stakeholder group should include (USAID, 2010):
- Senior management sponsor (authority to act on findings)
- Line managers from assessed units (operational knowledge)
- Staff representatives (ground-truth perspective)
- HR/training personnel (institutional knowledge of past interventions)
- External facilitator (objectivity, process expertise)
- Client/beneficiary representatives where feasible

## 3. The Learning Organisation (Senge)

### The Five Disciplines

Peter Senge's learning organisation framework provides the theoretical foundation for sustainable capacity development. Organisations that cannot learn cannot sustain capacity gains (Senge, 2006).

| Discipline | Description | Application to Capacity Development |
|-----------|-------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Systems Thinking** | Seeing interrelationships and feedback loops rather than linear cause-effect; identifying leverage points for change | Map the system before intervening; identify where small changes produce large results; avoid symptomatic fixes that worsen root causes |
| **Personal Mastery** | Individuals continuously clarifying personal vision and seeing current reality clearly; creative tension as energy source | Build individual commitment to professional excellence; align personal development goals with organisational mission |
| **Mental Models** | Surfacing and testing deeply held assumptions that shape perception and action | Challenge assumptions about "how things are done here"; use inquiry and advocacy to surface hidden beliefs blocking change |
| **Shared Vision** | Co-creating a genuine, compelling picture of the future that generates commitment (not mere compliance) | Build collective ownership of the CD agenda; move beyond imposed donor visions to locally owned aspirations |
| **Team Learning** | Aligning and developing the capacity of a team to create results members truly desire; dialogue vs discussion | Develop cross-functional teams that learn together; use action learning and peer review; create practice fields for new skills |

### Systems Archetypes Relevant to Capacity Building

Systems archetypes are recurring patterns of behaviour in organisations. Recognising them helps consultants diagnose problems and avoid common traps (Senge, 2006; Senge et al., 1994).

| Archetype | Pattern | CD Example |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| **Limits to Growth** | Initial success hits an unaddressed constraint | Training programme produces early results but stalls because organisational systems cannot absorb new skills |
| **Shifting the Burden** | Symptomatic fix weakens fundamental solution | Hiring international consultants to fill capacity gaps reduces pressure to develop local staff |
| **Eroding Goals** | Targets are lowered when performance falls short | CD targets are quietly reduced each reporting cycle rather than addressing root causes of underperformance |
| **Fixes that Fail** | Short-term fix creates long-term side effects | Rushing staff through compressed training to meet deadlines produces superficial learning that erodes confidence |
| **Success to the Successful** | Resources flow to what already works, starving alternatives | Strong units receive more CD investment while weak units — who need it most — are bypassed |
| **Tragedy of the Commons** | Shared resource is overused because no one manages it | Multiple programmes draw on the same small pool of skilled staff, burning them out |
| **Growth and Underinvestment** | Growth is limited by failure to invest in capacity ahead of demand | Organisation expands services without investing in staff development, quality collapses, demand falls |
| **Escalation** | Two parties competing for advantage, each escalating in response to the other | Departments competing for CD resources, each inflating their needs to secure funding |

### 11 Laws of the Fifth Discipline

These counterintuitive principles should inform CD design (Senge, 2006):

1. **Today's problems come from yesterday's solutions** — trace current capacity gaps to past interventions and their unintended consequences
2. **The harder you push, the harder the system pushes back** — forced change generates resistance; work with the system's own dynamics
3. **Behaviour grows better before it grows worse** — early quick wins from CD may mask deeper problems that surface later
4. **The easy way out usually leads back in** — simple training solutions rarely address systemic capacity constraints
5. **The cure can be worse than the disease** — poorly designed CD can create dependency, undermine confidence, or distort incentives
6. **Faster is slower** — sustainable capacity takes time; compressed timescales produce superficial results
7. **Cause and effect are not closely related in time and space** — look beyond the immediate intervention site for impacts
8. **Small changes can produce big results — but the areas of highest leverage are often the least obvious** — invest in diagnostic work to find leverage points
9. **You can have your cake and eat it too — but not at once** — apparent trade-offs between speed and quality dissolve over longer time horizons
10. **Dividing an elephant in half does not produce two small elephants** — capacity is systemic; piecemeal approaches fragment it
11. **There is no blame** — performance gaps are systemic, not personal; focus on structure, not individuals

### 7 Learning Disabilities in Organisations

These common dysfunctions block capacity development (Senge, 2006):

1. **"I am my position"** — staff identify with their role rather than the organisation's purpose; limits cross-functional learning
2. **"The enemy is out there"** — problems are attributed to external actors (donors, government, market) rather than internal factors
3. **Illusion of taking charge** — reactive busyness mistaken for proactive strategy; firefighting displaces learning
4. **Fixation on events** — focus on incidents rather than underlying patterns and systemic causes
5. **Parable of the boiled frog** — gradual deterioration goes unnoticed until crisis; slow erosion of standards
6. **Delusion of learning from experience** — limited feedback on consequences of decisions; learning is superficial or wrong
7. **Myth of the management team** — leadership group protects turf and avoids conflict rather than engaging in genuine inquiry

### Creative Tension Model

```
   VISION (desired capacity state)
        │
        │  ← Creative Tension (generates energy for change)
        │
   CURRENT REALITY (assessed capacity)
```

Creative tension arises from holding vision and current reality simultaneously. The gap generates energy — but only if both are honestly acknowledged. Proposals should help organisations articulate their vision *and* confront their current reality without defensiveness. This is the engine of genuine CD (Senge, 2006).

**Metanoia** (Greek: transcendence, shift of mind) — Senge's term for the deep learning that transforms capacity, not just adds to it. True CD involves metanoia: a fundamental shift in how individuals and organisations think and act, not merely the acquisition of new skills.

## 4. Capacity Assessment Tools

### ECDPM 5C Assessment Framework

For each of the five capabilities, assess on a 1–5 scale with qualitative evidence:

| Capability | Assessment Questions | Rating (1–5) | Evidence / Notes |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Act and Self-Organise | Is there strategic direction? Resource mobilisation? Leadership? Agency? | | |
| Generate Results | Are outputs delivered? Quality adequate? Results documented? | | |
| Relate | Are partnerships effective? Legitimacy maintained? Advocacy capacity? | | |
| Adapt and Self-Renew | Does the organisation learn? Innovate? Respond to change? | | |
| Achieve Coherence | Are functions integrated? Identity clear? Fragmentation avoided? | | |

**Rating scale**: 1 = Absent/critical weakness; 2 = Nascent/emerging; 3 = Developing/partially functional; 4 = Established/functional; 5 = Mature/self-sustaining

### USAID Performance Assessment Template

For each key performance area:

| Performance Area | Desired Standard | Actual Performance | Gap | Root Cause (6 Factors) | Priority |
|-----------------|-----------------|-------------------|-----|----------------------|----------|
| *e.g. M&E reporting* | *Quarterly reports submitted within 15 days of quarter-end, 95% data completeness* | *Reports submitted 45–60 days late, 60% data completeness* | *Critical* | *Information (no SOPs), Resources (no MIS), Knowledge (untrained M&E officers)* | *High* |

### Organisational Capacity Assessment Checklist

| Domain | Sub-Domain | Key Indicators |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Governance** | Board/oversight | Functioning board; clear ToRs; regular meetings; strategic oversight |
| | Strategic planning | Current strategic plan; annual work plans; alignment with mandate |
| | Accountability | Audit function; financial controls; transparency mechanisms |
| **Human Resources** | Staffing | Filled positions; qualifications match; retention rates |
| | Performance management | Appraisal system; feedback mechanisms; career development |
| | Policies | HR manual; code of conduct; grievance procedure |
| **Financial Management** | Budgeting | Realistic budgets; variance tracking; cash flow management |
| | Accounting | Chart of accounts; reconciliation; reporting timeliness |
| | Compliance | Audit opinions; donor compliance; procurement procedures |
| **Programme Delivery** | Planning | Logical frameworks; M&E plans; risk registers |
| | Implementation | Delivery against targets; quality standards; beneficiary satisfaction |
| | Learning | Evaluations conducted; lessons documented; adaptive management |
| **External Relations** | Partnerships | Active partnerships; MoUs; collaborative projects |
| | Communication | Communication strategy; stakeholder engagement; reporting |
| | Advocacy | Policy engagement; network participation; positioning |
| **Knowledge Management** | Systems | Document management; databases; institutional memory |
| | Learning culture | After-action reviews; knowledge sharing; innovation |
| | Technology | IT infrastructure; software; digital literacy |

### Skills Gap Analysis Matrix

| Competency Area | Required Level | Current Level | Gap | Priority | Intervention |
|----------------|---------------|--------------|-----|----------|-------------|
| *e.g. GIS for M&E* | *Intermediate* | *None* | *High* | *Critical* | *5-day training + 3-month mentoring* |
| *e.g. Report writing* | *Advanced* | *Basic* | *Medium* | *High* | *Writing workshop + peer review system* |

### Kirkpatrick 4-Level Training Evaluation

| Level | Measures | Methods | Timing |
|-------|---------|---------|--------|
| **1. Reaction** | Participant satisfaction, perceived relevance, training quality | Post-training questionnaires, feedback forms | Immediately after training |
| **2. Learning** | Knowledge gained, skills acquired, attitudes changed | Pre/post tests, skills demonstrations, case exercises | End of training; 2–4 weeks after |
| **3. Behaviour** | Application of learning on the job, changed work practices | Supervisor observation, 360-degree feedback, work product review | 3–6 months after training |
| **4. Results** | Organisational impact, performance improvement, ROI | Performance metrics, KPIs, organisational outcomes | 6–12 months after training |

**Key principle**: Most CD programmes evaluate only Levels 1–2 (reaction and learning). Proposals that include Level 3–4 evaluation demonstrate methodological rigour and commitment to actual performance change, not just training delivery.

### Training Needs Assessment Process

1. **Organisational analysis**: strategic priorities, resource constraints, enabling/hindering factors
2. **Task analysis**: job descriptions, competency frameworks, performance standards, workflow mapping
3. **Individual analysis**: current competencies vs required; skills gap matrix; learning preferences
4. **Prioritisation**: urgency, impact, feasibility, cost-effectiveness
5. **Design**: learning objectives, methods, materials, schedule, logistics
6. **Validation**: review with stakeholders, pilot testing, revision

## 5. Training and Knowledge Transfer

### Training of Trainers (ToT) Cascade Model

The ToT model is the standard approach for scaling capacity development beyond direct beneficiaries:

| Level | Participants | Role | Duration (typical) |
|-------|-------------|------|-------------------|
| **Master Trainers** | 10–15 senior professionals selected for expertise and facilitation skills | Train Tier 1 trainers; develop/adapt materials; provide quality assurance | 10–15 days intensive |
| **Tier 1 Trainers** | 30–50 trained by Master Trainers | Deliver training to end-users; provide on-the-job coaching; report on outcomes | 5–10 days training + practicum |
| **End Users** | 200–1,000+ trained by Tier 1 | Apply skills in daily work; provide feedback; participate in refresher sessions | 3–5 days training |

**Quality safeguards for the cascade**:
- Standardised training materials (facilitator guide, participant handbook, job aids)
- Observed practice teaching with feedback during ToT
- Competency assessment before certification as trainer
- Refresher training and community of practice for trainers
- Quality monitoring visits during roll-out
- Participant feedback loops at every level

**Common failure points**: dilution of content across cascade levels; loss of facilitation quality; trainers who lecture rather than facilitate; no follow-up support; no assessment of trainer competence.

### Adult Learning Principles (Knowles Andragogy)

Effective CD interventions for adults must follow these principles (Knowles, 1984):

| Principle | Implication for CD Design |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Self-directed** | Involve learners in planning; offer choices; respect autonomy |
| **Experience-based** | Build on existing knowledge; use case studies from participants' context; value peer exchange |
| **Relevance-driven** | Link all content to real work challenges; use job-relevant examples; show immediate applicability |
| **Problem-centred** | Organise around problems to solve, not subjects to cover; use action learning |
| **Internally motivated** | Connect to professional goals and values; avoid purely compliance-driven framing |
| **Need to know** | Explain why content matters before delivering it; show the performance gap |

### Blended Learning Approaches

| Component | Format | Strengths | Limitations |
|-----------|--------|-----------|------------|
| **Face-to-face workshops** | Classroom, 3–5 days | Interaction, practice, relationship building | Costly, pulls staff from duties, one-off |
| **On-the-job coaching** | 1:1 or small group, ongoing | Contextualised, immediate application, sustained | Resource-intensive, quality depends on coach |
| **E-learning modules** | Self-paced online | Scalable, flexible timing, standardised | Requires connectivity, low completion rates, limited interaction |
| **Peer learning / CoP** | Regular meetings (virtual or physical) | Contextualised, sustainable, ownership | Requires facilitation, risks becoming routine |
| **Study tours** | Structured visits to peer organisations | Powerful motivation, practical exposure | Expensive, can become tourism, limited transfer |
| **Action learning sets** | Small groups working on real problems | Deep learning, immediate application | Requires skilled facilitation, time commitment |
| **Mentoring** | Senior-junior pairing, 6–12 months | Sustained development, relationship-based | Matching quality varies, requires commitment |

### Knowledge Networking for CD

Based on Ubels et al. (Ch. 17), knowledge networks accelerate CD by connecting practitioners across organisations:

- **Communities of practice (CoPs)**: groups of practitioners sharing knowledge on a common domain; require facilitation, not just a mailing list
- **Peer assist**: structured process where peers from other organisations help a team facing a challenge
- **Knowledge fairs**: events where organisations showcase innovations and solutions
- **Online platforms**: curated repositories, discussion forums, webinars (but only effective with active facilitation)
- **South-South exchange**: connecting practitioners in similar contexts; often more relevant than North-South TA

### Mentoring and Coaching Frameworks

| Dimension | Mentoring | Coaching |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Focus | Career and professional development | Specific skill or performance improvement |
| Duration | 6–12 months (ongoing relationship) | Weeks to months (task-focused) |
| Structure | Semi-structured; agenda set by mentee | Structured sessions with clear objectives |
| Expertise | Mentor has domain experience | Coach has process expertise (not necessarily domain) |
| Relationship | Developmental, holistic | Performance-focused, bounded |

**Mentoring programme design for CD proposals**:
1. Define mentoring objectives aligned with CD goals
2. Develop mentor selection criteria and matching process
3. Train mentors in mentoring skills (listening, questioning, feedback)
4. Establish mentoring agreements (expectations, frequency, confidentiality)
5. Provide structured tools (meeting templates, development plans, reflection logs)
6. Monitor and evaluate (participation rates, mentee progress, relationship quality)
7. Celebrate and close (completion ceremony, lessons learned, sustainability plan)

### On-the-Job Training Design

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Structured work assignments** | Progressive tasks that build competency; clear learning objectives per assignment |
| **Job rotation** | Planned movement across functions to broaden understanding; particularly effective for systems thinking |
| **Shadowing** | Observing experienced practitioners; followed by supervised practice and independent performance |
| **Job aids** | Quick-reference guides, checklists, decision trees, templates deployed at the point of work |
| **After-action reviews** | Brief structured reflection after key tasks: what was planned, what happened, why, what to do differently |
| **Performance support** | Real-time assistance (helpdesks, WhatsApp groups, supervisory coaching) during skill application |

## 6. Practitioner Guidance for Proposals

### When to Propose Capacity Building

CD components should be included when the ToR or context involves:
- **Institutional strengthening**: building or reforming organisational systems, processes, and structures
- **System design and roll-out**: new MIS, M&E systems, financial management systems requiring adoption
- **Reform programmes**: governance reform, decentralisation, public sector modernisation
- **Sector development**: value chain development, sector coordination, regulatory capacity
- **Project sustainability**: ensuring gains continue after project closure; building local ownership
- **Knowledge transfer**: consultancy assignments where the client must operate the deliverable independently

### Common Deliverables in CD Assignments

| Deliverable | Description | Typical Effort |
|------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Capacity Assessment Report** | Baseline assessment using 5C or HICD framework; identifies gaps and priorities | 10–20 person-days |
| **Capacity Development Plan** | Costed, phased plan with targets, indicators, responsibilities, timeline | 5–10 person-days |
| **Training Plan** | Needs assessment, curriculum design, materials development, delivery schedule | 5–15 person-days |
| **Training Materials Package** | Facilitator guide, participant handbook, presentations, exercises, job aids | 10–30 person-days |
| **ToT Programme** | Master trainer selection, intensive training, practicum, certification, roll-out plan | 15–25 person-days (design + delivery) |
| **Mentoring Framework** | Mentor selection, training, matching, tools, monitoring plan | 5–10 person-days |
| **Knowledge Management Strategy** | Assessment of KM practices, CoP design, platform recommendations, KM plan | 10–15 person-days |
| **Sustainability/Exit Strategy** | Transition plan, institutional embedding, handover schedule, post-project support | 5–10 person-days |

### Linking CD to Sustainability and Exit Strategy

Capacity development is the primary mechanism for sustainability. Proposals should demonstrate:

1. **Ownership transfer trajectory**: clear timeline showing when local counterparts assume each function
2. **Institutional embedding**: CD outputs integrated into organisational structures (job descriptions, SOPs, budgets, strategic plans) — not standalone project artefacts
3. **Phased withdrawal**: consultant role moves from doing → coaching → advising → monitoring → exit
4. **Counterpart system**: named local counterparts for each consultant, with explicit learning objectives and handover milestones
5. **Recurrent budget provision**: ensuring the client's own budget can sustain CD activities after project funding ends
6. **Certification and recognition**: formal recognition of trained trainers, mentors, and champions to sustain motivation

### CD Budget Components

| Budget Line | Description | Guidance |
|------------|-------------|---------|
| **Assessment and design** | Capacity assessment, CD plan development, TNA | 5–10% of CD budget |
| **Training delivery** | Facilitator fees, venue, materials, participant costs | 30–40% of CD budget |
| **Materials development** | Curriculum design, manual production, translation, printing | 10–15% of CD budget |
| **Mentoring and coaching** | Mentor/coach fees, travel, coordination | 10–20% of CD budget |
| **Equipment and tools** | IT equipment, software, reference materials for trainees | 5–15% of CD budget |
| **Knowledge management** | CoP facilitation, knowledge platforms, study tours, exchange visits | 5–10% of CD budget |
| **M&E of CD** | Baseline/endline assessments, Level 3–4 evaluation, reporting | 5–10% of CD budget |
| **Contingency** | Adaptation based on mid-term findings | 5% of CD budget |

### M&E of Capacity Development

CD is notoriously difficult to measure because capacity is a latent, multi-dimensional construct. Drawing on Ubels et al. (Ch. 18), effective M&E of CD requires:

**Combining quantitative and qualitative methods**:
- Quantitative: training completion rates, test scores, performance metrics, system usage statistics, output indicators
- Qualitative: stories of change, organisational case studies, process documentation, key informant interviews, focus group discussions, self-assessment exercises

**Time-frame alignment**:

| Time Horizon | What to Measure | Methods |
|-------------|----------------|---------|
| **Short-term** (0–6 months) | Outputs: training delivered, materials produced, systems installed, assessments completed | Activity reports, attendance, satisfaction surveys, pre/post tests |
| **Medium-term** (6–24 months) | Capability outcomes: changed practices, improved performance, new systems functioning, institutional changes | Kirkpatrick Levels 3–4, 5C re-assessment, performance data, case studies, outcome harvesting |
| **Long-term** (2–5 years) | Impact: sustained organisational performance, development results, systemic change | Longitudinal studies, contribution analysis, institutional assessments, beneficiary outcomes |

**Indicators for the 5C Model** (examples):

| Capability | Output Indicator | Outcome Indicator |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Act and Self-Organise | Strategic plan developed; leadership training delivered | Organisation independently mobilises resources; takes strategic decisions without external prompting |
| Generate Results | SOPs developed; staff trained; systems installed | Service delivery targets met; output quality improved; client satisfaction increased |
| Relate | Stakeholder mapping completed; MoUs signed | Active partnerships producing joint results; organisation invited to policy fora |
| Adapt and Self-Renew | M&E system functional; learning events conducted | Organisation modifies approaches based on evidence; innovations adopted |
| Achieve Coherence | Coordination mechanisms established; integrated plans developed | Cross-functional collaboration observed; strategic alignment maintained during growth |

**Common M&E pitfalls in CD**:
- Measuring training delivery (outputs) and claiming capacity development (outcomes)
- Attributing organisational change to a single CD intervention in complex environments
- Using only quantitative metrics for inherently qualitative phenomena
- Neglecting baseline measurement, making change impossible to demonstrate
- Evaluating too early, before behaviour change and institutional embedding have had time to manifest
- Ignoring the enabling environment level and measuring only individual/organisational change
