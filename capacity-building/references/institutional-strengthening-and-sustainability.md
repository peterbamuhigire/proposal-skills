# Institutional Strengthening and Sustainability

> Synthesised from Ubels et al. (Capacity Development in Practice, 2010), USAID HICD Handbook (2010), Senge (The Fifth Discipline, 2006), and standard development practice frameworks (UNDP, OECD-DAC).

---

## 1. Institutional Analysis Frameworks

### 1.1 Organisations as Open Systems

Ubels and Boesen (Ch. 11) position organisations not as self-contained units but as **open systems** embedded in wider institutional environments. Capacity is shaped by forces that extend well beyond the organisation's formal boundaries — including legal frameworks, political incentives, social norms, and market structures. Any institutional assessment that stops at the organogram misses the drivers that determine whether capacity investments stick.

**Practical implication**: before proposing organisational interventions, map the external institutional environment. An efficient ministry department will still fail if the policy framework, budget cycle, or political incentives work against it.

### 1.2 Governance Assessment Framework (4 Dimensions)

Ubels et al. identify four dimensions that shape an organisation's capacity to perform:

| Dimension | What It Covers | Typical Assessment Questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Institutional context** | Laws, regulations, mandates, political economy | Does the legal mandate match the assigned functions? Are there conflicting mandates with other bodies? |
| **Organisational structure & systems** | Organograms, procedures, HR systems, IT, finance | Are reporting lines clear? Do information systems support decision-making? |
| **Human resources & capabilities** | Skills, knowledge, motivation, leadership | Are staffing levels adequate? Do incentive structures retain talent? |
| **Stakeholder relationships** | Partnerships, accountability lines, legitimacy | Who holds the organisation accountable? Where is the power? |

Use this as a diagnostic checklist in institutional assessment missions. Each dimension generates a distinct intervention stream.

### 1.3 Institutional Mapping Tools

Standard tools for institutional analysis in CD proposals:

- **Stakeholder-institutional mapping**: identify all organisations in the sector, their mandates, overlaps, and coordination mechanisms. Use matrix format (institution × function).
- **Functional analysis**: compare legal mandate against actual functions performed. Gaps reveal unfunded mandates; overlaps reveal coordination failures.
- **Regulatory mapping**: trace the chain from policy to regulation to implementation to enforcement. Identify where the chain breaks.
- **Organisational network analysis**: map formal and informal relationships between institutions. Identify gatekeepers, brokers, and isolated actors.
- **Service delivery chain mapping**: for public sector, trace the service from policy design through budget allocation, procurement, delivery, and citizen feedback.

### 1.4 Political Economy Analysis for CD

Ubels (Ch. 11) is explicit: **institutions are about power and politics**. Ignoring this produces technically sound plans that never get implemented.

Key PEA questions for institutional strengthening:

1. **Who benefits** from the current institutional arrangement? (Identify vested interests in the status quo.)
2. **Who loses** from proposed reforms? (Predict resistance sources.)
3. **What are the formal vs. informal rules** governing behaviour? (Formal procedures may be overridden by patronage, ethnic networks, or political cycles.)
4. **Where are the reform champions?** (Identify individuals and coalitions with both motivation and positional power.)
5. **What is the electoral/political cycle?** (Reforms launched 6 months before elections rarely survive.)

**OECD-DAC guidance**: political economy analysis should be conducted early and updated periodically — not as a one-off study but as a living assessment informing adaptive programming.

### 1.5 Power Analysis in Institutional Settings

Drawing on Lukes' three dimensions of power (visible, hidden, invisible):

- **Visible power**: formal authority, legal mandates, budget control, appointment powers. Mapped through organograms and legal review.
- **Hidden power**: agenda-setting, gatekeeping, control of information flows. Mapped through key informant interviews and process tracing.
- **Invisible power**: internalised norms, beliefs about "how things are done here," deference patterns. Mapped through cultural assessment and participatory methods.

Proposals should demonstrate awareness of all three levels. Interventions targeting only visible power (e.g., restructuring, new procedures) will fail if hidden and invisible power structures remain unchanged.

## 2. Institutional Strengthening Approaches

### 2.1 UNDP 3-Level Framework Applied to Institutional Strengthening

The UNDP Capacity Development Framework operates at three mutually reinforcing levels:

| Level | Focus | Typical Interventions |
|---|---|---|
| **Enabling environment** | Policies, legal frameworks, power relations, social norms | Policy reform, regulatory development, sector coordination mechanisms, public finance reform |
| **Organisational** | Internal structures, systems, resources, management | Organisational restructuring, systems development (HR, finance, M&E), strategic planning, quality management |
| **Individual** | Knowledge, skills, competencies, motivation | Training, mentoring, coaching, professional development, study tours, communities of practice |

**Critical principle**: interventions at all three levels must be aligned. Training individuals (Level 3) without fixing broken systems (Level 2) within a hostile policy environment (Level 1) wastes resources. Proposals should explicitly address how interventions at each level reinforce each other.

### 2.2 Organisational Development Interventions

Four intervention domains for organisational strengthening:

**Structure**: mandate clarification, functional review, restructuring, decentralisation of authority, departmental realignment, span of control adjustment, creation of new units (e.g., M&E, quality assurance, ICT).

**Systems**: HR management (recruitment, appraisal, promotion), financial management (budgeting, accounting, internal audit), information management (MIS, data governance), planning and reporting systems, procurement systems, records management.

**Culture**: values and ethics frameworks, performance culture development, client orientation, accountability norms, innovation incentives, knowledge-sharing behaviour, change readiness.

**People**: competency frameworks, training needs assessment, structured training programmes, on-the-job coaching, mentoring, leadership development, succession planning, retention strategies.

### 2.3 Public Sector Institutional Strengthening

Common public sector IS intervention packages:

- **Regulatory reform**: review and update of Acts, regulations, and guidelines; development of new regulatory instruments; regulatory impact assessment; stakeholder consultation processes.
- **Service delivery improvement**: service standards development, citizen charters, one-stop centres, process re-engineering, queue management, feedback mechanisms, mystery client exercises.
- **Decentralisation support**: capacity building of local governments, fiscal transfer systems, local revenue enhancement, accountability mechanisms, central-local coordination.
- **Public financial management**: budget preparation and execution, IFMIS implementation, internal audit strengthening, external audit follow-up, procurement reform.
- **Human resource management reform**: establishment reviews, job evaluation, performance management systems, training policies, payroll cleaning.

### 2.4 Civil Society Strengthening

**CBO Capacity Development Cycle** (Ubels, Ch. 15):

1. **Formation/mobilisation** — community identification of need, group formation, basic governance
2. **Organisational basics** — constitution, registration, leadership election, record-keeping
3. **Programme development** — needs assessment, activity planning, resource mobilisation
4. **Institutional maturity** — systems formalisation, financial management, partnerships, M&E
5. **Sustainability/graduation** — self-financing mechanisms, federation/networking, policy engagement

**Elements of CBO capacity** (Ubels): governance, management, financial viability, programme delivery, external relations, learning ability.

**HakiElimu Tanzania case** (Ubels, Ch. 12): demonstrates how CSOs can strengthen public accountability in education by combining community scorecards, media campaigns, and policy dialogue. Model for demand-side governance interventions.

### 2.5 Private Sector Institutional Development

**Value chain approach to CD** (Ubels, Ch. 14 — Uganda agricultural case):

- Multi-stakeholder processes involving farmers, processors, traders, input suppliers, regulators, and financiers.
- Institutional strengthening of sector associations, cooperatives, and market platforms.
- Business development services (BDS) market development rather than direct provision.
- Regulatory and standards frameworks that enable private sector growth.
- Public-private dialogue platforms for policy reform.

**Bridging the micro-macro gap** (Ubels, Ch. 13): connect local-level CD interventions to national policy processes. Local CBOs and farmer groups need pathways to influence district and national planning. This requires federation, networking, and intermediary organisations.

## 3. Leadership and Governance Development

### 3.1 Typology of Leaders

Ubels (Ch. 16) identifies several leader types relevant to CD:

- **Positional leaders**: hold formal authority (directors, ministers, board chairs). CD focuses on management competence and strategic vision.
- **Emergent leaders**: rise through demonstrated competence or charisma. CD focuses on formalising influence and building coalitions.
- **Thought leaders**: shape discourse and ideas. CD focuses on platforms, publications, and policy engagement.
- **Bridging leaders**: connect sectors, levels, or communities. CD focuses on convening skills, negotiation, and network management.
- **Quiet leaders**: effect change through consistent, low-profile effort. CD focuses on support systems and peer networks.

**Practical note**: proposals should identify which leader types exist in the client organisation and design interventions accordingly. A leadership programme for positional leaders differs fundamentally from one for emergent leaders.

### 3.2 Leadership for Change Programme Model

The PLC curriculum (Ubels, Ch. 16) structures leadership development around:

1. **Self-awareness modules**: personal leadership style, strengths and blind spots, values clarification
2. **Contextual analysis**: understanding the institutional environment, reading political dynamics, scanning for opportunities
3. **Vision and strategy**: articulating compelling direction, strategic planning, prioritisation
4. **Influencing and mobilisation**: stakeholder engagement, coalition building, negotiation, communication
5. **Managing complexity**: adaptive management, dealing with uncertainty, resilience under pressure
6. **Peer learning**: cohort-based learning, action learning sets, mentoring pairs

**Delivery format**: typically 4–6 residential modules over 12–18 months, with action learning projects between modules. Participants implement real change initiatives in their organisations and report back.

### 3.3 Leadership Theories Applied to CD Interventions

| Theory | CD Application |
|---|---|
| **Transformational leadership** | Vision-casting, inspirational motivation — used in senior leadership retreats |
| **Servant leadership** | Service orientation, empowerment — used in public service reform |
| **Adaptive leadership** (Heifetz) | Distinguishing technical from adaptive challenges — used in complex reform programmes |
| **Distributed leadership** | Building leadership capacity across the organisation, not just at the top — used in decentralisation |
| **Situational leadership** (Hersey-Blanchard) | Matching leadership style to follower maturity — used in coaching programmes |

### 3.4 Governance Capacity Building

Key governance interventions for institutional strengthening:

- **Board development**: governance frameworks, roles and responsibilities, fiduciary duties, committee structures, board evaluation, succession planning.
- **Oversight mechanisms**: internal audit, compliance functions, risk management committees, whistleblower systems.
- **Transparency and accountability**: reporting standards, information disclosure, citizen participation, social accountability tools (community scorecards, public expenditure tracking, citizen report cards).
- **Anti-corruption**: integrity frameworks, conflict of interest policies, asset declaration systems, ethics training.

### 3.5 Leader as Designer, Teacher, Steward (Senge)

Senge redefines leadership for learning organisations:

- **Leader as designer**: designing the organisation's governing ideas (purpose, vision, core values) and its structures, policies, and learning processes. Not commanding but creating conditions for others to succeed.
- **Leader as teacher**: not lecturing but helping people develop systemic understanding. Surfacing mental models. Facilitating learning from experience.
- **Leader as steward**: serving the organisation's larger purpose. Personal commitment to the mission. Modelling the values and behaviours expected of others.

**Proposal application**: use this framework when designing leadership development components that emphasise institutional culture change rather than individual skill-building.

## 4. Knowledge Management and Organisational Learning

### 4.1 Learning Organisation Principles (Senge's Five Disciplines Applied)

Senge's five disciplines translate into concrete institutional strengthening interventions:

**Personal mastery**: individual commitment to continuous learning. Interventions — professional development policies, learning budgets, reflective practice, coaching.

**Mental models**: surfacing and testing assumptions that drive institutional behaviour. Interventions — facilitated dialogue sessions, scenario planning, assumption-testing workshops, "ladder of inference" exercises.

**Shared vision**: building genuine commitment to a common direction. Interventions — participatory strategic planning, vision-building workshops, alignment of individual goals with organisational purpose.

**Team learning**: groups thinking together more effectively than individuals. Interventions — action learning sets, cross-functional teams, dialogue practices (Bohm), after-action reviews.

**Systems thinking**: understanding interconnections, feedback loops, and unintended consequences. Interventions — causal loop diagramming, system dynamics workshops, root cause analysis, "iceberg model" analysis (events → patterns → structures → mental models).

### 4.2 Knowledge Networking for CD

Ubels (Ch. 17) on knowledge networks:

- **Definition**: groups of practitioners and organisations that create, share, and apply knowledge around a common domain.
- **Network functions**: peer learning, joint problem-solving, innovation, advocacy, quality assurance.
- **Design principles**: voluntary participation, clear purpose, active facilitation, balance between structure and flexibility, mixed membership (practitioners, researchers, policymakers).
- **Success factors**: dedicated coordination, regular interaction rhythm, tangible outputs (case studies, tools, policy briefs), online platform for between-meeting exchange.
- **Risks**: elite capture, talk without action, donor dependency, coordination fatigue.

### 4.3 Communities of Practice

Practical model for embedding knowledge management in institutional strengthening:

1. **Domain**: clearly defined area of shared interest (e.g., "public procurement," "child protection case management").
2. **Community**: group of practitioners who interact regularly to deepen expertise.
3. **Practice**: shared repertoire of resources — tools, templates, case studies, standards.

**Launch sequence**: identify domain → recruit founding members → establish rhythm (monthly meetings, online forum) → facilitate knowledge creation → document and disseminate → connect to decision-making.

### 4.4 Action Learning Sets

Structured peer learning for institutional capacity building:

- Small groups (4–8) meeting regularly over 6–12 months.
- Each session: one member presents a real work challenge; others ask questions (not advice); presenter develops action plan; follow-up at next session.
- Facilitator ensures discipline (questions before solutions, equal air time, action commitment).
- Particularly effective for mid-level managers developing problem-solving and leadership capacity.

### 4.5 Documentation and Knowledge Capture

Institutional memory interventions:

- **Process documentation**: standard operating procedures, work instructions, decision registers.
- **Lessons learned systems**: structured capture after each project/activity, searchable database, mandatory review before starting new initiatives.
- **Case study development**: detailed write-ups of institutional change processes for peer learning.
- **Knowledge audits**: systematic assessment of what the organisation knows, what it needs to know, and where the gaps lie.
- **Handover protocols**: structured knowledge transfer when staff rotate or depart.

### 4.6 Peer Learning and Exchange Visits

Cost-effective CD tool for institutional strengthening:

- **South-South exchanges**: peer institutions in similar contexts sharing practical experience.
- **Study visits**: structured exposure to best practice institutions (not tourism — clear learning objectives, pre-visit preparation, post-visit action plans).
- **Twinning arrangements**: sustained partnership between two institutions for mutual learning and mentoring.
- **Peer review mechanisms**: structured assessment of one institution by peers from comparable institutions (e.g., APRM model, EU Open Method of Coordination).

## 5. Sustainability and Exit Planning

### 5.1 Phase-Down, Phase-Over, Phase-Out Typology

Three exit strategy models:

| Strategy | Description | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Phase-down** | Gradual reduction of external support while building internal capacity | Long-term programmes with strong local counterparts |
| **Phase-over** | Transfer of responsibilities to a specific local entity (government, CSO, private sector) | Where a credible successor institution exists |
| **Phase-out** | Complete withdrawal after achieving self-sustaining outcomes | Where the need for external support genuinely ends |

**Practical principle**: exit strategy should be designed at programme inception, not as an afterthought. Each year of implementation should demonstrate measurable progress towards sustainability.

### 5.2 Three Critical Sustainability Factors

For institutional strengthening outcomes to endure, three conditions must be met simultaneously:

**Sustained resources**: the institution can fund recurrent costs (staff, operations, maintenance) from domestic revenue or predictable income streams. Test: if donor funding stopped tomorrow, could the institution pay salaries and utility bills next month?

**Sustained capacity**: the institution retains the skills, systems, and knowledge to perform. Test: if the three most skilled staff left, could the institution still deliver its core functions within 90 days?

**Sustained motivation**: leadership and staff remain committed to the institution's mission and performance standards. Test: are there internal incentives (career progression, professional recognition, performance culture) that drive performance independent of external monitoring?

**Sustainability risk**: if any one factor fails, the other two will eventually degrade. A well-funded institution with no capacity wastes resources. A capable institution with no funding cannot retain staff. A resourced and capable institution with no motivation drifts into bureaucratic inertia.

### 5.3 Institutional Embedding Checklist

Use this checklist to assess whether CD outcomes are embedded in institutional fabric:

| Embedding Dimension | Question | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Policy** | Are new practices mandated or authorised by policy/regulation? | Gazette notice, ministerial directive, board resolution |
| **Budget** | Are recurrent costs included in the institution's approved budget? | Budget line items, MTEF allocation, evidence of disbursement |
| **Staffing** | Are positions on the approved establishment? Are they filled? | Establishment warrant, recruitment records, payroll |
| **Systems** | Are procedures documented in SOPs/manuals? Are systems operational? | Approved SOP manual, system uptime records, user activity logs |
| **Culture** | Do staff follow new practices without external prompting? | Observation, staff interviews, client feedback, mystery client results |
| **Incentives** | Do performance management systems reward the desired behaviours? | Appraisal criteria, promotion records, recognition programmes |
| **Accountability** | Are there reporting requirements and oversight mechanisms? | Reporting templates, oversight body minutes, audit findings |

### 5.4 Ownership Transfer Mechanisms

Structured approaches to transferring ownership from project to institution:

- **Counterpart staffing**: from Day 1, pair every external consultant with an internal counterpart who gradually assumes full responsibility.
- **Shadowing and co-delivery**: internal staff observe, then co-facilitate, then lead — with external support available but not driving.
- **Progressive handover milestones**: defined points at which specific functions transfer. Each milestone has readiness criteria and sign-off.
- **Residual support agreements**: after formal handover, limited technical backstopping available for a defined period (e.g., 6 months of helpdesk/remote support).
- **MOU for partner commitment** (USAID HICD): formalised agreement between project and partner institution specifying mutual commitments, milestones, and accountability for CD outcomes.

### 5.5 Financial Sustainability Planning

Interventions to ensure institutional financial viability post-project:

- **Revenue diversification**: identify multiple funding sources (government budget, fees, grants, earned income, membership dues).
- **Cost recovery mechanisms**: user fees, service charges, licensing fees — designed to cover recurrent costs.
- **Financial management strengthening**: budgeting, accounting, internal controls, audit — so the institution can manage funds credibly.
- **Fundraising/resource mobilisation capacity**: grant writing, proposal development, donor relationship management, public-private partnerships.
- **Endowment/reserve fund**: where appropriate, capitalise a fund whose returns cover core operations.

### 5.6 Risk Factors for Sustainability Failure

Common reasons institutional strengthening outcomes fail to sustain:

1. **Political turnover**: new leadership reverses predecessor's reforms (mitigate through legislative embedding, cross-party support).
2. **Staff turnover**: trained staff leave for better-paid positions (mitigate through retention strategies, institutional knowledge systems, training of trainers).
3. **Budget cuts**: fiscal pressure reduces allocations to reformed functions (mitigate through demonstrating value, ring-fencing mechanisms).
4. **Dependency syndrome**: institution becomes reliant on external technical assistance and cannot function without it (mitigate through deliberate capacity transfer from Day 1).
5. **Reform fatigue**: after prolonged change processes, staff revert to old habits (mitigate through quick wins, visible benefits, sustained change management).
6. **Parallel structures**: project creates units or systems that sit outside the institution's mainstream (mitigate through integration into existing structures from the outset).

## 6. M&E of Institutional Strengthening

### 6.1 Measuring Capacity Change

Combining qualitative and quantitative approaches (drawing on Ubels, Ch. 18):

**Quantitative indicators**: staff numbers, budget execution rates, service delivery volumes, processing times, error rates, compliance scores, system uptime.

**Qualitative indicators**: quality of decision-making, leadership effectiveness, organisational culture, stakeholder perceptions, adaptive capacity, innovation behaviour.

**Mixed methods approach**: quantitative data shows what changed; qualitative data explains why and how. Neither alone is sufficient for capacity measurement.

**Capacity assessment tools**:
- Organisational Capacity Assessment Tool (OCAT): scored self-assessment across governance, management, HR, financial management, service delivery, external relations.
- 5Cs framework (ECDPM): capability to act, to deliver, to relate, to adapt, to achieve coherence.
- Balanced scorecard adapted for institutional capacity: financial, internal processes, learning and growth, stakeholder perspectives.

### 6.2 Time Frames: Outputs, Capability Outcomes, Impact

Ubels (Ch. 19) insists that **time matters** in CD evaluation. Three distinct time frames must be tracked:

| Time Frame | What to Measure | Typical Indicators | When Measurable |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Short-term (outputs)** | CD activities delivered | Number trained, systems installed, documents produced, events held | During implementation |
| **Medium-term (capability outcomes)** | Institutional behaviour change | Staff apply new skills, systems used routinely, decision quality improves, procedures followed | 6–18 months after intervention |
| **Long-term (impact)** | Development results from strengthened institutions | Service delivery outcomes, citizen satisfaction, policy implementation, sector performance | 2–5 years post-intervention |

**Critical warning**: most CD programmes are evaluated only at output level (training delivered, manuals produced). This tells you nothing about whether the institution actually strengthened. Proposals should include capability outcome indicators and specify how they will be measured.

### 6.3 Capacity Development Indicators by Level

| UNDP Level | Sample Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Enabling environment** | Policy adopted and gazetted; regulatory framework operational; coordination mechanism meets regularly; budget allocation for the function |
| **Organisational** | Organogram reflects new functions; SOPs documented and in use; financial systems produce timely reports; M&E data used in decision-making; client satisfaction scores |
| **Individual** | Post-training competency assessment scores; staff performance appraisal ratings; staff retention rates; evidence of skill application on the job |

### 6.4 Outcome Mapping for CD

Outcome mapping (Earl, Carden & Smutylo) is particularly suited to institutional strengthening because it focuses on behaviour change in "boundary partners" rather than attribution of development outcomes:

1. **Intentional design**: define vision, mission, boundary partners, outcome challenges, progress markers.
2. **Outcome and performance monitoring**: outcome journals tracking behaviour change, strategy journal tracking CD activities, performance journal tracking organisational practice.
3. **Evaluation planning**: define evaluation questions focused on contribution rather than attribution.

**Progress markers** (three levels): expect to see → like to see → love to see. This graduated scale is realistic for institutional change, which is non-linear and context-dependent.

### 6.5 Most Significant Change Technique

Participatory M&E method well-suited to capturing institutional strengthening stories:

1. Collect stories of change from stakeholders at all levels.
2. Selection panels (including programme participants) discuss and select the most significant stories.
3. Selected stories are analysed for patterns and themes.
4. Process generates both evidence and learning.

**Strengths for IS**: captures unexpected changes, reveals institutional dynamics not visible through quantitative indicators, builds evaluative thinking capacity in partner institutions.

### 6.6 Reconciling Accountability and Learning

Ubels (Ch. 21) addresses the fundamental tension in CD M&E:

- **Accountability** demands proof of results, attribution, value for money — driving standardised measurement, upward reporting, and risk aversion.
- **Learning** demands honesty about failure, experimentation, adaptation — driving reflective practice, peer exchange, and tolerance for uncertainty.

**Practical resolution**:
- Separate accountability reporting (for donors) from learning processes (for programme teams and partners).
- Use theory of change as the common framework — accountability reports against the ToC; learning refines the ToC.
- Invest in safe spaces for honest reflection (peer reviews, after-action reviews, learning events) that are distinct from formal reporting.
- Locate complexity honestly: acknowledge what can be measured and attributed, and what can only be assessed through contribution analysis and qualitative evidence.

## 7. Proposal Strategy Notes

### 7.1 When to Propose Institutional Strengthening

Institutional strengthening is appropriate when the Terms of Reference signal any of the following:

- Establishment of a new institution, unit, or function
- Organisational review, restructuring, or reform
- Systems development or strengthening (HR, finance, M&E, MIS)
- Governance improvement, accountability, transparency
- Capacity building that goes beyond individual training to address systems and structures
- Sustainability planning, exit strategy design, handover
- Sector coordination, institutional framework, regulatory reform
- Decentralisation, devolution, or deconcentration support
- Civil society strengthening, CBO development
- Value chain development with institutional dimensions

### 7.2 Common Deliverables

Typical deliverable set for institutional strengthening assignments:

1. **Institutional/organisational assessment report** — baseline analysis using governance assessment framework, functional review, capacity scorecard
2. **Organisational development plan** — prioritised interventions across structure, systems, culture, and people; phased over 1–3 years
3. **Governance framework** — mandate clarification, board/committee ToRs, decision-making protocols, accountability mechanisms
4. **Training and mentoring programme** — needs assessment, curriculum design, delivery plan, competency framework, post-training follow-up
5. **Knowledge management strategy** — knowledge audit, KM system design, communities of practice plan, documentation standards
6. **Systems and procedures manuals** — SOPs, process maps, user guides, job descriptions, performance standards
7. **Sustainability/exit plan** — institutional embedding checklist, ownership transfer milestones, financial sustainability plan, risk assessment
8. **M&E framework for capacity change** — indicators at output, capability outcome, and impact levels; mixed methods evaluation plan

### 7.3 Budget Structure for Institutional Strengthening

| Cost Category | Typical Components | Budget Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| **Personnel** | Institutional development specialist, OD consultant, governance advisor, sector specialist, M&E specialist, training facilitators | Senior IS specialists: USD 800–1,500/day (East Africa market) |
| **Assessment and diagnosis** | Institutional assessment, functional review, capacity scorecard, stakeholder analysis | 10–15% of technical budget |
| **Systems development** | Procedures manuals, MIS/IT systems, HR systems, financial systems | 15–25% (higher if IT component) |
| **Training and mentoring** | Workshops, coaching, study visits, peer learning, action learning | 20–30% |
| **Knowledge management** | KM system, documentation, communities of practice, knowledge products | 5–10% |
| **Governance strengthening** | Board development, oversight mechanisms, accountability tools | 5–10% |
| **M&E of capacity change** | Baseline assessment, mid-term review, endline, MSC stories, outcome mapping | 5–8% |
| **Sustainability/exit** | Embedding activities, handover support, residual backstopping | 5–10% (back-loaded) |

### 7.4 Linking to Donor Frameworks

**World Bank**: institutional strengthening aligns with Implementation Support Reviews (ISR), institutional and policy reform components, and the Bank's Governance and Institutions practice area. Use ISR ratings (Moderately Satisfactory, Satisfactory, Highly Satisfactory) as benchmarks.

**USAID**: the Human and Institutional Capacity Development (HICD) methodology is the standard framework. Proposals for USAID-funded work should explicitly reference HICD's 7-step performance improvement process: (1) define desired performance, (2) identify performance gaps, (3) find root causes, (4) select interventions, (5) ensure enabling environment, (6) implement interventions, (7) monitor and evaluate.

**AfDB**: institutional support components feature prominently in AfDB project design. Reference the Bank's governance action plan, country strategy paper (CSP) governance pillars, and Project Completion Report (PCR) sustainability ratings.

**UNDP**: capacity development is central to UNDP programming. Reference the 3-level framework (individual, organisational, enabling environment) and UNDP's capacity development practice notes. Align with Country Programme Document (CPD) outcomes.

**OECD-DAC**: the Paris Declaration principles (ownership, alignment, harmonisation, results, mutual accountability) provide the normative framework. Proposals should demonstrate how institutional strengthening supports country ownership and uses country systems.

### 7.5 CD Market and Local Provision (Ubels, Ch. 23)

Ubels identifies the importance of **stimulating local CD support provision** rather than relying on international consultants:

- **Types of CD services**: training, coaching, facilitation, organisational development, research, evaluation, knowledge brokering, network management, policy advice.
- **Market terminology**: distinguish between supply-driven CD (providers push products) and demand-driven CD (institutions articulate needs). Best practice is demand-driven with informed choice.
- **Funding logics**: direct funding (institution buys CD services), voucher systems, competitive grants, embedded technical assistance, cost-sharing.

**Proposal implication**: where possible, propose using local CD providers, building their capacity alongside the client institution. This creates a sustainable local market for CD services after the project ends.

## Quick-Reference: Institutional Strengthening Design Sequence

For use during proposal methodology design:

1. **Assess** — institutional mapping, governance assessment, functional review, capacity scorecard, political economy analysis
2. **Design** — OD plan prioritising structure, systems, culture, people interventions at all three UNDP levels
3. **Engage** — stakeholder mobilisation, leadership engagement, change management, coalition building
4. **Implement** — phased delivery of systems, training, governance, knowledge management interventions
5. **Embed** — institutional embedding (policy, budget, staffing, systems, culture, incentives, accountability)
6. **Transfer** — progressive handover with counterpart development, residual backstopping, MOU commitments
7. **Sustain** — sustainability assessment, financial viability planning, risk mitigation, ongoing learning mechanisms
8. **Evaluate** — mixed methods M&E across output, capability outcome, and impact time frames
