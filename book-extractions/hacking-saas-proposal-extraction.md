# Hacking SaaS — Proposal Extraction (2026)

Internal synthesis for the proposal-skills engine. Source: Ben Cotton, *Hacking SaaS* (essay collection on SaaS commercial growth, sales enablement, marketing arbitrage, and team design). Use as audit trail and implementation source, not for redistribution.

## Source Awareness

The book is structured as essays on SaaS commercial growth: product-market fit, channel arbitrage (paid search, mobile, social channel rise/fall, podcasting, messaging/bots, CRO), the case for building sales teams of more than one rep from day one, sales enablement, sales and marketing alignment, inbound marketing, and the structure of a recurring-revenue go-to-market engine. The tone is practitioner essay, not framework manual.

## Proposal-Relevant Synthesis

### GTM Motion as a Costable Workstream
The book's central insight — that SaaS commercial growth depends on the right combination of motion (PLG vs inside-sales vs field vs hybrid), channel, and team shape — translates into a proposal artefact: a GTM motion design workstream that the agency scopes and prices. This is especially relevant when the SaaS implementation includes commercial launch (e.g., regulator launching a new digital service, bank launching a digital lending product, insurer launching a self-service portal).

### Channel Arbitrage and Decay
The "channels and tactics decay over time" framing supports a proposal anti-pattern call-out: never propose a launch plan tied to a single channel without a decay-monitoring layer. The methodology should commit to channel-portfolio review at fixed cadence with explicit reallocation triggers.

### Sales-Team Design as Deliverable
The principle that you should never build a SaaS sales team with just one rep translates to a delivery-design rule: any SaaS implementation that hands operating responsibility to a client team must propose redundancy in critical roles (admin, support, success, billing) — not because of the book, but because the same logic prevents single-point-of-failure risk in the client's operating model after handover.

### Sales Enablement and Sales-Marketing Alignment
The book's recurring theme of sales enablement (playbooks, content, training, CRM hygiene, lead-handoff rules) becomes a proposal workstream: when scoping SaaS implementation for an organisation with a sales function (banks, insurers, B2B SaaS clients of the agency), include a sales enablement workstream covering playbook authorship, training, CRM configuration, lead-routing rules, and a 90-day handover review.

### Pricing Experimentation and Growth Models
The essay collection's encouragement of structured experimentation (A/B, channel pilots, pricing tests) maps to two proposal artefacts: (1) a hypothesis-and-experiment register inside the methodology section, and (2) a pricing-experimentation plan as part of the commercial-launch deliverable for SaaS products where the client owns the SaaS revenue model.

### Two-of-Everything Principle
Wherever the book argues against single-rep teams, the proposal should mirror the principle in delivery design: two-of-everything for critical roles during implementation (lead + shadow, primary + backup admin, two trained product owners) so the client's operating capacity is resilient on day one of cutover.
