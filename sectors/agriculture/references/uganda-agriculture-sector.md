# Uganda Agriculture Sector Profile

## 1. Sector Overview

Agriculture remains the backbone of Uganda's economy, averaging **24.3% of GDP** over the period 2019–2024. The sector employs **70% of the workforce** (up from 61.4% in 2019), underscoring its centrality to livelihoods and poverty reduction.

**GDP sub-sector contributions:**

| Sub-sector | Share of GDP |
|---|---|
| Food crops | 11.8% |
| Cash crops | 4.1% |
| Livestock | 4.0% |
| Forestry | 3.9% |
| Fishing | 3.8% |

**Growth trajectory:** Agricultural growth has been volatile — reaching 7.4% in 2019, falling sharply to 1.2% in 2021 (driven by climate shocks and pandemic disruption), and rebounding to 7.2% in 2024.

**Export performance:** Agricultural exports grew by **42%**, rising from USD 1.7 billion (FY2020/21) to USD 2.4 billion (FY2024/25). Leading export commodities include coffee, dairy products, fish, and tea.

## 2. Food Security, Nutrition, and Fisheries

Uganda faces serious food security and nutrition challenges despite its agricultural potential:

- **Global Hunger Index:** 27.3 (2024), classified at the *serious* level.
- **Undernourishment:** 36.9% of the population (2022), well above the East Africa average of 28.5%.
- **Food insecurity:** 71.2% of the population experienced moderate or severe food insecurity (2022).
- **Stunting (children under 5):** 29% (2022), reflecting chronic malnutrition with long-term human capital consequences.

**Post-harvest losses** represent a major structural drain on food availability:

- Grains: **40%** lost post-harvest
- Fruits and vegetables: **30–80%** lost post-harvest
- Beans: **25%** lost post-harvest
- Fish: **45%** lost post-harvest

**Fisheries sub-sector:**

- Fisheries contribute approximately **3% of national GDP** (2024).
- **54% of Uganda's 159 national fish species** are listed as endangered (2024), raising sustainability concerns for both food security and export earnings.
- Only **51% of water bodies** meet good ambient water quality standards, affecting aquatic ecosystem health and fisheries productivity.

## 3. Productivity Constraints

Uganda's agricultural productivity remains well below potential, constrained by structural weaknesses across the production system:

- **Rain-fed farming dependence:** The overwhelming majority of agriculture relies on rainfall, exposing the sector to climate variability and drought.
- **Minimal irrigation coverage:** Only **71,179 hectares** are under irrigation, representing less than **3% of the estimated irrigation potential**.
- **Poor technology adoption:** Uptake of improved seeds, fertilisers, and mechanisation remains low, particularly among smallholders.
- **Weak extension services:** The farmer-to-extension-worker ratio is severely inadequate, limiting knowledge transfer and best-practice dissemination.
- **Inadequate post-harvest handling:** Losses remain high due to insufficient storage, drying facilities, and cold chain infrastructure.
- **Rising recurrent costs and climate shocks:** Increasing input costs, combined with more frequent droughts, floods, and pest outbreaks, erode farmer incomes and deter investment.
- **Fragmented value chains:** End-to-end integration — from inputs and extension services through to storage, processing, and market information — remains weak, limiting commercialisation and farmer returns.
- **Limited climate-smart agriculture adoption:** Drought-tolerant crop varieties, soil moisture conservation techniques, and integrated pest management practices have yet to reach scale, despite Uganda's acute climate vulnerability (ND-GAIN rank 172/191).

## 4. Spatial Distribution and Value Chains

Agricultural production and commercialisation are unevenly distributed across Uganda's regions:

- **Northern and Eastern Uganda** lag in infrastructure and commercialisation despite possessing vast tracts of uncultivated land with significant agrarian potential.
- **Central and Western Uganda** dominate high-value crop production, accounting for **72% of coffee output** and **80% of milk production**.
- **Emerging clusters** are developing around Mount Elgon (Arabica coffee) and Southwestern Uganda (dairy), driven by favourable agro-climatic conditions and nascent value chain organisation.
- **NDP IV strategic commodity zones** have been designated to exploit regional comparative advantages: West Nile (oilseeds), Eastern Uganda (cereals), and Southwestern Uganda (dairy).
- Uganda's **agro-climatic diversity** — spanning tropical lowlands, highland zones, and semi-arid areas — supports a broad range of crops and livestock systems.

## 5. Market Efficiency

Market systems remain underdeveloped, limiting the translation of production gains into farmer incomes:

- **Smallholder dominance** constrains the quality, consistency, and volume of marketed output.
- **Informal markets** lack standardisation, grading systems, and reliable linkages to formal buyers.
- **High transaction costs** stem from poor rural road networks, limited market information systems, and inadequate storage and transport infrastructure.
- **Regulatory gaps and middlemen dominance** hinder fair pricing, with farmers frequently receiving a fraction of final market prices.
- **Government interventions** to improve market efficiency include warehouse receipt systems, milk collection centres, and digital e-trade platforms.
- **Persistent barriers** include costly agricultural credit, weak crop and livestock insurance markets, and inadequate extension services to support market-oriented production.

## 6. Agro-Industrialisation Potential

Uganda has significant untapped potential for agro-industrialisation across multiple value chains:

**Priority processing opportunities:**
- Coffee roasting and specialty processing
- Dairy processing (UHT, yoghurt, cheese)
- Grain milling and fortification
- Fruit and vegetable packaging and preservation

**Enabling requirements:**
- Irrigation investment to stabilise raw material supply
- Mechanisation to increase production volumes and reduce unit costs
- Cold chain storage and logistics infrastructure
- Rural road networks connecting production zones to processing facilities
- Reliable and affordable energy access for processing operations

**Market linkage models:**
- Contract farming and outgrower schemes to guarantee supply volumes and quality
- Vertical integration linking smallholders to processors and exporters
- Quality standards, certification, and branding essential for premium market access, particularly for coffee and dairy in international markets

## 7. Recent Reforms

The Government of Uganda has undertaken several reform initiatives to transform agriculture:

- **Parish Development Model (PDM):** A flagship programme to integrate poor and subsistence farmers into the market economy through enterprise selection, financial inclusion, and community-level support structures.
- **Institutional consolidation:** The Coffee Development Authority and Dairy Development Authority have been integrated into the Ministry of Agriculture, Animal Industry and Fisheries to streamline coordination and reduce duplication.
- **NDP IV Agro-Industrialisation Programme:** Includes establishment of a national seed company and cost-sharing mechanisms for livestock vaccines to improve productivity at scale.
- **Agriculture Finance Policy:** Designed to facilitate value-chain credit lines and improve smallholder access to affordable agricultural finance.
- **EAC legal framework harmonisation:** Ongoing efforts to align standards and remove non-tariff barriers within the East African Community, facilitating cross-border agricultural trade.

## 8. SDG Pathway Alignment

Agriculture is central to Uganda's SDG acceleration strategy, cutting across two of the three nationally prioritised pathways identified in the UN Country Analysis 2025:

- **Pathway 1 — Industrialisation via agro-processing:** Agriculture provides the raw material base for value addition. Strengthening post-harvest handling, storage, and processing infrastructure directly supports the transition from commodity export to processed-goods export, with knock-on effects for employment, trade balance, and rural incomes.
- **Pathway 3 — Climate resilience:** Given Uganda's extreme climate vulnerability (ND-GAIN rank 172/191), scaling climate-smart agricultural practices — drought-tolerant varieties, soil moisture conservation, integrated pest management, and sustainable fisheries management — is essential for protecting livelihoods and food security under changing climatic conditions.

These pathway linkages provide a strong framing for proposals that position agricultural interventions as accelerators of broader national development outcomes.

## Sustainable Agriculture Frameworks

### Environmental Costs of Conventional Agriculture
When proposals involve agricultural modernisation or intensification, acknowledge externalities:
- **Pesticide costs**: health impacts on farm workers, water contamination, biodiversity loss, resistance development
- **Soil degradation**: erosion, nutrient depletion, compaction, salinisation from irrigation
- **Water pollution**: nitrate/phosphate runoff, eutrophication, pesticide residues in groundwater
- **GHG emissions**: methane from livestock/rice paddies, nitrous oxide from fertilisers, CO2 from land clearing
- **Biodiversity loss**: habitat destruction, monoculture, loss of pollinators

### Sustainable Intensification Approaches
For methodology sections, reference these evidence-based approaches (Pretty, 2005: 286 projects across 57 countries showed average 79% yield increase with resource-conserving methods):

| Approach | Description | Uganda Application |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Integrated Pest Management (IPM) | Biological control, crop rotation, resistant varieties, targeted chemical use | Coffee, cotton, horticulture |
| Conservation Agriculture | Minimum tillage, permanent soil cover, crop rotation | Maize-bean systems, semi-arid areas |
| Agroforestry | Trees integrated with crops/livestock | Coffee-banana systems, woodlot integration |
| Integrated Nutrient Management | Organic + targeted inorganic fertiliser, composting, green manure | Soil fertility restoration in degraded areas |
| Water Harvesting | Rainwater collection, micro-irrigation, valley tanks | Cattle corridor, Karamoja |
| Farmer Field Schools | Participatory learning, IPM training, experimentation | NAADS successor programmes |

### Trade and Market Sustainability
- **Fair trade**: premium prices for certified producers, social premiums for community development
- **Market access barriers**: tariffs, quotas, non-tariff barriers (SPS standards, quality requirements)
- **Subsidies impact**: developed-country agricultural subsidies depress world prices, undermining developing country competitiveness
- **Value addition**: processing locally rather than exporting raw commodities increases returns
- **Uganda-specific**: coffee, tea, cocoa, vanilla certification; fish export compliance (EU SPS)

### Climate-Smart Agriculture (CSA)
Three pillars relevant to Uganda proposals:
1. **Productivity**: sustainably increasing agricultural productivity and incomes
2. **Adaptation**: adapting and building resilience to climate change
3. **Mitigation**: reducing/removing GHG emissions where possible

Key CSA practices for Uganda: drought-tolerant varieties, improved water management, agroforestry, biogas from livestock waste, sustainable rice intensification (SRI).
