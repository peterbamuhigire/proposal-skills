## 1. Economic and Financial Governance

- PEFA 2022: improved since 2016, strong in fiscal transparency and debt management
- Key PFM systems rolled out: IFMS (385 entities), HCM (175 MDAs/LGs), IRAS for revenue collection
- Programme-Based Budgeting (PBB) and Integrated Bank of Projects (IBP) improved planning
- E-Government Procurement (e-GP) enhancing procurement efficiency
- PPDA Appeals Tribunal and Procuring and Disposing Entities (PDEs) reinforce procurement governance
- Remaining challenges: weak revenue forecasting, limited medium-term expenditure planning, delays in audit reviews, procurement integrity issues, budget reliability gaps

## 2. Governance Indicators

- Ibrahim Index of African Governance: 49.1/100 (rank 26/54, 2023); above EAC average (43.1) and SSA (48.9)
- Corruption Perception Index: 26/100 (2023) — indicating persistent governance integrity challenges
- INFORM Risk Index: ranked 12/191 (2024), up from 16/191 (2023) — increasing vulnerability to humanitarian and development risks
- Declines in security/rule of law and participation/rights/inclusion
- Modest improvements in foundations for economic opportunity
- AfDB CPIA: overall 4.254 (2023); Governance cluster (D) declined from 3.8 (2019) to 3.55 (2023)
- Economic Management (Cluster A): 5.0 (2023) — strong
- Structural Policies (Cluster B): 4.306
- Governance reforms lagging behind economic and structural policy progress

## 3. Fiscal Management

- Fiscal deficit: 7.6% of GDP (FY2024/25), projected to narrow to 6.5% then 5.5%
- Government revenue: 14.4% of GDP (up from 11.6% in 2015, but still below SSA average 16%)
- Tax revenue: 13.8% of GDP (below SSA average 16%)
- Grants declined from 3.3% to 0.2% of revenue
- Recurrent spending: 68.8% of total (up from 51% in FY2018/19)
- Development spending fell to 29.6%
- Interest payments: 19% of total spending (up from 10.4% in FY2018/19), consuming 31.1% of revenue
- Key development spending sectors: transport (27.1%), human capital (21.5%), governance/security (17.6%)

## 4. Public Debt

- Total: USD 32.3 billion (51.3% of GDP, June 2025)
- Domestic: 52% (UGX 16.8 billion); external: 48% (USD 15.5 billion)
- External debt: 56% concessional, 18.9% commercial (rising)
- Multilateral creditors: 66%; bilateral: 22.6%; private banks: 11%
- IMF debt distress: moderate risk, limited shock-absorption space
- S&P: B-/B (outlook revised to Positive, November 2025)
- Oil revenues expected from late 2026 (7.3% of total revenue in FY2026/27)
- Debt strategy: fiscal consolidation, prioritise concessional borrowing, develop local capital markets

## 5. State-Owned Enterprises

- 20 major SOEs in energy, transport, water, housing, aviation
- 13 profitable, 7 loss-making (FY2023/24)
- Reform focus: commercialisation, performance contracts, board restructuring
- Partial privatisation discussions for Uganda Airlines and other high-risk SOEs
- Need: performance-based management, independent boards, risk-based audits, fiscal risk reporting

## 6. Procurement Reform

- e-GP adoption improving efficiency but integrity issues persist
- PPDA (Public Procurement and Disposal of Public Assets Act) established tribunal and PDEs
- Procurement Policy and Management Department (PPMD) in MoFPED oversees reforms
- Technical support for parliamentary budget scrutiny is inadequate

## 7. Fragility Dimensions

- Political: centralised governance under long-standing leadership since 1986; democratic space concerns
- Economic: narrow tax base, high debt, corruption in public resource management, rural service gaps
- Social: ethnic divisions, youth bulge, urban-rural service delivery disparity, Karamoja tensions
- Environmental: rapid deforestation, limited disaster preparedness, climate vulnerability
- Resilience: young population (75% under 30), EAC membership, reform momentum, diaspora remittances

## 8. Reform Priorities

- Enhance PFM systems and procurement integrity
- Align revenue projections with realistic targets; enforce commitment controls
- Improve parliamentary capacity for budget scrutiny
- Expand e-GP, IFMS, HCM coverage and interoperability
- Enforce SOE transparency and performance-based management
- Operationalise beneficial ownership registries; strengthen whistleblower protections
- Build on FATF compliance (removed from grey list February 2024)
- Strengthen institutional resilience and data systems
- Gender-responsive budgeting and climate finance mainstreaming
- Address civil registration gaps: birth registration at only 40.4% (2022), with implications for identity systems, service delivery targeting, and voter rolls

## 9a. Gender and Political Inclusion

- Women in Cabinet: 45% (2025)
- Women in Parliament: 34% (2025)
- Continued need for gender-responsive governance reforms across local government and service delivery

## 9b. SDG Pathway 5 — Effective, Accountable, Transparent Institutions

Uganda's governance reform agenda aligns with SDG Pathway 5, which targets:

- Strengthening PFM systems at central and local government levels
- Improving local government planning, budgeting, supervision, and citizen feedback mechanisms
- Expanding open data, performance dashboards, and grievance redress systems
- Embedding rules-based regulation across key sectors
- Enhancing justice sector efficiency and access
- Improving coordination across central and local government levels

## 9. Key Statistics for Proposals

- IIAG score: 49.1/100; CPIA governance: 3.55; CPI: 26/100 (2023)
- INFORM Risk Index: 12/191 (2024)
- Government revenue: 14.4% of GDP; tax-to-GDP: 13.8%; fiscal deficit: 7.6% of GDP
- Public debt: 51.3% of GDP; interest: 31.1% of revenue
- 385 entities on IFMS; 175 MDAs/LGs on HCM
- Birth registration: 40.4% (2022)
- Women in Cabinet: 45%; women in Parliament: 34% (2025)
- Removed from FATF grey list February 2024
