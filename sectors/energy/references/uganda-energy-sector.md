## 1. Supply Side

- Electricity generation grew from 4,800 GWh (2019) to 5,470 GWh (2024).
- Installed capacity reached 2,048 MW (June 2024), up from 595 MW in 2010/11.
- Hydropower dominates the generation mix: 84% of output (UN 2025), with bagasse at 7%, thermal at 5%, and solar at 4%. Renewable sources account for 98% of total generation (AfDB CDN).
- Key recent projects: Isimba (183 MW) and Karuma (600 MW).
- Biomass accounts for 9% of generation; solar approximately 2%; off-grid installations total 13.35 MW.
- Thermal generation contributes less than 2%. Wind and geothermal resources remain untapped.
- Total renewable energy potential is estimated at 5,300 MW.
- Uganda has achieved a generation surplus, but demand growth has lagged behind new capacity additions.

## 2. Demand Side

- Peak demand reached 987.8 MW in 2024, or 867.9 MW excluding exports to neighbouring countries.
- This represents a 17% increase from 843 MW in 2022.
- Effective available capacity stands at approximately 1,243 MW, leaving a surplus of roughly 350 MW after accounting for losses.
- Electricity consumption has been growing at 14% annually.
- Industrial and commercial users account for 78% of demand; households consume just 22%.
- Domestic demand is projected to reach 300 MW by 2030.
- A tenfold increase to 9,185 MW is projected by 2035.

## 3. Access and Energy Mix

- Per capita electricity consumption remains extremely low at only 80 kWh per year.
- Electricity access reached 53% in 2024 (UN 2025), up from 51.5% in 2023 and 42.1% in 2019. Of the 53%, approximately 25% are grid-connected and 28% rely on off-grid solutions.
- The long-term trend is dramatic: access rose from just 5% in 2000 to 53% in 2024.
- Urban access stands at 66%, while rural access remains critically low at approximately 10%.
- Approximately 25% of the population is connected to the national grid; a further 28% rely on off-grid solutions such as solar home systems and mini-grids (UN 2025).
- Traditional biomass accounts for 87% of total energy use (2023), down marginally from 90% in 2019.
- Oil products make up 11% of the energy mix; electricity accounts for just 2%.
- Transport consumes 90% of oil products; households use 6% (primarily kerosene).
- Rural household electricity access is approximately 9%.
- **Clean cooking access remains critically low at just 4% of households** (UN 2025), underscoring the near-total dependence on traditional biomass for cooking.

## 4. Key Challenges

- **Surplus-access paradox.** Uganda has a generation surplus yet nearly half the population — and 90% of rural households — lack reliable electricity access.
- **Rural electrification below 20%.** Extending the grid to dispersed rural settlements is prohibitively expensive without targeted subsidies and innovative delivery models.
- **Heavy hydropower dependence.** With 85% of generation from hydropower, the system is vulnerable to droughts and climate variability.
- **Inadequate transmission and distribution infrastructure.** Bottlenecks in the T&D network prevent surplus generation from reaching demand centres.
- **Frequent outages and high system losses.** Technical and commercial losses erode the effective supply available to consumers.
- **High tariffs driven by take-or-pay PPAs and low consumption.** Power purchase agreements obligate payment for capacity whether or not demand materialises, pushing per-unit costs higher.
- **Poor-quality solar installations and weak after-sales service.** Substandard products and limited maintenance undermine consumer confidence in off-grid solutions.
- **Untapped geothermal potential.** Resource uncertainty and high upfront exploration costs have deterred investment.
- **Limited long-term financing for energy SMEs.** Small and medium enterprises in the energy sector struggle to access affordable capital.
- **Overlapping institutional roles.** Shared mandates between the Ministry of Energy and Mineral Development (MEMD) and the Electricity Regulatory Authority (ERA) cause coordination delays.
- **Low public awareness of modern energy services.** Many households and businesses are unaware of available alternatives to biomass and kerosene.

## 5. Reform Priorities

- **Diversify the generation mix** beyond hydropower into solar, wind, biomass, geothermal, and waste-to-energy.
- **Accelerate transmission and distribution reinforcement** to connect surplus generation to underserved areas.
- **Update quality standards** for renewable energy systems, particularly solar PV installations.
- **Reduce import taxes** on solar components to lower the cost of off-grid solutions.
- **Streamline regulatory processes** for land acquisition, water permits, and feasibility studies.
- **Create fair market conditions** for private-sector participation in both grid and off-grid electrification.
- **Expand long-term financing** instruments for energy-sector SMEs.
- **Establish a legal framework for carbon credits** under the Paris Agreement Article 6 mechanisms.
- **Implement the Clean Cooking Strategy**, targeting 50% of cooking with electricity by 2040.
- **Roll out LPG refilling stations** with appropriate safety protocols as a transitional clean cooking fuel.

## 7. SDG Pathway Context

Energy falls under **SDG Pathway 8: Digital Transformation and Energy Access** (UN Country Analysis 2025). This pathway links industrial energy efficiency, renewable power expansion, and water stewardship. Proposals should pair clean energy upgrades with cost-saving productivity improvements — demonstrating that energy access investments deliver economic returns alongside environmental benefits.
