# Uganda Education Sector Profile

## 1. Primary Education

- **Gross enrolment ratio (GER)**: 120%, reflecting over-age and under-age learners in the system.
- **Net enrolment ratio (NER)**: 78% overall (females 79%, males 77% in 2023/24), meaning 22% of primary-age children remain out of school.
- **Teacher-to-pupil ratio**: 1:75, well above the recommended 1:53 — classrooms are overcrowded and individual attention is limited.
- **Classroom-to-pupil ratio**: 1:101, indicating severe infrastructure deficits.

These figures suggest that while Uganda has achieved high nominal access through Universal Primary Education (UPE), the quality of learning remains critically constrained by teacher shortages and inadequate physical infrastructure.

## 2. Secondary Education

- **Gross enrolment ratio**: 34%.
- **Net enrolment ratio**: 23% overall (females 24%, males 22% in 2023/24).
- **Completion rate**: below 30%.
- **Household cost per learner**: approximately UGX 2,250,000 (USD 590) per year — a prohibitive burden for many families despite the Universal Secondary Education (USE) programme.

The steep drop from primary to secondary enrolment represents the single largest bottleneck in Uganda's human capital pipeline. Cost, distance, and quality perceptions all contribute to low transition and high dropout rates.

## 3. Adult Literacy

- **Women**: 84.7% literate (AfDB CDN).
- **Men**: 73.9% literate (AfDB CDN).
- **Overall literacy (2024, UN SDG)**: 74% — urban 81%, rural 77%; females 72%, males 77%. The UN figures use a different measurement methodology and capture a broader age range, yielding lower headline numbers than the AfDB CDN.
- Significant regional and socio-economic gaps persist beneath the national averages, with northern and north-eastern sub-regions recording substantially lower literacy rates.

## 4. Youth Employment and Skills Mismatch

### Labour Market Indicators
- **Youth unemployment (18–30)**: 16.5%.
- **NEET rate (15–24)**: worsening trend — 40.7% (2016) → 41.1% (2021) → **50.9% (2024)** (UN SDG). The AfDB CDN reported 42.6% using earlier data. By 2024, over half of young Ugandans are neither in employment, education, nor training — an alarming deterioration.
- Only **34% of youth** entering the labour market succeed in finding a job (UN SDG 2025).
- Only **12.86% of graduates** secure formal employment.
- **30% of employed youth** report that their first job did not match their qualifications — a clear signal of systemic skills mismatch.

### Structural Imbalance
- Approximately **600,000 young people** enter the labour market annually, against only **80,000 formal sector jobs** created — a 7.5:1 mismatch.
- **Median youth income**: UGX 168,000 (approximately USD 44) per month, reflecting the predominance of low-productivity informal employment.

### STEM and Innovation
- Shortages in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) graduates are constraining Uganda's industrialisation ambitions and its ability to attract technology-intensive investment.
- Uganda ranks **124th out of 139 countries** on the Global Innovation Index (2025), underscoring the weak link between the education system and innovation-driven economic growth.

## 5. Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET)

- TVET is expanding in recognition of the formal employment gap, but the sub-sector remains **underfunded and poorly aligned with industry needs**.
- Curricula often lag behind labour market demands, and employer engagement in programme design is limited.
- Equipment and workshop facilities are frequently outdated or insufficient for practical skills development.

## 6. National Employment Strategy

- The **National Employment Strategy 2023–2028** identifies **7 strategic sectors** for job creation and skills development.
- The strategy acknowledges the need for demand-driven skills development, apprenticeship reform, and stronger linkages between training institutions and employers.

## 7. Education Financing

- Education's share of GDP has **dropped from 7% to 4%**, undermining the sector's capacity to absorb a rapidly growing school-age population.
- The financing squeeze is most visible in teacher recruitment freezes, delayed infrastructure investment, and stagnant capitation grants to schools.

## 8. Gender and Equity

- **Girls face disproportionately high dropout rates**, driven by early marriage, teenage pregnancy, and household economic pressures.
- The adolescent birth rate of 111.4 per 1,000 girls aged 15–19 (see health sector profile) directly reduces female educational attainment.
- Regional disparities are severe: sub-regions in Karamoja and parts of northern Uganda record enrolment and completion rates far below the national average.

## 9. SDG Pathway Context (UN Country Analysis 2025)

The UN SDG Analysis Report positions education reform within two interconnected acceleration pathways:

- **Pathway 2 — Full Employment and Decent Work**: School-to-skills-to-work pathways need fundamental strengthening. Flexible vocational training models, structured apprenticeships, and demand-driven curricula are essential to close the gap between education outputs and labour market needs. With only 34% of youth finding jobs, the current pipeline is failing.
- **Pathway 4 — Human Capital Development**: Education reform should prioritise **learning outcomes and foundational skills** (literacy and numeracy) over enrolment expansion alone. Uganda's high primary GER but low secondary NER and worsening NEET rate demonstrate that access without quality and retention produces limited human capital returns.

These pathways reinforce the case for integrated programming that links education quality improvements with employment-oriented skills development.

## 10. Implications for Proposals

Proposals addressing Uganda's education and skills challenges should consider:

1. **Secondary transition and retention**: Tackling the 78% primary NER to 23% secondary NER cliff through fee reduction, bursary programmes, and community sensitisation.
2. **Quality over access**: Reducing the 1:75 teacher-to-pupil and 1:101 classroom-to-pupil ratios through recruitment, construction, and multi-shift models.
3. **TVET reform**: Aligning curricula with employer needs, modernising workshops, and establishing structured apprenticeship pathways.
4. **Skills-to-jobs pipeline**: Bridging the 600,000 entrants vs 80,000 jobs gap through entrepreneurship support, digital skills, and labour market information systems.
5. **STEM investment**: Expanding STEM teacher training, laboratory infrastructure, and scholarship programmes.
6. **Gender-responsive programming**: Addressing the specific barriers girls face — early marriage, pregnancy, safety, and opportunity cost.
7. **Education financing advocacy**: Making the case for restoring education spending from 4% towards 7% of GDP.
8. **Youth employment programmes**: Targeting the 50.9% NEET rate (2024) through integrated livelihoods, skills training, and job-matching interventions.
9. **Learning outcomes focus**: Shifting from enrolment-driven metrics to foundational literacy and numeracy outcomes, aligned with the UN SDG Pathway 4 emphasis on human capital quality.
10. **School-to-work transition**: Designing flexible vocational pathways and structured apprenticeships that respond to employer demand, addressing the finding that only 34% of youth entering the labour market find work.
