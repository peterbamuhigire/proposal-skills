# World Bank ESS6: Biodiversity Conservation and Sustainable Management of Living Natural Resources

> Dense practitioner reference for proposal methodology sections. Paragraph numbers reference the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (2018, updated 2024).

---

## 1. ESS6 Overview

### Objectives (Paragraph 1)

1. **Protect and conserve** biodiversity and habitats.
2. **Apply the mitigation hierarchy** (avoid, minimise, restore/rehabilitate, offset) to achieve no net loss or net gain of biodiversity where feasible.
3. **Promote sustainable management** of living natural resources through standards of good practice.
4. **Support livelihoods** of local communities, including indigenous peoples, that depend on biodiversity and living natural resources.

### Scope of Application (Paragraphs 2-5)

ESS6 applies to all projects that:
- Potentially affect biodiversity or habitats (natural, modified, or critical).
- Involve primary production of living natural resources (agriculture, forestry, fisheries, animal husbandry).
- Procure primary resource commodities from primary suppliers.

ESS6 does **not** apply to pathogens, micro-organisms, or genetically modified organisms (these fall under national law and ESS1).

### Assessment of Risks and Impacts (Paragraphs 6-11)

The Borrower must conduct a biodiversity-related assessment as part of the environmental and social assessment under ESS1. Key requirements:

| Element | Requirement | Paragraphs |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| **Baseline characterisation** | Identify species, habitats, ecosystem services; use existing data, field surveys, remote sensing; characterise seasonal and migratory patterns | 6-7 |
| **Precautionary approach** | Where scientific certainty is incomplete, favour conservation; absence of evidence is not evidence of absence | 8 |
| **Adaptive management** | Monitor outcomes, adjust measures as new information emerges; triggered when predicted impacts diverge from actual | 9 |
| **Mitigation hierarchy** | Avoid → Minimise → Restore/Rehabilitate → Offset; offsets are last resort, not first option | 10 |
| **Cumulative impacts** | Consider project impacts in context of other developments, land-use changes, and climate change trends in the area | 11 |

### Biodiversity Management Plan (BMP) — Indicative Content (Paragraph 12)

Required when a project may have significant impacts on biodiversity or habitats. The BMP sits within or alongside the Environmental and Social Management Plan (ESMP).

| BMP Component | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Conservation objectives** | Measurable targets tied to habitat type (no net loss for natural habitat; net gain for critical habitat) |
| **Baseline data** | Species inventories, habitat mapping, ecosystem service assessment, seasonal calendars |
| **Mitigation activities** | Specific actions per mitigation hierarchy step; includes avoidance zones, buffer areas, restoration protocols |
| **Project requirements** | Construction phase restrictions (seasonal no-go periods, noise limits, lighting protocols), operational controls |
| **Monitoring programme** | Indicators, frequency, methods, trigger thresholds for adaptive management, reporting schedule |
| **Schedule** | Phased implementation aligned with project construction and operation timelines |
| **Institutional responsibilities** | Roles (Borrower, contractor, supervising engineer, independent monitor), capacity-building needs |
| **Budget** | Line-item costs for surveys, restoration, monitoring, offsets, and contingencies |
| **Offset plan** | If offsets required: location, design, management arrangements, long-term financing, legal protections |

## 2. Habitat Classification and Requirements

### Modified Habitat (Paragraphs 19-20)

**Definition**: Areas where the natural habitat has been substantially modified by major long-term human activities — typically cultivated land, planted forests, reclaimed coastal zones, drained wetlands (paragraph 19).

**Requirements**: Where modified habitats retain **significant biodiversity value**, the Borrower must minimise further conversion or degradation and explore opportunities to enhance habitat and protect biodiversity (paragraph 20).

**Examples of modified habitats with biodiversity value**:
- Agricultural landscapes with hedgerows, woodlots, or wetlands supporting threatened species
- Planted forests harbouring understorey species or acting as wildlife corridors
- Urban green spaces or peri-urban wetlands with bird congregation areas
- Irrigation canals that serve as aquatic habitat corridors

### Natural Habitat (Paragraphs 21-22)

**Definition**: Areas composed of viable assemblages of plant and/or animal species of largely native origin, and/or where human activity has not essentially modified the area's primary ecological functions and species composition (paragraph 21).

**Requirements** (paragraph 22): The Borrower must not significantly convert or degrade natural habitat unless **all three** conditions are met:

1. No **technically and financially feasible** alternatives exist.
2. The project benefits (including the Borrower's explicit assessment of environmental services and benefits) substantially outweigh the environmental costs.
3. Any conversion or degradation is mitigated to achieve **no net loss** (preferably **net gain**) of biodiversity through appropriate measures, which may include biodiversity offsets.

**Biodiversity offsets** serve as a last resort after avoidance, minimisation, and restoration have been applied.

### Critical Habitat (Paragraphs 23-24)

**Definition**: Areas with high biodiversity value, determined by one or more of **five criteria** (paragraph 23):

| Criterion | Description | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **(a) CR/EN species** | Habitat of significant importance to Critically Endangered or Endangered species (IUCN Red List) | Nesting sites for CR vultures, spawning grounds for EN fish species |
| **(b) Endemic/restricted-range** | Habitat of significant importance to endemic or restricted-range species | Montane forests with single-site endemics, island ecosystems |
| **(c) Migratory/congregatory** | Habitat supporting significant concentrations of migratory or congregatory species | Flyway stopovers, calving grounds, fish spawning aggregations |
| **(d) Threatened ecosystems** | Highly threatened or unique ecosystems | IUCN Red List of Ecosystems CR/EN categories, remnant primary forests |
| **(e) Key ecological processes** | Areas associated with key evolutionary or ecological processes | Ecological corridors, hydrological source areas, pollinator habitat |

**Seven conditions for project activities in or near critical habitat** (paragraph 24) — **all** must be met:

1. No feasible project alternatives within the region exist.
2. The project does not lead to measurable adverse impacts on the biodiversity values for which the critical habitat was designated.
3. The project does not lead to a net reduction in the global and/or national/regional population of any CR or EN species over a reasonable time period.
4. The project does not lead to conversion or degradation of any critical habitat that is a discrete management unit.
5. A robust, appropriately designed, and long-term **Biodiversity Management Plan** is prepared and implemented.
6. **Independent expert(s)** verify the BMP design and implementation.
7. **Net gain** of the biodiversity values for which the critical habitat was designated is achieved.

## 3. Legally Protected and Internationally Recognised Areas

### Legally Protected Areas (Paragraphs 25-27, Footnote 15)

**Definition** (footnote 15): Areas designated by law as protected — national parks, wildlife reserves, forest reserves, marine protected areas, community conservancies with legal gazettal, buffer zones defined by legislation, and areas proposed for protection by governments.

### Internationally Recognised Areas

| Designation | Governing Body | Relevance |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| **World Heritage Sites** (natural/mixed) | UNESCO | Outstanding universal value; highest scrutiny |
| **Biosphere Reserves** | UNESCO MAB Programme | Core, buffer, and transition zones |
| **Ramsar Wetlands** | Ramsar Convention | Wetlands of international importance |
| **Key Biodiversity Areas (KBAs)** | IUCN / KBA Partnership | Significant contribution to global persistence of biodiversity |
| **Important Bird Areas (IBAs)** | BirdLife International | CR/EN bird species, congregations, restricted-range assemblages |
| **Alliance for Zero Extinction (AZE) Sites** | AZE Partnership | Single remaining population of CR/EN species |

### Requirements (Paragraphs 26-27)

For projects sited in or potentially affecting legally protected or internationally recognised areas:

1. **Legal permission**: Demonstrate that the proposed project is consistent with the area's legal status and management objectives; obtain all required approvals.
2. **Management plan consistency**: Ensure project design aligns with the existing management plan for the protected area. If no plan exists, support the development of one.
3. **Stakeholder consultation**: Consult and engage area managers, local communities, indigenous peoples, and other relevant stakeholders. Apply Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) requirements under ESS7 where indigenous peoples are present.
4. **Additional conservation programmes**: Implement measures to strengthen the area's conservation, including financial support, capacity building, or co-management arrangements where appropriate.

## 4. Biodiversity Offsets

### Definitions (Footnote 6, Paragraphs 13-18)

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Biodiversity offset** | Measurable conservation outcomes resulting from actions designed to compensate for significant residual adverse biodiversity impacts arising from project development, after appropriate avoidance, minimisation, and restoration measures have been applied (footnote 6) |
| **No net loss** | Point at which project-related biodiversity losses are balanced by gains through offsets, so that no net reduction in biodiversity results; required for **natural habitat** |
| **Net gain** | Positive biodiversity outcomes exceeding project-related losses; required for **critical habitat** |

### Offset Design Principles (Paragraphs 14-18)

| Principle | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mitigation hierarchy first** | Offsets address only residual impacts after avoidance, minimisation, and restoration are exhausted |
| **Like-for-like or better** | In-kind offsets (same habitat type, same biodiversity values) preferred; out-of-kind offsets (trading up to higher conservation priority) may be acceptable if demonstrably better conservation outcomes result |
| **Additionality** | Offset gains must be additional to what would have occurred without the offset |
| **Long-term sustainability** | Legal protection, long-term financing (endowment funds, trust funds), and institutional arrangements must be secured for the offset site |
| **Measurable outcomes** | Quantified biodiversity targets, baseline surveys, monitoring indicators, and reporting protocols |
| **Independent expert verification** | Mandatory for critical habitat offsets; expert(s) must verify offset design, implementation, and monitoring |
| **Limits on offsetting** | Offsets are **not appropriate** where impacts affect biodiversity values that are **unique and irreplaceable** — in such cases, the project must be redesigned or relocated |

### When Offsets Cannot Be Used

- Loss of biodiversity for which no equivalent habitat or species population exists elsewhere.
- Impacts on AZE sites where the species would face extinction.
- Irreversible destruction of unique ecosystems with no feasible restoration pathway.

## 5. Invasive Alien Species (Paragraphs 28-30)

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **No intentional introduction** | The Borrower must not intentionally introduce species with high invasive risk unless a risk assessment shows negligible risk and appropriate management measures are in place (paragraph 28) |
| **Risk assessment** | Required before any species introduction; must consider invasion history, climate suitability, dispersal pathways, and potential impacts on native biodiversity (paragraph 29) |
| **Accidental introduction prevention** | Implement measures to prevent unintentional introduction — ballast water management, clean construction equipment, biosecurity protocols for imported materials (paragraph 29) |
| **Existing invasive species management** | Where invasive species already present, include eradication or management measures in the BMP/ESMP; avoid activities that worsen invasion (paragraph 30) |

## 6. Sustainable Management of Living Natural Resources (Paragraphs 31-40)

### Primary Production and Harvesting (Paragraph 31)

Applies to projects involving management or commercial use of natural forests, fisheries, agriculture, and other living natural resources. The Borrower must apply **Good International Industry Practice (GIIP)** and comply with applicable national law.

### Forest Management (Paragraphs 32-35)

| Scale | Requirements |
|-------|-------------|
| **Industrial-scale commercial forestry** | Must obtain or work towards obtaining an **internationally recognised independent forest certification** system (e.g., FSC, PEFC) within a timeframe agreed with the Bank (paragraph 33) |
| **Small-scale and community forestry** | Must apply GIIP appropriate to the scale; certification encouraged but not mandatory; must maintain or improve forest ecosystem integrity (paragraph 34) |
| **Salvage logging** | Permitted only where the Bank determines it is necessary and it is carried out in accordance with a management plan addressing environmental and social risks (paragraph 35) |

### Fisheries (Paragraph 36)

- Sustainable harvesting consistent with the best available scientific evidence.
- Compliance with applicable fisheries management plans and regulations.
- Avoidance of overfishing, destructive fishing practices, and bycatch of threatened species.

### Agriculture and Animal Husbandry (Paragraphs 37-38)

| Context | Requirements |
|---------|-------------|
| **All agricultural projects** | Apply GIIP for pest management, soil conservation, water use efficiency, and agrochemical handling (paragraph 37) |
| **Commercial plantations** | New large-scale plantations must be established on **already converted or degraded land**, not on natural or critical habitat (paragraph 38) |
| **Livestock** | Humane treatment, disease management, sustainable rangeland practices |

### Small-Scale Producers (Paragraphs 39-40)

- Where projects involve smallholders or small-scale resource users, the Borrower must adopt a **phased approach** to improving sustainability practices.
- **Cumulative risk assessment**: Assess the combined impacts of many small-scale producers in the landscape/seascape.
- Support gradual improvement towards GIIP through extension services, training, and market incentives.

## 7. Primary Suppliers (Paragraphs 41-43)

### Due Diligence Requirements

Where a project procures natural resource commodities (timber, fish, agricultural produce, minerals) from **primary suppliers**, the Borrower must:

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **1. Identify sources** | Map the supply chain to identify the origin of commodities and the nature of production/harvesting (paragraph 41) |
| **2. Assess risk** | Evaluate whether primary suppliers are causing significant conversion or degradation of natural or critical habitat (paragraph 42) |
| **3. Mitigate** | Where significant risk is identified, take action: (a) limit procurement to suppliers that can demonstrate sustainable practices; (b) shift to alternative suppliers; (c) require time-bound action plans for improvement (paragraph 43) |

### Certification Systems

- Independent, third-party certification (FSC, MSC, RSPO, Rainforest Alliance) provides evidence of sustainable sourcing.
- Where certification is not feasible, the Borrower must implement equivalent verification measures — audits, traceability systems, supplier codes of conduct.

## 8. Proposal Strategy Notes

### When to Reference ESS6

| Project Type | ESS6 Trigger | Typical Instruments |
|-------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Infrastructure** near protected areas, forests, wetlands, coastlines | Habitat conversion, fragmentation, disturbance | Biodiversity baseline survey, BMP, habitat assessment, offset plan |
| **Agriculture/agribusiness** | Land conversion, agrochemicals, water abstraction, supply chain sourcing | ESMP with biodiversity chapter, pest management plan, supply chain due diligence |
| **Forestry** (plantations, REDD+, commercial logging) | Forest conversion, certification, salvage logging | Forest management plan, certification gap analysis, BMP |
| **Fisheries/aquaculture** | Overharvesting, habitat degradation, invasive species | Fisheries management plan, environmental monitoring plan |
| **Energy** (hydro, wind, solar, geothermal) | Aquatic connectivity, bird/bat collision, habitat loss | BMP, wildlife monitoring plan, offsets, cumulative impact assessment |
| **Water supply/irrigation** | Wetland drainage, altered hydrology, downstream ecosystems | Environmental flow assessment, wetland management plan, BMP |
| **Extractives** (mining, oil, gas) | Habitat destruction, pollution, access roads fragmenting habitat | BMP, offset plan, progressive rehabilitation plan, closure plan |
| **Tourism** near natural areas | Disturbance, waste, infrastructure footprint | Ecotourism management plan, carrying capacity study, BMP |

### Key Terminology for Methodology Sections

Use these terms precisely in proposals — they signal ESS6 competence:

- **Mitigation hierarchy** (avoid–minimise–restore–offset)
- **No net loss** / **net gain**
- **Critical habitat** (with the five criteria)
- **Like-for-like or better** (offset design)
- **Adaptive management** (iterative monitoring and response)
- **Good International Industry Practice (GIIP)**
- **Precautionary approach**
- **Biodiversity Management Plan (BMP)**
- **Cumulative impact assessment**
- **Ecosystem services** (provisioning, regulating, cultural, supporting)

### Common Safeguard Instruments

| Instrument | When Required |
|------------|--------------|
| **Biodiversity Baseline Survey** | All projects in or near natural/critical habitat; must be seasonal where relevant |
| **Habitat Assessment** | Classify habitat (modified/natural/critical); check protected area proximity; map KBAs |
| **Biodiversity Management Plan** | Mandatory for critical habitat; recommended for significant natural habitat impacts |
| **Biodiversity Offset Plan** | When residual impacts on natural/critical habitat remain after mitigation |
| **Environmental Flow Assessment** | Water-related projects affecting downstream ecosystems |
| **Invasive Species Management Plan** | Projects introducing species or operating in invasion-prone environments |
| **Supply Chain Due Diligence Report** | Projects procuring primary natural resource commodities |

### Integration with Other Environmental and Social Standards

| ESS | Integration Point |
|-----|-------------------|
| **ESS1** (Assessment and Management) | ESS6 biodiversity assessment is conducted within the ESS1 framework; BMP sits within or alongside the ESMP |
| **ESS4** (Community Health, Safety, and Security) | Ecosystem services that communities depend on (water purification, flood regulation, pollination) — loss triggers both ESS4 and ESS6 |
| **ESS5** (Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use) | Access restrictions to natural resources in protected areas must be assessed under both ESS5 and ESS6 |
| **ESS7** (Indigenous Peoples/Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Traditional Local Communities) | Where indigenous peoples depend on biodiversity and natural resources, FPIC requirements under ESS7 intersect with ESS6 habitat and resource management |
| **ESS8** (Cultural Heritage) | Sacred natural sites, culturally significant species, and landscapes with intangible heritage value require joint ESS6/ESS8 consideration |
| **ESS10** (Stakeholder Engagement) | Meaningful consultation with communities, indigenous peoples, and conservation organisations on biodiversity impacts and offset design |
