# Communication and Presentation Structures

Frameworks for structuring deliverables, recommendations, experience narratives, data interpretation, and qualitative analysis. Use when designing how consulting outputs will be presented to clients.

---

## 1. SCORE Storytelling Framework

For structuring engagement narratives in proposals and presentations:

| Element | Description | Proposal Application |
|---|---|---|
| **S — Situation** | Brief description of context | Client background in understanding of assignment |
| **C — Complication** | Issues faced, why it was challenging | The problem or gap that creates the need |
| **O — Outcome Expectation** | Worst possible outcome if no intervention | Creates urgency — "If nothing changes, [consequence]" |
| **R — Remedial Action** | Detailed actions to overcome challenges (80–90% of content) | The methodology — what we will do |
| **E — End Result** | Successful outcome | Expected benefits and deliverables |

### Why SCORE is more persuasive than a simple project description

Most experience entries in proposals read: "We did X for Client Y." This is forgettable. SCORE creates narrative tension through the Outcome Expectation element — the reader understands what was at stake before learning what the consultant did.

**Template:** "[Client] faced [situation]. [Complication] threatened [worst outcome if unaddressed]. We [detailed remedial actions]. The result: [quantified end result]."

### SCORE vs. SCQA

- **SCQA** (Situation → Complication → Question → Answer) is for structuring analytical sections — it opens with what the reader accepts, introduces tension, then resolves with the proposed approach
- **SCORE** is for structuring past experience narratives — it adds the "what was at stake" element that creates urgency

Both can be used in the same proposal: SCQA for the methodology sections, SCORE for the experience entries.

## 2. Top-Down Recommendation Structure

The consulting standard for all recommendations and final deliverables:

```
"I recommend [specific action] for the following [N] reasons:

1. [Evidence-based reason]
2. [Evidence-based reason]
3. [Evidence-based reason]

For these reasons, I recommend [specific action].

For next steps, I would like to look into:

1. [Further analysis area / implementation step]
2. [Further analysis area / implementation step]"
```

### Rules

- Lead with the answer, not the analysis
- State the count before listing ("for three reasons")
- Every reason must be evidence-based, not opinion
- Close with next steps and risks
- A strong recommendation should take less than one minute to deliver verbally

**Methodology language:** Signal in the methodology that all recommendations will follow this structure: "Every recommendation will be presented with the recommended action first, followed by supporting evidence and next steps — ensuring outputs are immediately actionable."

## 3. Seven-Step Exhibit Interpretation Method

A rigorous method for interpreting any data visualisation in deliverables:

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Restate the purpose** — what hypothesis is being tested |
| 2 | **Briefly describe** — title, units, labels, legends, axes |
| 3 | **Clarify** — confirm assumptions about what is displayed |
| 4 | **Structure thoughts** — 30–60 seconds to identify insights |
| 5 | **Communicate key insights** — 2–4 most important data points, usually outliers |
| 6 | **Communicate implications** — the "so what?" in context |
| 7 | **Communicate next steps** — what additional analyses or actions follow |

### Four categories of insights to look for

| Category | What to Identify |
|---|---|
| **Comparison** | Internal vs. external, unit vs. unit, scenario vs. scenario |
| **Segmenting** | Breakdown of process, financials, business segments, geographies |
| **Relationships** | Positive or negative correlations between variables |
| **Changes and trends** | Absolute/relative changes over time, trend reversals, spikes |

### Types of outliers

- Figure very small or large compared to others
- Figure very different from hypothesis expectation
- Breakdown different from comparable segments
- High or low growth compared to peers
- Significant change at a specific point (spike, reversal)
- Average looks good but segments show deviation

**Proposal application:** "All quantitative data will be interpreted using a structured protocol: establishing what the data measures, identifying patterns and outliers, calculating derived metrics, and synthesising cross-exhibit insights before drawing conclusions."

## 4. Analytical Sense-Checking

Always perform a sense check on quantitative results before presenting:

1. **Reasonableness test** — Does this number make sense given what I know?
2. **Benchmarking** — Can I compare this to a known reference point?
3. **Order of magnitude check** — Am I in the right ballpark?

**Methodology language:** "All quantitative findings will be validated against benchmarks, comparable projects, or known reference data before being presented to the client."

## 5. Action Title Principle

From *Conquer Your Consulting Case Interview* — slide and section headings should convey the implication, not describe what was done:

| Weak (Descriptive) | Strong (Action Title) |
|---|---|
| "Cost Analysis Results" | "The client needs to cut costs by 15% to achieve profitability" |
| "Stakeholder Interview Findings" | "Three departments report misaligned incentives as the primary barrier" |
| "Market Size Estimate" | "The addressable market is USD 45M and growing at 12% annually" |

**Rule:** The headings of a deliverable, read in sequence, should tell the full story. If someone reads only the headings, they should understand the core argument.

**Proposal application:** When describing deliverable formats in the methodology, reference this standard: "All deliverable sections will use action titles that convey the key finding or recommendation, enabling rapid executive-level review."

## 6. Numbering and Signposting

Every time multiple ideas are presented, use explicit structure:

- **Announce the count:** "I want to look at five things"
- **Number each:** "First… Second… Third…"
- **Signal transitions:** "Now I want to go back to my second point"
- **If count unknown:** "Here, I have a few points" and number as you go

**Proposal application:** All methodology descriptions, recommendation lists, and deliverable structures should use explicit numbering. This is not just formatting — it signals structured thinking.
