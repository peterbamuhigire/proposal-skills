# Strategy Frameworks

Frameworks for strategy, market entry, growth, competitive positioning, and acquisition assignments. Use when the ToR involves strategic advisory, market analysis, or institutional transformation.

---

## 1. Market Entry Methodology

Use when evaluating whether a client should enter a new market, launch a new service line, or expand geographically.

| Phase | Analytical Focus | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| Market Attractiveness Assessment | Size, growth rate, average margins | Is this market large enough and growing? |
| Company Capabilities Assessment | Leverageable capabilities, relevant experience, risk tolerance | Can the client actually compete? |
| Competitive Landscape Analysis | Market concentration, competitor differentiation, barriers to entry | How hard is it to enter and survive? |
| Financial Feasibility | Profitability projections, breakeven timeline | Will it be profitable, and when? |

---

## 2. Competitive Response Framework

Use when a competitor takes a specific action (new entrant, price drop, product launch) and the client must decide how to respond.

| Component | Key Questions |
|---|---|
| **Threat sizing** | How large and direct is the threat? Does the competitor have differentiation? |
| **Customer response** | How will customers react? How likely are they to switch? |
| **Competitor response** | Will other competitors also react? How? |
| **Strategic options** | Do nothing / Match / Counter-attack / Differentiate / Exit |

**Methodology language:** "The methodology sequences threat assessment before strategy development — sizing the competitive threat, modelling customer and market response, then evaluating strategic options."

---

## 3. Build vs. Buy vs. Partner Framework

Three modes of acquiring new capability or entering a new market:

| Mode | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|
| **Build internally** | Full control, no integration risk | Slow, requires investment, hiring |
| **Partnership / JV** | Access to partner's capabilities, shared risk | Shared profits, less control, dependency |
| **Acquire** | Immediate capabilities, brand, customer base | High cost, integration risk, cultural clash |

**Methodology language:** "The options appraisal will evaluate three capability acquisition modes — build, partner, and acquire — against [criteria], with a recommended pathway and implementation sequence."

## 4. Adjacent Market Proximity Model

Structure adjacent market opportunities by distance from core:

| Tier | Description | Feasibility | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core-adjacent** | Same domain, different product | Highest | Lowest |
| **Product-adjacent** | Same product type, different domain | High | Low-Medium |
| **Sector-adjacent** | Same broad sector, different product type | Medium | Medium |
| **Sector-distant** | Same sector, entirely different business model | Lowest | Highest |

**Principle:** Closest adjacencies are most feasible and likely to succeed. Evaluate from inside out.

**Methodology language:** "Growth opportunities will be mapped by proximity to the core business, with closer adjacencies evaluated first for feasibility and risk-adjusted return."

## 5. Merger and Acquisition Evaluation Methodology

Use when evaluating a potential acquisition, merger, partnership, or institutional consolidation.

| Phase | Analytical Focus | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| Target Attractiveness Assessment | Profitability, differentiated capabilities, brand strength | Is the target worth acquiring? |
| Market Attractiveness Assessment | Market size, growth rate, average margins of target's market | Is the target's market attractive? |
| Synergy Analysis | Revenue synergies, cost synergies, realisability, timeline | What value does the combination create? |
| Financial Evaluation | Acquisition price fairness, breakeven analysis, exit strategy | Does the deal make financial sense? |

---

## 6. Pricing Strategy Methodology

Use when the assignment involves pricing a new product, service, or offering.

| Phase | Analytical Focus | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| Customer Willingness-to-Pay Analysis | Survey/focus group findings, ability to pay, subsidy factors | How much will customers actually pay? |
| Competitor Pricing Benchmarking | Competitor prices, product attributes, relative positioning | Where do competitors price, and why? |
| Cost-Plus Profitability Analysis | Production costs, margin targets, breakeven requirements | What price covers costs and meets targets? |
| Alternative Pricing Strategy Evaluation | Discounting, bundling, subscription, tiered pricing | Which pricing model maximises revenue? |

---

## 7. Profitability Diagnosis Methodology

Use when diagnosing why a client is losing money, why performance has declined, or how to improve financial results.

| Phase | Analytical Focus | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| Financial Decomposition | Revenue (quantity × price) vs. costs (variable + fixed) | Where exactly is money being lost? |
| Competitive Landscape Review | Competitor actions, new entrants, competitor profitability | Is this company-specific or market-wide? |
| Customer Segment Analysis | Preference shifts, purchasing behaviour, customer attrition | Are customers leaving or changing? |
| Market Trends Review | New technologies, regulations, market structural changes | Are external forces driving the decline? |

## 8. Blue Ocean Strategy — ERRC Grid

From Kim & Mauborgne via *Essential Management Models* — a structured approach to creating uncontested market space:

### The ERRC Grid

| Action | Question | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Eliminate** | Which factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated? | Removes cost without removing value |
| **Reduce** | Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard? | Strips over-designed features |
| **Raise** | Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard? | Creates differentiation |
| **Create** | Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered? | Generates new demand |

**Proposal application:** Use as a workshop tool during solution design phases: "We will facilitate an ERRC analysis — systematically identifying which elements of the current [service/product/process] should be eliminated, reduced, raised, or created to deliver greater value at lower cost."

## 9. Discipline of Market Leaders

From Treacy & Wiersema via *Essential Management Models* — three value disciplines that determine competitive positioning:

| Discipline | Focus | Key Capability | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Operational Excellence** | Low cost, high reliability, convenience | Standardised processes, lean operations, volume | Commoditisation, race to bottom |
| **Product Leadership** | Best product, innovation, performance | R&D, speed to market, creative culture | High cost, unpredictable returns |
| **Customer Intimacy** | Best total solution, deep relationships | Customer knowledge, customisation, flexibility | Scalability, client dependency |

**The rule:** Choose one as the primary focus. Maintain acceptable performance in the other two. Organisations that try to excel at all three achieve mediocrity in each.

**Proposal application:** In strategy proposals, help the client identify their value discipline and align operations accordingly: "Our assessment will determine which value discipline — operational excellence, product leadership, or customer intimacy — best positions [client] for sustainable competitive advantage."

## 10. Disruptive Innovation Assessment

From Bower & Christensen via *Essential Management Models* — a framework for evaluating low-end competitive threats:

**Performance Trajectories Model:** Disruptive innovations start by offering less functionality at lower cost, serving an underserved niche. As their performance trajectory rises and intersects mainstream requirements, they displace incumbents who dismissed them as "inferior."

**Warning signs:**
- A new entrant serving a segment you consider unprofitable
- A simpler, cheaper alternative that "good enough" customers begin adopting
- Your internal reaction is "that is not a real competitor"

**Proposal application:** In competitive threat assessments: "Our analysis applies the disruptive innovation lens — evaluating whether lower-end alternatives that [client] currently dismisses may be on a trajectory to challenge the core business."

## 11. Structured Choice Decision-Making

From Martin, Rivkin, Siggelkow & Lafley via *Essential Management Models* — a seven-step methodology for strategic decisions:

| Step | Action | Key Principle |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Frame a choice** | Present mutually exclusive options — not "should we?" but "A or B?" |
| 2 | **Generate possibilities** | Brainstorm alternatives without judging |
| 3 | **Specify conditions** | For each option, ask "What would have to be true for this to work?" |
| 4 | **Identify barriers** | Which conditions are least likely to hold? |
| 5 | **Design tests** | Create ways to test the most uncertain conditions |
| 6 | **Conduct tests** | Start with the conditions you are least confident about |
| 7 | **Make your choice** | Based on evidence, not opinion |

**The reframe:** Instead of "this will never work," ask "what would have to be true for this to work?" — this shifts debate from advocacy to analysis.

**Proposal application:** "Our strategy process uses a structured choice methodology — framing strategic alternatives as testable hypotheses, identifying the conditions required for each to succeed, and designing evidence-based tests to determine the best path forward."

## 12. Scenario Planning

From Pierre Wack / Shell via *Essential Management Models* — a methodology for strategy in uncertain environments:

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Identify the two most uncertain variables affecting the client's future |
| 2 | Plot these as two axes, creating four quadrants |
| 3 | Name each quadrant as a plausible future scenario |
| 4 | Develop a narrative for each scenario |
| 5 | Test the proposed strategy against each scenario |
| 6 | Identify "no regret" moves (work in all scenarios) and "hedging" moves (provide options) |

**Proposal application:** In strategy assignments involving high uncertainty: "Our strategic planning methodology includes scenario planning — testing the recommended strategy against four plausible futures to ensure resilience across a range of outcomes."

## 13. Rumelt's Kernel of Strategy

From *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy* via *Essential Management Models* — the minimum structure of a good strategy:

| Element | Description | Consulting Phase |
|---|---|---|
| **Diagnosis** | What is going on? — a clear-eyed assessment of the situation | Assessment and diagnostic |
| **Guiding Policy** | What approach will we take? — the overall direction, not a goal | Strategy formulation |
| **Coherent Action** | What specific actions? — coordinated steps that carry out the policy | Implementation planning |

**The bad strategy test:** If the "strategy" is just a list of goals without a diagnosis and without coherent actions, it is not a strategy — it is wishful thinking.

**Proposal application:** Structure strategy deliverables around Rumelt's kernel: "Our final deliverable will present a clear diagnosis of the current situation, a guiding policy for [client's] strategic direction, and a set of coherent, coordinated actions to implement it."

## 14. Innovation-Change-Learning Matrix

From *Innovation in Business and Enterprise* — maps the intersection of innovation type, change type, and learning type to determine the depth of intervention required:

| Innovation Type | Change Type | Learning Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Technical** (new technology, process) | **Developmental** (improvement of existing) | **Single-loop** (doing things better) |
| **Administrative** (new structure, governance) | **Transitional** (managed shift to known future state) | **Double-loop** (changing the rules) |
| **Ancillary** (supporting, peripheral) | **Transformational** (fundamental shift, future state unknown) | **Triple-loop** (changing how we learn) |

**Key finding:** Even administrative innovation requires triple-loop learning in 88% of cases — firms systematically underestimate the learning investment required for organisational change.

**Proposal application:** In digital transformation, institutional reform, or organisational restructuring proposals: "Our methodology calibrates the depth of change management and learning support to the type of innovation being introduced — recognising that administrative and structural changes often require deeper organisational learning than technical changes."
