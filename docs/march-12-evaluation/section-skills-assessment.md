## Assessment Criteria

Each section skill is evaluated on five dimensions:

| Dimension | What It Means |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Structure** | Does it define clear subsections and output format? |
| **Depth** | Is there enough substance to generate compelling content? |
| **Procurement compliance** | Does it address World Bank, PPDA, UNDP, AfDB variants? |
| **Evaluation awareness** | Does it guide scoring optimisation? |
| **Cross-references** | Does it connect to domain skills and references appropriately? |

Rating scale: **Strong** / **Adequate** / **Weak** / **Missing**

## Detailed Assessment by Section

### 01. Cover Letter (81 lines) — Rating: Adequate

**Strengths:**
- Clear one-page constraint
- TRUST framework (Truthful, Responsive, Uniform, Safe, Trained)
- Submission sheet template included
- Relationship signals guidance

**Weaknesses:**
- No procurement framework variants (World Bank cover letter tone differs from PPDA)
- No guidance on confidentiality clauses or technical compliance statements
- No strong-vs-weak opener examples
- No cross-references to other skills
- Does not address evaluation criteria referencing

**What McKinsey would do differently:** Lead with a quantified value proposition tied to the client's specific pain point, not a generic expression of interest.

---

### 02. Executive Summary (97 lines) — Rating: Adequate

**Strengths:**
- Pyramid of Ideas (Minto) structure — Level 1 through Level 4
- S1→S2→B opening formula
- Four differentiators framework (experience, approach, team, local presence)
- Benefits-forward closing (during, at end, beyond project)
- Scope summary table

**Weaknesses:**
- Only 3 brief narrative examples — insufficient for calibration
- No guidance on weighting the four differentiators by assignment type
- No process for testing whether the Big Idea is defensible
- No guidance for commodity assignments where differentiation is difficult
- No evaluation scoring guidance

**What McKinsey would do differently:** Include a quantified "so what" for every differentiator and a named proprietary framework that signals unique methodology.

---

### 03. Understanding of the Assignment (204 lines) — Rating: Strong

**Strengths:**
- **Deepest strategic content** of any section skill
- S1→S2→B logic explicitly framed
- Overriding Question derivation (problem → question → methodology bridge)
- Three Facets of Problem Analysis (Rational, Cognitive, Political) — Wickham framework
- Story-Questions-Closing structure (Freed)
- ToR comments format (Reference, Observation, Recommendation)
- Realistic worked example

**Weaknesses:**
- No guidance on balancing the three problem facets in narrative (cognitive and political are less common but powerful)
- No worked example of the full three-facet analysis
- No guidance on detecting genuine ToR gaps vs fabricated issues
- No evaluation scoring guidance

**What McKinsey would do differently:** Frame the understanding section as a "hypothesis about the client's real problem" — not just proof of comprehension but a signal that the firm has already started solving.

---

### 04. Firm Profile (87 lines) — Rating: Adequate

**Strengths:**
- Dual format (firm + individual consultant)
- Clear section structure (overview, service areas, sector expertise, geographic footprint)
- Small firm positioned as advantage (senior attention, no delegation)

**Weaknesses:**
- No guidance on positioning a young firm against legacy competitors
- Geographic footprint section too brief — no credibility strategies for regions without office presence
- No formal weighting of what evaluators prioritise (sector match? size? track record?)
- Individual consultant format lighter than company format
- No examples of strong vs generic sector expertise statements

**What McKinsey would do differently:** Include specific client testimonials or quantified outcomes in the profile itself, not just in the experience section.

---

### 05. Relevant Experience (72 lines) — Rating: Adequate

**Strengths:**
- The Outcomes Rule — every card must state what was achieved, not just what was done
- Summary table + project card structure
- Order by relevance, not chronology
- Evaluators-may-verify warning

**Weaknesses:**
- No guidance on handling commercially confidential outcomes
- No weak-vs-strong outcomes statement comparison
- No guidance on grey-area attribution (did the project really cause the outcome?)
- No international-vs-local experience weighting
- No reference contact selection guidance

**Evaluation awareness:** Notes this is "typically the most heavily weighted in evaluation scoring" — one of only two sections to state scoring weight.

---

### 06. Methodology (177 lines) — Rating: Strong

**Strengths:**
- **Richest and most technically advanced** section skill
- Logic tree construction with MECE principle
- P-I-P (Persuade-Inform-Persuade) structure within each phase
- Hypothesis-driven methodology framework
- Three project types (Insight, Planning, Implementation) with specific verb sets
- Deliverables summary table
- What-Why-How for recommendations
- Quality assurance (Done-Done standard)
- Extensive cross-references to 6 external reference files

**Weaknesses:**
- No full worked example of a realistic phase breakdown
- No guidance for highly uncertain/exploratory assignments where hypothesis-driven feels forced
- No procurement framework variants (PPDA vs World Bank compliance)
- Risk management section brief — delegates to risk-management/ skill
- No evaluation scoring guidance

**Cross-references (excellent):**
- consulting-frameworks/ (all 5 files)
- references/consulting-delivery-excellence.md
- references/proposal-strategy-and-persuasion.md
- references/world-class-proposal-patterns.md
- project-management/references/project-controls-and-earned-value.md
- risk-management/references/risk-quantification-and-response.md

### 07. Team Composition (128 lines) — Rating: Strong

**Strengths:**
- **Only section that explicitly names QCBS weighting** (30–40% of technical score)
- Dual CV formats: World Bank TECH-6 and PPDA narrative
- TECH-6 structure is correct and complete
- Organogram template (text-based)
- Role and responsibility matrix

**Weaknesses:**
- No guidance on common TECH-6 errors (certification signatures, date format)
- No joint venture team composition guidance
- No part-time vs full-time allocation signalling (what "120 person-days" means to evaluators)
- No guidance on handling team gaps (ToR asks for specialist the firm lacks)
- No gender balance or diversity considerations
- No team change management (person leaves before submission)

---

### 08. Work Plan and Timeline (95 lines) — Rating: Adequate

**Strengths:**
- Critical Path Method (CPM) identification
- Resource levelling
- PERT three-point estimation formula (explicit formula provided)
- Buffer rule: at least one week per three months
- Staffing schedule tied to financial proposal
- Time-cost trade-off principle

**Weaknesses:**
- No worked Gantt example — hard to visualise activity sequencing
- No guidance on building contingency without visible padding
- No concurrent-vs-serial activity trade-off guidance
- No risk integration (how risk materialisation affects critical path)
- No guidance on client-imposed fixed dates
- No holiday/unavailability calendar handling

---

### 09. Expression of Interest (75 lines) — Rating: Weak

**Strengths:**
- Clear shortlisting mindset (prove eligibility and relevance, not persuasion)
- Condensed versions of sections 01, 04, 05
- Outcomes rule applied consistently
- Realistic four–six page target

**Weaknesses:**
- No EoI evaluation guidance (how does EoI scoring differ from full proposal?)
- No procurement framework variants (World Bank shortlisting, PPDA two-stage, UNDP)
- No strong-vs-weak EoI examples
- No pass/fail vs scored shortlisting criteria guidance
- No guidance on priority conflicts (shortlisted for wrong assignment)

---

### 10. Financial Proposal (134 lines) — Rating: Strong

**Strengths:**
- Day rate reference table (2025–2026, USD and UGX, 5 seniority levels) — highly practical
- PCTS constraint (Performance-Time-Scope-Cost)
- Risk contingency √ formula — elegant and defensible
- Separate sealing requirement for PPDA/World Bank
- Life cycle costs for ICT/infrastructure
- NPV analysis for multi-year assignments
- Submission sheet and four table templates
- 520-line budgeting reference file

**Weaknesses:**
- No World Bank FIN-1–4 form structures (mentioned but not provided)
- No PPDA detailed budget format
- No multi-currency guidance (when to quote UGX vs USD)
- Reimbursable line-items very sparse
- No "all-in" vs itemised pricing guidance
- No currency fluctuation risk for long assignments
- No worked NPV example

## Recommendations by Priority

### Must Fix (for $1M+ credibility)

1. **Add evaluation scoring guidance to every section** — what percentage of total score, what evaluators prioritise, how to optimise points
2. **Add procurement framework variants** — at minimum, a "World Bank format" vs "PPDA format" vs "UNDP format" note per section
3. **Create worked weak-vs-strong examples** for sections 01, 02, 04, 05, 09
4. **Add World Bank FIN-1–4 templates** to section 10

### Should Fix (for competitive advantage)

5. Expand section 01 (cover letter) from 81 to ~150 lines with opener examples and compliance guidance
6. Expand section 09 (EoI) from 75 to ~120 lines with procurement variant structures
7. Add cross-references to proposal-strategy-and-persuasion.md in every section that uses its concepts
8. Add a "pre-submission checklist" section to the parent SKILL.md

### Nice to Have

9. Create a "worked example" directory showing all 10 sections for one realistic assignment
