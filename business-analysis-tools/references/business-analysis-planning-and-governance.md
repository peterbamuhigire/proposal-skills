# Business Analysis Planning and Governance

Additional guidance for assignments where the proposal must show how analysis work will be governed, sequenced, and converted into a usable solution definition. This is most useful for diagnostic studies, process redesign, digital transformation, service improvement, and requirements-heavy system assignments.

Synthesised from PMI's *Business Analysis for Practitioners* and Robertson & Robertson's *Mastering the Requirements Process*.

---

## 1. Situation Framing Before Solution Design

Do not begin with requirements lists if the real problem is still unclear. Start by making the business situation explicit.

### Situation Statement

Define:
- the current state
- the trigger for change
- the pain, missed opportunity, or compliance risk
- the desired business outcome

### Goal Statement

State the future condition in measurable terms where possible:
- service improvement target
- cost, speed, quality, or coverage target
- policy or compliance objective
- user or beneficiary outcome

### Scope and Boundary

Use a context diagram to define:
- what is inside the assignment boundary
- which actors and systems are outside but connected
- the major inputs, outputs, and dependencies

This prevents solution creep and reveals interfaces that often drive complexity.

## 2. Stakeholders, Segments, and Business Events

### Stakeholder Set

For each material stakeholder group, capture:
- role in the current process
- decision authority
- knowledge contribution
- likely support or resistance
- engagement method

### Customer Segments and Personas

Segment users or beneficiaries where needs differ meaningfully. Common split criteria:
- role or job family
- geography
- transaction volume
- digital maturity
- vulnerability or inclusion needs

Use personas only where they sharpen design decisions. Avoid creating more personas than the assignment can realistically use.

### Business Events

A business event is the trigger that causes work to happen. Typical event types:
- external request or application
- deadline reached
- threshold breached
- approval required
- exception or complaint raised
- data or status change received

Business events help uncover the real workflow because they focus on what must happen when the world changes. They are a practical bridge from problem framing into use cases, process models, user stories, and functional requirements.

## 3. Business Value Assessment Flow

Use this flow in methodology sections:

1. Understand the situation.
2. Find the gaps between current and desired state.
3. Define the high-level solution approach.
4. Confirm why the solution is worth doing.

### Artefacts To Name In Proposals

- current-state assessment
- gap analysis matrix
- root cause summary
- high-level solution options
- roadmap or transition approach
- benefits and success measures

### Business Case and Go/No-Go Logic

When the assignment includes a decision stage, show that the team will test whether the proposed change is justified. Useful questions:
- what value is created if the problem is solved
- what happens if nothing is done
- what assumptions must hold for the solution to succeed
- what risks make the initiative unattractive or non-viable

## 4. Business Analysis Governance

Business analysis governance defines how analysis decisions will be made and controlled.

### Governance Elements

- analysis sponsor and approval authority
- who can define, refine, or reject requirements
- documentation standard and required rigour
- baseline and version control approach
- change request route after approval
- link between requirements decisions and project governance

### Predictive vs Adaptive Calibration

| Delivery Context | Governance Pattern |
|---|---|
| Predictive / regulated | Formal sign-off, baselines, RTM, change control board |
| Hybrid | Baseline high-level requirements, refine details iteratively |
| Adaptive | Product backlog, acceptance criteria, rolling refinement, product owner decisions |

Proposal language should show that rigour is chosen deliberately, not copied mechanically.

## 5. Stakeholder Engagement Approach

Define the engagement strategy before detailed elicitation begins.

### Minimum Engagement Map

| Stakeholder Group | Why They Matter | Engagement Method | Frequency | Decision Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Group] | [Need/input/approval] | [Interview/workshop/review] | [Cadence] | [Approve/consult/inform] |

### Selection Rules

- use interviews for sensitive or specialist topics
- use workshops where cross-functional alignment is required
- use observation when actual practice differs from official procedure
- use surveys when coverage matters more than depth
- use prototypes when stakeholders need something concrete to react to

## 6. Planning Business Analysis Work

Show the analysis work as a roadmap, not an informal set of meetings.

### What To Plan

- analysis activities by phase
- required client inputs and turnaround assumptions
- artefacts to be produced
- review and approval points
- dependencies on technical design, procurement, or implementation
- how traceability will be maintained

### Proposal-Ready Sequence

1. Mobilise and confirm scope.
2. Conduct document review and context mapping.
3. Elicit information from priority stakeholders.
4. Analyse and structure the findings.
5. Package the requirements, models, and decisions.
6. Validate, baseline, and hand over for design or implementation.
7. Support transition and evaluation.

This gives evaluators confidence that analysis is managed work, not improvised conversation.

---

## 7. Refining and Packaging Solution Information

After elicitation, convert information into usable artefacts:

- requirements catalogue
- process model
- use cases or user stories
- business rules catalogue
- data model
- interface list
- assumptions and constraints register
- acceptance criteria

Package the output for the audience that must use it:
- executives need decisions, scope, and value
- delivery teams need precise and testable requirements
- clients and sponsors need traceability and approval clarity

---

## 8. Fit Criteria and Testability

Each important requirement should be testable. A fit criterion defines how success will be verified.

### Weak vs Strong Requirement Expression

| Weak | Stronger |
|---|---|
| user-friendly interface | new users complete the core task without assistance after 30 minutes of orientation |
| fast reporting | monthly reports generated within 2 minutes for the standard data set |
| secure access | role-based access enforced with MFA for privileged users |

Use fit criteria whenever the proposal promises performance, quality, service levels, or user outcomes.

---

## 9. Transition Planning and Solution Evaluation

Business analysis should continue into transition, not stop at requirement approval.

### Transition Requirements

Capture temporary but necessary needs such as:
- data migration
- training and onboarding
- communication and stakeholder readiness
- cutover planning
- coexistence with legacy processes
- support materials and SOP updates

### Solution Evaluation

Define:
- acceptance criteria
- pilot or UAT approach
- early performance indicators
- benefits realisation measures
- lessons learned and refinement cycle

Proposal language: "We will define both the solution requirements and the transition conditions needed for successful uptake, then evaluate the implemented solution against agreed acceptance and benefits measures."

---

## 10. Common Proposal Mistakes

- starting with screens, forms, or technology before defining the business problem
- listing stakeholders without an engagement strategy
- documenting requirements that cannot be tested
- omitting transition requirements such as migration or training
- treating analysis as a one-off discovery phase with no evaluation loop
