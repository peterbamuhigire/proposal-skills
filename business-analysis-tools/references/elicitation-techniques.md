# Elicitation Techniques

Techniques for drawing information from stakeholders, documents, and other sources. Elicitation is not requirements "gathering" — stakeholders have wants, needs, and information, but not ready-made requirements. The analyst's job is to help define the problem and determine what should be done.

---

## Planning Elicitation

Before eliciting, answer four questions:

| Question | Detail |
|---|---|
| **What to elicit** | What does the analyst need to know to define or solve the problem? |
| **Where to find it** | Which documents, sources, or stakeholders hold the information? |
| **How to obtain it** | Which technique will be used? |
| **Sequencing** | In what order should activities be conducted? |

**Best practice:** Identify at least two sources for each topic to avoid single-source bias.

**Prioritise elicitation where there are:** significant business value, greater risks, many unknowns, significant technical challenges, dependencies, required third-party resources, or limited key resources.

---

## Conducting Sessions — Four Stages

1. **Introduction** — Frame the session (state the problem, objectives, benefits); use a "parking lot" for off-topic items.
2. **Body** — Active listening, empathy, question sequencing, probing for details.
3. **Close** — Graceful termination; summarise what was covered.
4. **Follow-up** — Consolidate and confirm information with participants.

## Technique Reference

### 1. Document Analysis

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Cheapest and easiest starting activity; introduces business terminology |
| **What to review** | System documentation, procedure manuals, policy handbooks, online resources, existing reports |
| **When to use** | Always first — before meeting stakeholders; when stakeholders are unavailable |
| **Best practice** | Not everything is accurate or current; use as starting point then validate through interviews |
| **Proposal language** | "We will commence with a desk review of existing documentation including..." |

### 2. Interviews

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Systematic questioning to elicit responses; quickest and most convenient form |
| **Structure** | Fully structured (predetermined questions) to unstructured (conversational) |
| **Formats** | Face-to-face, telephone, video conference |
| **Planning** | Know the interviewee's position, involvement, decision-making authority |
| **Follow-up** | Send notes to interviewee promptly for correction and trust building |
| **Best for** | Sensitive topics, geographically dispersed stakeholders, deep-dive on specific areas |
| **Goal format** | "I would like to come away with a clear understanding of [specific topic]" |
| **Proposal language** | "We will conduct structured key informant interviews with..." |

### 3. Facilitated Workshops

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Structured working sessions bringing together people from different business areas |
| **Distinction** | Not meetings — planned sessions with carefully chosen participants |
| **When to use** | More than two viewpoints must be represented; project initiation; detailed requirements |
| **Roles** | Facilitator, recorder, timekeeper |
| **Benefits** | Multiple viewpoints, immediate resolution of differences, priority balancing, team building |
| **Proposal language** | "We will facilitate stakeholder workshops with cross-functional representation to..." |

### 4. Focus Groups

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Pre-qualified group shares opinions on a specific topic |
| **Led by** | Professional, independent facilitator who does not participate in discussion |
| **Size** | Smaller and more targeted than workshops |
| **Key rule** | Free-flowing conversation; facilitator does not influence opinions |
| **Proposal language** | "We will convene focus group discussions with [target group] to explore..." |

### 5. Observation (Shadowing)

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Most enlightening way to learn about a business area; see how work is actually done |
| **Key insight** | Three descriptions of any process exist: (1) how it should be done, (2) how an employee describes it, (3) how it is actually done |
| **Planning** | Consider timing, daily/weekly/seasonal fluctuations, access restrictions, team rotations |
| **Tips** | Limit questions during observation; write them down for follow-up; consider recording |
| **Caution** | Workers may behave differently when watched; encourage normal behaviour |
| **Proposal language** | "Our team will conduct field observation (gemba walks) at operational sites to..." |

### 6. Surveys and Questionnaires

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Same questions across a large group; removes subjective interviewing bias |
| **Best for** | Geographically dispersed stakeholders; validating findings from other techniques; large populations |
| **Design tips** | Clear layout, explain participation value, keep short, limit to two subject areas |
| **Question types** | Closed-ended (yes/no, multiple choice) for tabulation; open-ended for qualitative insight |
| **Strength** | Stakeholders have more time to respond; no schedule coordination needed |
| **Proposal language** | "We will design and administer structured questionnaires across [N] respondents to..." |

### 7. Brainstorming

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Generate creative ideas in group sessions |
| **Two phases** | (1) Idea generation — no criticism, all ideas accepted, rapid generation; (2) Analysis and evaluation |
| **Ground rules** | No bad ideas, one person speaks at a time, no evaluation during generation, no organising during session |
| **For shy groups** | Round-robin approach |
| **Breaking groupthink** | Formal brainstorming to generate diverse ideas |
| **Proposal language** | "We will facilitate brainstorming sessions to generate potential solutions for..." |

### 8. Prototyping

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Early feedback by showing a working model before building the full solution |
| **Low-fidelity** | Sketches, diagrams, notes — visual representation only (storyboarding, paper prototyping, wireframes) |
| **High-fidelity** | Functioning representation: throwaway prototypes (discarded after confirmation) or evolutionary prototypes (functionality added iteratively) |
| **Approach** | Think in iterations: present, get feedback, incorporate, repeat |
| **Risk** | Can set unrealistic expectations; stakeholders may focus on aesthetics over functionality |
| **Proposal language** | "We will develop low-fidelity prototypes to validate requirements before full development..." |

### 9. Collaborative Games

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Most engaging and interactive form of elicitation; build joint understanding |
| **Types** | Product boxes, affinity maps, fishbowl approach, gamification techniques |
| **Structure** | Clear purpose, defined process, allow collaboration, close with reaffirming goals |
| **Value** | Capture end-user feedback, open perspectives, encourage equal participation, enable role playing |

### 10. Context Diagrams and Mind Maps

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Identify what you know and don't know about a change effort |
| **Approach** | Place the project in the middle; brainstorm related elements; draw connections |
| **Types** | Mind maps (free-flowing), interface analysis, data flow diagrams |
| **Value** | Gets people on the same page visually; good starting point before meeting stakeholders |

### 11. Process Modelling

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Graphical way to show how processes work; fundamental BA activity |
| **Components** | Activities, events, directional flows, decision points, roles/functions |
| **Core activity** | Model as-is (current) and to-be (future state) to articulate differences |
| **Notations** | Value Stream Mapping (VSM), UML, BPMN, SIPOC, Data Flow Diagrams |
| **Tools** | Visio, whiteboard sketching, modelling software with simulation capabilities |

### 12. Job and Persona Analysis

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Job analysis** | Review job descriptions, competencies, role definitions for understanding functions |
| **Persona analysis** | Create detailed fictional characters who would operate in the solution space |
| **Persona value** | Removes personal attachment; enables free discussion about what a process should be |
| **Limit** | Maximum 3–4 personas to avoid "designing for everybody" |

### 13. Competitive and Market Analysis

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Understand the competitive landscape and external market forces |
| **Key questions** | Who are the competitors? Does the project involve direct customer impact? Does the organisation lead or lag? How does it differentiate? |
| **Includes** | Industry standards and best practices for HR, IT, finance, risk, operations |

## Question Types for Elicitation

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **Open-ended** | Allows free-form response; good for exploring |
| **Closed-ended** | Limited answer choices (forced choice, confirmation); good for validation |
| **Contextual** | About the problem domain or proposed solutions |
| **Context-free** | May be asked in any situation; good lead-ins |

### The Journalist Model

Match questions to the appropriate stakeholder level:

| Question Type | Target Stakeholders |
|---|---|
| **Why** questions | Executives and managers |
| **Who/what/where** questions | Middle-level managers and supervisors |
| **How** questions | Business workers |

## Listening for Requirements — Key Phrases

| Words/Phrases | Possible Requirement Type |
|---|---|
| Has, performs, does | Functional requirement |
| If ..., Sometimes ..., Only when ..., When ... | Business rule |
| Of course ... | Assumption |
| It would be nice ... | Low priority or unnecessary requirement |
| Hopefully, envision, imagine | TO-BE solution possibility |
| Existing, current | AS-IS procedure |
| Quickly | Performance requirement |
| Always, never | Mandatory business rule |
| Save, store, capture | Data requirement |
| Access ... | Nonfunctional requirement |

## Selecting Techniques

Consider:
- **Stakeholder characteristics** — attitude, complexity, culture, experience, influence, location, availability
- **Project approach** — waterfall requires more formal techniques; agile favours lightweight approaches
- **Information type** — strategic (interviews, workshops), operational (observation, document analysis), attitudinal (focus groups, surveys)
- **"The right technique is the one that works"** — use variety; combine techniques for validation
